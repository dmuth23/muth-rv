# RV Classes 101: Class A vs. B vs. C, Gas vs. Diesel, New vs. Used, and 30 ft vs. 35 ft

You've already done the hard part: you toured three rigs at Camping World Lakewood, and your gut told you something useful. The 35-foot Class A is where you want to be; the 30-footer was fine but not exciting; the 25-foot Class C didn't grab you. This document pressure-tests that gut call with real numbers — engine costs, depreciation curves, campsite length limits, and a safety issue that doesn't get talked about enough with two 4-year-olds in the mix — so when you write a check between 2026 and 2028, you know exactly what you're buying and why.

Short version: your instincts are broadly good. Gas-and-used is a well-trodden, defensible path for a family like yours, and 35 feet is a real tradeoff, not a mistake, given how you plan to camp. But where your kids ride deserves ten minutes of attention before you fall in love with a floor plan.

## Class A vs. B vs. C, honestly

**Class B (camper van)** is not a real contender, worth saying rather than skipping. Class Bs run on a cargo van chassis (Sprinter, Transit, ProMaster) — easy to drive and park, meeting full automotive crash standards because the cab is unmodified. But they top out around 200-300 sq ft — no room for two kids' beds, a real dual-monitor desk, six bikes, and boondocking-grade water/power all at once. Rule it out and move on.

**Class C** is built on a cutaway van chassis (usually Ford E-450 or RAM ProMaster), keeping the factory cab — steering wheel, dash, and front seats are the same crash-tested unit Ford or RAM sells in a delivery van (matters more than people realize; see the car-seat section below). Class Cs run 20-35 ft, generally get better mileage than a Class A (10-15 mpg per [Sportsman's RV](https://www.sportsmansrvrentals.com/post/motorhome-class-comparison-class-c-vs-class-a-motorhome-key-differences)), are easier to park, and the over-cab bunk is genuinely great for kids — a built-in loft that doesn't eat living space. Camp Addict calls the Class C "the minivan of the RV world" for this reason ([Camp Addict](https://campaddict.com/free-rv-camping/rv-class-a-vs-b-vs-c-which-motorhome-type-is-actually-better-for-your-2026-budget/)). The 25-footer you toured was almost certainly too short for your bike count and a real home office; a 30-33 ft Class C would have been the fairer comparison, and you didn't see one that size — not a dealbreaker for the Class A plan, but worth one more look if you want the data point.

**Class A** is the bus-shaped rig you've fallen for, and for good reason: real garage/basement storage for six bikes, room for a proper two-monitor desk, tank and battery capacity that scales to genuine boondocking, and living space that doesn't feel like camping on a Tuesday video call. The tradeoffs: driving experience (wider turning radius, taller, more wind-sensitive) and — critically — a cab that is not automotive-grade (more below). Riverside Camping Center has a good plain-English overview of the size/space/price differences ([Riverside Camping Center](https://riversidecampingcenter.com/class-a-vs-class-c-motorhome)).

**Bottom line on class:** Class A is a reasonable choice for a family that wants to live in the rig for full workdays and haul serious gear, not just camp in it. You're not wrong to want the 35-footer — just read the seatbelt section below before you fall in love with a specific floor plan.

## Gas vs. diesel: does your gas decision hold up?

Yes — and the numbers back it more strongly than most brochures suggest.

**Fuel economy** is closer than people expect. Gas Class As run roughly 6-10 mpg; diesel pushers run roughly 7-14 mpg ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)). But diesel typically costs more per gallon than 87-octane gas, plus you're paying for DEF fluid, so **per-mile fuel cost ends up roughly a wash** — diesel's mpg advantage is mostly offset at the pump.

**Purchase price is the real gap, and it's enormous.** Entry-level gas Class As run about $100,000-$135,000 new; entry-level diesel pushers start around $225,000-$300,000 new. Direct, same-brand example: Winnebago's diesel Inspire 34AE starts around $379,419 versus the gas Adventurer 35F at $257,575 — a gap of roughly $122,000 ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)). That gap compresses in the used market but doesn't disappear, which is exactly why "gas, used" gets dramatically more coach for the same money than "diesel, used."

**Maintenance costs favor gas decisively.** A gas Class A oil change runs about $150-$200; the same service on a diesel pusher runs $400-$450+ due to larger oil capacity and extra filtration ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)). Diesel engines do outlast gas engines on paper — 300,000-500,000 miles versus 150,000-200,000 for gas — but you will never come close to that kind of mileage on a family rig used for road trips and long weekends. The longevity edge is real but wasted on your use case.

**Engine specifics that matter for a 2026-2028 purchase window.** The Winnebago models you like (Sunstar, Adventurer, Vista) are all built on the **Ford F-53 chassis**. Ford replaced the old **Triton V10** with a **7.3L PFI V8** starting around 2020-2021. The 7.3L V8 is the simpler, newer design — fewer moving parts than the V10, and Ford extended the oil-change interval from roughly 7,000 to 10,000 miles with synthetic oil, plus an oil-life monitor ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)). Practically: **favor a used rig with the 7.3L V8 (model year 2021+) over an older V10 unit** if the price gap is reasonable — mechanically newer, lower-maintenance, and increasingly available as your window progresses.

**Where diesel genuinely wins**, for completeness: quieter cab, better mountain performance via a built-in engine (jake) brake, generally higher payload, and smoother air-ride/independent front suspension. None of this is decisive for campground road trips out of central New Jersey rather than crossing the Rockies weekly. iRV2's regulars generally agree the decision comes down to use case and floor plan first, budget second ([iRV2 forums](https://www.irv2.com/threads/gas-versus-diesel-class-as.1991191/)).

**Bottom line on gas vs. diesel:** your gas choice is correct for this family. The fuel-cost argument for diesel is largely a myth once you account for DEF and diesel's price premium; the purchase-price and maintenance-cost gaps are real and large; and you'll never rack up enough miles for diesel's longevity edge to pay off.

## New vs. used: does your used decision hold up?

Also yes, and depreciation is the reason why. Class A motorhomes lose roughly **20-30% of value in year one alone**, then another **5-10% per year in years 2-5** ([MyDepreciation](https://www.mydepreciation.org/calculators/rv); [RV Loan Wizard](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/rv-depreciation-by-type-and-age)). Concrete example: an $85,000 new Class A is worth roughly $68,000 after year one and around $45,000 after five years — **about 47% of its value gone**, most of it front-loaded into the first two. A $150,000 unit can be worth roughly $75,000 after five years — a straight 50% loss.

Practical implication for your timeline: since you're not buying until late 2026 at the earliest, and possibly not until 2028, shop specifically for coaches that are **already 3-6 years old** by the time you buy — someone else has already absorbed the worst of the curve, and you're buying past the steepest part of the drop, not the cliff.

**One caution that matters for a first RV purchase:** budget for a professional pre-purchase inspection regardless of how good the coach looks. NRVIA-certified inspectors specifically check for water intrusion (soft floors, delaminated sidewalls, mold near windows/vents), roof seal condition, and mechanical issues a walkthrough won't catch; a full inspection typically takes 2-6 hours ([Camper Report](https://camperreport.com/pdi-inspection-checklist/); [Happily RV](https://www.happilyrv.com/buying-used-motorhome-inspection-checklist/)). Water damage is the single most expensive thing to discover after closing — worth the $200-$500 an independent inspection costs.

**Bottom line on new vs. used:** used is the financially obvious call. You're not sacrificing much by skipping "new car smell" — you're skipping the two years where the previous owner ate 30-40% of the sticker price on your behalf.

## 30 ft vs. 35 ft: the real tradeoff

This is the one area where the numbers push back on your stated preference, so it's worth laying out plainly rather than softening it.

**Length limits are a real constraint, and 30 feet is the practical threshold.** Roughly 80-90% of state park campsites nationwide accommodate rigs up to 30 feet; past that, restrictions become "meaningful" — you're filtering for specific loops rather than booking freely ([America's State Parks](https://www.americasstateparks.org/rv-sizes-at-state-parks/)). At 35-40 feet, "many state parks max out at 35 feet." In national parks, 84% of campgrounds accept RVs up to 29 ft, dropping to 73% at 35 ft ([RVshare](https://rvshare.com/blog/national-parks-rv-length-limits/); [America's State Parks](https://www.americasstateparks.org/rv-sizes-at-state-parks/)). New Jersey and the Northeast skew *more* restrictive — Vermont, New Hampshire, and Maine often cap at 20-30 feet, with tighter, more wooded loops than newer Sun Belt parks.

**But** — and this should reassure you — your stated usage is "mostly campgrounds with electric/water hookups," pointing toward private campgrounds and RV resorts far more than backcountry state park loops. Private campgrounds are overwhelmingly built for 35-40+ ft rigs with pull-through sites, since that's their paying customer base. The length-limit problem is concentrated in older state/national parks, not the hookup-campground world you'll live in most weekends.

**What 35 ft buys you**: meaningfully more basement/garage storage for six bikes (a 30-footer's bays are noticeably tighter), room for a real two-monitor desk without eating into living space, and — for a family of four living in the rig for stretches — more separation between a work-call zone and a kids'-play zone. The 30-footer you called "acceptable" is likely acceptable specifically because it's more cramped on exactly those dimensions.

**What 35 ft costs you**: harder backing into tight or wooded sites (30-35 ft rigs are "pretty safe in most parks if you plan ahead," per RVshare, but angles, trees, and uneven ground get harder past 35 ft), a wider/longer turning radius on narrow Northeast roads, and heavier curb weight, which interacts with the payload issue below.

**Bottom line on length:** 35 ft is a legitimate choice for your family's actual usage pattern, not an oversized indulgence — but expect to occasionally skip a state park loop or call ahead to confirm site length, especially in the Northeast. If you end up boondocking on public land more than expected, that's the scenario where the 30-footer's flexibility matters more.

## Cargo capacity: the number nobody shows you on the lot

This doesn't come up on a showroom walkthrough, but it directly affects a family carrying six bikes plus boondocking supplies, so it belongs here.

Every RV has a sticker (usually inside a cabinet or the driver's door) listing GVWR (max total weight) and CCC or OCCC (Cargo Carrying Capacity / Occupant and Cargo Carrying Capacity — how much you're allowed to add: people, water, gear, bikes). **Gas Class As are specifically called out as one of the worst offenders for low cargo capacity** — commonly only 2,000-4,000 lbs of OCCC, versus much higher figures on diesel pushers ([Camp Addict](https://campaddict.com/free-rv-camping/rv-class-a-vs-b-vs-c-comparing-fuel-efficiency-payload-capacity-and-long-term-maintenance-costs/); [iRV2](https://www.irv2.com/threads/class-a-motorhome-low-payload-numbers.2051780/)). That number has to cover two adults, two kids, a full fresh water tank for boondocking (water is 8.3 lbs/gallon — a 60-gallon tank is 500 lbs), propane, food, clothes, work gear, and six bikes plus rack. It adds up fast, and manufacturers have little incentive to be generous — capacity is largely their discretion as long as tire ratings are met ([RV Travel](https://www.rvtravel.com/cargo-capacity-nightmare-1077/)).

**This is a high-stakes item, not a nice-to-know.** Overloading a motorhome is a safety issue (tire failure, brake strain, handling problems at speed) and, after an accident, a potential liability/legal problem if you're found over GVWR. Before buying any specific used unit — 30 or 35 ft — **find the yellow cargo-capacity sticker and do the math on your actual load**: people + full water + propane + six bikes + gear. If it's tight, that's a real factor in choosing between coaches, worth checking at every serious showing.

## Where your kids actually ride: the safety issue that needs airtime

This is the one item here that isn't primarily about money — it's about where two 4-year-olds sit while the rig is moving, and it deserves more attention than it typically gets in RV shopping.

**Class A motorhome cabs are not built to automotive crash standards the way a car, minivan, or Class C cab is.** A Class C keeps the factory Ford or RAM van cab — the same crash-tested structure as any delivery van on the road. A Class A cab is built by the RV manufacturer on a bare chassis, and critically: **seatbelt anchorages in the living area are not required to meet the federal crashworthiness standard (FMVSS 208) that governs regular car seats**, and in some documented cases those "seatbelts" are anchored into a piece of board with no real structural backing ([SafeRide4Kids](https://saferide4kids.com/blog/traveling-children-rvs/); [MARVAC](https://www.michiganrvandcampgrounds.org/child-passenger-safety-in-motorized-rvs/)). **LATCH anchors — required in every regular passenger vehicle since 2002 — are not required in RVs at all.** Many belted seats in a Class A living area are side-facing or rear-facing bench/dinette seating, and child restraints are only rated for forward-facing installation, which rules many seats out regardless of belt quality.

The good news, specific to the brand you like: **Winnebago's own materials confirm that some of their Class A models — they specifically name the Adventurer — include "a child seat tether anchor in a forward-facing dinette seat," in addition to the cab's factory three-point belts** ([Winnebago](https://www.winnebago.com/lifestyle/winnebagolife/life/buckling-up-kids-in-an-rv-1)). So it's not a lost cause — but it is **floor-plan-specific, not universal**, so don't assume any given used Adventurer, Sunstar, or Vista has a crash-rated forward-facing spot for two child seats.

**Make this a checklist item, not an afterthought.** When you walk a specific coach, physically count forward-facing, factory three-point-belted seating positions (cab captain's chairs plus any tethered dinette/sofa spots) and confirm two child seats actually fit. This matters even more if you plan to flat-tow a toad (below) — every family member, including both kids, then rides in the coach for the entire drive, every day. Raise it with the dealer directly and confirm it yourself: "sleeps 6" and "seats 6 safely while moving" are different claims, and only the second matters here.

## Towing a toad: what changes with two 4-year-olds

A "toad" is a car flat-towed behind the motorhome (all four wheels down, no dolly) so you have a car to drive once you're set up at a campsite instead of breaking camp every time you need groceries. **The case for one** is strong for your use case: motorhomes are a pain to detach from hookups for a milk run, a toad lets you leave the coach set up, and it offsets the fuel cost of local sightseeing versus driving an 8-mpg motorhome around town ([Mortons on the Move](https://www.mortonsonthemove.com/rv-toad/); RV LIFE). Popular family-friendly toads are mid-size SUVs like a Chevy Equinox or Ford Escape — chosen because they flat-tow well and still have a real back seat.

**The case against, specific to your family:** you cannot back up with a toad attached without disconnecting it, so every tight pull-in, gas stop, or wrong turn requires a pull-through site or a stop-and-unhook — a 35 ft coach plus toad becomes a 45+ ft combined rig for parking and fuel stops. It adds length and weight to what's already the largest vehicle you've driven, increasing braking distance and turning radius further. It's genuinely more setup/teardown friction with two 4-year-olds in the mix — one more thing between "we're ready" and "why haven't we left yet." And there's a bike storage tension: a rear-mounted bike rack typically competes with the hitch a toad needs, and flat-tow setups generally preclude a rear hitch rack entirely — you may need to keep bikes in basement/garage storage instead and leave the hitch free for towing.

**For boondocking specifically**, a toad becomes more valuable, not less — once you're set up at a dispersed site with no hookups, you don't want to break down solar/leveling just to find drinking water. That's a point in favor of planning for a toad eventually, even if not on every trip.

**Bottom line on towing:** don't treat this as day-one. Buy the coach first, get comfortable driving and camping in it for a season, confirm your actual boondocking pattern, then decide whether a toad earns its keep. If you add one, prioritize an SUV that flat-tows well and doesn't force a rear-hitch-vs-bike-rack conflict.

## Bottom line for this family

- **Class A is the right class** for a family that wants to live and work in the rig, not just camp in it — the storage, desk space, and boondocking capacity you need don't fit in a Class B, and you'd need a 30+ ft Class C for comparable space, which you haven't test-driven yet.
- **Gas is the right engine choice.** The fuel-cost gap versus diesel is smaller than it looks once you account for DEF and diesel's price premium; the $100K+ purchase-price gap and roughly 2-3x maintenance cost are real and decisive for a family that won't put diesel-scale mileage on the rig. Favor the 7.3L V8 (2021+ Ford F-53) over the older V10 when comparing used units.
- **Used is the right call.** You're skipping the 30-47% of value a new buyer burns off in the first five years; target coaches already 3-6 years old by your purchase date, buying past the steepest part of the depreciation curve. Budget for an independent pre-purchase inspection regardless of how good a coach looks.
- **35 ft is a defensible target, not an overreach**, given your camping will be mostly hookup campgrounds rather than tight state-park loops — but expect to occasionally skip a park or call ahead, especially in the Northeast, and remember a 35 ft coach plus a future toad becomes a 45+ ft combined rig to maneuver.
- **Before you fall for a floor plan, check two stickers most buyers never look at:** the seatbelt/tether layout (two real forward-facing, properly anchored spots for the kids?) and the cargo-capacity (yellow) sticker (people + full water + six bikes + gear actually fit under the rated limit?). Both are coach-specific, not brand-guaranteed, and matter more here than horsepower or floor-plan aesthetics.
- **Hold off on the toad decision** until you've lived with the coach for a season — a real quality-of-life upgrade for boondocking, but it adds length, weight, and a bike-storage conflict you don't need to solve on day one.
