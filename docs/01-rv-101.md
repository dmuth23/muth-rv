# RV Classes 101: Class A vs. B vs. C, Gas vs. Diesel, New vs. Used, and 30 ft vs. 35 ft

You've already done part of the hard work: you toured rigs at a local dealer, and your gut told you something useful. The 35-foot Class A felt like the right size; the 30-footer was fine but not exciting; the 25-foot Class C felt too small. This document pressure-tests that gut call with real numbers — engine costs, depreciation curves, campsite length limits, and a safety issue that doesn't get talked about enough with young kids in the mix — so when you write a check between late 2026 and 2028, you know exactly what you're buying and why.

This is the map, not the destination. Where the choice deserves a full deep dive of its own, this page hands you off: **diesel vs. gas** gets its own head-to-head in the [diesel primer](17-diesel-primer.html); the **large Class C** as a serious alternative is worked through in the [Class C landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html); whether to **tow a car** — and whether either of your current cars can be towed — is its own doc, [towing a car](19-towing-a-car.html); and the fact that **Sara is unlikely to want to drive a Class A** is a big enough decision-driver to have its own page, [the one-driver family](22-one-driver-family.html).

Short version, updated for July 2026: your instincts are reasonable, but nothing here is a foregone conclusion. Gas-and-used is a well-trodden, defensible path for a family like yours — but diesel is no longer ruled out, and a 30–33 ft Class C is a genuinely different animal from the cramped 25-footer that didn't grab you. Where your kids ride deserves ten minutes of attention before you fall in love with any floor plan.

## Class A vs. B vs. C, honestly

**Class B (camper van)** is not a real contender, worth saying rather than skipping. Class Bs run on a cargo van chassis (Sprinter, Transit, ProMaster) — easy to drive and park, meeting full automotive crash standards because the cab is unmodified. But they top out around 200-300 sq ft — no room for two kids' beds, a real dual-monitor desk, six bikes, and boondocking-grade water/power all at once. Rule it out and move on.

**Class C** is built on a cutaway van chassis (usually Ford E-450 or RAM ProMaster), keeping the factory cab — steering wheel, dash, and front seats are the same crash-tested unit Ford or RAM sells in a delivery van (matters more than people realize; see the car-seat section below). Class Cs run 20-35 ft, generally get better mileage than a Class A, and the over-cab bunk is genuinely great for kids — a built-in loft that doesn't eat living space. And critically, a Class C **drives more like a van or a small moving truck**, whereas a larger Class A is "more similar to driving a bus" ([Camping World](https://blog.campingworld.com/find-your-rv/class-a-vs-class-c-motorhomes/)) — a difference that matters a great deal for a second, less-confident driver. That last point is the one to sit with. The 25-footer that felt too small was too short for your bike count and a real home office — but a **30-33 ft Class C is a fundamentally different animal**, and you never toured one that size. It's the closest thing to Class-A living space you can get while keeping a factory cab and a driving experience Sara might actually be comfortable with. Because this cuts to a real decision for your family, it has its own two docs: the [Class C landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html) works through which 30 ft+ models fit the profile, and [the one-driver family](22-one-driver-family.html) treats the driving-comfort question head-on.

**Class A** is the bus-shaped rig you've fallen for, and for good reason: real garage/basement storage for six bikes, room for a proper two-monitor desk, tank and battery capacity that scales to genuine boondocking, and living space that doesn't feel like camping on a Tuesday video call. The tradeoffs: driving experience (wider turning radius, taller, more wind-sensitive) and — critically — a cab that is not automotive-grade (more below). Riverside Camping Center has a good plain-English overview of the size/space/price differences ([Riverside Camping Center](https://riversidecampingcenter.com/class-a-vs-class-c-motorhome)).

**Bottom line on class:** Class A is a strong fit for a family that wants to live in the rig for full workdays and haul serious gear, not just camp in it. But a 30-33 ft Class C is a legitimate alternative on the table — not a consolation prize — especially given that Sara is unlikely to want to drive a full Class A. Read the seatbelt section below before you fall in love with any specific floor plan, and read [the one-driver family](22-one-driver-family.html) before you assume the Class A is settled.

## Gas vs. diesel: an honest comparison, not a closed case

Earlier drafts of this docket treated gas as decided. That's changed: **diesel is no longer excluded.** The initial cost is much higher and a diesel is not something Doug would wrench on himself — but since realistically most maintenance goes to a shop either way, the "I can't DIY it" objection is weaker than it first looks. The goal now is to be an informed buyer, so that if a genuinely good used diesel appears in your window you're not walking away out of ignorance. This section is the summary; the full head-to-head — including what to inspect on a used diesel and the specific engine/chassis families to know — lives in the [diesel primer](17-diesel-primer.html).

**Fuel economy** is closer than people expect. Gas Class As run roughly 6-10 mpg; diesel pushers run roughly 7-14 mpg ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)). But diesel typically costs more per gallon than 87-octane gas, plus you're paying for DEF fluid (a recurring cost on newer diesels; [Camping World](https://blog.campingworld.com/find-your-rv/diesel-rv-vs-gas-rv/)), so **per-mile fuel cost ends up roughly a wash** — diesel's mpg advantage is mostly offset at the pump.

**Purchase price is the biggest gap, and it's large.** Direct, same-brand new example: Winnebago's diesel Inspire 34AE starts around $379,419 versus the gas Adventurer 35F at $257,575 — a gap of roughly $122,000 ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)). Another same-brand cut: Winnebago's diesel Forza starts around $361,072 versus the gas Vista/Sunstar at roughly $208,885 — again well over $100,000 apart ([Lichtsinn RV](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/diesel-pusher-versus-gas-motorhome/)). This gap compresses in the used market but doesn't vanish, which is exactly why "gas, used" gets more coach for the same money than "diesel, used" — a real consideration against your $100k-150k financing target (as of July 2026). The counterpoint the diesel primer develops: an older, higher-end diesel pusher that has already taken its depreciation hit can sometimes land *inside* that band and deliver build quality a same-price gas coach can't.

**Maintenance costs favor gas.** A gas Class A oil change runs about $150-$200; the same service on a diesel pusher runs $400-$450+ due to larger oil capacity and extra filtration ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)). Diesels also carry larger, pricier tires and the DEF system. Diesel engines do outlast gas engines on paper — 300,000-500,000 miles versus 150,000-200,000 for gas ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)) — but you're unlikely to come close to that mileage on a family rig used for road trips and long weekends, so the longevity edge is real but largely wasted on your use case.

**Gas engine specifics for a late-2026-to-2028 window.** Common gas Class A models (Winnebago Sunstar, Adventurer, Vista) are built on the **Ford F-53 chassis**. Ford replaced the old **Triton V10** with a **7.3L PFI V8** starting around 2020-2021. The 7.3L V8 is the simpler, newer design — fewer moving parts than the V10, with a longer synthetic-oil interval and an oil-life monitor ([RV LIFE](https://rvlife.com/gas-or-diesel-motorhome/)). Practically: on the gas side, **favor a used rig with the 7.3L V8 (model year 2021+) over an older V10 unit** when the price gap is reasonable.

**Where diesel genuinely wins:** quieter cab, stronger mountain-grade performance via a built-in engine (jake) brake, generally higher payload — which directly helps the cargo-capacity problem below — smoother air-ride suspension, and substantially more towing capacity (a gas Class A tows around 5,000 lbs; a diesel pusher 10,000-15,000 lbs; [Camping World](https://blog.campingworld.com/find-your-rv/diesel-rv-vs-gas-rv/)). Camping World's own rule of thumb: if you're out a few weekends a year, a gas Class A may be worth the savings; if you're full-timing, diesel may be the better fit ([Camping World](https://blog.campingworld.com/find-your-rv/diesel-rv-vs-gas-rv/)) — which lands your weekends-and-long-trips family on the gas side by default.

**Bottom line on gas vs. diesel:** for how this family plans to camp — hookup campgrounds out of central New Jersey, not weekly Rocky Mountain crossings — **gas remains the sensible default**: lower buy-in, lower maintenance, and you can wrench on it yourself. But diesel is a legitimate option if a well-kept used pusher lands in budget, and the [diesel primer](17-diesel-primer.html) exists so you can evaluate one on the merits rather than dismiss it. The higher payload alone can matter for a family carrying six bikes and full water.

## New vs. used: does your used decision hold up?

Also yes, and depreciation is the reason why. Class A motorhomes lose roughly **20-30% of value in year one alone**, then another **5-10% per year in years 2-5** ([MyDepreciation](https://www.mydepreciation.org/calculators/rv); [RV Loan Wizard](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/rv-depreciation-by-type-and-age)). Concrete example: an $85,000 new Class A is worth roughly $68,000 after year one and around $45,000 after five years — **about 47% of its value gone**, most of it front-loaded into the first two. A $150,000 unit can be worth roughly $75,000 after five years — a straight 50% loss.

Practical implication for your timeline: since you're not buying until late 2026 at the earliest, and possibly not until 2028, shop specifically for coaches that are **already 3-6 years old** by the time you buy — someone else has already absorbed the worst of the curve, and you're buying past the steepest part of the drop, not the cliff.

**One caution that matters for a first RV purchase:** budget for a professional pre-purchase inspection regardless of how good the coach looks. A thorough inspection should check for water intrusion (soft floors, delaminated sidewalls, mold near windows/vents), roof seal condition, and mechanical issues a walkthrough won't catch ([Camper Report](https://camperreport.com/pdi-inspection-checklist/); [Happily RV](https://www.happilyrv.com/buying-used-motorhome-inspection-checklist/)) — this substance is well supported by both sources, though neither is itself a description of a professional NRVIA-certified inspection service, and neither states an inspection duration. Water damage is the single most expensive thing to discover after closing. On cost: figures commonly cited for these DIY checklists put a pro inspection around $200-$500, but that number is reported but unverified here — other sources found during fact-checking (RVShare, CozyCamperATX) put real NRVIA-certified motorhome inspections meaningfully higher, roughly $400-$1,500+ and sometimes $2,500+, so verify current pricing before budgeting rather than relying on a single figure.

**Bottom line on new vs. used:** used is the financially obvious call. You're not sacrificing much by skipping "new car smell" — you're skipping the two years where the previous owner ate 30-40% of the sticker price on your behalf.

## 30 ft vs. 35 ft: the real tradeoff

This is the one area where the numbers push back on your stated preference, so it's worth laying out plainly rather than softening it.

**Length limits are a real constraint, and 30 feet is the practical threshold.** Roughly 80-90% of state park campsites nationwide accommodate rigs up to 30 feet; past that, restrictions become "meaningful" — you're filtering for specific loops rather than booking freely ([America's State Parks](https://www.americasstateparks.org/rv-sizes-at-state-parks/)). At 35-40 feet, "many state parks max out at 35 feet." In national parks, 84% of campgrounds accept RVs up to 29 ft, dropping to 73% at 35 ft ([RVshare](https://rvshare.com/blog/national-parks-rv-length-limits/); [America's State Parks](https://www.americasstateparks.org/rv-sizes-at-state-parks/)). New Jersey and the Northeast skew *more* restrictive — Vermont, New Hampshire, and Maine often cap at 20-30 feet, with tighter, more wooded loops than newer Sun Belt parks.

**But** — and this should reassure you — your stated usage is "mostly campgrounds with electric/water hookups," pointing toward private campgrounds and RV resorts far more than backcountry state park loops. Private campgrounds are overwhelmingly built for 35-40+ ft rigs with pull-through sites, since that's their paying customer base. The length-limit problem is concentrated in older state/national parks, not the hookup-campground world you'll live in most weekends.

**What 35 ft buys you**: meaningfully more basement/garage storage for six bikes (a 30-footer's bays are noticeably tighter), room for a real two-monitor desk without eating into living space, and — for a family of four living in the rig for stretches — more separation between a work-call zone and a kids'-play zone. The 30-footer you called "acceptable" is likely acceptable specifically because it's more cramped on exactly those dimensions.

**What 35 ft costs you**: harder backing into tight or wooded sites (30-35 ft rigs are "pretty safe in most parks if you plan ahead," per RVshare, but angles, trees, and uneven ground get harder past 35 ft), a wider/longer turning radius on narrow Northeast roads, and heavier curb weight, which interacts with the payload issue below.

**Bottom line on length:** 35 ft is a legitimate choice for your family's actual usage pattern, not an oversized indulgence — but expect to occasionally skip a state park loop or call ahead to confirm site length, especially in the Northeast. If you end up boondocking on public land more than expected, that's the scenario where the 30-footer's flexibility matters more.

## Cargo capacity: the number nobody shows you on the lot

This doesn't come up on a showroom walkthrough, but it directly affects a family carrying six bikes plus boondocking supplies, so it belongs here.

Every RV has a sticker (usually inside a cabinet or the driver's door) listing GVWR (max total weight) and CCC or OCCC (Cargo Carrying Capacity / Occupant and Cargo Carrying Capacity — how much you're allowed to add: people, water, gear, bikes). **Gas Class As are specifically called out as one of the worst offenders for low cargo capacity** — commonly only 2,000-4,000 lbs of OCCC, versus much higher figures on diesel pushers ([Camp Addict](https://campaddict.com/free-rv-camping/rv-class-a-vs-b-vs-c-comparing-fuel-efficiency-payload-capacity-and-long-term-maintenance-costs/); [iRV2](https://www.irv2.com/threads/class-a-motorhome-low-payload-numbers.2051780/)). That number has to cover two adults, two kids, a full fresh water tank for boondocking (water is 8.3 lbs/gallon — a 60-gallon tank is 500 lbs), propane, food, clothes, work gear, and six bikes plus rack. It adds up fast, and manufacturers have little incentive to be generous — capacity is largely their discretion as long as tire ratings are met ([RV Travel](https://www.rvtravel.com/cargo-capacity-nightmare-1077/)).

**This is a high-stakes item, not a nice-to-know.** Overloading a motorhome is a well-established safety issue — tire failure, brake strain, and handling problems at speed are physically well-documented and consistent with real-world overloaded-coach reports ([Camp Addict](https://campaddict.com/free-rv-camping/rv-class-a-vs-b-vs-c-comparing-fuel-efficiency-payload-capacity-and-long-term-maintenance-costs/)). The claim that being found over GVWR after an accident creates a specific liability/legal problem is reported but unverified here — it varies by state and insurer and wasn't confirmed against a statute, policy clause, or case law, so verify before relying on this if it matters to your decision. Before buying any specific used unit — 30 or 35 ft — **find the yellow cargo-capacity sticker and do the math on your actual load**: people + full water + propane + six bikes + gear. If it's tight, that's a real factor in choosing between coaches, worth checking at every serious showing.

## Where your kids actually ride: the safety issue that needs airtime

This is the one item here that isn't primarily about money — it's about where the kids sit while the rig is moving, and it deserves more attention than it typically gets in RV shopping. (The full legal picture — what New Jersey and federal law actually *require* versus what safety experts *recommend*, and how it shifts as the kids move from 4 to 6 across your shopping window — is worked through in [family and kids](05-family-and-kids.html); this is the class-shopping summary.)

**Class A motorhome cabs are not built to automotive crash standards the way a car, minivan, or Class C cab is.** A Class C keeps the factory Ford or RAM van cab — the same crash-tested structure as any delivery van on the road. A Class A cab is built by the RV manufacturer on a bare chassis, and critically: **seatbelt anchorages in the living area are not required to meet the federal crashworthiness standard (FMVSS 208) that governs regular car seats**, and in some documented cases those "seatbelts" are anchored into a piece of board with no real structural backing ([SafeRide4Kids](https://saferide4kids.com/blog/traveling-children-rvs/); [MARVAC](https://www.michiganrvandcampgrounds.org/child-passenger-safety-in-motorized-rvs/)). **LATCH anchors — required in every regular passenger vehicle since 2002 — are not required in RVs at all.** Many belted seats in a Class A living area are side-facing or rear-facing bench/dinette seating, and child restraints are only rated for forward-facing installation, which rules many seats out regardless of belt quality.

The good news, specific to the brand you like: **Winnebago's own materials confirm that some of their Class A models — they specifically name the Adventurer — include "a child seat tether anchor in a forward-facing dinette seat," in addition to the cab's factory three-point belts** ([Winnebago](https://www.winnebago.com/lifestyle/winnebagolife/life/buckling-up-kids-in-an-rv-1)). So it's not a lost cause — but it is **floor-plan-specific, not universal**, so don't assume any given used Adventurer, Sunstar, or Vista has a crash-rated forward-facing spot for two child seats.

**Make this a checklist item, not an afterthought.** When you walk a specific coach, physically count forward-facing, factory three-point-belted seating positions (cab captain's chairs plus any tethered dinette/sofa spots) and confirm both kids' seats actually fit. This matters even more if you tow a car (below) — every family member, including both kids, then rides in the coach for the entire drive, every day. It's also a real point in the Class A vs. large Class C comparison: a Class C keeps a factory, crash-tested cab, so its front belted positions carry a different pedigree than a Class-A-built cab. Raise it with the dealer directly and confirm it yourself: "sleeps 6" and "seats 6 safely while moving" are different claims, and only the second matters here.

## Towing a car (a "toad"): now its own question

A "toad" is a car towed behind the motorhome — flat-towed on all four wheels, on a dolly, or on a trailer — so you have a vehicle to drive once you're set up instead of breaking camp every time you need groceries. This used to be an afterthought in this docket; it isn't anymore, because you already own two candidate vehicles (a 2017 Toyota RAV4 Limited AWD and a 2022 Hyundai Tucson) and whether *either* can be towed — and by which method — is a specific, checkable question with real safety and warranty stakes. All of that now lives in its own doc, [towing a car](19-towing-a-car.html): flat-tow vs. dolly vs. trailer vs. buy-a-purpose-toad vs. no toad, and what each of your cars can and can't do.

The short version to carry into the rest of this page: **the case for a toad is strong** — motorhomes are a pain to detach from hookups for a milk run, and a toad offsets the fuel cost of local sightseeing versus driving an 8-mpg coach around town ([Mortons on the Move](https://www.mortonsonthemove.com/rv-toad/)). But it has two costs that ripple through the class decision. First, **you cannot back up with a flat-towed car attached**, so a 35 ft coach plus toad becomes a 45+ ft combined rig you can only take forward — a real maneuvering penalty on top of an already-large Class A. Second, there's a **bike-storage tension**: a rear-mounted bike rack competes with the hitch a toad needs, so with a toad you'll likely keep bikes in basement/garage storage and leave the hitch free (worked through in [carrying bikes](08-carrying-bikes.html)). For boondocking a toad becomes *more* valuable, not less — once you're set up at a dispersed site you don't want to break down solar and leveling just to find drinking water.

Note how this interacts with the class choice: a Class C you can comfortably drive and park may make you *want* a toad less (you can more easily take the whole rig to the trailhead), while a large Class A more or less assumes one. Don't treat the toad as a day-one decision — but do read [towing a car](19-towing-a-car.html) before assuming your RAV4 or Tucson can come along, because towing an unsuitable vehicle the wrong way can damage its transmission.

## Bottom line for this family

- **Class A and a large (30-33 ft) Class C are both live options** for a family that wants to live and work in the rig, not just camp in it — a Class B is too small for six bikes, a real desk, and boondocking capacity. The Class A gives you the most living space and storage; the large Class C keeps a factory crash-tested cab and drives far more like a van, which is the single biggest argument if Sara is going to share the driving. Don't lock this in from the 25-footer that felt cramped — see the [Class C landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html) and [the one-driver family](22-one-driver-family.html).
- **Gas is the sensible default, but diesel is no longer excluded.** Gas means lower buy-in, lower maintenance, and DIY-able service. Diesel costs much more up front and you wouldn't wrench on it — but most maintenance goes to a shop anyway, and a well-kept used pusher that's taken its depreciation can occasionally land in budget with build quality and payload a gas coach can't match. On the gas side, favor the 7.3L V8 (2021+ Ford F-53) over the older V10. Evaluate any diesel on the merits using the [diesel primer](17-diesel-primer.html).
- **Used is the right call.** You're skipping the 30-47% of value a new buyer burns off in the first five years; target coaches already 3-6 years old by your purchase date, buying past the steepest part of the depreciation curve. Budget for an independent pre-purchase inspection regardless of how good a coach looks.
- **35 ft is a defensible target, not an overreach**, given your camping will be mostly hookup campgrounds rather than tight state-park loops — but expect to occasionally skip a park or call ahead, especially in the Northeast, and remember a 35 ft coach plus a towed car becomes a 45+ ft combined rig to maneuver.
- **Before you fall for a floor plan, check two stickers most buyers never look at:** the seatbelt/tether layout (two real forward-facing, properly anchored spots for the kids?) and the cargo-capacity (yellow) sticker (people + full water + six bikes + gear actually fit under the rated limit?). Both are coach-specific, not brand-guaranteed, and matter more here than horsepower or floor-plan aesthetics.
- **The towing-a-car decision is now its own doc.** You already own two candidate vehicles, so before assuming your RAV4 or Tucson can come along, read [towing a car](19-towing-a-car.html) — towing an unsuitable car the wrong way can damage its transmission, and the choice interacts with both the class decision and where your bikes live.
