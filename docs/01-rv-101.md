# RV Classes 101: How We Got to Class C, and What's Still Open

This document used to be a live comparison — Class A vs. B vs. C, gas vs. diesel, 30 ft vs. 35
ft — written while the class decision was still open. As of July 2026, **it isn't open
anymore: the family has decided on a used, gas Class C.** This page is now the record of how
that decision got made, what it cost to give up the Class A dream, and — honestly — the one
big question it didn't answer.

If you want the earlier open-comparison research intact, it's preserved in the
[archive](archive/index.html): the [gas Class A model landscape](archive/02-model-shortlist.html)
and the [diesel pusher primer](archive/17-diesel-primer.html) are both still there, unedited,
for reference.

## The pivot: why Class A came off the table

The instinct that drove this docket for months was real — a 35-foot Class A, toured in
person, felt like the right size. Big living space, a proper two-monitor desk, garage storage
for six bikes, tank and battery capacity that scales to real boondocking. Nothing about that
instinct was wrong for a family that wants to *live* in a rig, not just camp in one.

What changed the decision wasn't the Class A losing an argument on its own turf. It was the
**mission getting re-centered.** Earlier drafts of this docket assumed hookup campgrounds would
dominate the family's camping, with boondocking as a nice-to-have extra. That's no longer the
frame: the family now wants a rig that can genuinely reach and camp at **national and state
parks across the country** — Sequoia, Canyonlands, the kind of places that are the point of
having an RV in the first place, not just a weekend loop near Brick. And that mission runs
straight into a hard physical fact: **national and state park campgrounds skew short, and a
meaningful share of loops and sites cap out well under a 35-foot coach's length** — the
specific numbers, and how they change the calculus between a 30-footer and a 35-footer, are
worked through in the length-limits research (forthcoming in this docket; see [Decisions &
Open Questions](16-open-questions.html) for the current state of that analysis). The Class A's
signature strength — more length buys more living space and storage — is also its liability
against that specific mission. A big rig that has to skip loops, book months ahead for the few
long pull-throughs, or park a mile from the trailhead isn't actually delivering the
cross-country park access the family is buying an RV for.

That's the pivot, stated plainly: **it was never that Class A is a bad rig. It's that the
mission changed underneath it**, from "comfortable home base with some travel" to "a rig built
to reach the parks," and a Class A is the wrong shape for that mission at any length that still
feels like "living" rather than "camping."

## What a Class A offered, and what got consciously given up

It's worth naming what's actually being given up, rather than pretending the Class A was never
a real contender — it was, for most of this docket's history.

- **Living space and garage storage.** A 35-foot Class A's basement bays are simply bigger than
  anything a Class C offers — more room for six bikes without a rack fight, more separation
  between a work-call zone and a kids'-play zone. The full model-by-model picture of what that
  space looked like across the gas Class A market is preserved in the
  [archived Class A landscape](archive/02-model-shortlist.html).
- **Boondocking-scale tank and battery capacity.** Class A coaches generally carry larger fresh
  water, gray/black, and house-battery capacity than a Class C on the same budget, which matters
  more the further off-grid the family camps.
- **The diesel-pusher option, entirely.** Diesel pushers are Class A coaches by definition — a
  rear-mounted diesel engine on a heavy, purpose-built commercial chassis (Freightliner or
  Spartan) with air ride and air brakes. Giving up Class A means giving up that whole category:
  the quieter cab, the mountain-grade engine braking, the much higher towing capacity, and the
  higher payload that a diesel pusher offers over any gas rig. That research — what a diesel
  pusher actually is, the real costs, what to inspect used — is intact in the [archived diesel
  primer](archive/17-diesel-primer.html) for anyone who wants to understand what was passed up
  and why.

None of that is a small thing to walk away from. It's named here so the decision reads as a
trade the family made with eyes open, not a box that got checked.

## Why Class B is still too small

This part of the analysis hasn't changed and doesn't need to. **Class B (camper van)** runs on
a cargo van chassis (Sprinter, Transit, ProMaster) and tops out around 200–300 square feet of
living space. There's no way to fit two kids' sleeping spaces, a real dual-monitor desk, six
bikes, and boondocking-grade water and power into that footprint at once. A Class B is easy to
drive and meets full automotive crash standards because the cab is unmodified — genuine
advantages — but the space math simply doesn't work for a family of four living and working
aboard. It was ruled out early in this docket and stays ruled out.

## Gas vs. diesel: now close to moot

For most of this docket's history, gas-vs-diesel was a live, well-developed comparison — the
[archived diesel primer](archive/17-diesel-primer.html) is a genuinely thorough field guide to
diesel pushers, written specifically so the family could recognize a good used one if it showed
up in budget. That comparison mattered because it was a same-class question: gas Class A vs.
diesel Class A, same body style, different engine and chassis underneath.

**A standard Class C makes that comparison nearly moot**, for a structural reason rather than a
preference: the dominant Class C chassis, the **Ford E-450**, hasn't been offered with a diesel
engine since Ford discontinued the 6.0L Power Stroke option after the 2010 model year — every
E-450 built since has shipped gas-only, most recently with the 7.3L V8 ([Diesel Army, 2004-2010
6.0L Power Stroke E-Series](https://www.dieselarmy.com/features/the-neglected-2004-to-2010-6-0-liter-power-stroke-ford-e-series-van/);
[Ford Pro, 2026 E-450 cutaway chassis specs](https://www.ford.com/commercial-trucks/e-series-cutaway/2026/models/e450-cutaway-drw/)).
Any used E-450-based Class C recent enough to be a sound buy in this family's window is, by
construction, a gas rig. There's no gas-vs-diesel decision left to make on that chassis — it
was made by Ford's product line fifteen years ago.

The one place diesel survives inside the Class C category is the **Super C** — a different,
much heavier animal built on a medium-duty truck chassis (commonly a Ford F-550/F-600 or a
Freightliner M2) rather than the E-450, running a genuine diesel engine like a Cummins B6.7,
with towing capacity up to roughly 20,000 lbs ([RV Trader, What Exactly Is a Super C
Motorhome?](https://www.rvtrader.com/blog/2025/09/22/what-exactly-is-a-super-c-motorhome/)).
A Super C is worth knowing exists — it's effectively a diesel pusher's capability in a shorter,
cab-forward footprint — but it comes with diesel-pusher-adjacent pricing and complexity, and it
hasn't been the family's working target. Anyone curious how it stacks up against a true diesel
pusher should start from the same ground truth in the [archived diesel
primer](archive/17-diesel-primer.html), since a Super C shares more mechanically with a pusher
than with a standard gas Class C.

## The open question this docket hasn't answered: size

Here's the honest state of things, and the reason this document's job changed from "help decide
the class" to "set up the next decision."

In person, a 25-foot Class C felt too small — cramped for six bikes, a real home office, and
two kids growing into their own space. That's a real data point, not a mistake to explain away.
But the park-length reality that drove the pivot away from Class A points the opposite
direction: **shorter rigs get into more sites, more loops, and more parks without restriction**,
and that pressure only intensifies at destinations like Sequoia or Canyonlands, where narrow,
switchback park roads and older campground loops weren't built with 35-footers in mind.

So the docket now has two data points in tension — a felt sense that smaller is cramped, and
park-access data that favors smaller — and no resolved answer between them yet. That's
deliberate, not an oversight: **the right length is a question the evidence should answer, not
a gut call**, and the evidence (specific park length-limit data, and what floorplan actually
buys space efficiently at 28–32 ft versus a compressed but workable 25–27 ft) is being worked
through directly, not settled here. Track the current state of that question in [Decisions &
Open Questions](16-open-questions.html), and treat "what size Class C" as the live decision this
whole docket is now organized around — the way "which class" used to be.

## Where this leaves things

- **Class A and diesel are off the table** — not because either was a bad rig, but because the
  mission re-centered on national/state-park reach, and park length limits punish large coaches
  at exactly the destinations that now matter most. What was given up — Class A's space, and the
  diesel-pusher category entirely — is preserved, unedited, in the
  [archived Class A landscape](archive/02-model-shortlist.html) and [archived diesel
  primer](archive/17-diesel-primer.html).
- **Class B is still too small** for four people, WFH, and six bikes — that hasn't changed and
  isn't being revisited.
- **Gas vs. diesel is nearly moot on the family's actual chassis.** The Ford E-450 that
  underlies most standard Class Cs has been gas-only since Ford dropped the diesel option after
  2010. The only diesel path left inside "Class C" is the Super C — a heavier, pricier, more
  pusher-like animal the family isn't currently targeting.
- **Budget is $60–90k, financed** (revised down from the earlier $100–150k band, consistent with
  a smaller class and shorter rig).
- **Size is now the docket's central open question.** A 25-footer felt too small in person; park
  data favors shorter rigs. Resolving that tension — not re-litigating the class — is what the
  rest of this docket is for. Start at [Decisions & Open Questions](16-open-questions.html) for
  where that stands.
