# The Used Gas Class A Landscape: Model Shortlist for a 30-35' Family Rig

## Why this matters for your family

You've already toured three real coaches at Camping World Lakewood, and you know the 35-footer is where you want to end up, with the 30-footer acceptable. That tells us you want the space and payload of a bigger coach but aren't committed to a single badge yet — the right posture, because gas Class A quality varies enormously not just brand-to-brand but **year-to-year and unit-to-unit**. Most of these coaches share a handful of chassis (mainly the Ford F-53) with fiberglass or aluminum-frame boxes bolted on by different manufacturers using overlapping supply chains. The brand badge matters less than you'd think; floorplan, chassis year, and prior maintenance matter more.

This document covers every brand on your list — including flagging the one that doesn't belong (the Forza) — and narrows it to a shortlist for two adults, two 4-year-olds, six bikes, a remote-work setup, and genuine boondocking capability.

## First, a correction: the Forza is not a gas coach

You listed the Winnebago Forza alongside Sunstar, Adventurer, and Vista. The Forza is a **diesel pusher** — rear-engine, air-ride, on a Freightliner chassis, starting around $361,000 new vs. roughly $209,000-$253,000 for the gas Vista/Sunstar/Adventurer line ([Lichtsinn RV, diesel vs. gas comparison](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/diesel-pusher-versus-gas-motorhome/)). It's a fine coach, but a different category entirely — different chassis, 2-3x the purchase price even used, a Cummins/Freightliner service network instead of Ford — and outside your gas/used scope. Just filter it out when scrolling listings; "Winnebago diesel" results sometimes get mixed in alongside Forza and Journey.

## The chassis underneath almost everyone

Before the brands: nearly every gas Class A on your list — Winnebago, Thor, Tiffin's gas line, Newmar Bay Star, Jayco, Coachmen, Holiday Rambler, most Fleetwood — rides on a **Ford F-53** stripped chassis. This matters because engine/transmission problems are largely chassis problems, not brand problems.

- **1998-2020 model years**: Ford 6.8L Triton V10, 320 hp / 460 lb-ft. Extremely common, well-understood by independent mechanics, but with a documented weak spot: the V10's offset mounting in the F-53 exposes the passenger-side exhaust manifold to road spray, causing accelerated stud fatigue and cracking on some units ([SuperSteer, Ford F53 Chassis overview](https://supersteerparts.com/ford-f53-chassis)). Well-maintained V10 coaches regularly see 150,000-250,000+ miles.
- **Late 2020/2021-present**: Ford switched to the 7.3L "Godzilla" V8, with a stiffer chassis and larger sway bars for better handling. Reviewers and owners generally consider this the better platform — more power, better fuel economy, less body roll ([RV Journal, F-53 chassis history](https://rvjournal.org/ford-f-53-chassis-discontinued/); [Tiffin Motorhomes specs](https://tiffinmotorhomes.com/models/open-road-allegro/specifications/)).
- **Practical takeaway for a used search**: all else equal, prefer a 2021+ model year for the 7.3L V8, or budget for a pre-purchase inspection focused on the exhaust manifold/stud area if you're looking at a 2018-2020 V10 coach. This is a bigger driver of your ownership experience than which brand's name is on the outside.

One honest note on the gas-vs-diesel tradeoff you've already resolved to skip: gas engines are generally expected to last roughly 150,000-200,000 miles vs. 400,000+ for a diesel pusher, gas GVWR tops out around 22,000 lbs vs. 26,000-38,700 lbs for diesel, and gas tow capacity is roughly 5,000 lbs vs. 10,000 lbs on diesel ([Lichtsinn RV](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/diesel-pusher-versus-gas-motorhome/)). For a family not towing a car and not chasing six-figure mileage, gas remains the sensible, cheaper choice — just know the payload ceiling is real, which bears directly on your six-bike, two-monitor loadout (more below).

## Brand-by-brand rundown

### Winnebago — Sunstar, Vista, Adventurer

The Vista and Sunstar are essentially the same coach built on the same line — Winnebago uses the Sunstar name for some dealer/regional variants — with the **Adventurer sitting a notch above** on full body paint and a heavier chassis rated for 22.5" wheels ([iRV2, Sunstar vs. Vista thread](https://www.irv2.com/threads/newbie-question-regarding-sunstar-vs-vista.1974502/)). RV Insider aggregated owner reviews score the Vista at 4.3/5 (69 reviews) and the Adventurer at 4.3/5 (89 reviews), with the Sunstar lower at 3.9/5 but from a much smaller sample ([RV Insider, Winnebago Class A Gas reviews](https://www.rvinsider.com/Winnebago-Class-A-Gas-RV-Reviews)).

**Known problem areas**: water pumps, step motors, generator carburetors, windshields, and cooling-system valve leaks show up repeatedly in owner complaints. More serious reports include hydraulic leveling-jack failures, slide-out failures running into the thousands of dollars, and on at least one Adventurer, engine compression issues quoted at $12,000-$20,000 to fix ([RV Insider aggregated reviews](https://www.rvinsider.com/Winnebago-Adventurer-Class-A-Gas-RV-Reviews)). Some owner sentiment holds that Winnebago's build quality dipped between roughly 2005 and 2020 before improving — treat any specific model year as needing its own VIN-specific inspection rather than assuming "Winnebago" is a blanket quality signal.

**Family fit**: Winnebago's bunk floorplans put two twin bunks in a rear or mid-coach slide, separate from the primary bedroom — workable for two young kids, and a layout you've already seen firsthand at Lakewood.

### Thor Motor Coach — Windsport, Hurricane, ACE

Thor is the highest-volume RV manufacturer in North America (it also owns Jayco, Entegra, and Coachmen), and the Windsport/Hurricane are close siblings differing mainly in trim and dealer channel. Owner sentiment here is the roughest of any brand on this list: an aggregate PissedConsumer score of 1.5/5 across 185 reviews, with recurring complaints about delamination (roof, slides, sidewalls), water leaks, electrical failures, and denied warranty claims ([PissedConsumer, Thor Motor Coach reviews](https://thor-motor-coach.pissedconsumer.com/review.html)). iRV2 forum sentiment is more mixed, with some long-term owners reporting solid mid-quality experiences and others frequent shop visits ([iRV2, Thor Motorhome Opinions](https://www.irv2.com/threads/thor-motorhome-opinions.2003498/)).

**Practical read**: Thor coaches are inexpensive relative to comparable-size competitors, and the ACE (a shorter Class A, often 30' and under, on a lighter chassis) may show up in your 30' search. Given the delamination pattern, a used Thor purchase is a case where a **pre-purchase inspection with a moisture meter is not optional** — walk the roof edges, slide seals, and lower sidewalls with an inspector who will physically test for wet lamination, not just look for stains.

### Tiffin — Open Road (Allegro) 32SA / 34PA

Tiffin's Open Road Allegro line is the gas sibling to their diesel Allegro Bus, built with a tubular aluminum wall skeleton rather than wood framing — a genuine construction-quality differentiator versus stick-and-staple competitors. The **32SA** is the more popular floorplan for a family of 2-4: rear private bedroom, king-bed slide, split bath, and — critically for you — an optional drop-down front bunk plus a dinette/sofa that converts to sleeping space ([Tiffin Motorhomes, Open Road specs](https://tiffinmotorhomes.com/class-a/open-road-allegro/specs); [RV Insider, 32SA reviews](https://www.rvinsider.com/Tiffin-Open-Road-32SA-Class-A%20-%20Gas-RV-Reviews)). Note the factory-stated 250 lb weight limit on the drop-down bunk — fine for two 4-year-olds now, worth remembering as they grow. The **34PA** trades bunk flexibility for four opposing slides and more open floor space, aimed more at a couple than a family with kids.

Later Open Road units (2021+) moved to the Ford 7.3L V8, and owners consistently report better fuel economy over the older V10. Tiffin also has a reputation — earned over decades with its diesel Allegro Bus/Phaeton lines — for being more responsive on warranty and factory service than the Thor family of brands, though the gas Open Road line gets less forum attention simply because fewer are sold.

### Newmar — Bay Star / Bay Star Sport

Newmar is broadly regarded by the forum community as a step above the Thor/Fleetwood/Winnebago mainstream on fit-and-finish — "Newmar quality is very much apparent in the coach," with owners specifically calling out cabinetry fit and smooth finish work ([iRV2, Bay Star Sport owner feedback](https://www.irv2.com/threads/feedback-from-bay-star-sport-owners.2066631/)). The Bay Star Sport is Newmar's entry-level, smallest Class A, built on the same Ford F-53 chassis as everyone else, with bunk-bed floorplans available. That said, recent threads (2025) show some erosion of the old reputation — slide-out issues, weak batteries, doors and molding coming loose on first trips ([iRV2, 2025 Bay Star build quality](https://www.irv2.com/threads/2025-bay-star-build-quality.2085835/)).

**Practical read**: Newmar coaches command a real used-price premium over Thor/Fleetwood/Winnebago of comparable age and length. Worth crossing if budget stretches, but confirm the premium is buying fit-and-finish, not just the badge, on the specific unit you inspect.

### Entegra — Vision, Emblem

Entegra is a Jayco sister-brand under the Thor Industries umbrella. Forum commentary is blunt: some experienced owners describe Entegra's gas coaches (Vision, Emblem) as essentially Jayco coaches with a different badge, sharing chassis, slide mechanisms, and much of the interior build with Jayco's Precept/Alante line. Treat them as close siblings rather than distinct products, and let price and floorplan — not brand mystique — decide.

### Fleetwood — Bounder, Flair, Fortis

The **Bounder** is one of the best-known nameplates in Class A history — "among the greatest-selling motorhomes of all time" — and in head-to-head comparisons a Bounder 36F bunk-model has been rated ahead of the Jayco Precept 36A for family bunk configurations ([RVs on Autotrader, Best Class A RV](https://rvs.autotrader.com/articles/best-class-a-rv)). Fleetwood's reputation is solidly mid-pack: not the fit-and-finish of Newmar or Tiffin, but reliable, well-supported (large dealer network via parent company REV Group), and easy to get serviced. The **Flair** is Fleetwood's shorter/lighter gas Class A, useful if you gravitate toward 30-32' rather than 35'. Correction: the **Fleetwood Fortis is a gas Class A, not a diesel pusher** — it's built on the same Ford F-53 chassis with the 7.3L V8 (335 hp/468 lb-ft) as the Bounder and Flair, and belongs on this gas shortlist alongside them, not excluded from it. Fleetwood's actual diesel pushers are the Discovery, Discovery LXE, Palisade, and Frontier. (Per Fleetwood's own model lineup at [fleetwoodrv.com](https://www.fleetwoodrv.com/) and chassis/spec confirmation at [RVUSA, 2021 Fleetwood Fortis 34MB Class A specs](https://www.rvusa.com/rv-guide/2021-fleetwood-fortis-34mb-class-a-specs-tr48546).)

### Jayco — Alante, Precept

Jayco (also Thor Industries) is frequently cited as the best value-for-money entry point into Class A gas ownership — slam-latch storage doors, MCD shades, and an **industry-leading 2-year bumper-to-bumper warranty** on new units, which signals factory confidence even if it matters less to a used buyer ([RVs on Autotrader, Best Class A RV](https://rvs.autotrader.com/articles/best-class-a-rv)). The F-53 chassis under Jayco coaches gets consistently positive reliability mentions — "requiring mainly an annual oil change and minor maintenance" per long-term owner reports. Precept is the larger/higher-trim sibling to the more compact, beginner-friendly Alante.

### Coachmen Mirada

Coachmen (also Thor Industries) — the Mirada is a solid, unremarkable mid-market gas Class A. It doesn't generate the complaint volume Thor's own house brands do, nor the praise Newmar/Tiffin get; priced accordingly, usually below Winnebago and Fleetwood for comparable specs. Worth a look opportunistically, not a brand to specifically hunt for.

### Holiday Rambler — Admiral, Vacationer

Holiday Rambler (part of REV Group, sister brand to Fleetwood) still actively produces gas Class A coaches: the **Admiral** is the entry-level gas model and the **Vacationer** sits above it with more customization ([Holiday Rambler, current lineup](https://www.holidayrambler.com/); [MHSRV listings, 2025-2026 Admiral units](https://mhsrv.com/holiday-rambler-rv)). Both share DNA and factory floor with Fleetwood's gas lineup, and reputation follows suit — mid-pack, well-supported, unremarkable in either direction. Not worth chasing specifically, but not a red flag if the used unit and price are right.

## Family-specific floorplan guidance

For two 4-year-olds plus two adults, you want one of these bunk patterns, all found across multiple brands above:

1. **Rear bunk slide** (Winnebago Adventurer/Vista bunk models, Bounder 36F-style, Jayco Precept bunk floorplans) — two fixed twin bunks in their own slide-out room, separate from the primary bedroom. Best long-term option: gives kids a defined space that survives them growing past toddlerhood.
2. **Drop-down front overhead bunk** (Tiffin 32SA, many Thor/Coachmen units) — folds down from the ceiling over the cab, usually rated ~250 lbs combined. Great for younger kids and doubles as storage when stowed, but it's a ceiling-height compromise eventually outgrown.
3. **Convertible dinette/sofa** — present in nearly every floorplan as a third option; fine as backup or for guests, not ideal as a primary nightly bed for two kids over a multi-week trip.

Since you'll be full-time remote working from the rig, prioritize floorplans where the **dinette or a dedicated desk area is separate from the kids' sleeping zone** — you don't want your video-call backdrop to be bunk curtains, or to break down your monitor mount every night to make up a bed.

## Payload, bikes, and your monitor setup — the real constraint

This is where gas Class A limitations bite hardest for your use case. Gas coaches run GVWR roughly 18,000-22,000 lbs vs. 26,000-38,700 lbs for diesel pushers ([Lichtsinn RV](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/diesel-pusher-versus-gas-motorhome/)), and **cargo carrying capacity (CCC)** — what's left after dry weight, full fuel, and full fresh water — is often surprisingly thin, sometimes only 1,500-3,000 lbs depending on the unit. Six bikes (adult mountain/gravel/road bikes at 25-35 lbs each, a spouse's bike, two kids' bikes), a hitch rack (40-70 lbs), two monitors and a mount, camping gear, and a family's luggage eat into that fast. **Before buying any specific used unit, get the actual weight sticker/CCC for that VIN** (a yellow or white sticker inside a cabinet or the driver's doorframe) and weigh the loaded coach at a CAT scale before your first big trip — standard RV community advice, not overcaution.

For the bikes: a hitch-mounted rack on a 2" receiver is standard and handles 4-6 bikes depending on the rack (Swagman's 4-bike RV-rated rack, or 5-6 bike vertical racks from JB Racks; ladder-mounted racks only handle 1-2 lighter bikes, a poor fit for a full family fleet) ([eTrailer, Choosing a Bike Rack for a Motorhome](https://www.etrailer.com/faq-Motorhome-RV-Bike-Racks.aspx); [Swagman RV racks](https://www.swagman.net/collections/rv-racks)). A 35' coach's factory 5,000 lb-rated rear hitch has ample tow-rating margin for a loaded rack, but **the rack's tongue weight counts against your rear-axle GVWR**, not just the hitch rating — another reason the real CCC number matters more than the spec sheet.

## Boondocking reality check for a gas coach

You've said genuine primitive/boondocking capability is required, not optional. The honest picture: gas Class A coaches can absolutely boondock, but stock factory setups (one or two 6V/12V lead-acid batteries, little or no factory solar prep, a gas generator) aren't enough for more than a night or two of comfortable off-grid camping, especially running two monitors and a work setup all day. RV solar retailers and boondocking guides describe capable off-grid gas Class A setups needing real upgrades — battery banks in the 400-800+ Ah lithium range, 800-1600W of roof solar, and a proper inverter — to approach what a diesel pusher does with bigger factory systems ([RV.solar, Class A Motorhome solar guide](https://rv.solar/class-a-motorhome); [The RV Geeks, Class A Boondocking guide](https://www.thervgeeks.com/class-a-rv-boondocking/)). Tank sizes vary, but a well-equipped 35-37' gas coach with roughly 100-gallon fresh/grey and 75-gallon black tanks can stretch to about two weeks for a conservative family of three to four. Since you're handy and plan to do your own upgrades anyway, that's good news: **buy the best-condition used coach in your size/floorplan/price range and budget separately for a lithium/solar retrofit**, rather than paying a premium for a unit that already has one — aftermarket systems on a used coach are a bit of a lottery on installation quality anyway.

## Where used prices are trending in 2026

Class A motorhomes depreciate meaningfully in the first few years — often cited around 30% — then the curve flattens ([RVShare, NADA/JD Power RV Values guide](https://rvshare.com/blog/rv-values/)). For your 2018-2025 model-year window:

- **2018-2020 (older V10 chassis)**: past the steepest depreciation, most attractively priced, but budget for the V10 exhaust-manifold inspection and some deferred maintenance on a 5-7 year old unit.
- **2021-2023 (early 7.3L V8 chassis)**: the sweet spot right now — modern chassis, still meaningfully cheaper than new, and plenty of supply as the huge 2020-2021 pandemic-boom cohort of new-RV buyers moves on and resells.
- **2024-2025**: minimal savings vs. new in most cases; only worth chasing at a real discount (trade-in, dealer demo, motivated seller).
- Diesel units "retain value better than gas motorhomes" per RV valuation guidance — gas coaches represent better relative value used precisely because they depreciate faster, which works in your favor as a buyer ([RVShare, RV Values guide](https://rvshare.com/blog/rv-values/)).
- 2026 market commentary notes continued buyer's-market softness in used Class A, driven by that same 2020-2021 resale wave — worth revisiting pricing again closer to your actual purchase window.

## Bottom line for this family

**Your realistic shortlist, in rough priority order:**

1. **Winnebago Adventurer (bunk floorplan), 2021+** — heavier chassis and full-paint trim over the Vista/Sunstar, strong forum presence for parts/support, and you've already got hands-on time with the brand from Lakewood. Confirm CCC and inspect for the known slide/jack issues.
2. **Tiffin Open Road 32SA, 2021+** — the aluminum-frame build-quality edge, a drop-down front bunk plus dinette flexibility that works well for two young kids, and a factory service reputation that's a real asset for a family that will use this rig hard.
3. **Fleetwood Bounder (bunk model, e.g. 36F-style floorplan)** — the deepest, most proven nameplate in this class, mid-pack reliability, wide dealer network, generally priced below Newmar/Tiffin for comparable size.
4. **Jayco Precept (bunk floorplan)** — best value-for-money feature set carrying into the used market, F-53 chassis owners describe as low-drama; a lightly used one from the 2020-2021 boom should be well within budget.
5. **Newmar Bay Star Sport** — include if budget stretches, for the fit-and-finish reputation, but weigh the 2025 forum chatter about quality softening and inspect accordingly.

**Skip for your search**: Winnebago Forza (diesel, out of scope). The Fleetwood Fortis is gas (see correction above) and is not excluded on that basis. Don't specifically chase Thor's own house brands (Windsport/Hurricane/ACE) or Entegra, given delamination complaint volume and badge-engineering overlap with Jayco respectively — unless a specific well-inspected unit is priced compellingly enough to override the caution.

**Regardless of brand**: get the actual CCC/payload sticker for any VIN before falling in love with it, budget for a pre-purchase inspection (roof/slide moisture check especially on Thor-family units, exhaust manifold check on pre-2021 V10 units), and treat your lithium/solar boondocking upgrade as a separate post-purchase project rather than a buying criterion — you're handy, and it'll be cheaper and better-installed doing it yourself than paying for someone else's aftermarket job baked into the asking price.
