# Buying Out-of-State: Temp Tags, Insurance-in-Transit, and Getting It Home to NJ

If you follow the channels laid out in [how to actually buy a used Class A](04-used-buying-inspection.html) — RV Trader, RVUSA, and especially [PPL Motor Homes](https://www.pplmotorhomes.com/) in Houston, "the largest RV consignment operation in the country" — an out-of-state purchase isn't an edge case for this family. It's the *expected* path. Widening the search to [Class C rigs with a toad](18-class-c-landscape.html) only makes that more true: the coach that fits your budget and floorplan could just as easily be sitting in Ohio, Florida, or Texas as in New Jersey. What's missing from the rest of this docket is the gap in between: you've agreed to buy a coach a thousand miles from Brick — now what, legally and practically, gets it into your driveway? This document is that gap. It does not repeat [doc 04's](04-used-buying-inspection.html) channel and inspection material or [doc 10's](10-ownership-costs.html) NJ sales-tax and registration-fee numbers — it picks up exactly where those leave off: the transaction is agreed, and the rig is still in the seller's state.

## What the law requires: driving it home before NJ registration

**The core question — can you legally drive a newly purchased motorhome across state lines to New Jersey before it's registered in your name anywhere?** The honest answer is: it depends entirely on what's affixed to the vehicle when you leave the seller's lot or driveway, and the two legitimate paths are not equivalent.

**Path 1 — a dealer- or DMV-issued temporary tag/transit permit.** If you buy from a dealer or a consignment operation like PPL, the seller can typically issue (or help you obtain) a temporary tag or transit permit from that state's motor vehicle agency, valid for a short window (commonly 5-30 days depending on the state) to drive the vehicle to your home state for titling. This is the clean, documented path. **But the process is state-specific and not guaranteed to fit a motorhome.** Texas is a useful cautionary example, since PPL is Houston-based: Texas's standard **Vehicle Transit Permit** is free and valid for one trip, but per the [Texas DMV](https://www.txdmv.gov/motorists/register-your-vehicle/temporary-permits) it's capped at passenger vehicles up to 6,000 lbs and light trucks up to 10,000 lbs GVWR — well under the loaded weight of most 30-35' Class A motorhomes (see [chassis, weights & payload](03-chassis-weights-payload.html)), so the standard TX permit may simply not cover your rig. Confirm the specific permit type, weight class, and validity-for-out-of-state-travel with the selling state's DMV *before* you fly out for closing — don't assume "temp tag" means the same thing in every state.

**Path 2 — driving on the seller's existing plate.** This is the path that gets discussed most on RV forums and is the one to be most careful about. Once title transfers to you, the seller's personal registration and plate generally belong to *them*, not to the vehicle, in most states — a private-party seller keeping their plate affixed so you can "drive it home" is a common informal practice, but it is not a documented legal right and its legality varies state to state; some buyers report doing this without incident, others report it creating a registration/insurance mismatch if stopped. **Treat this as an unverified community practice, not a confirmed legal path** — the safest documented options are a properly issued temporary tag/transit permit (Path 1) or professional transport (below), not driving on a plate that no longer matches the vehicle's owner.

**The private-party gap Doug should know about going in:** unlike a dealer, a private-party seller in a state with no consignment-style operation may not be able to issue *any* temporary tag — that authority often sits with dealers or the state DMV directly, not with an individual. If you're buying private-party (Facebook Marketplace, an individual RV Trader listing) rather than through a dealer or PPL, **plan to get the temporary tag yourself from that state's DMV using the signed title**, not assume the seller can hand you one. This is consistently reported across RV-buyer forum threads ([iRV2 Forums](https://www.irv2.com/) discussions on private out-of-state purchases) — labeled here as community-experience guidance, since no state DMV documents a categorical "private sellers cannot issue temp tags" rule; it follows from the fact that temp-tag issuance authority is generally a dealer/DMV function, confirmed state by state.

**What New Jersey's side of this looks like:** NJ does not require you to register a motorhome in NJ the moment you cross the state line — it gives new owners a window to complete the transfer. Per the [NJ MVC](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/transowner.htm), a private-party title transfer must be completed within 10 working days of the sale (see [doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html)); separately, the MVC's general guidance for a vehicle acquired out of state gives new owners up to 60 days to title and register. Confirm your specific timing against the [NJ MVC's Moving to New Jersey](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/drivertopics/movetonj.htm) and [transferring ownership](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/transowner.htm) pages, since the exact window can depend on whether you're a NJ resident buying elsewhere versus a new resident. The bottom line: **you have a real but bounded window to drive the coach home before it must be titled in NJ — get the seller-state paperwork (temp tag or transit permit) right first, because that's what makes the drive itself legal, independent of the NJ clock.**

## Insurance-in-transit: the law requires it before you drive, not after you arrive

Every state — including New Jersey, per the [NJ MVC's motorhome page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/motorhomes.htm), which requires proof of insurance at registration — requires proof of liability insurance to legally operate a vehicle on public roads. The practical consequence for an out-of-state purchase: **your policy has to be bound, naming the specific VIN of the coach you're buying, before you drive it off the seller's lot or out of their driveway — not after you get home.** You cannot legally drive away on the strength of "I'll insure it when I'm back in NJ."

In practice, buyers handle this with an **insurance binder** — a temporary proof-of-coverage document your insurer issues electronically, often within minutes, once you give them the VIN, make, model, and year. Per [Progressive's explainer](https://www.progressive.com/answers/insurance-binder/), a binder "serves as a placeholder until your formal policy is issued" and is what lets you legally drive a newly purchased vehicle before the full policy paperwork catches up. The practical sequence: get the VIN from the listing or seller before you fly out, get a quote and have the binder ready to activate same-day, and call your insurer (or agent) to bind coverage effective the moment closing is final — coordinate this with the closing appointment so there's no gap between "I own it" and "it's insured." Don't rely on an existing auto policy's "newly acquired vehicle" grace period for this — that grace period (commonly around 30 days on some policies) is designed for adding a car you already possess to an existing policy, not for driving a brand-new-to-you motorhome a thousand miles home; bind the actual RV policy first. For the coverage-type and agreed-value questions (what to actually buy, not just how to bind it same-day), see the insurance article — [`23-rv-insurance-and-toad-coverage.html`](23-rv-insurance-and-toad-coverage.html) (cross-linked here; refer to that doc for coverage-type detail rather than repeating it).

## Getting it home: drive it yourself vs. professional transport

Once the coach is legally yours, insured, and tagged for transit, you have two honest paths to get it to Brick.

**Option A — fly out, close in person, and drive it home yourself.** This is the path most buyers take, and it pairs naturally with [doc 04's](04-used-buying-inspection.html) advice to be present for the NRVIA inspection and test drive. The catch is real: you're taking a multi-day drive in an unfamiliar rig, in a vehicle class that behaves nothing like a car (see doc 04's test-drive section on F-53 chassis body roll and braking feel), that you've owned for an hour. Practical mitigations:
- **Keep day one short.** Don't plan a 500-mile first day — get a feel for the coach's handling, braking, and turning radius in daylight, then stop.
- **Have doc 04's inspection findings in hand**, including generator hours and any noted soft items, so you know what to watch for on the road (a marginal item the inspector flagged is exactly what to monitor on a multi-day drive).
- **Know the nearest dealer service network and mobile-RV-tech options along your route** before you leave, not after something goes wrong — a breakdown a thousand miles from home with two young kids in the rig is the scenario to plan against, not discover.
- **If you're not confident piloting the specific class of rig you bought** (a diesel pusher especially, or anything Sara isn't going to help drive), see [the one-driver-family doc](22-one-driver-family.html) for the driver-training and confidence-building material — that doc's guidance applies with extra weight to a first drive that's also a 1,000+ mile trip home.

**Option B — hire a professional RV transport or driveaway service.** A driveaway service sends a professional (often CDL-licensed) driver to drive your coach home for you; flatbed/towed transport is also available for non-driveable units, though that shouldn't apply to a coach that passed inspection and a test drive. Current pricing (as of July 2026) per industry cost guides: **driveaway service runs roughly $1.00-$2.00 per mile**, and broader RV transport (including flatbed) runs **$1.50-$4.00+ per mile** depending on RV size, distance, and route density, per [CitizenShipper's 2026 cost guide](https://citizenshipper.com/blog/rv-transport-cost/) and [SpeedComd's 2026 rate guide](https://speedcomd.com/rv-transport-rates-per-mile-ultimate-cost-guide-2026/) — both vendor/industry sources, treated here as directional pricing, not verified invoices. A cross-country haul (roughly 1,500-2,500 miles, e.g., Houston to Brick) lands in the neighborhood of **$1,500-$5,000** at driveaway rates. **When it's worth it:** a diesel pusher you're not yet confident piloting (see [doc 22](22-one-driver-family.html) and the [diesel primer](17-diesel-primer.html) on the different driving/mechanical profile), a rig from a region with weather or route risk on short notice, or simply valuing your time and nerves over the cost — a few thousand dollars is a small fraction of a $100k-150k purchase. **When flying out and driving yourself makes more sense:** a gas Class A on a familiar chassis, a shorter multi-day drive you're comfortable with, and wanting the shakedown miles yourself before the family's first real trip.

Either way, confirm with the transport company (if hired) how they handle registration/plates during transit — a legitimate driveaway operator will have their own answer for this (commercial transporter plates, or requiring you to have the temp tag/transit permit already in place); ask before booking rather than assuming.

## NJ titling and sales tax on an out-of-state purchase: is there double taxation?

Short answer: **in the most common scenario for this family, no** — you're very unlikely to pay two states' full sales tax on the same coach. Here's why, sourced directly to the [NJ Division of Taxation's Use Tax publication, ANJ-7](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/pdf/pubs/sales/anj7.pdf) (the primary legal source for this, current as of its May 2025 revision):

**The common case: you owe NJ's 6.625% and nothing (or little) to the origin state.** Many states exempt a vehicle sale to an out-of-state buyer from their own sales tax entirely, provided the buyer doesn't register the vehicle in that state. Texas, for example, offers a documented **nonresident sales-tax exemption** ([Texas Comptroller, Form 14-312](https://comptroller.texas.gov/taxes/publications/96-254/oos-mv-transports.php)) when the vehicle is purchased for use outside Texas and not registered there. If the selling state doesn't collect its own tax from you as a nonresident buyer, there's simply nothing to credit — you owe New Jersey's [6.625% use tax](10-ownership-costs.html) in full, calculated the same way doc 10 lays out, and there's no double taxation to worry about because you only paid once.

**The edge case: you did pay another state's sales tax.** If the selling state's tax was actually collected from you — because that state doesn't offer a nonresident exemption, or the deal was structured in a way that triggered it — ANJ-7's Reciprocity section is explicit: **"When taxable property and services are purchased and received in another state, and sales tax is paid to that state, New Jersey will allow a credit against the Use Tax due, provided that the other state allows a similar credit for Sales Tax paid to New Jersey."** If the other state's rate is equal to or higher than NJ's 6.625%, no further NJ use tax is due at all. If it's lower, NJ credits what you paid and you owe only the difference — ANJ-7's own worked example: a $100 purchase taxed at 4% elsewhere leaves a **$2.63** NJ use-tax balance on every $100 of price (6.625% minus the 4% already paid), not the full 6.625% again.

**The condition that matters for your drive-vs-transport decision:** ANJ-7 draws a specific line between property **"purchased and received in another state"** (credit allowed) and property **"purchased in another state and delivered to New Jersey"** (no credit allowed — full NJ use tax due on the purchase price). Read plainly, this distinction turns on where you take possession/title of the coach, not on how it physically gets to Brick afterward — closing on the rig and taking title in the seller's state, then either driving it yourself or hiring a driveaway service to move *your own already-purchased property*, both look like "received in another state" under this test. The "delivered to New Jersey" no-credit case more naturally describes a seller-arranged delivery where the sale itself isn't complete until the coach reaches you in NJ. **This reading isn't explicitly confirmed for RVs by a worked example in ANJ-7 or an MVC page** — if the tax difference is material to your deal (i.e., you actually paid meaningful sales tax to the selling state), confirm this specific point with the [NJ Division of Taxation's Customer Service Center](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/pdf/pubs/sales/anj7.pdf) (609-292-6400) before assuming the credit applies, rather than resting a five-figure tax question on an inference.

**The practical MVC step once the coach is home:** per [doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html) and the [NJ MVC](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/transowner.htm), title transfer must be done **in person** at an MVC agency — it cannot be done by mail — and the sales/use tax is paid directly to the MVC at that appointment, not to the seller. Bring the out-of-state title (signed over to you), your bill of sale, proof of NJ insurance, and — if you paid tax to the selling state — documentation of what you paid, in case the credit question above applies to your deal.

## Practical checklist

1. **Agree on price and terms** with the seller (dealer, PPL, or private party) — see [doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html).
2. **Pre-purchase inspection** — NRVIA Level 2, before you're financially committed ([doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html)).
3. **Get the VIN and bind insurance on it**, effective the day of closing — coordinate the binder with the closing appointment ([`23-rv-insurance-and-toad-coverage.html`](23-rv-insurance-and-toad-coverage.html)).
4. **Complete closing/title-transfer paperwork in the selling state** — confirm lien payoff, get the signed title, and (if a dealer or PPL) get any sales-tax exemption paperwork for nonresident buyers (e.g., Texas Form 14-312) if applicable.
5. **Secure a temporary tag or transit permit from the selling state** — confirm weight class and out-of-state validity before you fly out; if buying private-party, plan to get this yourself from that state's DMV rather than assuming the seller can issue it.
6. **Drive it home or arrange professional transport** — weigh the shakedown-drive risk (mitigations above) against driveaway cost (~$1.00-$4.00+/mile depending on service level).
7. **Title transfer and use-tax payment at an NJ MVC agency**, in person, within the required window — bring the signed title, insurance proof, bill of sale, and any out-of-state tax documentation ([doc 10](10-ownership-costs.html), [doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html)).

## Bottom line for this family

Buying out-of-state is not a complication to avoid — given where the best PPL and nationwide-listing inventory actually sits, it's close to the default path for this search, and there's no legal reason to shy away from it. The two things to get right, in order: **insurance bound to the specific VIN before you drive away** (non-negotiable, every state requires it), and **a properly issued temporary tag or transit permit from the selling state** rather than relying on the seller's own plate to still be legally valid for you. Once those two things are true, driving it home yourself or paying a driveaway service a few thousand dollars is a comfort-and-confidence call, not a legal one — and on the tax side, the realistic outcome is you pay New Jersey's 6.625% once, with a credit mechanism (confirmed by NJ Division of Taxation guidance) protecting you from paying twice in the rarer case where the selling state's tax was actually collected.
