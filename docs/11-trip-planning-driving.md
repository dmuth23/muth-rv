# Trip Planning and Driving: Apps, Northeast Landmines, Reservations, and Learning to Drive a 28-31' Class C

A 28-31 foot gasoline Class C on a Ford E-450 or E-350 cutaway chassis is closer in length and turning behavior to a large delivery box truck than a school bus — noticeably more maneuverable than the 33-35' Class A this docket originally scoped around, but still a rig you'll be piloting out of central New Jersey, one of the most GPS-app-dependent, parkway-riddled, tunnel-and-bridge-restricted corners of the country to learn in. The routing hazards below are about the rig's dimensions and its onboard propane, not its size class — they apply whether the specific unit lands at 28 ft or 31 ft, and most also apply to the larger 30-35 ft Class A/diesel coaches this docket no longer targets (see the [archived Class A shortlist](archive/02-model-shortlist.html) and [chassis/weights doc](archive/03-chassis-weights-payload.html) if you want that history). If you end up towing a car — see [towing a car](19-towing-a-car.html) — remember that a flat-tow or dolly setup adds length and changes some of the trailer-specific rules noted below. None of this is scary once you know the rules, but a few of the "shortcuts" your phone will cheerfully suggest are the exact kind of mistake that ends a trip with a peeled-off roof AC unit or a state trooper writing a ticket. This document covers the tools that keep you routed correctly, the specific Northeast hazards worth memorizing before you ever leave Brick, how the campground reservation game works, how to compare campground types for a family with young kids, a shakedown-trip progression starting a day from home, and what driving-skills training actually looks like for a rig this size.

**A note on scope:** this docket pivoted from Class A to Class C in July 2026 (used, $60-90k financed, 28-31 ft) — see [the Class C landscape doc](18-class-c-landscape.html) for why. Most of what's below (GPS apps, Northeast parkway/tunnel law, the reservation system, campground types) doesn't change with size class. What *does* change is the coach's real height (a Class C on a van-cab chassis is commonly in the **11-11.5 ft** range rather than the 12-13 ft of a Class A — always confirm the actual number on your specific unit, roof AC and all) and, increasingly, the fact that **park campground length limits are now a primary trip-planning input, not an afterthought** — see the next section.

## RV-safe GPS and trip-planning apps

Regular Google Maps or Waze will happily route you under a 10'6" bridge or down a weight-restricted local road, because they don't know your vehicle's height, weight, or length. You need at least one RV-aware routing tool, and in practice most experienced RVers run two: one for planning, one for live turn-by-turn.

- **[RV Life / RV Trip Wizard](https://tripwizard.rvlife.com/)** is the planning-side favorite. You enter your coach's height, weight, and length once, and it builds routes that actively avoid low bridges and weight-restricted roads, plus layers in tens of thousands of campground listings with photos, reviews, and amenity filters ([RV LIFE Pro](https://rvlife.com/rv-life-pro/) bundles the Trip Wizard planner with the RV Safe GPS app). Its real-time turn-by-turn is generally considered the weaker half of the package — the RV-forum consensus (iRV2 and brand-specific RV Life community threads) is roughly "great for planning, cross-check what you actually drive by" (*community experience, not a spec*). ([RV LIFE Trip Wizard propane/tunnel reference](https://tripwizard.rvlife.com/rv-info/propane-tanks-in-tunnels); [iRV2 forum comparison thread](https://www.irv2.com/threads/rv-gps-app-vs-garmin.2079694/))
- **[Garmin RV / Garmin Camper](https://www.garmin.com/)** dash units or the Garmin Drive app are preferred for actual live navigation — better offline reliability when you lose cell signal (which you will, in rural PA, upstate NY, and most National Forests), voice guidance, and more consistent hazard alerts. Both Garmin and RV Life pull from the same underlying HERE map data, and neither one reliably catches every low-clearance side road, so cross-checking matters regardless of which app you use. ([iRV2 RV GPS comparison](https://www.irv2.com/threads/rv-gps-app-vs-garmin.2079694/))
- **[Campendium](https://www.campendium.com/)** and **[The Dyrt](https://thedyrt.com/)** are crowd-sourced campground review apps, strong on real user photos, cell-signal reports per carrier, and road-condition warnings for the actual access road (a campground can be "fine" for a truck camper and undriveable for a 28-31' rig, or vice versa). Campendium in particular carries per-carrier signal reports from other campers, which matters directly for Doug's remote-work requirement: you can filter and read for sites reporting decent Verizon/AT&T signal before you ever drive there ([Campendium cell-signal filter](https://www.campendium.com/about/cell-signal)). *(Signal reports are user-submitted community data, not a guarantee — treat them as a strong hint, not a promise.)*
- **[Harvest Hosts](https://harvesthosts.com/)** (wineries, farms, breweries, golf courses — overnight parking in exchange for supporting the business, with 5,500+ Classic locations and 9,500+ on the top tier) and its sister network **[Boondockers Welcome](https://www.boondockerswelcome.com/)** (private driveways/yards, better for longer stays) both require your rig be fully self-contained (holding tanks, no hookup needed) to qualify as a host guest. As of July 2026 Harvest Hosts sells three annual tiers — **Classic $99, Classic + Boondockers Welcome $169, All Access $179** — so Boondockers Welcome is now bundled into a Harvest Hosts plan rather than sold as a separate ~$79 membership. ([Harvest Hosts plans & pricing](https://www.harvesthosts.com/plans)) These are dry-camping/boondocking practice reps disguised as a fun stop, which is useful since genuine boondocking capability is one of your requirements. ([Boondockers Welcome vs Harvest Hosts comparison](https://www.boondockerswelcome.com/blog/guest-blog-boondockers-welcome-vs-harvest-hosts/))

**Practical setup for this family:** RV Life Trip Wizard (or at minimum its free tier) for planning each trip's route and campground search; Garmin (dash unit or app) or CarPlay-integrated RV Life for live driving; Campendium as a second opinion on any campground before booking, filtered by cell signal since Doug will be working from the rig.

## Northeast driving landmines

This is the section to actually memorize before your first trip out of Brick. Post your coach's real height (measured with the AC units and roof vents on, not the brochure number) on a sticky note on the dash — nearly every hazard below comes down to knowing that one number cold.

### Garden State Parkway
The Parkway's clearances range from **13' up to 13'7" and above**, with the Route 22 overpass in Union County — the lowest point on the whole Parkway — posted at **13'6"**; vehicles over 13' are directed to use the middle lanes. A 28-31' Class C on a van-cab chassis typically runs **11-11.5 ft** tall with AC and roof vents on — comfortably under 13'6" — but "typically" isn't "always": confirm the actual number on your specific unit before assuming you clear it, since raised roofs and roof-mounted solar/AC add height fast. Note: an earlier version of this doc cited a specific "12'2\" outer-lane" figure for the Union County stone-arch section; that number could not be verified — the cited NJTA PDF no longer resolves (404) and the AARoads page does not state it — so it has been removed. Treat any lane-specific number below 13' as unconfirmed until you find a live, authoritative source. ([AARoads: GSP North overpass/lane-clearance notes](https://www.aaroads.com/guides/garden-state-pkwy-north-newark); [vhride.com: the lowest overpass on the Garden State Parkway](https://vhride.com/whats-the-lowdown-on-the-lowest-overpass-on-the-garden-state-parkway/))

**What the law requires vs. what's just repeated on forums:** a private motorhome on passenger registration is a permitted vehicle on the Garden State Parkway — the Parkway's own hard rules are dimensional (that 13'6" clearance) rather than an RV ban ([NJ Turnpike Authority — GSP](https://www.njta.com/)). The often-repeated "no vehicles over 10,000 lbs / no RVs north of Exit 105" line is *not* something we could confirm against an authoritative NJTA rule, and RVers disagree about it in the forums — so treat it as **unverified community anecdote**, not law. Don't plan around it either way without confirming directly with the [New Jersey Turnpike Authority](https://www.njta.com/). Practically, it rarely matters: Brick sits near Exit 91, so any trip south (Jersey Shore, Cape May) stays well clear of the disputed zone, and for trips north or out of state (New England, upstate NY) the cleaner move is the interstates — NJ Turnpike, I-287, I-95 — rather than the Parkway anyway.

### New York parkways — a completely different and harder rule than the GSP
This is the single most important rule in this document, and it's easy to conflate with the Parkway rule above because both have "Parkway" in the name. **New York's parkway system (Taconic, Palisades Interstate, Southern State, Northern State, Bronx River, Hutchinson River, Saw Mill River, and similar) was built in the 1930s for passenger cars and has posted vertical clearances as low as 6'11" — up to roughly 10-12 feet in spots, but frequently well under any Class A's height — with narrow lanes and tight curves designed for a different era of vehicle.** ([NYSDOT parkway restrictions brochure](https://www.dot.ny.gov/programs/repository/Parkways%20brochure%20text.txt); [NYC DOT truck/parkway restriction guide (PDF)](https://www.nyc.gov/html/dot/downloads/pdf/trucks_nycarea_parkways.pdf))

Here it's worth being precise, because the exact legal picture is more nuanced than "RVs are banned":

- **What the law requires:** New York regulation prohibits **commercial vehicles, tractor-trailers, trucks, and buses** on these parkways, and *some* parkways also bar towed **trailers** — so a motorhome flat-towing or dolly-towing a car can be caught by a trailer/tow restriction even where a solo coach would not be. The posted low-clearance limits are themselves legally enforceable. A private motorhome on passenger registration is *not*, as a category, banned "as an RV": the state regulation covering parkways under the Office of Parks jurisdiction ([9 NYCRR § 397.3](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/new-york/9-NYCRR-397.3)) explicitly lists "recreational vehicles, including... motor homes" as **permitted**, and the NYSDOT commercial-vehicle brochure bans trucks, tractor-trailers, and buses without naming RVs at all. ([NYSDOT brochure](https://www.dot.ny.gov/programs/repository/Parkways%20brochure%20text.txt); [9 NYCRR § 397.3](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/new-york/9-NYCRR-397.3))
- **What physically stops you anyway:** the clearances. A 28-31' Class C typically stands roughly 11-11.5 feet tall — shorter than a Class A, but a bridge posted anywhere from 6'11" up to ~11 feet is still an immovable no for most of these parkways, legal category aside — and RVs shear roof AC units and vents off on these overpasses every summer. So the *operative rule is unchanged:* **in the NYC/NY-metro area, if a road is called a "Parkway," assume your motorhome cannot use it — physically, and legally the moment you're towing — and route around it entirely.** Use interstates (I-87, I-95, I-287, I-84) instead. ([NY State DOT parkway vehicle restrictions summary](https://www.tlc-mag.com/archive_issues/NYS_DOT_pkwy_restrictions_oct13.html))

### Propane restrictions in tunnels and on the GWB — and onboard propane is well over the limit
Multiple Baltimore- and NYC-area tunnels ban propane transport outright. **This one is genuinely a matter of law**, not just guidance: it matters because a Class C's fixed ASME propane tank typically holds well more than the "10 lb per container" thresholds these crossings allow, so for practical purposes **you cannot legally take this rig through these crossings with propane aboard at all**, hookup or not — the restriction is about the propane tank, not the size class or engine:

- **What the law requires — Baltimore's Fort McHenry Tunnel (I-95) and Baltimore Harbor Tunnel (I-895):** both prohibit vehicles carrying bottled propane in excess of 10 lbs per container (max 10 containers), all bulk fuel, and other hazmat — full *or empty* bottles count. ([MDTA official FAQ](https://mdta.maryland.gov/Toll_Facilities/qanda.html); [MDTA hazmat / tunnel restrictions](https://mdta.maryland.gov/TunnelRestrictionsAndVehiclePermits)) The historical bypass many older guides mention — the **Francis Scott Key Bridge (I-695)** — **collapsed on March 26, 2024** and is **not rebuilt**; as of July 2026 the replacement project is targeting a **late-2030 reopening**, with cost estimates that have grown to roughly $4.3-5.2 billion. **Do not route toward the Key Bridge as a bypass — it does not exist right now.** MDTA directs propane-carrying / hazmat vehicles to the **western leg of the I-695 Baltimore Beltway** around the harbor, not a bridge substitute for the tunnels. Confirm current routing with a trip-planning tool immediately before any trip through Maryland, since this is an active, multi-year construction situation. ([Key Bridge Rebuild official project site](https://keybridgerebuild.com/); [Maryland Matters, Nov 2025 — cost/opening delay to 2030](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/11/17/key-bridge-replacement-costs-soar-as-high-as-5-2-billion-opening-delayed-to-2030/))
- **What the law requires — New York City's Lincoln Tunnel and Holland Tunnel, and the lower level of the George Washington Bridge:** all prohibit propane/flammable-gas transport. The **upper level of the GWB** is the standard propane-legal crossing between NJ and Manhattan/the Bronx (the lower level is enclosed and closed to HAZMAT; the upper level permits it under the Port Authority's rules) — **but** the upper level has its own height/weight posting, so verify your coach clears it, and confirm current Port Authority rules directly before counting on this route. ([Port Authority bridge/tunnel restrictions](https://www.panynj.gov/bridges-tunnels/en/restrictions.html); [Port Authority Holland/Lincoln Tunnel rules (PDF)](https://www.panynj.gov/content/dam/bridges-tunnels/pdfs/green-book.pdf); [AAA RV Bridge/Tunnel/Ferry Restriction Guide (PDF)](https://triptik.aaa.com/help/pdf/RV-Bridge-Ferry-Tunnel-Guide-3-26-20.pdf))

Bottom line on this whole category: before any trip that touches Baltimore or NYC, run the route through RV Life Trip Wizard or a similar RV-aware planner specifically checking propane/hazmat restrictions, and have a fallback interstate route in mind. This isn't optional trivia — getting turned around at a tunnel entrance with the kids in the back and no alternate route loaded is a bad afternoon.

## The campground reservation game

**[Recreation.gov](https://www.recreation.gov/)** (federal campgrounds — National Parks, National Forests, Army Corps of Engineers lakes, BLM) runs on a strict **6-month rolling booking window**. Most campgrounds open at **7am PT / 8am MT / 10am ET, but not always** — Recreation.gov's own guidance says the exact release time varies by campground, so check each site's "Seasons & Fees" tab before assuming 10:00 AM Eastern applies (reported as typical but unverified as a universal rule). Popular sites (anything near a National Park in summer) can fill within minutes of the window opening. Serious campers use the **"book-and-trim" strategy**: reserve the maximum allowed nights (often 7-14) ending on your actual desired checkout date the moment the window opens, then go back later and trim off the extra nights you don't need — this effectively gives you a head start over people who wait to book just their real dates. There's also an **18-day lockout rule**: after a 14-night max stay, you can't rebook in the same area for 18 days, which mostly matters for snowbirds, not a family doing week-long trips. Cancellations cluster **10-14 days before check-in** as other campers finalize plans — turn on Recreation.gov's "Notify Me" alerts for a sold-out site you want, and check back in that window. ([Recreation.gov official booking tips](https://www.recreation.gov/articles/list/tips-for-making-campsite-reservations-at-popular-locations/281); [Milepost Planner: 6-month window explainer](https://milepostplanner.com/blog/recreation-gov-6-month-booking-window/))

**New Jersey state parks** run their own separate system through the [NJ Campground Reservation portal](https://www.njportal.com/DEP/NJOutdoors/), requiring a free "My New Jersey" account, and open bookings **11 months in advance** — much more relaxed than the federal 6-month window. (An earlier version of this doc stated bookings release at "7:00 AM Eastern"; that specific opening time is not stated anywhere on the cited NJ Outdoors FAQ and could not be verified — confirm the release time directly with NJ DEP before relying on it.) ([NJ DEP camping reservation procedures](https://www.nj.gov/dep/parksandforests/parks/campreserv.html); [NJ Outdoors reservation FAQ](https://www.njportal.com/DEP/NJOutdoors/Support/Faq)) **[ReserveAmerica](https://www.reserveamerica.com/)** covers many other states' state park systems and works similarly. Rules, booking windows, and cancellation policies vary state to state, so always check the specific system before assuming Recreation.gov's rules apply.

**Private campgrounds** (KOA, Sun Outdoors, independent RV resorts) typically have far more flexible, direct-booking reservation systems with looser cancellation policies — usually the easiest tier to book last-minute, which makes them the natural choice for early shakedown trips where you don't want the added stress of a competitive booking window.

### Park-length data now drives trip planning, not just campground booking

For a 33-35' Class A, "will it fit" was mostly a Northeast-parkway-and-tunnel question. For a 28-31' Class C, it's a *destination* question, because the family's actual mission — national and state park camping, including long reaches like Sequoia and Canyonlands — runs straight into park campground length caps that cluster tighter than most people expect: roughly **84% of national park campgrounds accept up to 29 ft**, with the share dropping fast past that, and marquee stops like **Canyonlands cap both developed campgrounds at 28 ft** while **Sequoia's interior roads carry road-based length advisories (22-24 ft) that are harsher than any campground limit**. That means the *specific* coach length inside the 28-31' band — not just the size class — determines which parks are actually reachable, and it's worth knowing before falling in love with an itinerary rather than after. The full model-by-model park-fit analysis, including the E-350-vs-E-450 chassis trade-off that comes with chasing a shorter length, lives in the [Class C landscape doc](18-class-c-landscape.html) — read it alongside this one before locking in a summer itinerary that includes a national park.

### A specific NJ sizing landmine
Several close-to-home NJ state forests that look like obvious "first trip" options are worth checking carefully rather than assuming a 28-31' Class C is automatically small enough. The clearest example is **Cheesequake State Park**: the NJ DEP's own park page states there is an **11-foot height restriction for vehicles entering the camping area** — and a 28-31' Class C typically stands roughly 11-11.5 feet tall, which puts it right on the line rather than clearly under it (a 33-35' Class A, by contrast, at 12-13 ft, was flatly excluded). **Do not assume you clear this gate — measure your specific unit's actual height, AC and roof vents included, and call the park to confirm before planning around it**; a few inches either way changes the answer. ([NJ DEP — Cheesequake State Park (official)](https://dep.nj.gov/parksandforests/state-park/cheesequake-state-park/); community listings put the campground's max rig length around 35 feet, well within reach for a 28-31' coach on length alone — [Campsite Photos: Cheesequake](https://www.campsitephotos.com/campground/nj/cheesequake-state-park/)) Beyond that, camper and guide reports (*community experience, verify per-site before booking*) describe **Wharton State Forest** campgrounds as difficult for rigs much over the low-20-foot range and **Jenny Jump State Forest** RV sites topping out around 25 feet — both still tight for a 28-31' coach; **Bass River State Forest** has far more sites but no hookups. Treat Wharton and Jenny Jump as smaller-rig destinations for this family, and confirm the Cheesequake height gate directly before using it as a shakedown stop. ([Wharton State Forest RV guide](https://www.outdoorsy.com/guide/wharton-state-forest-nj); [Jenny Jump camping listing](https://thedyrt.com/camping/new-jersey/new-jersey-jenny-jump-state-forest))

## A shakedown-trip progression from central New Jersey

The goal of a shakedown trip isn't the destination — it's finding out what breaks, what you forgot to pack, and how the rig actually handles, close enough to home that a problem is a minor inconvenience, not a crisis.

1. **Trip 1 — one night, big-rig-friendly private campground within 30-45 minutes of Brick.** Given the NJ state park size and height limits above, skip the state parks for this first trip and pick a private campground in the Jackson/Pine Barrens/shore-adjacent area explicitly rated for rigs your length (confirm via Campendium or the park's own site before booking — call and ask for their longest pull-through or back-in site). The entire point is: practice hooking up water/electric, run the generator, test the slide(s), sleep in it, and be home in 45 minutes if something's wrong. Don't drive anywhere else once you arrive.
2. **Trip 2 — one long weekend, 1-1.5 hours out, full hookups.** Slightly farther, still full hookups, ideally a park with a pool/playground so the kids have a reason to be excited rather than just tolerating a test. This is where you dial in the bike rack loading/unloading routine for all 6 bikes and practice a full "break camp and drive" cycle, not just arrival.
3. **Trip 3 — 3-4 hours, Cape May area, long weekend or full week.** This is a real trip: full hookups still (large private resorts in the Cape May area such as [Sun Outdoors Cape May](https://www.sunoutdoors.com/new-jersey/sun-outdoors-cape-may) and [Beachcomber Camping Resort](https://rvparkway.com/parks/beachcomber-campground-inc) advertise big-rig sites with 30/50-amp service — confirm your exact length when booking), but far enough to test highway cruising, a fuel stop or two, and actual vacation logistics with the kids.
4. **Trip 4 — first partial-hookup or dry-camping night**, once 1-3 have gone smoothly — a Harvest Hosts stop or a state park with electric/water only, to start exercising the boondocking-adjacent systems (tank levels, water conservation, generator runtime) before attempting genuine boondocking farther from home.

Only after this progression is a longer, farther, or more boondocking-heavy trip worth planning.

## RV driving schools and skills for a 28-31' coach

A Class C's cab-forward, van-based layout is generally considered easier to learn on than a Class A's flat-fronted cockpit — you're sitting in a familiar truck cab with a hood in front of you, not behind a wall of glass with no visual reference for where the front of the vehicle ends. That said, the rear overhang still swings *outward* on a tight turn — the tail tracks wider than the point the mirrors show you up front, which remains the single most common way new drivers of any coach clip a gas-pump island, a curb, or a parked car (*this "tail swing" is a well-known handling trait rather than a single published number — practice it in a lot before the road*). Dedicated instruction is still worth it before your first real trip, not after a close call.

- **Dealer/manufacturer driving courses** are the easiest entry point — Camping World and similar large dealers sometimes offer "driver confidence" courses bundled with a purchase or as a paid add-on: a couple hours of classroom time covering mirror setup, tail swing, and braking, followed by supervised driving time in a parking lot or low-traffic area (*availability varies by dealer — ask the specific store*). ([iRV2 thread on dealer driving courses](https://www.irv2.com/threads/class-a-motorhome-driving-school.441995.html))
- **Dedicated RV driving schools** (e.g., RVDrivingSchool.com-style multi-day couples courses, or [Lazy Days' Driver Confidence program](https://www.lazydays.com/)) run longer — often a full day or two — and cover mirror adjustment using reference dots on the windshield/mirror, defensive driving specific to a top-heavy vehicle, and supervised real-road driving, sometimes with both spouses trained together so either of you can drive comfortably. This matters directly for this family: Sara is reported as more likely to be comfortable driving a Class C than a Class A (see [the one-driver question](22-one-driver-family.html)), and a Class C's smaller footprint and more car-like driving position is exactly the kind of thing worth testing hands-on during rent-before-buy, not assuming from a spec sheet.
- **RV driving skills specific to a 28-31' coach**, in priority order for this family:
  - **Mirror setup and tail swing** — learn to see your own rear tires and the full length of the coach in your side mirrors before your first drive, not on the road.
  - **Wind limits** — a tall, boxy Class C is still meaningfully wind-susceptible, even if less so than a taller Class A. Community reference charts and RV-owner writeups (*community guidance, not a regulation*) describe crosswinds becoming noticeable around 10 mph and handling seriously degraded by ~20 mph, with many owners citing personal "stop and wait it out" thresholds in the 25-30 mph sustained / 35-40 mph gust range. ([RV Life: driving in windy conditions](https://rvlife.com/driving-a-class-a-rv-in-windy-conditions/); [GudGear wind-limit reference charts](https://gudgear.com/how-much-wind-can-an-rv-withstand/)) Treat those mph numbers as reported estimates, not hard limits. A defensible personal rule tied to an official trigger: when the National Weather Service issues a **Wind Advisory** (generally sustained winds 31+ mph or gusts 46+ mph, per NWS criteria), just don't drive a high-profile rig — check the wind forecast, not only the rain, before every travel day. ([NWS Wind Advisory / high-wind criteria](https://www.weather.gov/safety/wind-ww))
  - **Mountain grades** — pick your descent gear *before* you start down a long grade rather than riding the brakes and downshifting reactively, and let engine/transmission braking do most of the work; service brakes alone can overheat and fade on a long descent. This matters for East Coast trips (the Poconos, Catskills, Appalachians) and matters even more for the family's named cross-country reaches like Sequoia and Canyonlands, which involve real mountain and high-desert grades. A gas Class C on the E-450/E-350 chassis has no exhaust brake or engine retarder the way a diesel pusher does — plan descents conservatively and don't ride the service brakes. ([RV Life: mountain grade driving tips](https://rvlife.com/tips-driving-an-rv-on-extreme-mountain-grades/))
  - **Fuel stops** — a 28-31' Class C is noticeably easier to fuel at a standard gas station than a 35-footer, but truck stops (Pilot/Flying J/Love's) are still the more reliable choice on longer trips, especially the cross-country reaches — easier in-and-out, and diesel-truck-lane islands aren't a concern on a gas chassis. Preplan stops on the route rather than fueling opportunistically once you're in unfamiliar terrain.

## Bottom line for this family

The tools are simple: run RV Life Trip Wizard (or similar) for planning every route, keep a live nav app (Garmin or another RV-aware app) for turn-by-turn, and read Campendium's community cell-signal reports before committing to a site given Doug's remote-work need. The Northeast-specific rule to tattoo on your brain is that **"Parkway" means something different and far more dangerous in New York than in New Jersey** — NY parkways run as low as 6'11" of clearance, which physically excludes most Class Cs (and every Class A), and the law there bans commercial vehicles, trucks, buses, and (on many parkways) towed trailers, so a coach towing a car is illegal there even setting clearance aside; the Garden State Parkway, by contrast, is generally passable for a private motorhome at 13'6" in the center lanes — comfortably above a 28-31' Class C's typical 11-11.5 ft height — with the "no RVs above Exit 105" line being unverified forum lore you can simply avoid by staying south or switching to interstates. For propane, treat NYC's Lincoln/Holland tunnels and Baltimore's harbor tunnels as hard legal no-go zones with a loaded tank, use the GWB's upper level (never the lower) when you must cross into the city, and don't route toward the Francis Scott Key Bridge as a Baltimore bypass — it's still years from reopening. For the shakedown sequence, confirm height and length limits at the closer NJ state parks rather than assuming a Class C automatically clears them (Cheesequake's 11-ft gate is now a real check, not an automatic pass), and start with a private, size-appropriate campground 30-45 minutes from Brick, working up to Cape May as the first real trip. Beyond the shakedown sequence, let the [park-length data](18-class-c-landscape.html) drive itinerary planning for national and state park trips — the family's named reaches (Sequoia, Canyonlands) both have real length constraints that narrow which coach in the 28-31' band actually works. And budget for at least a half-day of hands-on driving instruction — ideally with both of you behind the wheel, since Sara is reported more likely to be comfortable driving a Class C than a Class A but hasn't yet driven one (see [the one-driver question](22-one-driver-family.html)) — before the Cape May trip; tail swing, wind limits, and mountain-grade braking are exactly the kind of things that are cheap to learn in a parking lot and expensive to learn on the road.
