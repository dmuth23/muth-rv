# Trip Planning and Driving: Apps, Northeast Landmines, Reservations, and Learning to Drive a 33-35' Class A

A 33-35 foot gasoline Class A is roughly the size and turning behavior of a small school bus, and you'll be piloting it out of central New Jersey — one of the most GPS-app-dependent, parkway-riddled, tunnel-and-bridge-restricted corners of the country to learn in. None of this is scary once you know the rules, but a few of the "shortcuts" your phone will cheerfully suggest are the exact kind of mistake that ends a trip with a peeled-off roof AC unit or a state trooper writing a ticket at exit 105. This document covers the tools that keep you routed correctly, the specific Northeast hazards worth memorizing before you ever leave Lakewood, how the campground reservation game works, how to compare campground types for a family with two 4-year-olds, a shakedown-trip progression starting a day from home, and what driving-skills training actually looks like for a rig this size.

## RV-safe GPS and trip-planning apps

Regular Google Maps or Waze will happily route you under a 10'6" bridge or down a weight-restricted local road, because they don't know your vehicle's height, weight, or length. You need at least one RV-aware routing tool, and in practice most experienced RVers run two: one for planning, one for live turn-by-turn.

- **[RV Life / RV Trip Wizard](https://tripwizard.rvlife.com/)** is the planning-side favorite. You enter your coach's height, weight, and length once, and it builds routes that actively avoid low bridges and weight-restricted roads, plus layers in 25,000+ campground listings with photos, reviews, and amenity filters. Its real-time turn-by-turn is considered weaker — RV forum consensus (iRV2, multiple brand-specific RV Life community forums) is "great for planning, not what you drive by." A 2026 app update (version 3.0) added CarPlay support, closing part of that gap. ([RV LIFE Trip Wizard propane/tunnel reference](https://tripwizard.rvlife.com/rv-info/propane-tanks-in-tunnels); [iRV2 forum comparison thread](https://www.irv2.com/threads/rv-gps-app-vs-garmin.2079694/))
- **[Garmin RV / Garmin Camper](https://www.garmin.com/)** dash units or the Garmin Drive app are preferred for actual live navigation — better offline reliability when you lose cell signal (which you will, in rural PA, upstate NY, and most National Forests), voice guidance, and more consistent hazard alerts. Both Garmin and RV Life pull from the same underlying HERE map data, and neither one reliably catches every low-clearance side road, so cross-checking matters regardless of which app you use. ([iRV2 RV GPS comparison](https://www.irv2.com/threads/rv-gps-app-vs-garmin.2079694/))
- **[Campendium](https://www.campendium.com/)** and **[The Dyrt](https://thedyrt.com/)** are crowd-sourced campground review apps, strong on real user photos, cell-signal reports per carrier, and road-condition warnings for the actual access road (critical for a 35-footer — a campground can be "fine" for a truck camper and undriveable for you). Campendium in particular is well regarded in the full-time-remote-worker RV community for its per-carrier signal-strength filter, which matters directly for Doug's remote-work requirement: you can filter for sites reporting decent Verizon/AT&T signal before you ever drive there.
- **[Harvest Hosts](https://harvesthosts.com/)** (wineries, farms, breweries, golf courses — free overnight parking in exchange for supporting the business, ~8,000+ locations) and its sister membership **Boondockers Welcome** (private driveways/yards, better for longer stays) both require your rig be fully self-contained (holding tanks, no hookup needed) to qualify as a host guest. Combined membership runs roughly $100-150/year; Boondockers Welcome alone is about $79/year. These are dry-camping/boondocking practice reps disguised as a fun stop, which is useful since genuine boondocking capability is one of your requirements. ([Boondockers Welcome vs Harvest Hosts comparison](https://www.boondockerswelcome.com/blog/guest-blog-boondockers-welcome-vs-harvest-hosts/))

**Practical setup for this family:** RV Life Trip Wizard (or at minimum its free tier) for planning each trip's route and campground search; Garmin (dash unit or app) or CarPlay-integrated RV Life for live driving; Campendium as a second opinion on any campground before booking, filtered by cell signal since Doug will be working from the rig.

## Northeast driving landmines

This is the section to actually memorize before your first trip out of Lakewood. Post your coach's real height (measured with the AC units and roof vents on, not the brochure number) on a sticky note on the dash — nearly every hazard below comes down to knowing that one number cold.

### Garden State Parkway
The Parkway's general height clearance is **13'6"**, and that number is genuinely tight against a lot of Class A roof heights — check yours before assuming you clear it. Older stone overpasses, mostly concentrated between mileposts 130 and 173, are the sections to be cautious on; stay in the center lane, since these older bridges are often lowest at the sides. Length limits on the Parkway/turnpike system cap out around 45 feet, so your rig is fine there. ([The Camping Advisor: GSP height restrictions](https://thecampingadvisor.com/garden-state-parkway-height-restrictions-trucks-rvs/); [iRV2 Garden State Parkway thread](https://www.irv2.com/threads/garden-state-parkway.1945258/))

North of **Exit 105**, there's a widely cited rule barring vehicles over 10,000 lbs GVW — but sources genuinely disagree on whether that applies to private RVs or only commercial trucks; some forum posts report troopers don't enforce it against personal motorhomes, others say it's real and enforced. **Don't rely on a forum thread for this** — verify directly with the [New Jersey Turnpike Authority](https://www.njta.com/) before planning a trip that requires the Parkway north of Exit 105. Practically, this is easy to sidestep: Lakewood is around Exit 89, so any trip south (Jersey Shore, Cape May) never touches the ambiguous zone. For trips north or out of state (New England, upstate NY), just take the interstates — NJ Turnpike, I-287, I-95 — instead of the Parkway.

### New York parkways — a completely different and harder rule than the GSP
This is the single most important rule in this document, and it's easy to conflate with the Parkway rule above because both have "Parkway" in the name. **New York's parkway system (Taconic, Palisades Interstate, Southern State, Northern State, Bronx River, Hutchinson River, Saw Mill River, and similar) was built in the 1930s for passenger cars and has vertical clearances as low as 6'11" to roughly 10-12 feet in spots, with narrow lanes and tight curves designed for a different era of vehicle.** Commercial vehicles, buses, and RVs are **prohibited by NY state law** on the majority of these — not just discouraged, actually illegal — and RVs peel roof AC units and vents off on these overpasses every single summer. The rule of thumb from experienced RVers: **in the NYC/NY metro area, if a road is called a "Parkway," assume your motorhome cannot legally or physically use it, and route around it entirely.** Use interstates (I-87, I-95, I-287, I-84) instead. ([NYC DOT truck/parkway restriction guide (PDF)](https://www.nyc.gov/html/dot/downloads/pdf/trucks_nycarea_parkways.pdf); [NY State DOT parkway vehicle restrictions](https://www.tlc-mag.com/archive_issues/NYS_DOT_pkwy_restrictions_oct13.html))

### Propane restrictions in tunnels and on the GWB — and onboard propane is well over the limit
Multiple Baltimore- and NYC-area tunnels ban propane transport outright, and it matters because a Class A's fixed ASME propane tank typically holds 80+ pounds of propane — far above the "10 lb per container" thresholds some tunnels allow, so for practical purposes **you cannot legally take this rig through these crossings with propane aboard at all**, hookup or not:

- **Baltimore's Fort McHenry Tunnel (I-95) and Baltimore Harbor Tunnel (I-895)** both prohibit vehicles carrying more than 10 lbs of bottled propane, all bulk fuel, and other hazmat. The historical bypass many guides mention — the **Francis Scott Key Bridge (I-695)** — **collapsed on March 26, 2024** and is **not rebuilt**; as of mid-2026 the replacement project is targeting an **end-of-2030 reopening**, with cost estimates that have grown to $4.3-5.2 billion. **Do not route toward the Key Bridge as a bypass — it does not exist right now.** The actual current workaround for a propane-carrying RV heading around Baltimore's harbor is the western/southern legs of I-695 (the Baltimore Beltway) away from the harbor crossings, not a bridge substitute for the tunnels. Confirm current routing with a trip-planning tool immediately before any trip through Maryland, since this is an active, multi-year construction situation. ([MDTA official FAQ](https://mdta.maryland.gov/Toll_Facilities/qanda.html); [Key Bridge Rebuild official project site](https://keybridgerebuild.com/); [Baltimore Sun, March 2026 rebuild status](https://www.baltimoresun.com/2026/03/24/key-bridge-rebuild-baltimore/))
- **New York City's Lincoln Tunnel and Holland Tunnel, and the lower level of the George Washington Bridge**, all prohibit propane transport. The **upper level of the GWB** is generally the propane-legal crossing between NJ and Manhattan/the Bronx — but the upper level has its own height/weight posting, so verify your coach clears it before counting on this route. ([AAA RV Bridge/Tunnel/Ferry Restriction Guide (PDF)](https://triptik.aaa.com/help/pdf/RV-Bridge-Ferry-Tunnel-Guide-3-26-20.pdf); [RV Life Trip Wizard propane-in-tunnels guide](https://tripwizard.rvlife.com/rv-info/propane-tanks-in-tunnels))

Bottom line on this whole category: before any trip that touches Baltimore or NYC, run the route through RV Life Trip Wizard or a similar RV-aware planner specifically checking propane/hazmat restrictions, and have a fallback interstate route in mind. This isn't optional trivia — getting turned around at a tunnel entrance with two 4-year-olds in the back and no alternate route loaded is a bad afternoon.

## The campground reservation game

**[Recreation.gov](https://www.recreation.gov/)** (federal campgrounds — National Parks, National Forests, Army Corps of Engineers lakes, BLM) runs on a strict **6-month rolling booking window**, opening at exactly **10:00 AM Eastern** on the date 6 months out from your desired arrival. Popular sites (anything near a National Park in summer) can fill within minutes of the window opening. Serious campers use the **"book-and-trim" strategy**: reserve the maximum allowed nights (often 7-14) ending on your actual desired checkout date the moment the window opens, then go back later and trim off the extra nights you don't need — this effectively gives you a head start over people who wait to book just their real dates. There's also an **18-day lockout rule**: after a 14-night max stay, you can't rebook in the same area for 18 days, which mostly matters for snowbirds, not a family doing week-long trips. Cancellations cluster **10-14 days before check-in** as other campers finalize plans — turn on Recreation.gov's "Notify Me" alerts for a sold-out site you want, and check back in that window. ([Recreation.gov official booking tips](https://www.recreation.gov/articles/list/tips-for-making-campsite-reservations-at-popular-locations/281); [Milepost Planner: 6-month window explainer](https://milepostplanner.com/blog/recreation-gov-6-month-booking-window/))

**New Jersey state parks** run their own separate system through the [NJ Campground Reservation portal](https://www.njportal.com/DEP/NJOutdoors/), requiring a free "My New Jersey" account, and open bookings **11 months in advance** — much more relaxed than the federal 6-month window. **[ReserveAmerica](https://www.reserveamerica.com/)** covers many other states' state park systems and works similarly. Rules, booking windows, and cancellation policies vary state to state, so always check the specific system before assuming Recreation.gov's rules apply.

**Private campgrounds** (KOA, Sun Outdoors, independent RV resorts) typically have far more flexible, direct-booking reservation systems with looser cancellation policies — usually the easiest tier to book last-minute, which makes them the natural choice for early shakedown trips where you don't want the added stress of a competitive booking window.

## Campground types compared, for a family with young kids

| Type | Typical cost/night | Hookups | Good for this family when... |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Private RV resort/park** (KOA, Sun Outdoors, etc.) | $50-100+ | Full (water/sewer/electric, often 50-amp), often pool/playground/wifi | First few trips, need reliability, kids want a pool/playground, Doug needs solid wifi for work |
| **State park campground** | $25-45 | Often partial (electric+water) or none; varies hugely by state and even by loop | Good value once you're comfortable; NJ state parks specifically often **cannot fit a 35' rig** — check size limits per campground, not just per park (see next section) |
| **National Park campground** | $15-35 | Usually none (dry camping) | Bucket-list destinations later on; book 6 months out via Recreation.gov; often has length limits that exclude larger Class As, so verify before falling in love with an itinerary |
| **National Forest / BLM dispersed (boondocking)** | Free-$10 | None | Once you've proven out solar/generator/water/holding-tank capacity on shorter dry-camping stints (Harvest Hosts is good practice for this) |
| **Harvest Hosts location** | Free (membership fee amortized) | None — must be self-contained | One-night stopovers between destinations, fun kid-friendly novelty (farms, wineries with playgrounds) |

Sources: [Full-hookup vs partial vs dry camping definitions, RVmapper](https://www.rvmapper.com/blog/rv-campground-types-explained); [State park RV camping guide, America's State Parks](https://www.americasstateparks.org/rv-sites-guide/)

### A specific NJ sizing landmine
Several close-to-home NJ state forests that look like obvious "first trip" options are actually **too small for a 35' Class A**: Cheesequake State Park's camping loop has an **11-foot height restriction on the entrance road** and sites suited to rigs around 25 feet; Wharton State Forest's campgrounds are explicitly noted as difficult for anything over 22 feet; Jenny Jump State Forest's RV sites max out around 25 feet. Bass River State Forest is more accommodating on site count (175+ sites) but doesn't offer hookups at all. **None of these are a good first shakedown stop for your actual 35-footer** — treat them as later, smaller-rig-era destinations, or skip them for this rig entirely. ([Cheesequake State Park campground info](https://thedyrt.com/camping/new-jersey/new-jersey-cheesequake-state-park); [Wharton State Forest RV guide](https://www.outdoorsy.com/guide/wharton-state-forest-nj); [Jenny Jump camping listing](https://thedyrt.com/camping/new-jersey/new-jersey-jenny-jump-state-forest))

## A shakedown-trip progression from central New Jersey

The goal of a shakedown trip isn't the destination — it's finding out what breaks, what you forgot to pack, and how the rig actually handles, close enough to home that a problem is a minor inconvenience, not a crisis.

1. **Trip 1 — one night, big-rig-friendly private campground within 30-45 minutes of Lakewood.** Given the NJ state park size limits above, skip the state parks for this first trip and pick a private campground in the Jackson/Pine Barrens/shore-adjacent area explicitly rated for rigs your length (confirm via Campendium or the park's own site before booking — call and ask for their longest-pull-through or back-in site). The entire point is: practice hooking up water/electric, run the generator, test the slide(s), sleep in it, and be home in 45 minutes if something's wrong. Don't drive anywhere else once you arrive.
2. **Trip 2 — one long weekend, 1-1.5 hours out, full hookups.** Slightly farther, still full hookups, ideally a park with a pool/playground so the kids have a reason to be excited rather than just tolerating a test. This is where you dial in the bike rack loading/unloading routine for all 6 bikes and practice a full "break camp and drive" cycle, not just arrival.
3. **Trip 3 — 3-4 hours, Cape May area, long weekend or full week.** This is a real trip: full hookups still (Sun Outdoors Cape May and Beachcomber Camping Resort both explicitly accommodate 35-40 foot rigs with 30/50-amp service), but far enough to test highway cruising, a fuel stop or two, and actual vacation logistics with two 4-year-olds. ([Sun Outdoors Cape May](https://www.sunoutdoors.com/new-jersey/sun-outdoors-cape-may); [Beachcomber Camping Resort](https://rvparkway.com/parks/beachcomber-campground-inc))
4. **Trip 4 — first partial-hookup or dry-camping night**, once 1-3 have gone smoothly — a Harvest Hosts stop or a state park with electric/water only, to start exercising the boondocking-adjacent systems (tank levels, water conservation, generator runtime) before attempting genuine boondocking farther from home.

Only after this progression is a longer, farther, or more boondocking-heavy trip worth planning.

## RV driving schools and skills for a 33-35' coach

A 35-foot Class A is roughly 15-30 inches wider in its rear tail swing than what the mirrors show you at the front — the back of the coach sweeps out further than intuition suggests on tight turns, and this is the single most common way new Class A drivers clip a gas pump island, a curb, or a parked car. Dedicated instruction is worth it before your first real trip, not after a close call.

- **Dealer/manufacturer driving courses** are the easiest entry point — Camping World and similar large dealers (including likely at or near the Lakewood location you already visited) sometimes offer "driver confidence" courses bundled with a purchase or as a paid add-on: a couple hours of classroom time covering mirror setup, tail swing, and braking, followed by supervised driving time in a parking lot or low-traffic area. ([iRV2 thread on dealer driving courses](https://www.irv2.com/threads/class-a-motorhome-driving-school.441995.html))
- **Dedicated RV driving schools** (e.g., RVDrivingSchool.com-style multi-day couples courses, or [Lazy Days' Driver Confidence program](https://www.lazydays.com/)) run longer — often a full day or two — and cover mirror adjustment using reference dots on the windshield/mirror, defensive driving specific to a top-heavy vehicle, and supervised real-road driving, sometimes with both spouses trained together so either of you can drive comfortably.
- **RV driving skills specific to a 33-35' coach**, in priority order for this family:
  - **Mirror setup and tail swing** — learn to see your own rear tires and the full length of the coach in your side mirrors before your first drive, not on the road.
  - **Wind limits** — a tall, flat-sided Class A is highly wind-susceptible; GudGear's reference charts describe handling as noticeably affected around 10 mph crosswinds and dramatically impaired by 20 mph, and other RV-owner sources report personal stop thresholds in the 25-30 mph steady / 35-40 mph gust range. Treat specific mph thresholds as reported estimates rather than hard limits, and verify current guidance before relying on it — the National Weather Service's "Wind Advisory" threshold (sustained 31-39 mph or gusts 46-57 mph) is a reasonable personal line to just not drive past for a high-profile vehicle like this. Check wind forecasts specifically, not just rain, before travel days. ([RV Life: driving in windy conditions](https://rvlife.com/driving-a-class-a-rv-in-windy-conditions/); [GudGear wind-limit reference charts](https://gudgear.com/how-much-wind-can-an-rv-withstand/))
  - **Mountain grades** — learn your coach's speed-per-gear at roughly 90% of governed engine RPM on a descent, pick that gear before starting down a long grade rather than riding the brakes and downshifting reactively, and let engine/transmission braking do most of the work; service brakes alone can overheat and fade on long descents. This matters even for East Coast trips — the Poconos, Catskills, and anything toward the Appalachians have real grades. ([RV Life: mountain grade driving tips](https://rvlife.com/tips-driving-an-rv-on-extreme-mountain-grades/))
  - **Fuel stops** — plan fuel stops around truck stops (Pilot/Flying J/Love's) rather than typical gas stations; a 35-footer often can't physically maneuver a standard gas station's pump islands and driveways without major fuel costs. Preplan stops on the route rather than fueling opportunistically.

## Bottom line for this family

The tools are simple: run RV Life Trip Wizard (or similar) for planning every route, keep a live nav app (Garmin or CarPlay-integrated) for turn-by-turn, and use Campendium to check cell signal before committing to a site given Doug's remote-work need. The Northeast-specific rule to tattoo on your brain is that **"Parkway" means something different and far more dangerous in New York than in New Jersey** — NY parkways are frequently under 10 feet and illegal for RVs outright, while the Garden State Parkway is generally passable at 13'6" except for a genuinely ambiguous rule above Exit 105 that's easy to just avoid by staying south or switching to interstates. For propane, treat NYC's Lincoln/Holland tunnels and Baltimore's harbor tunnels as hard no-go zones with a loaded tank, and don't route toward the Francis Scott Key Bridge as a Baltimore bypass — it's still years from reopening. For the shakedown sequence, skip the tempting-but-too-small NJ state parks for this particular rig and start with a private, size-appropriate campground 30-45 minutes from Lakewood, working up to Cape May as the first real trip. And budget for at least a half-day of hands-on driving instruction — ideally with both of you behind the wheel — before the Cape May trip; tail swing, wind limits, and mountain-grade braking are exactly the kind of things that are cheap to learn in a parking lot and expensive to learn on the road.
