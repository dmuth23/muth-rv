# The Bigger Picture: Why We're Doing This, and What It Isn't

Every other doc in this docket is about a vehicle: what class, what size, what it costs,
what breaks. This one is about the decision underneath the vehicle. It exists because a
purchase this size can quietly become a stand-in for bigger questions — what our family time
looks like for the next several years, what a "yes" to this costs us elsewhere — and those
deserve to be answered on purpose, not by default.

## 1. The waiting isn't waiting

Sara's concern, stated plainly, deserves to be stated plainly back: two years is a long time
to tell two young kids "we're getting an RV" and then not go anywhere. If the purchase window
is late 2026 through 2028, the years in between can't be a holding pattern. They should be
full.

The reframe that matters here: **the interim isn't a waiting room for the real trips — it's
where a chunk of the real trips happen.** Two kinds of travel both count, on equal footing:

- **Ordinary vacations** — flights, hotels, a friend's beach house, a cabin rental. These
  don't need to justify themselves against the RV plan. A trip is a trip.
- **Rental-RV trips** — the [rent-before-you-buy protocol](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) is
  framed there as a test of the coach, and it is. But every one of those trips is also just
  a vacation, on its own terms, the week it happens — not merely a data-gathering exercise
  that happens to look like a trip.

Rental pricing supports doing more than one of these before any purchase decision locks in:
Class C rentals were running a median of roughly $185–245 per night in mid-2026 depending on
market and season, with off-season weeks landing 30–50% cheaper than summer peak
[[rvivy.com]](https://rvivy.com/rv-types-and-classes/class-c-rvs-for-rent/)
[[bestrvrentalcompanies.com]](https://www.bestrvrentalcompanies.com/blog/2026-q1-rv-rental-pricing-report).
A shoulder-season rental week is genuinely cheaper than it looks from the sticker price on a
summer weekend — which means "let's rent again in October" is a real option, not an indulgence.

Two rental trips a year, spread across 2026 and 2027, is not a compromise version of the plan.
It's the plan working as intended: real information about a real coach, on a real vacation,
more than once, before six figures move.

## 2. The RV has a budget line. International travel does the same.

The family's ties to Europe mean travel abroad isn't a hypothetical someday-trip — it's a
standing possibility that can surface on its own schedule, and it has to survive whatever
happens with this purchase. Concretely, that means two guardrails:

**The RV budget and the travel-abroad budget are separate lines, not one pool that the RV
draws down first.** The financed Class C target sits at $60,000–$90,000
(see [the math doc](27-honest-math.html) for the full financing breakdown), and
that number is sized to leave the household's discretionary travel capacity intact — not
sized up to "whatever's left after the coach." If a European trip and a big RV year are both
on the table in the same twelve months, the honest answer is that both can happen on a modest
coach payment, but neither should get quietly cut to protect the other by default. That's a
conversation to have explicitly when it comes up, not a default the budget makes for you.

**If a work-related opportunity in Europe materializes, it folds in — it doesn't compete.**
An RV built around U.S. national and state parks doesn't stop being useful because a European
trip happens in a given year; it just means that year has a European trip *and* whatever
domestic camping season fits around it, rather than the coach owning the whole travel
calendar. The point of keeping the RV budget modest is precisely so a chunk of unexpected
travel — European or otherwise — never has to be turned down to make a coach payment.

## 3. A tool, not an identity

It's worth naming a trap directly: RV culture has a strong pull toward "we are an RV family"
as an identity, complete with the decals, the forums, the sense that if you're not using it
every free weekend you're doing it wrong. That's not the goal here.

The RV is one way this family travels — a genuinely good one for national-park-heavy,
boondocking-capable, six-bikes-in-tow trips, which is real and worth having. It sits alongside
flights to see family, an occasional international trip, a weekend at a friend's, a plain old
hotel stay. None of those modes has to justify its existence against the others. The question
for any given trip is "what gets us there well," not "did we use the RV enough this year to
have been worth it."

This matters concretely for the size question the rest of the docket is chewing on: a rig
sized for real national-park access (see the park-length-limit research feeding the size
call) is also a rig that's easy to *not* use for a given trip without feeling like a waste —
which keeps it a tool instead of an obligation.

Being homeschooled, the kids don't tie any of this to a school calendar — a real advantage
noted elsewhere in this docket — but it cuts both ways: nothing forces a trip cadence either.
The RV gets used when it's the right tool for that trip, not on a schedule invented to justify
the purchase.

## Gratitude, and the actual point of deciding

It's worth saying plainly: being able to spend a research docket's worth of energy on *which*
way to travel — RV or flights, national park or Europe, this size coach or that one — is a
genuinely fortunate position, and none of the tradeoffs in this doc carry real hardship. A
family that owns a home in Brick, has work flexible enough to fold in overseas trips, and can
weigh a $60–90k discretionary purchase against vacation budgets rather than against rent or
groceries is doing fine by almost any measure that matters. That's not a caveat to apologize
for — it's context, held with plain gratitude.

Naming that doesn't shrink the decision, though. Good options still require choosing well
between them: a coach that's the wrong size, a budget that quietly eats the travel-abroad
fund, or a purchase timed to end the "aren't we going anywhere" feeling instead of to fit an
actual trip we're planning — those are all real ways to spend this well or spend it poorly,
regardless of how comfortable the starting position is. The point of this whole docket, and
of this page in particular, isn't to arrive at *an* RV. It's to arrive at *this family's*
answer to how it wants to spend its travel years — on purpose, with the numbers and the
values both on the table at the same time.
