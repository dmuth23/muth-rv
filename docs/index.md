# The Muth Family RV Docket

Research for buying a **used gasoline Class C motorhome** for two adults and two homeschooled
kids (4 now — 5 and 6 by purchase time), with full-time remote work aboard, six bikes, and one
mission above the others: **camping in national and state parks**, from the New Jersey shore
all the way out to Sequoia and Canyonlands. Working budget: **$60–90k financed**. Purchase
window: **late 2026 through 2028** — and just as deliberately, *maybe not at all* (see
[The Honest Math](27-honest-math.html)).

This is the docket's third life. It started as a Class A search, and that research isn't
gone — it lives in the [Archive](archive/index.html), superseded when the parks mission took
the wheel: park campgrounds don't ban coach classes outright, but they do publish **site
length limits**, and those limits punish big rigs badly enough that the family pivoted to a
right-sized Class C. How short is short enough is now the docket's central open question.

## How much to trust this

Every doc carries **inline source citations plus a Sources section** — click through and vet
anything. Research was compiled and adversarially fact-checked in July 2026; what couldn't be
confirmed is hedged in the text and gathered in
[Decisions & Open Questions](16-open-questions.html). Docs separate **what the law requires**
from **what experts recommend**. It's still research, not gospel: **verify current limits,
prices, and laws before booking or buying** — park rules in particular change season to season.

## Where to start

1. **[National Park RV Length Limits](25-national-parks-rv-limits.html)** — the chart that
   caused the pivot: campground-by-campground limits with fit percentages at 24/27/30/32 ft,
   plus the road-access gotchas (Sequoia's Generals Highway, the Zion tunnel) that matter
   more than the campsites.
2. **[State Parks and the Road West](26-state-parks-rv-limits.html)** — the same chart for
   home turf (NJ first) and the corridor west to Utah and California.
3. **[The Honest Math](27-honest-math.html)** — the anti-hype doc: owning beats renting only
   around **~44 nights a year** on honest midpoints, and the industry median is 21–30. This
   is the go/no-go number, and it deserves to be read before any floorplan turns anyone's head.
4. **[The Class C Landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html)** — which coaches actually thread the
   needle between "big enough for four" and "short enough for the parks."

## The one-paragraph takeaway

The parks mission makes the shape of the answer clear: a used gas Class C, short enough that
the gates at Squaw Flat and the switchbacks on the Generals Highway are invitations instead of
problems, big enough that four people and two careers can live in it for a week at a time —
the data says that window is real but narrow, and the rental week exists to find our spot in
it. The number that decides everything isn't a price, it's **nights per year**: below roughly
44, renting wins and that's a perfectly good outcome. And whatever the verdict, the travel
starts now, not on delivery day — the trips we take while deciding are the point, not the
preview. That's a fortunate set of problems to have, and the docket tries to treat them that way.
