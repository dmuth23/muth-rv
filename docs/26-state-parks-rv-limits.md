# State Parks and the Road West: RV Limits Near Home and En Route

## The premise, corrected honestly

Going in, the working theory was that some parks simply ban Class A or Class C motorhomes outright — that "class" is the gate. Having now pulled the official camping pages for roughly 70 campgrounds from New Jersey to Utah, that theory is **mostly wrong, and it's worth saying so plainly.**

What actually gates a rig almost everywhere is **published site or campground length — not vehicle class.** Across this entire survey, exactly one campground (Hungry Mother State Park's Royal Oak loop in Virginia) turned out to be a real equipment-type restriction, and even that's "tents only," not "no Class A/C." The other real gates found were **dimensional road restrictions** — height clearances and hard length/width caps on the *access road*, not the campsite — at Cheesequake (NJ, 11-ft bridge), Baxter State Park (ME, a 22/44-ft road-entry rule), and Sequoia National Park (CA, a stepped 22-24-ft advisory and 40-ft prohibition on the historic Generals Highway). Those three are genuinely serious for a 28-35 ft coach and are called out below. But the *routine* gate, campground after campground, is a length number on a reservation page, or — just as often — **no published number at all**, which turns out to be its own kind of problem.

This document is the evidence base for the size question the rest of the docket keeps returning to. It does not resolve it — a campground with "none published" is not a guaranteed fit, and a numeric cap is not a guarantee either, since a *park's* stated max is often the longest site in the park, not what's typically available. Use this as a filter, not a green light; the "how to verify" section at the bottom is how you turn a filtered short list into an actual reservation.

A note on scope: this is a **state-park and select federal-campground** survey (NY DEC, NPS units, one Army Corps campground), not a national-park-wide study — figures like "what fraction of all NPS campgrounds nationally accept a 29-ft rig" belong to a different, not-yet-written document and are not repeated here to avoid a claim this doc can't back.

Allaire is the other easy first outing once its family loop reopens. Both pair with the shakedown sequence in [11-trip-planning-driving.html](11-trip-planning-driving.html).

## Fit analysis: how many of these campgrounds actually fit a 24 / 27 / 30 / 32-ft coach?

Of the roughly 68 campground rows researched, **about 50 publish no numeric length cap at all** — which is not the same as "fits anything." A published "none" usually means either the campground genuinely has no size gate, or (more often, per the unofficial cautions scattered through this list — Watoga's Beaver Creek loop, Custer's non-Sylvan-Lake loops) that big rigs fit *some* sites and not others, and the only way to know is to check the site map or call. Counting those rows as "fits" would overstate the picture; excluding them from the discussion entirely would understate how much fitting actually depends on legwork rather than a rulebook.

So the fraction below is computed **only over the subset of campgrounds that state an actual numeric length ceiling on an official page** — 20 campgrounds, following these rules: the figure used is the top of the campground's stated range (a park with sites "up to 60 ft" counts as 60, even if most sites are shorter); Kettletown, CT is excluded because it's closed for the entire 2026 season; Baxter (ME) and the Sequoia Generals Highway are excluded because they're road/vehicle-dimension rules, not campsite specs; Hungry Mother's Royal Oak loop is excluded because it's tents-only, not length-gated.

| Rig length | Fits (of 20 numeric-cap campgrounds) | Share |
|---|---|---|
| 24 ft | 20 of 20 | 100% |
| 27 ft | 20 of 20 | 100% |
| 30 ft | 19 of 20 | 95% |
| 32 ft | 16 of 20 | 80% — **unverified, see caveat below; treat as ~73-83%** |

The only campground with a published cap below 30 ft is Custer's Sylvan Lake loop (27 ft). The campgrounds that drop out between 30 and 32 ft are Lake Durant (NY, 30 ft), Deep Creek Lake's shorter loops (MD, 30 ft top-of-range), and Hungry Mother's Creekside loop (VA, 30 ft) — Sylvan Lake is already excluded by 30 ft.

**Caveat on the 32-ft figure — unverified, confirm before relying:** the 80% headline depends on an inconsistent read of this doc's own inclusion rule (line 132: campgrounds that "state an actual numeric length ceiling"). Four rows have only a per-site *range* with no stated park-wide ceiling — Deep Creek Lake MD ("No single stated max; individual sites list 15-30 ft"), Watkins Glen NY ("no park-wide max stated"), Pawtuckaway NH ("No hard limit; sites 24-30 ft"), and Bear Brook NH ("No hard limit; sites 24-30 ft"). The table above keeps the first two and drops the last two — which is what produces n=20 and 80%. Applying the same rule consistently in either direction changes the answer: including all four (n=22) gives 32-ft fit = 16/22 = **73%**; excluding all four (n=18) gives 32-ft fit = 15/18 = **83%** (and 30-ft shifts to ~94% either way). So the real range for 32 ft is roughly **73-83%**, not a clean 80% — treat any single-number citation of the 32-ft fit rate as unverified until the denominator question is resolved. The 24-ft and 27-ft figures (100%) and the 30-ft figure (~94-95%) are robust across all three ways of drawing the line and don't have this problem.

**The honest read: published campground caps rarely bite until well past 30 ft, and the picture only gets fuzzy in the low-30s** — so a 28-31 ft coach, the range doc [18-class-c-landscape.html](18-class-c-landscape.html) is built around, clears nearly every campground here that actually states a number. That does **not** mean size stops mattering. The shorter rig's real advantage shows up in three places this table can't turn into a clean percentage: the ~50 "none published" campgrounds where a shorter coach has first pick of the few genuinely large sites in a small pool (Watoga, Custer's other loops); the road/height gates that have nothing to do with campsite specs (Cheesequake's 11-ft bridge, Baxter's 22/44-ft rule, Sequoia's 22-24 ft Generals Highway advisory); and simple reservation odds — fewer long sites means more competition for them regardless of the posted cap. A ~30-ft coach clears essentially every numeric limit in this table, keeps first-pick odds in the unpublished-limit parks, and is the only size in this survey that comfortably survives all three road gates.

## How to verify any specific site before you book

Nothing above substitutes for checking the actual site you're about to reserve:

- **Recreation.gov and state ReserveAmerica portals let you filter by RV length at the site level**, not just the campground level — use the site-length filter on the booking page itself rather than relying on a campground-wide figure, which is often just the longest single site in the park.
- **Call the ranger station or park office directly** when a page states "none published" or when a third-party figure is the only number available — nearly every park above lists a phone number for exactly this purpose, and several notes here recommend it explicitly (Watoga, Cape Henlopen, Killens Pond, First Landing's 50-ft sites, Grayson Highlands before a big rig).
- **Cross-check road access separately from site length** — a site can be long enough and still be unreachable by a rig your size, as Cheesequake, Baxter, and Sequoia all demonstrate. Ask about bridge heights, tunnel clearances, and any steep-grade advisories on the access road, not just the campsite pad.
- **Re-verify before every booking, not once per park** — several rows here changed for 2026 alone (Kettletown's flood closure, Allaire's construction closure, Letchworth's phased loop closures), so a figure confirmed last season is not guaranteed current.
