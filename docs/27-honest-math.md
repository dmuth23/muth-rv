# The Honest Math

Every other document in this docket helps you buy the right rig. This one asks whether to buy at all — or, more precisely, how *much* use turns "buy" into the better answer than "rent" or "fly and stay in a hotel." That's a fair question to put in writing, not a betrayal of the plan. You're a fortunate family weighing a genuinely good problem: which of several good ways to spend real money on real family time together. Nothing here argues you shouldn't buy. It argues you should buy with your eyes open about what the number actually is, because the number the dealer shows you is never the whole number, and the industry's own data says most buyers never do this math at all.

## The number nobody shows you: cost per night, not cost per month

RV financing offices sell monthly payments. What actually matters is **cost per night of use** — the all-in annual cost divided by the nights you genuinely camp in it. That single ratio is where RV ownership quietly succeeds or fails, and a widely cited 2026 industry analysis lays the math out explicitly: a mid-range Class C running typical annual ownership costs of roughly **$11,000/year** against the RV industry's own median usage figure of **21 nights a year** for Class B/C motorhomes works out to **roughly $500+ per night** — "the calculation almost no buyer does, and almost no dealer ever shows" ([AOL/RVIA-sourced analysis, "Most RV owners use their rig just 21 nights a year"](https://www.aol.com/most-rv-owners-rig-just-140007328.html)). That same analysis notes the number improves fast with use: retirees running 80–100 nights a year land around $110–$140/night, and full-timers pushing 300+ nights get to roughly $38/night. **Usage, not the sticker price, is what makes or breaks the math.**

The nights figure itself comes from RVIA's own research: the **2025 Go RVing RV Owner Demographic Profile** (Ipsos survey) puts the median RV usage at **30 nights/year across all RV types** — up from 20 nights in the 2021 study — with **Class B and C motorhomes running lower, at a median of 21 nights/year** ([RVIA, 2025 Go RVing RV Owner Demographic Profile](https://www.rvia.org/2025-go-rving-rv-owner-demographic-profile); usage breakdown as reported by [the AOL/RVIA-sourced analysis](https://www.aol.com/most-rv-owners-rig-just-140007328.html) — the RVIA member report itself wasn't independently accessible to confirm the class-level breakdown, so treat 21 nights as reported-but-not-primary-source-verified). Even taking the *encouraging* headline — usage nearly doubled since 2021 — the median Class C owner is still using their rig well under a month a year.

**Worth naming plainly:** 30 nights/year sounds low against the plan this docket assumes (homeschooling, no calendar tether, national/state-park reach). Your family's *planned* usage is likely well above the median precisely because you're not fenced in by a school year — that's a real structural advantage over the typical owner these statistics describe. But "we're not typical" is exactly the sentence to test against real numbers before, not after, the purchase — which is the whole point of the rental week ([doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html)).

## Three ways to get the same trips: own, rent, or fly

### Option 1 — Own a $60–90k financed used Class C

Building on the [ownership-cost framework](10-ownership-costs.html) and scaled to this profile's **$60–90k financed** band (down from the eliminated Class A's $100–150k), a realistic all-in annual cost:

| Category | Annual estimate (Class C, $60–90k financed) |
|---|---|
| Loan payment (~$68k financed after ~15% down on $80k, 8% APR, 12-yr) | ~$8,000–$8,900 |
| Insurance (Class C recreational use) | $1,000–$2,000 |
| Storage (driveway in Brick likely permitted — see [doc 20](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html)) | $0–$1,000 |
| Fuel (4,000–6,000 mi/yr @ 8–12 mpg, $3.50/gal) | ~$1,300–$2,600 |
| Maintenance (Class C, gas chassis) | $2,000–$3,500 |
| Memberships (Good Sam + Passport America) | ~$75 |
| Depreciation (economic cost of value lost, not cash out-of-pocket — see below) | $3,500–$5,500 |
| **Total annual cost of ownership** | **~$16,000–$21,000/year** |

The loan-payment math uses the same methodology as [doc 10](10-ownership-costs.html): scale the $100k/12-year illustration (~$1,082/mo, ~$13,000/yr at 8% APR) down proportionally to a ~$68k balance. Depreciation is added here as a real economic cost even though it isn't a monthly bill — a motorhome that drops 20–30% in year one ([MyDepreciation](https://www.mydepreciation.org/calculators/rv), which puts gas Class A even higher at 30–35%) and roughly 4–10%/year after that ([RV Loan Wizard's depreciation-by-type guide](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/rv-depreciation-by-type-and-age), which itself states 15–19% for year one, not 20–30%) is quietly spending money whether or not you sell.

### Option 2 — Rent a comparable Class C per trip

[Doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) already priced the shakedown week; the same rates apply to every future trip. Nightly Class C rates run roughly **$135–$275/night** nationally, with a peak-season median around **$185/night** ([BestRV 2026 Q1 RV Rental Pricing Report](https://www.bestrvrentalcompanies.com/blog/2026-q1-rv-rental-pricing-report)); a specific NJ-area Class C search shows averages in the **$200–$260/night all-in** range once fees are folded in ([RVezy New York Class C listings](https://www.rvezy.com/rv-rentals/class-c/destination/united-states/new-york/new-york)). Add the same stack [doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) itemized — service fees, insurance, mileage, generator, cleaning — and **the advertised nightly rate is typically only 40–60% of the real total**, meaning all-in cost runs **roughly 44% to 150% above the sticker rate** depending on the platform and season ([BestRV, RV Rental Costs 2026: Complete Breakdown + Hidden Fees](https://www.bestrvrentalcompanies.com/guides/rv-rental-costs)). A representative week-long Class C rental starts around **$2,400 all-in** on a ~$1,295 base ([BestRV 2026 Q1 report](https://www.bestrvrentalcompanies.com/blog/2026-q1-rv-rental-pricing-report)), with the same guide's separate cost breakdown citing **up to $3,200 all-inclusive** at the higher end ([BestRV, RV Rental Costs 2026](https://www.bestrvrentalcompanies.com/guides/rv-rental-costs)) — call it **$340–$460/night** for a 7-night trip once everything is added, or a cleaner planning number of **$300–$450/night all-in** for shorter or off-peak trips.

### Option 3 — Fly and stay in a hotel

For the same national/state-park destinations, budget **$200–$460/person/day** depending on comfort level, with the national-park-specific version — camping or budget lodging near the park, one pass covering the week — clustering around **$2,360–$2,770 for a family of four over seven nights on the ground** at parks like Grand Canyon, Yosemite, or Yellowstone, excluding flights, per a 2026 family-travel cost index ([Endless Travel Plans, Family Vacation Cost Index 2026](https://www.endlesstravelplans.com/guides/family-travel/family-vacation-cost-index-2026)) — call it **~$2,400** as a round planning number. A separate 2026 national-park budget planner gives comparable per-trip cost bands for a couple over 5 days (Budget $445, Mid-Range $1,130, Luxury $3,355), which corroborate the per-person daily range above but don't themselves cover a family-of-four week ([TravelDataLab, National Park Budget Planner 2026](https://www.traveldatalab.com/guides/national-park-budget-planner-2026)). Flights alone run **$200–$600/person round-trip domestic**, a rental car **$40–$80/day**, and hotels **$120–$250/night** depending on tier ([ComfortMyTrip, USA Road Trip Cost in 2026](https://comfortmytrip.com/blog/usa-road-trip-cost-2026)). A family-of-four week at a farther destination like Sequoia — flight + rental car + mid-range lodging + food — reasonably lands in the **$2,500–$4,000 range for the week**, or roughly **$350–$570/night** all-in. This option skips the RV entirely — no towing, no coach maintenance, no driveway question — but also skips the boondocking capability, the bikes-everywhere flexibility, and the WFH-aboard plan this whole docket is built around.

## The break-even table

| Nights/year of actual use | Owning ($16,500/yr midpoint ÷ nights) | Renting instead ($375/night midpoint) | Cheaper option |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7 nights (one week/year) | $2,357/night | $2,625 total | **Renting** — by a wide margin |
| 14 nights (two weeks/year) | $1,179/night | $5,250 total | **Renting** — still clearly |
| 21 nights (RVIA Class B/C median) | $786/night | $7,875 total | **Renting** — the "average owner" case |
| 30 nights (RVIA all-RV median) | $550/night | $11,250 total | **Renting cheaper by ~$5,250** |
| 45 nights | $367/night | $16,875 total | **Owning** — but only by ~$375 total, essentially the break-even point |
| 60 nights (~2 months/year) | $275/night | $22,500 total | **Owning, clearly** |
| 90 nights | $183/night | $33,750 total | **Owning, decisively** |

This table uses the ownership midpoint (~$16,500/year, before financing-band flex) against the rental midpoint (~$375/night, blending the $300–$450 range). It is deliberately simplified — it ignores the time-value of tying up a down payment, ignores that a rental unit is younger and better-equipped than a used purchase, and ignores that owning lets you leave gear packed and go on short notice, which itself has value. But the shape of the result is the honest one: on these midpoints alone, **break-even is $16,500 ÷ $375/night ≈ 44 nights/year** — meaningfully higher than either the RVIA all-RV median (30) or the Class B/C median (21) — and below that, renting is not a compromise, it's the financially smarter choice, full stop.

**Your household's structural edge:** homeschooling with no calendar tether is exactly the factor that could push real usage from the "typical Class C owner" 21-night median toward the 45–90-night range where ownership wins clearly. That's a real, credible argument for buying — but it is a *plan*, not yet a *fact*, until a season of actual trips confirms it.

## How fast RV enthusiasm decays — the industry's own data

The encouraging RVIA number — usage nearly doubled from 20 to 30 median nights between 2021 and 2025 — masks a well-documented pattern: initial enthusiasm is real, but it fades for a meaningful share of buyers, and it fades fast. Community-sourced accounts point to **buyer's remorse commonly surfacing within the first few months of ownership**, most often triggered by underestimated monthly costs, a floorplan that doesn't fit daily life, or maintenance issues that strain the budget ([Endless RVing, via RVTravel's coverage of RV buyer's remorse](https://www.rvtravel.com/rv-buyers-remorse-1201/) — **note: the widely repeated "70% of RV owners regret their purchase" figure traces to a single podcast/blog source (Endless RVing), not a peer-reviewed or industry-body survey; treat it as anecdotal, directionally consistent with other regret reporting, but not a rigorously sourced statistic**). Even RVIA's own framing — that 86% of current owners say they're using the RV as much or more than expected — is measuring *current* owners, which by definition excludes everyone who already sold and left the pool; it's a survivor-biased number, not evidence that regret is rare.

The practical translation: enough buyers stop using the rig heavily enough, soon enough, that a healthy secondary market of **lightly-used 1–3-year-old units** exists — which is good news for you as a *used* buyer (someone else absorbed both the depreciation and the disappointment), but a caution about what happens if the same pattern hits your family. **Depreciation doesn't pause because you stopped using it** — a motorhome sitting in the driveway keeps losing roughly 4–10%/year in years 2–5 whether or not it ever leaves the curb ([RV Loan Wizard, RV Depreciation by Type and Age](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/rv-depreciation-by-type-and-age)).

## The "shiny new toy" failure mode, named plainly

It has a shape, and it's worth naming before you're in it rather than after: the first two or three trips are magic — new territory, the novelty of cooking dinner in a parking lot with a view, the kids sleeping somewhere different. Then real life reasserts itself. Winterizing is a chore. The roof needs resealing. A tire needs replacing on the same 5–7-year schedule regardless of mileage ([doc 10](10-ownership-costs.html)). Scheduling a week off gets harder as work and homeschooling routines settle in. The rig sits in the driveway more months than it moves, quietly depreciating and quietly reminding the town it's there ([doc 20](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html)). None of this is a prediction about your family specifically — it's the modal story behind the used-RV listings that make [doc 10](10-ownership-costs.html)'s "buy the newest used rig in-band" strategy work in the first place. Somebody else's shiny-new-toy year is your bargain.

**The honest version of the risk, stated as a bet:** you are betting that your family's structural advantages — no school calendar, WFH mobility, a genuine boondocking interest — will keep you closer to the 45–90-night range than the 21-night Class B/C median. That's a reasonable bet given what's different about your setup. It is still a bet, and the rental week is how you shorten the odds before six figures move.

## Exit costs if it sits

If the bet doesn't pay off and the rig mostly sits, the costs don't stop — they just change character:

- **Depreciation continues on a fixed schedule** regardless of use — roughly 4–10%/year in years 2–5 ([RV Loan Wizard](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/rv-depreciation-by-type-and-age)), so an unused rig loses value at close to the same rate as a used one.
- **The loan doesn't pause.** A 12–15-year term (recommended in [doc 10](10-ownership-costs.html) to limit total interest) means years of payments regardless of nights used — the single biggest reason to avoid stretching to a 20-year term "to make the payment smaller."
- **Selling a lightly-used unit recovers less than expected.** A rig sold after 1–2 years of light use competes against other lightly-used listings from other families who had the same experience — precisely the used-market dynamic this docket exploits as a *buyer*. As a *seller* in that position, expect to be on the other side of that same discount.
- **Insurance and any paid storage keep running** even in a slow year — insurance doesn't prorate by nights used, and a paid storage contract is typically month-to-month or annual regardless of trips taken ([doc 10](10-ownership-costs.html)).

None of this argues against buying — it argues for sizing the bet honestly and having a plan for what happens if it doesn't pay off, which is exactly what the walk-away triggers below are for.

## Walk-away triggers — decide these now, not in the moment

Set these before the rental week, so they're decision rules rather than after-the-fact rationalizations:

1. **If a full season (12 months) of realistic trip-planning — with the actual calendar you have, not an aspirational one — doesn't produce a plausible path to roughly 44+ nights/year, rent instead of buying.** That's the break-even point in the table above; below it, renting wins on the numbers every time.
2. **If the rental week (doc 13) surfaces a hard "no" on Sara driving, the kids'-restraint logistics, or the WFH setup, don't buy a Class C on the theory that a different floorplan will fix it — rent a second, different unit and retest before committing capital.** A rental's whole purpose is to be a cheap "no."
3. **If by month 6 of ownership the rig has been used fewer than 3 times, stop and re-run this doc's break-even math with your actual numbers** — not as a failure, but as the checkpoint doing its job. A rig used 3 times in 6 months is tracking toward the 21-night median, not the 45–90-night target this bet needs.
4. **If two consecutive planned trips get cancelled or shrunk for reasons that will recur** (work travel, homeschool co-op commitments, a kid's activity schedule solidifying) — treat that as data about your real cadence, not bad luck, and revisit whether ownership still pencils out.
5. **If selling at any point would require dipping into savings beyond the down payment already spent** (i.e., you'd be meaningfully underwater), that's the moment to talk to a lender about the payoff number before compounding the mistake by holding on longer out of sunk-cost pressure.

## Decision checkpoints tied to the rental week

The [rental week](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) isn't just a mechanical test — it's the first data point for this doc's math. Use it as a checkpoint, not just a shakedown cruise:

- **Before booking the rental:** write down, as a family, your honest best guess at nights/year for year one. Put a number on paper. This doc's break-even table only works if you commit to a number before the trip colors your expectations.
- **During the rental week:** track how much of the week actually felt like "vacation" versus "managing a system" (winterizing, WFH troubleshooting, kid logistics). That ratio is a preview of the maintenance-tax reality that erodes enthusiasm over time.
- **After the rental week:** revisit the nights/year guess. If the week made you want to book three more trips before the summer's out, that's real signal toward the 45+ night range. If the honest reaction is relief that the rental is over, that's real signal too — and it's cheaper to hear it now, having spent $1,800–$3,500 ([doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html)), than after a $60–90k purchase.
- **Six months after purchase (if you buy):** run trigger #3 above for real, with real numbers, not projections.

## Bottom line

None of this is an argument against buying — a used Class C in this family's $60–90k band, bought after a rental week that confirms the plan, is a perfectly sound choice for a homeschooling family with genuine national-park ambitions and no calendar tether. But the industry's own data says the median Class B/C owner uses their rig only about 21 nights a year, that ownership at that pace costs several hundred dollars a night once every cost is counted honestly, and that a meaningful share of buyers feel real regret within months — not because RVs are bad, but because the gap between hoped-for use and real use is the single biggest hidden cost in this whole purchase. Your structural advantages are real and worth betting on. Just make it a bet you've sized on purpose, with a number on paper before the rental week and walk-away triggers you agreed to before the excitement of a new coach makes them harder to honor.
