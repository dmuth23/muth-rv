# Decisions & Open Questions

This docket started with five big open questions and a Class A frame. As of July 2026, Doug and Sara have gone further than "shape of the search" — they've made a **body-style pivot**: gas Class A is off the table, and the search is now a **used gas Class C, $60,000–$90,000 financed**. This page records what's decided under that new frame, what's still genuinely open, and a path to purchase consistent with all of it. Docs written for the old Class A frame (model shortlist, chassis/weights, the diesel primer, and the school-calendar cadence doc) are preserved in [`archive/`](archive/) and linked from here only where they're still historically relevant.

## Decided

- **Body style: used gas Class C, not Class A.** The primary mission is national and state park camping — including long cross-country reaches like Sequoia and Canyonlands — and park campground length caps punish big rigs more than expected once the data was actually pulled. [National park length limits](25-national-parks-rv-limits.html) and [state park limits near home and en route](26-state-parks-rv-limits.html) both show real campgrounds turning away anything much past 30–35 ft, which is the core evidence behind eliminating Class A rather than just deprioritizing it. Class-A-specific research (model shortlist, chassis/weights/payload, the diesel primer) is archived at [`archive/02-model-shortlist.html`](archive/02-model-shortlist.html), [`archive/03-chassis-weights-payload.html`](archive/03-chassis-weights-payload.html), and [`archive/17-diesel-primer.html`](archive/17-diesel-primer.html) — useful history, no longer the active search. The live landscape doc for the new frame is [doc 18](18-class-c-landscape.html).

- **Budget: $60,000–$90,000, financed, used.** This replaces the earlier $100,000–$150,000 band, which was sized for Class A. It's read as the coach's *purchase price* (what a loan is written against), not a post-down-payment number — see [doc 26](10-ownership-costs.html) for exactly how that math works, current 2026 RV-loan rates (roughly 7.5–10% APR for this band per [Bankrate](https://www.bankrate.com/loans/personal-loans/rv-loans/) and [RV Loan Wizard](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/rv-loan-interest-rates)), and the lender age/mileage cutoffs that bite harder on an older, cheaper used coach. Any dollar figure still floating around this docket from the old six-figure Class A framing is stale — read it against this band instead.

- **Kids ride legally first, living area only if a lawful and safe position exists.** Unchanged by the pivot, and if anything strengthened by it: New Jersey's child-restraint statute, [N.J.S.A. 39:3-76.2a](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-76-2a/), requires a harness or booster for both kids across the entire purchase window, and a Class C's cab-forward, factory front bucket seats are generally an easier, more conventional belted position to verify than a Class A's living-area seating. The decision is still: **shortlist and buy only around floorplans where a real forward-facing, chassis-anchored belted position can be verified by a CPST**, with a CPST check required on any specific candidate before it's taken seriously. Full law-vs-guidance breakdown and the front-seat legal ambiguity: [doc 05](05-family-and-kids.html).

- **Homeschooling: no calendar tether, decided as a lived reality, not just an option.** The kids are homeschooled, full stop — there is no school-year, no kindergarten cutoff, no attendance statute pinning any trip (or the rental week, or a purchase date) to a specific calendar window. This replaces the old "free 2026–27 travel year before kindergarten" framing entirely; that framing assumed public-school enrollment, which is no longer the plan. What homeschooling does *not* erase: the honest logistics of running lessons on the road, and the fact that a long stretch abroad (below) still needs a real plan for co-op activities and rhythm, not just an absence of legal deadline. Full treatment, including where homeschooling's flexibility genuinely helps and where it doesn't: [doc 09](29-homeschool-freedom.html). The archived school-calendar cadence doc, [`archive/21-school-and-travel-cadence.html`](archive/21-school-and-travel-cadence.html), is now historical — its Brick-schools kindergarten-cutoff math no longer applies to this family's plan.

- **Abroad travel is protected, not competing for the same dollars.** The family's work ties to Europe may create real travel opportunities during this purchase window, and the RV budget and the travel-abroad budget are treated as **separate lines**, not one pool the coach can quietly absorb. A European trip in a given year doesn't have to be forgone to make a coach payment, and a good year for the RV doesn't rule out a good year abroad — see [doc 8](28-bigger-picture.html) for the full reasoning. Practically, this also means **interim trips — renting, flying, a hotel-based trip abroad — are guilt-free** while the family is still shopping or between RV trips; they're not a failure to use the coach, they're the normal texture of a family that travels more than one way.

- **Rent-first is the agreed plan, and the Class C pivot makes it easier.** A shakedown rental — now specifically a Class C, not a Class A — remains the agreed mechanism for testing the size question, Sara's comfort behind the wheel, the WFH setup, and the kids' restraint logistics before real money moves. The pivot actually simplified this step: instead of hunting peer-to-peer listings for a specific Class A chassis, **Cruise America's Cliffwood, NJ depot (20–25 minutes from Brick) rents the exact size class now under evaluation** — a standardized C30 — with insurance built into a predictable price. Full plan, pricing, and the structured test protocol: [doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html).

- **Sara is likely more comfortable driving a Class C than a Class A** — one of the real inputs behind the pivot itself, not just a footnote surviving it. A Class C's more familiar cab-forward driving position (built on a van/truck chassis Sara has more analogous experience with) was already flagged as a genuine alternative worth walking at a show; the rental is still the actual test of the instinct, not a substitute for it. Full breakdown of what's fixable with training versus what's just physics, plus the NJ licensing law: [doc 22](22-one-driver-family.html).

- **No e-bikes, now or in the future.** This closes off a whole branch of the bike-carrying analysis in [doc 08](08-carrying-bikes.html) — rack weight capacity planning can target conventional bike weights only, permanently, regardless of body style.

- **Toad ratings just changed with the pivot — trailering is back on the table, but not automatic.** The old Class A / Ford F-53 chassis had a 5,000-lb factory hitch that killed towing either current car outright. The Class C shortlist runs on the **Ford E-450** chassis, whose manufacturer-installed hitch is commonly rated **7,500 lb on many floorplans** (confirmed on the Winnebago Outlook; some trims, like the Jayco Redhawk **SE**, still ship at the old 5,000-lb rating) — a real, verified-per-model reopening of the trailer question, not a guarantee for every coach. Neither the 2017 Toyota RAV4 Limited AWD nor the 2022 Hyundai Tucson can be flat-towed four-down or ride a tow dolly regardless of chassis — both owner's manuals require all four wheels off the ground ([Toyota RAV4 XA40 owner's manual, dinghy-towing section](https://www.trav4.net/dinghy_towing-82.html); [Hyundai Tucson owner's manual, emergency towing](https://www.htmanual.net/emergency_towing-400.html)) — so any toad plan for the current cars means a full car trailer, now plausible in weight terms on an upgraded-hitch E-450 where it wasn't on the F-53. Full menu and the hitch-rating math: [doc 19](19-towing-a-car.html); toad insurance: [doc 23](23-rv-insurance-and-toad-coverage.html).

## Now open

These are the decisions the pivot newly surfaces — the ones still genuinely unresolved:

1. **The length sweet spot.** This is now the single highest-leverage open question in the whole docket. A 25-ft coach felt too small in person when Doug sat in one; park length data ([doc 25](25-national-parks-rv-limits.html), [doc 26](26-state-parks-rv-limits.html)) says the sweet spot for national/state park access tops out well short of the old 35-ft Class A target. The right size sits somewhere between those two facts, and it should come from evidence, not vibes — which is exactly what the rental week is built to test. The [Class C landscape doc](18-class-c-landscape.html) lines up specific models against real park cutoffs; the [rental protocol](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) includes a mandatory night at a length-constrained state-park site (Cheesequake) specifically to pressure-test this.

2. **Toad vs. trailer vs. caravan, under the E-450's actual hitch rating.** The chassis pivot reopened trailering the RAV4 or Tucson, but only on coaches with the upgraded ~7,500-lb hitch — some E-450 floorplans still ship with the base 5,000-lb unit. Until a specific candidate coach's hitch rating is confirmed in writing, the real choice stays open between: trailering the current car, buying a light purpose-built flat-tow vehicle, or running a two-vehicle caravan (which also sidesteps the car-seat-in-the-coach question entirely, at the cost of splitting the family on travel days). Full math: [doc 19](19-towing-a-car.html).

3. **How many nights a year the family honestly believes.** [Doc 27 — the honest math](27-honest-math.html) puts a real number on the table: **break-even between owning and renting a comparable Class C lands around 44 nights/year**, well above both the RVIA's all-RV median (30 nights) and its Class B/C-specific median (21 nights). Homeschooling removes the calendar constraint that caps most families' usage, which is a genuine structural advantage — but "we're not typical" is a claim to test with an honest number on paper, not assume. This is the number that decides go/no-go on buying at all, not just which coach: if a full season of realistic trip-planning doesn't produce a plausible path to 44+ nights/year, the financially honest answer is to keep renting.

4. **When to book the rental.** With no school calendar to work around, there's no reason to wait past the current camping season — [doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) recommends booking a Cliffwood, NJ Cruise America C30 for the soonest dates that work, rather than letting "sometime in the next year" drift. The plan is agreed; picking the actual week is the remaining step.

5. **Which shakedown park to test the length question at.** [Doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) names **Cheesequake State Park** (Matawan, NJ) as the natural first pick — close to both Brick and the Cliffwood rental depot, with a confirmed 11-ft height restriction and a gravel-spur site layout that will surface real length friction. The ~30-ft length guidance reported for the campground is not stated on the park's own DEP page, so the site-specific limit still needs a phone confirmation (732-566-2161) before the rental is booked against it.

6. **CPST verification of specific floorplans.** Once real candidate coaches are in view, each one still needs an actual certified child-passenger-safety technician to physically verify whether its front seats or dinette can legally and safely anchor a car seat or booster — this can't be resolved from spec sheets or dealer claims (see doc 05's warning on "family floorplan" marketing).

## A suggested path to purchase

1. **Now (July–fall 2026):** book the rental week ([doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html)) — a Cruise America C30 out of Cliffwood, NJ — for the soonest dates the family and the depot's availability line up, since homeschooling means there's no calendar reason to wait. While waiting on it, start watching used Class C listings in the $60k–90k band (doc 18) to calibrate real July-2026-era asking prices, and start pricing E-450 hitch ratings by model (doc 19) — no buying yet, just watching.
2. **Rental week:** run the structured test — Sara at the wheel, a mandatory night at Cheesequake State Park (a real length-constrained site) to pressure-test the size question, the kids' actual restraint logistics on travel day, a real WFH day from the coach, and an off-grid night with the bike fleet loaded. Treat this as the evidence that resolves the length sweet spot, not a fun trip to enjoy and move past.
3. **After the rental, before serious shopping:** lock the length target against real park data (docs 25/26) and the rental's own experience; write down the family's honest nights/year estimate *before* the rental colors expectations, and check it against the 44-night break-even in doc 27; decide the toad/trailer/caravan question once a real hitch rating is in view; call Brick Township Zoning/Code Enforcement to confirm the driveway-storage plan against § 288-8 ([doc 20](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html)).
4. **When those are locked:** widen the floorplan search within the confirmed length band and the $60k–90k budget, with the confirmed hitch rating and seating requirement as hard filters; bring or hire a CPST to verify belted positions on any coach that reaches serious consideration.
5. **On any serious candidate:** NRVIA inspection, weight-sticker check, tire DOT dates, hitch-rating confirmation in writing if towing, the negotiation playbook ([doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html)), and — if buying out of state — the titling and in-transit insurance steps in [doc 24](24-buying-and-titling-out-of-state.html).

## Things the fact-checkers couldn't fully confirm

These claims survived across the docket but with hedged wording — treat them as "probably right, verify before relying on it":

- The **21-nights/year Class B/C median** cited in [doc 27](27-honest-math.html) is reported via a secondary AOL/RVIA-sourced analysis; the RVIA's own 2025 Go RVing member report wasn't independently accessible to confirm the class-level breakdown directly.
- The **44-night break-even figure** in doc 27 is a deliberately simplified model (ownership midpoint ÷ rental midpoint) — it ignores time-value of a down payment, the fact a rental unit is newer/better-equipped than a used purchase, and the convenience value of gear staying packed in an owned rig.
- Whether a specific E-450 Class C floorplan carries the upgraded **7,500-lb hitch** versus the base 5,000-lb unit is not knowable in general — [doc 19](19-towing-a-car.html) confirms it for the Winnebago Outlook but flags the Jayco Redhawk SE trim as a documented exception; get it in writing per coach.
- Dealer-vs-private price gaps and per-model Class C price bands ([doc 18](18-class-c-landscape.html)) — several source listings are price-gated or weren't independently re-confirmed; the $60–90k market snapshots in [doc 26](10-ownership-costs.html) are explicitly flagged unverified against a live listing.
- The exact dual-monitor workstation power draw (150–250W range) and professionally-installed lithium system pricing ($14.5K+ quotes) in [doc 06](06-wfh-connectivity.html) / [doc 07](07-power-boondocking.html).
- The "90% of hitch racks aren't RV-rated" figure and NRVIA inspection pricing ($400–900) in [doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html) / [doc 08](08-carrying-bikes.html).
- Current tire-age replacement guidance (5 vs. 7 years) varies by manufacturer — check the tire maker's own bulletin for whatever is on the rig you're inspecting.
- **Whether a Class C cab counts as a "rear seat" or "no rear seat" under NJ's child-restraint statute** for edge-case floorplans ([doc 05](05-family-and-kids.html)) — no NJ court decision, MVC guidance, or Attorney General document resolves this; a written question to the NJ MVC or a NJ traffic attorney is the only way to fully settle it for a specific floorplan.
- Two of Brick Township's five prohibited-RV-storage circumstances under § 288-8 — "inoperable" and "not belonging to the owner/occupant" — were surfaced in verification but their exact code wording wasn't recovered ([doc 20](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html)); confirm on the zoning call.
- The exact ~30-ft length guidance reported for Cheesequake State Park's family campground ([doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html)) isn't stated on the park's own DEP page; treat it as unverified until confirmed by phone.
