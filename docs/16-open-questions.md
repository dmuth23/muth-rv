# Decisions & Open Questions

This docket started with five big open questions. As of July 2026, Doug and Sara talked through them and made real calls on most of them — not final floorplan picks, but the *shape* of the search: budget, priorities, and which paths stay on the table. This page records what's **decided**, what those decisions **newly open up**, and a **suggested path to purchase** that's consistent with all of it.

## Decided

- **Budget: financing $100,000–$150,000, flexible for a genuine deal.** This replaces the earlier open-ended budget question. It's a financed band, not a hard ceiling or floor — a great deal could land somewhat below it, and the range stays flexible rather than a fixed number. Every dollar figure elsewhere in the docket (used gas Class A prices in [doc 02](02-model-shortlist.html), diesel price bands in [doc 17](17-diesel-primer.html), Class C prices in [doc 18](18-class-c-landscape.html)) should be read against this band, not against the older "$55K–$110K+" framing from the original shortlist research.

- **Kids ride legally first, living area only if a lawful and safe position exists.** The docket's most consequential finding stands: New Jersey's child-restraint statute, [N.J.S.A. 39:3-76.2a](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-76-2a/), requires a harness or booster for both kids across the entire purchase window, and most Class A living-area seating (side- or rear-facing sofas and dinettes) cannot safely or legally anchor one. Doug also corrected an error in an earlier draft: putting a child in the **front passenger seat is not simply "the answer"** — it's legally unsettled how NJ's "rear seat" default applies to a motorhome cab, and CPST guidance actually ranks staying out of the motorized coach entirely *above* any seat inside it. The decision is: **shortlist and buy only around floorplans where a real forward-facing, chassis-anchored belted position can be verified by a CPST** — whether that's a front cab seat, a factory captain's chair, or a genuinely structural dinette — and treat the two-vehicle option below as a first-class alternative, not a fallback. Full law-vs-guidance breakdown, the restraint timeline as the kids age from 4 to 6, and the front-seat correction: [doc 05](05-family-and-kids.html).

- **Toad: newly under study, not decided — but the current cars are ruled out for flat-tow/dolly.** Doug put towing a car on the table for the first time and asked for the full landscape on the two vehicles actually in the driveway. The research verdict: **neither the 2017 Toyota RAV4 Limited AWD nor the 2022 Hyundai Tucson (in AWD or hybrid form) can be flat-towed four-down or ride on a tow dolly** — both owner's manuals require all four wheels off the ground for towing ([Toyota RAV4 XA40 owner's manual, dinghy-towing section](https://www.trav4.net/dinghy_towing-82.html); [Hyundai Tucson owner's manual, emergency towing](https://www.htmanual.net/emergency_towing-400.html)). For either car, the only method is a full car trailer, which likely exceeds a standard gas Class A's ~5,000-lb hitch rating once loaded. That leaves the real choices open: buy a light purpose-built flat-tow toad (e.g., a used Jeep Wrangler), run no toad and use a two-vehicle caravan instead, or lean toward a diesel/larger-hitch coach that can pull a trailer. Full menu, costs, and the legal supplemental-braking requirement: [doc 19](19-towing-a-car.html); how the toad is actually insured while towed: [doc 23](23-rv-insurance-and-toad-coverage.html).

- **Travel cadence: flexible and undecided, by design — but kindergarten puts a real deadline on the calendar.** Doug and Sara are not locking a cadence archetype yet, and the docket treats that as legitimate rather than something to force. What is settled as fact: under Brick Township Public Schools' cutoff, the kids' on-time kindergarten year is **September 2027** ([Brick Township Public Schools, Registration](https://www.brickschools.org/our-district/registration-2/)), while New Jersey's compulsory-attendance statute, [N.J.S.A. 18A:38-25](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-18a/section-18a-38-25/), doesn't legally compel attendance until age six (March 2028) — meaning 2026–27 is a fully free travel year and even the 2027–28 kindergarten year has legal give in it. The cadence archetypes, veteran-family reports, and how usage days interact with the budget: [doc 21](21-school-and-travel-cadence.html).

- **Home storage: likely the Brick driveway, pending the confirmation call.** The plan of record — coach lives outside, in the driveway, at the family's Brick Township home — was researched against Brick's actual municipal code rather than assumed. The governing rule, § 288-8 of Brick's Chapter 288, is **more permissive than typically feared**: a registered, operable, family-owned motorhome kept in the driveway (out of a corner-lot sight triangle, not parked on the front lawn, moved at least once every 12 months) appears to satisfy every recovered condition ([Brick Township eCode360, Chapter 288](https://ecode360.com/6900944)). This is "likely fine" rather than "certain" because the code was recovered via search index and a mirror site (eCode360 blocks automated page pulls), and two of the five prohibited circumstances couldn't be fully re-verified — see [doc 20](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html) for exactly what to confirm with Brick Zoning/Code Enforcement (732-262-1000) before buying.

- **Rent-first is the agreed plan, not a recommendation to weigh.** A one-week rental of a 30–35 ft coach, booked for later 2026 or early 2027, is the agreed mechanism for testing the 35-foot assumption, Sara's comfort behind the wheel, the WFH setup, and the kids' actual restraint logistics before real money moves. Full plan, rental sources near Brick, and the structured test protocol: [doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html).

- **Sara is likely not comfortable driving a Class A** — treated as a real design input, not a footnote. This doesn't rule out a Class A (thousands of families run one-driver-capable), but it puts real weight behind two things already in this docket: the rental as the actual test of the instinct, and the large Class C's more familiar cab-forward driving position as a genuine alternative worth walking at a show. Full breakdown of what's fixable with training versus what's just physics, plus the NJ licensing law: [doc 22](22-one-driver-family.html).

- **No e-bikes, now or in the future.** This closes off a whole branch of the bike-carrying analysis in [doc 08](08-carrying-bikes.html) — rack weight capacity planning can target conventional bike weights only, permanently.

- **No shortlisting or ranking — full landscape stays open, including Class C and diesel.** Doug does not want a "winner" named at this stage. The docket continues to map the **full field**: used gas Class A across every major brand tier ([doc 02](02-model-shortlist.html)), the large Class C (30 ft+, paired with a toad) as a genuine parallel path with its own front-cab belted-seating advantage ([doc 18](18-class-c-landscape.html)), and diesel pushers — no longer excluded — as a category Doug wants to recognize a good deal in in real time, even though he'd shop-service rather than DIY one, versus wrenching on a gas coach himself ([doc 17](17-diesel-primer.html)).

## Now open

These are the decisions the answers above surface next — the ones still genuinely unresolved:

1. **Which toad path, if any?** Buy a purpose-built flat-tow vehicle (new cost layered on top of the coach budget), accept the car-trailer route behind a heavier-hitch coach, or skip a toad and run two vehicles. This can't be fully settled until the Class A vs. Class C question (below) is closer, since hitch capacity and chassis differ.

2. **Two-vehicle caravan vs. toad — which actually fits this family better?** A caravan (drive the family car separately, kids ride there) directly solves the car-seat problem *and* the toad problem at once, at the cost of splitting the family on travel days and running two vehicles' worth of fuel and tolls. Worth pressure-testing on the rental week rather than deciding on paper.

3. **Class A vs. large Class C, after the rental gives real data.** The rental (doc 13) is explicitly designed to surface whether Sara's driving comfort, the kids' seating reality, and the WFH workflow point toward one body style over the other. This is the single highest-leverage undecided question, because it reshapes nearly everything downstream — chassis, hitch rating, floorplan search, even the diesel question (diesel pushers are effectively Class-A-only in this size/price range).

4. **Buy earlier (use the free 2026–27 travel year) vs. buy later (no rush once the calendar is school-bound anyway).** Doc 21 lays out the argument for both sides; this is a genuine values call about how much the family wants to front-load big trips before kindergarten versus take more time finding the right used coach.

5. **CPST verification of specific floorplans.** Once real candidate coaches are in view, each one needs an actual certified child-passenger-safety technician to physically verify whether its front seat, captain's chairs, or dinette can legally and safely anchor a car seat or booster — this can't be resolved from spec sheets or dealer claims (see doc 05's warning on "family floorplan" marketing).

6. **When to book the rental week.** The plan is agreed; the calendar slot isn't. Given the free 2026–27 year, booking sooner rather than later both tests the assumptions earlier and leaves more runway to react to what's learned — worth picking an actual week rather than letting "sometime in the next year" drift.

## A suggested path to purchase

1. **Now (July–fall 2026):** book the rental week ([doc 13](13-rent-before-you-buy.html)) for a 30–35 ft gas Class A; while waiting on it, start watching RV Trader listings across the model landscape (doc 02, doc 18) and diesel listings (doc 17) to calibrate real July-2026-era asking prices against the $100K–$150K band — no buying yet, just watching.
2. **Rental week:** run the structured test — Sara at the wheel, the kids' actual restraint logistics on travel day, a real WFH day from the coach, and an off-grid night with the bike fleet loaded. Treat this as the evidence that resolves the Class A vs. Class C question and the caravan-vs-toad question, not a fun trip to enjoy and move past.
3. **After the rental, before serious shopping:** lock the body-style decision (Class A vs. large Class C) and the toad/caravan decision; call Brick Township Zoning/Code Enforcement to confirm the driveway-storage plan against § 288-8 (doc 20); decide buy-earlier-vs-later against the kindergarten calendar (doc 21).
4. **When those are locked:** widen the floorplan search across the full landscape (still no shortlist) with the confirmed body style and seating requirement as hard filters; bring or hire a CPST to verify belted positions on any coach that reaches serious consideration.
5. **On any serious candidate:** NRVIA inspection, weight-sticker check against the payload math ([doc 03](03-chassis-weights-payload.html)), tire DOT dates, hitch-rating confirmation if towing, the negotiation playbook ([doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html)), and — if buying out of state — the titling and in-transit insurance steps in [doc 24](24-buying-and-titling-out-of-state.html).

## Things the fact-checkers couldn't fully confirm

These claims survived across the docket but with hedged wording — treat them as "probably right, verify before relying on it":

- Dealer-vs-private price gaps (~$15–20K claimed) and per-model Class C price bands ([doc 18](18-class-c-landscape.html)) — several source listings are price-gated or weren't independently re-confirmed.
- The exact dual-monitor workstation power draw (150–250W range) and professionally-installed lithium system pricing ($14.5K+ quotes) in [doc 06](06-wfh-connectivity.html) / [doc 07](07-power-boondocking.html).
- The "90% of hitch racks aren't RV-rated" figure and NRVIA inspection pricing ($400–900) in [doc 04](04-used-buying-inspection.html) / [doc 08](08-carrying-bikes.html).
- Crosswind speed thresholds and tail-swing distance for a Class A ([doc 22](22-one-driver-family.html)) — sourced to community driving guides, not an engineering standard.
- Current tire-age replacement guidance (5 vs. 7 years) varies by manufacturer — check the tire maker's own bulletin for whatever is on the rig you're inspecting.
- **Whether a Class A cockpit legally counts as a "rear seat" or "no rear seat" under NJ's child-restraint statute** ([doc 05](05-family-and-kids.html)) — no NJ court decision, MVC guidance, or Attorney General document resolves this; it's a genuine open legal question, not a hedge that more research will close. A written question to the NJ MVC or a NJ traffic attorney is the only way to fully settle it for a specific floorplan.
- Two of Brick Township's five prohibited-RV-storage circumstances under § 288-8 — "inoperable" and "not belonging to the owner/occupant" — were surfaced in verification but their exact code wording wasn't recovered ([doc 20](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html)); confirm on the zoning call.
- The RAV4/Tucson tow-dolly-for-FWD-Tucson "gray area" ([doc 19](19-towing-a-car.html)) — physically possible per the dolly's mechanics, but Hyundai doesn't bless it for recreational use, so treat any dolly plan for a FWD Tucson as unverified against the manual.
