# Key Findings — The Short Version

The whole docket in one read. Every number here is cited and explained in its home doc
(linked); this page is the map, not the territory.

## 1. Parks don't ban classes — they publish length limits

Across the 137 park campgrounds surveyed, exactly **one** equipment-type ban turned up
(a tents-only loop in Virginia). What actually gates a motorhome is **site length limits
and the roads on the way in**. Details and every source:
[national parks](25-national-parks-rv-limits.html) · [state parks](26-state-parks-rv-limits.html).

The numbers that matter most to us:

| Finding | Number |
|---|---|
| Canyonlands, Squaw Flat campground | **28 ft max** |
| Sequoia, Generals Highway access advisory | **~22–24 ft** (but there's a workaround: enter via Hwy 180 through the Kings Canyon side) |
| State parks with published caps that fit **27 ft** | **100%** |
| …that fit **30 ft** | **95%** |
| …that fit **32 ft** | **80%** |
| Only sub-30-ft cap in the whole state survey | Custer SP (SD), Sylvan Lake loop — 27 ft |

Road-access gotchas that matter more than campsites: the Zion–Mt. Carmel tunnel
(escort/fee rules) and Glacier's Going-to-the-Sun Road (21 ft vehicle limit —
you camp low, you don't drive the rig over).

## 2. The size answer is converging on ~27–30 feet

The 25-footer we toured felt too small; the park data says don't go much past 30. The
window between those is real but narrow — and there's a catch:
**the shortest coaches disproportionately ride the lighter E-350 chassis**, so there is no
length where we get maximum cargo/towing capacity *and* the most park-friendly footprint at
once. That trade is mapped model-by-model in [The Class C Landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html).

## 3. The money says: renting wins below ~44 nights a year

On honest midpoints, owning a $60–90k used Class C beats renting only above roughly
**44 nights per year** — and the industry median owner uses a Class B/C about **21 nights**
(~30 across all RVs). That's the go/no-go number, and it deserves a written-down honest
answer *before* the rental trip colors it. Full math, assumptions, and walk-away triggers:
[The Honest Math](27-honest-math.html). (One flag: the underlying cost-of-ownership doc is
still awaiting its second fact-check pass — treat exact dollar figures as good estimates,
not gospel.)

## 4. Towing came back from the dead

Neither of our cars can be flat-towed — that verdict stands. But the Class C pivot changes
the math: many E-450 coaches carry **7,500–8,000 lb hitches** (vs. the 5,000 lb that killed
trailering in the Class A era), which puts **the RAV4 on a car trailer** back in rated
territory. It's a per-unit check — some builds still ship the 5,000 lb hitch — and the same
receiver has to choose between trailer and bike rack.
Details: [Towing a Car](19-towing-a-car.html) · [Carrying Six Bikes](08-carrying-bikes.html).

## 5. Homeschooling is a structural advantage — with limits

No school calendar means shoulder-season parks: easier reservations, lower rates, empty
weekdays — the exact conditions that make hitting 44+ nights plausible for us when it isn't
for the median family. The honest counterweights (co-ops, sports, home rhythms) are in
[Homeschooling as a Travel Superpower](29-homeschool-freedom.html), and the guilt-free plan
for travel *before* any purchase — including trips abroad — is in
[The Bigger Picture](28-bigger-picture.html).
