# Carrying 6 Bikes: Racks, the Toad, and the Trailer Alternative

Six bikes is the sneaky-hard number in this whole RV plan. Two bikes on a cheap hitch rack is a solved problem. Six bikes — three adult bikes (Doug's gravel, road, and mountain), Sara's bike, and two small kids' bikes — pushes past what a single rack comfortably carries, adds four feet of rack behind an already-long coach, and raises a security question worth thinking about early (a rolling bike collection hanging off the back at a campground is a real target). This document walks through what actually works, what breaks, and how the whole picture changes now that a **towed car (a "toad")** is on the table for this family.

Two upfront facts reshape everything below:

- **These are all conventional bikes — no e-bikes, now or ever** (a decision the family has locked in). That matters more than it sounds. A loaded e-bike runs 55-70 lbs; a conventional adult bike with disc brakes and wide tires runs about 28-35 lbs, and a kid's 20-inch bike about 18-24 lbs ([The Pro's Closet bike-weight guide](https://www.theproscloset.com/blogs/news/the-weight-equation-how-important-is-a-light-bike); [Two Wheeling Tots 20-inch bike testing](https://www.twowheelingtots.com/best-kids-20-inch-bikes/) — both cycling-community sources). All six of your bikes together weigh roughly **150 lbs**, not the 400-500 lbs an e-bike fleet would. So the old worry — *will the hitch even hold the weight?* — mostly dissolves. The real constraints become **rack slots, rear overhang, campsite length, weather, and theft.**
- **A toad rewrites the mounting question.** If you tow a car, the back of the coach is now doing two jobs — and the bikes might not need to ride on the coach at all. That's the central decision this document is built around, and it connects directly to [Towing a Car Behind the Motorhome](19-towing-a-car.html), which covers the towability of your specific cars in full.

## Why "just get a bike rack" isn't the whole answer

A hitch rack on the back of a Class A behaves nothing like one on the back of a minivan. Two things work against you:

1. **Long rear overhang amplifies bounce.** The distance between a motorhome's rear axle and its hitch is far greater than on a car, so the hitch point acts like the far end of a see-saw. Every pothole and expansion joint gets multiplied into vertical and side-to-side motion at the rack. Rack makers describe this bluntly: the forces on an RV can make a 30-lb bike *feel* like twice that to the rack and hitch, and a rack built for a Honda Odyssey can crack welds or drop your bike on the highway if you put it on a motorhome ([Let's Go Aero: What Makes a Bike Rack RV Approved](https://www.letsgoaero.com/blogs/resources/what-makes-bike-rack-rv-approved)).
2. **Most racks on the market aren't rated for it.** Many hitch bike racks are explicitly *not* rated for use behind a motorhome or trailer — they're rated for tow vehicles only, and RV-rated racks often exclude towable/trailer use because of lateral fatigue ([Let's Go Aero: Bike Racks for Travel Trailers](https://www.letsgoaero.com/blogs/resources/bike-racks-for-travel-trailers); [etrailer: What Is an RV Bike Rack?](https://www.etrailer.com/faq-what-is-an-rv-bike-rack.aspx); [RockyMounts: Why You Should Avoid RV and Trailer Use With Bike Racks](https://rockymounts.com/blog/why-you-should-avoid-rv-and-trailer-use-with-bike-racks/)). No source pins down an exact percentage, so treat "most racks" as directionally right rather than precise; using a car-rated rack anyway is how people lose bikes on I-95.

This is why "RV-approved" or "RV-rated" is a real, meaningful label — not marketing fluff — and it's the first filter you should apply to any rack that rides on the coach.

## The central decision: rack on the coach, or a toad?

Here is the tension that reshapes everything: **a Class A has exactly one rear receiver hitch, and a toad's tow bar wants that same hitch.** So the instinctive "just put a bike rack on the back" and "just tow the car" plans collide — by default it's **a rack *or* a toad, not both**, unless you add hardware to split the receiver (Branch B) or move the bikes off the coach entirely (Branch C/D). Because this family is now seriously considering a toad ([doc 19](19-towing-a-car.html)), the bike-carrying plan can't be settled in isolation — the two decisions are one decision. Settle the mounting question before you buy anything. There are more than two branches here.

### Branch A — no toad: hitch rack on the coach

The classic setup. The coach's factory rear receiver carries the bikes. Gas Class A coaches on the **Ford F-53 chassis** (under most Winnebago Sunstar, Adventurer, and Vista models, among many) come with a factory receiver typically rated **5,000 lbs trailer / 500 lbs tongue weight** as a dead-weight hitch ([etrailer F-53 towing discussion](https://www.etrailer.com/question-683869.html); [2024 Ford F-53 chassis specs](https://www.galpinford.com/models/2024/Ford/F-53_Motorhome_Stripped_Chassis/208%22_WB/)). With conventional bikes that rating is enormous headroom on weight — but two things still matter:

- **The tongue rating is a *towing* number, not a *rack* number.** A bike rack loads the hitch through leverage and vibration, not static downward weight, and some coach makers publish a *separate, lower* vertical/carrier limit. Winnebago has been reported capping **vertical load around 300 lbs** on some models because of how the hitch mounts to the rear overhang ([Winnie Owners forum: Limits on Winnebago Installed Receiver Hitch](https://www.winnieowners.com/threads/limits-on-winnebago-installed-receiver-hitch.700881/) — owner-reported). Your ~150 lbs of bikes plus a 40-60 lb rack sits comfortably under even that lower figure, but always confirm your specific coach's rated carrier load in its owner's manual rather than assuming the F-53 towing number applies.
- **Rear weight lightens the front axle.** Hanging load off the back of an F-53 shifts weight rearward and can produce vaguer steering and worse crosswind behavior — noticeable on the Turnpike or Garden State Parkway with a loaded coach ([iRV2: Front Hitch / weight-shift discussion](https://www.irv2.com/forums/f23/front-hitch-for-ford-f-53-chassis-294568.html) — community). With conventional bikes this is minor, but it's one more reason to keep the load reasonable and the heaviest items low and centered.

### Branch B — toad, bikes on the coach *and* the toad (dual-receiver)

If you tow a car, the coach's single receiver is now occupied by the tow bar. You are **not** automatically forced to choose rack-or-toad: a **dual-receiver adapter** (e.g., the Roadmaster RM-077-4) slides into the coach's 2-inch receiver and gives you two receivers — tow bar on the bottom, bike rack on the top — and some owners use a multi-position drop hitch to the same end ([etrailer: bike rack while flat towing](https://www.etrailer.com/question-291656.html); [etrailer: how to use a bike rack while flat towing](https://www.etrailer.com/question-291656.html) — expert Q&A + community threads). Real, but with honest caveats you should weigh before treating it as a clean solve:

- **You're cantilevering *even farther* back.** The rack now sits behind the tow-bar coupling, adding leverage at the very tail of an already-long overhang.
- **Tow-bar fold interference.** If your tow bar folds for storage, a loaded rack in the upper receiver can block it — meaning you remove the rack (or bikes) before folding the bar.
- **Turn clearance.** Bikes projecting behind the coach *and* ahead of the toad tighten your swing; owners are told to test the full rig in an empty lot before the first trip.
- **Rack rating still applies.** Use an RV-rated rack even here; a car rack fails just as fast in this position.

### Branch C — toad, bikes on the *toad*

Often the simplest answer once a toad exists: put the bikes on the car, not the coach. The bikes then ride ahead of the coach's rear (or on the toad's own roof/hitch), out of the coach's overhang entirely. Your two candidate cars carry bikes differently:

- **Roof racks (both cars): ~165 lb *dynamic* limit.** Both a 2017 RAV4 and a 2022 Tucson factory roof system are commonly cited at about **165 lbs of moving (dynamic) load** — plenty for two or three bikes on fork/wheel mounts plus the crossbars ([RAV4 roof-rack weight guide](https://autoreviewnest.com/toyota-rav4-roof-rack-weight-limit-explained/); [Hyundai Tucson roof-rail capacity thread](https://www.tucson-forum.com/threads/roof-rails-weigth-capacity.859/) — both community-reported; **confirm against your car's owner's manual before loading**). Roof mounting keeps bikes off the back but adds height (mind garage and drive-thru clearances) and is a lift for the adult bikes.
- **Hitch rack on the toad (both cars accept a 2-inch Class III):** A bolt-on Class III hitch for the Tucson is rated **3,500 lbs trailer / 525 lbs tongue** ([Curt part #13485 — manufacturer spec](https://www.curtmfg.com/part/13485)); comparable hitches exist for the RAV4. That easily carries a hitch-mount bike rack. **But** a hitch rack on the *back* of a flat-towed car sits between the toad and nothing — fine while parked, and workable while towing only if the rack is rated for towed-vehicle use and doesn't foul the ground on driveway ramps.
- **Hatch/trunk-strap racks:** the cheapest option, but they contact the car's paint and rear glass and are the least secure — acceptable for short hops, not ideal for a loaded cross-country haul.

**The catch that ties Branch C to doc 19:** whether the bikes-on-the-toad plan even applies depends on *how* the car is towed. The **2017 RAV4 Limited AWD you own today is not approved for flat (four-down) towing, and its AWD driveline also rules out a tow dolly** — it would need a full car-hauler trailer ([Four Wheel Trends: can a RAV4 be flat towed](https://fourwheeltrends.com/can-toyota-rav4-be-flat-towed-behind-a-motorhome-rv/) — community; verify in the RAV4 owner's manual "Recreational Towing" section, the primary source). If the toad rides on a trailer anyway, the bikes can simply ride *on the trailer with it* (see the trailer section below). The full towability breakdown for both your cars lives in [Towing a Car Behind the Motorhome](19-towing-a-car.html) — this document only cares about where the bikes end up.

### Branch D — enclosed or open trailer (covered below)

A cargo trailer or car-hauler that carries the bikes (and possibly the toad too). Detailed in [The trailer alternative](#the-trailer-alternative).

## RV-approved racks: what to actually buy

For any rack that rides on the coach (Branch A or B), look for one explicitly rated for motorhome/trailer use — usually labeled "RV-approved" or "RV-rated." Check:

- **2-inch (Class III) receiver, welded/reinforced steel construction**, not the thin-wall tubing of economy car racks ([Let's Go Aero RV-approved guide](https://www.letsgoaero.com/blogs/resources/what-makes-bike-rack-rv-approved)).
- **Anti-rattle hitch pin or clamp** to remove receiver slop — RV owners recommend adding a hitch tightener even on a good rack, because play in the receiver turns into destructive banging over miles ([iRV2 installation thread](https://www.irv2.com/forums/f23/front-hitch-for-ford-f-53-chassis-294568.html) — community).
- **Per-bike and total rating that covers your heaviest bike.** Your gravel and mountain bikes can hit 30-35 lbs each; don't buy to the lightest bike in the family.

Products that repeatedly come up as genuinely RV-rated (not car racks with an "RV" sticker):

- **Swagman Traveler XC2** — an established RV-approved 2-bike rack, rated to 35 lbs/bike, welded steel, with a bumper-adapter option; reviewed over multi-year ownership ([FoxRVTravel three-year review](https://foxrvtravel.com/swagman-rv-approved-bike-rack/) — community; [Swagman product page](https://www.swagman.net/products/traveler-xc2-rv-approved) — manufacturer). Its frame-clamp design is a poor fit for skinny carbon frames — relevant if the road bike here is carbon.
- **VelociRAX 412 MAX** — a 4-bike vertical roll-in/roll-out platform rack rated to **100 lbs per tire basket, 300 lbs total** ([VelociRAX 412 MAX product page](https://www.velocirax.com/products/412-max) — manufacturer; [GearJunkie MAX-series review](https://gearjunkie.com/biking/velocirax-5x-max-hitch-bike-rack-review) — cycling media). Its no-lift roll-on loading matters when you're wrangling a bike plus small kids in a parking lot; a strong candidate for four of the six bikes. Note it requires a **2-inch Class III receiver with a 500-lb tongue rating** to insert — well within an F-53's numbers, but confirm your specific coach's *carrier* limit (see Branch A) before assuming.
- **1Up USA Super Duty** and **Küat Piston Ion** — both rated to roughly 100 lbs/bike and repeatedly cited by cyclist-heavy RV forums as the "buy once" tier for someone who doesn't want to worry about a $2,000+ mountain bike ([Switchback Travel hitch-rack guide](https://www.switchbacktravel.com/biking/best-hitch-bike-racks); [Bikerumor 2026 tested roundup](https://bikerumor.com/best-hitch-bike-racks/) — both cycling media). Expensive (often $700-1,200+ as of July 2026), but the tray design (wheel cradles, no frame contact) is gentler on nice frames and harder to snatch than hang-style racks.

**Bumper-mount caution:** Swagman and others sell bumper-adapter kits for RVs with a continuous welded steel rear bumper — but many Class A rear bumpers are cosmetic sheet metal, not structural. Don't bumper-mount without confirming your coach's bumper is the reinforced continuous-weld type; the hitch receiver is the safer default ([etrailer: How to Choose a Bike Rack for a Motorhome](https://www.etrailer.com/faq-Motorhome-RV-Bike-Racks.aspx)).

**Ladder racks: skip them for real bikes.** RV roof ladders are built for climbing, not for carrying moving cargo, and most ladder bike racks are rated only 50-60 lbs ([RV Parenting: Are RV Ladder Bike Racks Safe?](https://rvparenting.com/are-rv-ladder-bike-racks-safe/) — community). The dynamic shock of a bouncing bike at highway speed can exceed a ladder's static limit by a wide margin ([Bike-Hook 2026 ladder-rack guide](https://bike-hook.com/blogs/news/best-rv-ladder-bike-rack-options-2026-guide) — community). Fine for one light kid's bike as overflow at most; wrong for a $1,500 gravel bike.

## Splitting the load: what goes inside

Six bikes on one rack is a lot of slots and four feet of tail behind an already-long coach. The pattern serious cyclist-RVers use is a **hybrid**: some bikes on a good RV-rated rack, the rest — especially the kids' bikes — stored inside. (With conventional bikes the driver here is *space and access*, not weight.)

**Basement/pass-through storage.** Many 35-foot Class A coaches have a front-to-back pass-through basement bay. Two adult bikes fit standing with the **front wheel removed** (a five-minute quick-release/thru-axle job), cutting the footprint sharply; fork-mount blocks or bungee-and-foam setups keep frames off the cabinetry ([DoItYourselfRV basement storage mod](https://www.doityourselfrv.com/rv-basement-storage-mod-pass-through/); [Camping World: pass-through storage ideas](https://blog.campingworld.com/gear-and-accessories/rv-pass-through-storage-ideas/) — community/vendor). This is the best place for bikes you want protected from grime and weather, and the most theft-resistant spot once the bay is locked.

**Kids' bikes specifically** are small enough to get creative: horizontal rail mounts in a bunk alcove, hooks in an oversized cabinet, or simply strapped upright in a corner of the basement with pool-noodle padding ([Habitatista: 7 Ways to Maximize Bike Storage in RVs](https://www.habitatista.com/75407/7-ways-to-maximize-bike-storage-in-rvs/); [Two Wheeling Tots bike-storage ideas](https://www.twowheelingtots.com/best-bike-storage-ideas/) — community). A 20-inch kid's bike is more a space-and-scratching problem than a weight one, and interior storage solves both.

A workable split for your mix: **3 adult bikes on a rated 4-bike hitch rack** (one slot spare), **Sara's bike plus a kid's bike in the basement pass-through** (front wheels off), and the second kid's bike inside or on the toad. That keeps the rack to 3-4 bikes, which matters for rear overhang and for backing into a site without four extra feet of rack — even though the *weight* would be fine at six.

## Protecting the bikes: grime, weather, and theft

**Road grime and weather.** A hitch rack puts bikes in the road-film zone behind the coach, and a season of highway miles will visibly foul drivetrains. A fitted bike cover is cheap insurance and doubles as theft camouflage by hiding what's back there ([iRV2: Bikes on Hitch Racks — Cover or No Cover](https://www.irv2.com/forums/f92/bikes-on-hitch-racks-cover-or-no-cover-498343.html) — community).

**Theft.** Casual campers underestimate this; cyclist-RVers don't. A rack-mounted bike is visible, sits in public lots and campgrounds, and is often left unattended overnight at a site. iRV2 threads are blunt: nice bikes on a hitch rack are a target, cable locks don't stop an angle grinder, and the realistic goal isn't "theft-proof" but "harder to steal than the rig next to you" ([iRV2: Securing Bikes & Bike Rack](https://www.irv2.com/forums/f59/securing-bikes-and-bike-rack-632008.html); [The Fit RV: How to Lock Your Bike for RVers](https://www.thefitrv.com/blog/how-to-lock-your-bike-for-rvers/) — community). Practical layers:

- A **hardened chain or heavy cable through both wheels and the frame, locked to the rack itself** — not bike-to-bike, or the whole cluster walks away as a unit.
- **Covers** to disguise a nice bike as an ordinary one, cutting opportunistic theft.
- **A second, independent lock point**, so cutting one cable doesn't free the bike.
- **Insurance**: check whether your homeowner's/renter's policy already covers bikes away from home (many do, with a per-item sublimit often too low for a $3,000+ bike), and consider a standalone bike policy (Velosurance or similar) for the expensive adult bikes. Check *before* you need it, not after.

## The trailer alternative

If keeping all six bikes weatherproofed, locked, and out of sight matters more than simplicity, a small enclosed cargo trailer is a real option — with real tradeoffs. And there's a **new reason it may make sense for this family specifically**: since the 2017 RAV4 AWD can't be flat-towed *or* dollied and would need a car-hauler anyway (see [Towing a Car](19-towing-a-car.html)), a trailer that hauls the toad **can carry the bikes with it** — folding two problems into one tow.

**Weight is not the constraint.** A small 4x6 or 5x8 enclosed cargo trailer weighs roughly 400-1,800 lbs empty depending on size and axles, well within the F-53's 5,000-lb hitch rating even loaded with six ~30-lb bikes and gear ([Curt trailer-weight reference](https://www.curtmfg.com/trailer-weight); [Haulmark small-trailer specs](https://makemytrailer.com/enclosed-trailer-small-4x6/) — vendor). Length and maneuverability are the binding constraints.

**Length and campsite fit — the real catch.** Adding even a compact 8-10 foot trailer behind a 35-foot coach pushes combined length past what many campgrounds accommodate. Combined-length limits of 65 feet are common on highways, but the sharper problem is campground site length: many national- and state-park sites cap total rig length at 27-35 feet, so a trailer-toting Class A frequently can't fit standard sites and must seek the fewer, pricier "big rig" or pull-through sites ([FMCA: Towing a Cargo Trailer Behind a Class A](https://community.fmca.com/topic/14462-towing-cargo-trailer-behind-a-class-a/) — community). Owners report sometimes dropping the trailer in an overflow lot before backing in — which cuts against the simplicity of the RV in the first place, especially with young kids in tow.

**License implications.** For a small utility/cargo trailer well under the GVWR thresholds that trigger a non-commercial Class E license in NJ, standard licensing almost certainly applies. Confirm your specific coach + trailer combination with NJ MVC, but this is secondary to the site-fit issue. (Licensing is covered in full in the towing and driving docs.)

**Bottom line on the trailer:** it solves weather, theft, and capacity completely, and it *combines cleanly with a car-hauler toad plan* if you go that route — but on its own it turns every travel day into a two-piece parking puzzle and narrows your campground choices. Given the boondocking requirement (where dispersed sites are tighter and less improved than a commercial pull-through), a rack-plus-basement hybrid is very likely the better fit *unless* you're already trailering the RAV4 anyway — in which case the trailer earns its keep.

## Bottom line for this family

- **Settle the toad question first** ([doc 19](19-towing-a-car.html)) — it decides whether bikes ride on the coach, on the car, or on a trailer. With a toad, "bikes on the toad" (roof or the toad's own hitch) is often simplest; a dual-receiver adapter lets you keep a coach rack too, with honest caveats (extra rear leverage, tow-bar-fold interference, turn clearance).
- **The weight math is no longer the worry.** Six conventional bikes total ~150 lbs — well under any coach hitch's carrier limit. The real limits are rack slots, rear overhang and backing, weather, and theft.
- **Buy only an RV-approved/RV-rated rack for anything that rides on the coach.** A car rack will not survive the bounce and leverage at the back of a Class A.
- **Split the load rather than cramming six onto one rack** — 3-4 bikes on a rated platform rack (VelociRAX 412 MAX, 1Up USA Super Duty, or similar), the rest in the basement pass-through (front wheels off) or on the toad. Keeps the tail short and protects the good bikes.
- **Treat security as a line item, not an afterthought** — a hardened lock anchored to the rack frame, a cover, and a look at whether the good bikes need a standalone insurance policy.
- **Skip the ladder rack for anything but the lightest kid's bike**, and be skeptical of bumper-mount adapters unless the coach's rear bumper is confirmed structural.
- **The enclosed trailer is a fallback for weather/theft — with one twist:** if the RAV4 ends up on a car-hauler anyway (it can't be flat-towed or dollied), that trailer can carry the bikes too, and the trailer stops being extra complexity and starts being the plan.
