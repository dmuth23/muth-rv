# Towing a Car Behind the Motorhome (the "Toad")

A "toad" (RV-speak for the car you tow behind a motorhome — a pun on "towed") solves the single most annoying problem of Class A life: once you've leveled the coach, run out the slides, hooked up power and water, and settled the kids, you do not want to break all of that down just to buy milk. A toad is your errand car, your date-night car, your "one kid spiked a fever at 9 p.m." car. Doug never considered one until now, so this document lays out **everything** — starting with the two cars already in your driveway, because the honest answer for *your* specific vehicles reshapes the whole decision.

Here is the punchline up front, and the rest of the document earns it:

> **Neither the 2017 Toyota RAV4 Limited nor the 2022 Hyundai Tucson can be flat-towed four-down, and neither AWD version can ride on a tow dolly either.** For your current cars, the *only* towing method is a full car trailer — and a loaded car trailer likely **exceeds the 5,000-lb hitch rating** of a standard gas Class A. That pushes the real choices to: (a) buy a light, purpose-built flat-tow toad, (b) go with no toad and use a two-vehicle caravan, or (c) upgrade to a coach with a heavier-rated hitch. We'll show the math.

## Part 1 — Your actual cars, one at a time

Every discussion of toads online assumes you're starting from scratch and will buy whatever tows easily. You're not — you own two SUVs today. So the first question is simply: **can either of these be towed, and how?**

There are exactly three ways to tow any car behind a motorhome, and each car has to be checked against all three:

1. **Flat-tow (four-down / "dinghy"):** all four wheels on the ground, car connected to the coach by a tow bar. Cheapest to hitch and unhitch, but only *specific* vehicles are engineered to survive it.
2. **Tow dolly:** the car's two *driven* wheels ride up on a small two-wheel trailer, the other two roll on the ground. Works only for front-wheel-drive cars (front wheels up, rear wheels rolling).
3. **Car trailer:** all four wheels up off the ground on a full trailer. Works for **anything** — but it's the heaviest and bulkiest option.

### The 2017 Toyota RAV4 Limited (AWD, automatic)

> **Verdict box — 2017 RAV4 Limited AWD**
> - **Flat-tow four-down? NO.** Prohibited by the owner's manual.
> - **Tow dolly? NO.** AWD — no wheels may be on the ground while towed.
> - **Car trailer? YES** (all four wheels up) — subject to the weight math in Part 3.

This isn't a judgment call — it's the manual. The RAV4 (XA40 generation, 2013–2018) owner's manual states plainly: *"Your vehicle is not designed to be dinghy towed (with 4 wheels on the ground) behind a motor home."* For **AWD** models specifically it goes further: *"never tow this vehicle with any of the wheels on the ground. This may cause serious damage to the transmission and dynamic torque control AWD system"* ([Toyota RAV4 XA40 owner's manual, dinghy-towing section (trav4.net reproduction)](https://www.trav4.net/dinghy_towing-82.html)).

That last sentence closes *both* the flat-tow and the tow-dolly doors: a dolly leaves two wheels on the ground, and the manual says **zero** wheels may touch. This tracks with the broader reality that modern automatic-transmission RAV4s are simply not on any manufacturer's flat-tow list — the 2026 dinghy-towing guides don't include the RAV4 at all ([RV LIFE 2026 Dinghy Towing Guide](https://rvlife.com/2026-dinghy-towing-guide-an-rv-life-exclusive/)). The mechanism is transmission lubrication: with the engine off and the wheels spinning the driveline, the transmission's internal oil pump isn't running, so the gears and the AWD coupling run dry and cook themselves ([Edmunds: What cars can be flat-towed](https://www.edmunds.com/car-buying/what-cars-can-be-flat-towed-behind-an-rv.html)).

So for the RAV4, a **car trailer is the only option** — hold that thought for Part 3, where the weight math decides whether it's even legal behind a gas coach.

### The 2022 Hyundai Tucson (gas AWD vs. gas FWD vs. hybrid)

The Tucson is more nuanced because it ships in three relevant flavors, and the drivetrain — **not** the model name — determines what you can do. Hyundai's guidance is framed as *emergency* towing (getting a broken car to a shop), not *recreational* flat-towing, and that distinction matters: Hyundai does **not** publish the Tucson as an approved recreational four-down dinghy vehicle, and it does not appear on the 2026 dinghy guides either ([RV LIFE 2026 Dinghy Towing Guide](https://rvlife.com/2026-dinghy-towing-guide-an-rv-life-exclusive/)). Treat any "you can flat-tow a Tucson" claim you find on a blog as **not manufacturer-approved**.

What Hyundai's own manual *does* say, in its emergency-towing instructions, tells you exactly how the driveline tolerates having wheels turning while towed ([Hyundai Tucson owner's manual, emergency towing (htmanual.net reproduction)](https://www.htmanual.net/emergency_towing-400.html)):

- **AWD (gas or hybrid):** *"must be towed with a wheel lift and dollies or flatbed equipment with all the wheels off the ground."* Zero wheels down — same as the RAV4. **No flat-tow, no dolly.**
- **FWD (gas):** may be towed short distances with the front wheels off the ground, but Hyundai caps even *emergency* front-wheels-down towing at **under 1 mile and 10 mph** to protect the automatic transaxle. That speed/distance limit makes it useless for real recreational flat-towing.

> **Verdict box — 2022 Hyundai Tucson**
> - **AWD (gas or hybrid): Flat-tow NO, dolly NO, trailer YES.** All four wheels must be up.
> - **FWD (gas): Flat-tow NO** (not manufacturer-approved; emergency-only under 1 mile / 10 mph). **Dolly — physically possible** (front wheels up, rear rolling — a dolly lifts the *driven* wheels of a FWD car), but Hyundai does not bless it for long-distance recreational use, so treat it as a gray area, not a green light. **Trailer YES.**

**Which Tucson do you own?** Your household car is the practical question here. If it's the **AWD** Tucson, it behaves exactly like the RAV4: trailer-only. If it's a **FWD gas** Tucson, a tow dolly becomes *physically* an option — but without Hyundai's recreational approval you'd be relying on community practice, not the manual, and you'd own the risk to the transaxle and the warranty. Either way, **neither of your cars is a clean flat-tow candidate**, which is the finding that drives the rest of this document.

*(One more note for completeness: the Tucson **Hybrid** and **plug-in hybrid** add an electric drive unit and high-voltage battery. Hybrids are almost universally *not* flat-towable — spinning the wheels can back-drive the electric motor — and Hyundai's all-wheels-up rule for the AWD hybrid confirms it. If your Tucson is a hybrid, it's trailer-only, full stop.)*

## Part 2 — The four paths, with real July-2026 costs

Now zoom out from your two cars to the whole menu, because the likely conclusion is that you'll either buy a different toad or run no toad at all.

### Path 1 — Flat-tow a car that's actually built for it

This is the setup most Class A owners land on, because once it's installed, hitching up takes a couple of minutes and there's no trailer to store. The catch: you can only do it with a vehicle the manufacturer explicitly approves.

**The classic flat-towable choices** (from the current dinghy guides):

- **Jeep Wrangler (4WD)** — the perennial #1, because its transfer case has a true neutral. Also **Jeep Gladiator (4WD)** and **Jeep Grand Cherokee (4WD)**.
- **Ford Bronco, F-150 (4WD), Expedition (4WD)**; **Chevrolet Colorado (4WD), Silverado (4WD), Tahoe/Suburban (4WD)** ([RV LIFE 2026 Dinghy Towing Guide](https://rvlife.com/2026-dinghy-towing-guide-an-rv-life-exclusive/)).

Notice the pattern: they're mostly **body-on-frame 4WD trucks and Jeeps with a manual transfer case you can shift to neutral** — the opposite of your car-based, automatic-AWD crossovers. (A used Jeep Wrangler is the "just works" answer if you decide a flat-tow toad is worth buying — abundant on the used market, holds value, and every RV shop knows the install cold.)

**What it costs to set up (installed, as of July 2026):** a complete four-down kit is a **baseplate** (bolts to the toad's frame), a **tow bar** (connects toad to coach), **diode wiring with a charge line** (so the toad's brake/turn lights mirror the coach), **safety cables**, a **breakaway system**, and a **supplemental braking system** (more on why that's legally required in Part 3). Installed, budget roughly **$4,000–$5,000**; a current 2026 quote for a Jeep Wrangler JL package ran **$4,495–$4,997** including RV-side verification and training ([Auto Boss RV: Jeep Wrangler JL Flat Tow](https://autobossrv.com/jeep-wrangler-jl-flat-tow/)), and community build sheets cluster around **$5,000** all-in (e.g., one itemized job: ~$3,729 parts + $1,320 labor ≈ $5,088) ([iRV2 forum: cost to create a toad — community-experience thread](https://www.irv2.com/threads/cost-to-create-a-toad.2059878/)). That's *on top of* buying the toad itself.

### Path 2 — Tow dolly

A tow dolly is a small two-wheel trailer that carries the front (driven) wheels of a **front-wheel-drive** car while the rear wheels roll on the road. **It does not work for AWD** — an AWD car with any wheels turning risks differential and coupling damage ([Tow Smart Trailers / cartowdolly.com — community/vendor source](https://cartowdolly.com/how-much-does-a-car-tow-dolly-cost/)), which is exactly why your AWD RAV4 (and an AWD Tucson) are dolly-disqualified.

- **Cost:** a new dolly runs about **$2,200–$3,800** for a quality unit, with budget models near **$1,300** ([cartowdolly.com — vendor/community source](https://cartowdolly.com/how-much-does-a-car-tow-dolly-cost/)). Cheaper than a flat-tow install, and no baseplate to bolt to the car.
- **The storage problem nobody mentions until they're standing in a campsite:** once you've dollied the car *to* the campground and unloaded it, **where does the empty dolly go?** It's a 400–600-lb, 8-foot object you now have to park on your site (or wherever the campground allows), and you have to reload the car onto it — a jack-and-strap ritual — every time you break camp. For a family juggling two little kids at setup and teardown, that friction is real and recurring. It's the reason many people who *could* dolly end up either flat-towing or skipping the toad.

### Path 3 — Car trailer (the only option for your current cars)

A full car trailer carries **all four wheels off the ground**, so it works for *anything*: your AWD RAV4, an AWD or hybrid Tucson, literally any car. It's the safest for the drivetrain (nothing is turning) and the most flexible.

- **Cost:** an open aluminum car hauler is light (~**1,450 lb** empty for an 18-ft aluminum deck) but pricier; a steel open hauler is cheaper but heavy (~**2,000–3,200 lb** empty) ([Trailer Outlet — vendor spec](https://www.traileroutlet.com/10032-2/); [Towing Service Hub trailer-weight guide — community/vendor source](https://towingservicehub.com/blog/trailer-weight-guide)). New open car haulers commonly run **$3,000–$6,000+**.
- **The downsides:** it's the **heaviest** option (which collides head-on with your coach's hitch rating — see Part 3), the **bulkiest** to store at home and maneuver at a campground, and loading/unloading a car up ramps adds time at both ends. But for a family that wants to keep the exact cars they already own, it's the *only* road-legal path — if the coach's hitch can handle the weight.

### Path 4 — No toad at all

Don't skip this one. Plenty of families run a big Class A with no toad, and for your specific situation it may be the smartest starting point (especially paired with the rent-first plan). Two sub-flavors:

**4a — Truly no second vehicle.** You drive the coach everywhere. This means **breaking camp for groceries** — unhooking utilities, bringing in the slides, and driving a 30–35-ft coach to the store, then re-setting up on return. Manageable at a full-hookup resort you're using as a base for a few days; genuinely painful if you're moving often or boondocking somewhere remote. Many people solve the "small errand" version of this with bicycles for close-by trips — which ties directly into your bike plans ([carrying 6 bikes](08-carrying-bikes.html)).

**4b — The two-vehicle caravan (Sara drives the family car).** Instead of *towing* a car, you simply **drive both**: Doug pilots the coach, Sara drives the RAV4 or Tucson, and you travel in a two-vehicle convoy. This is the option that quietly solves three of your hardest problems at once — but it has its own big catch:

- **It sidesteps the whole towing problem.** No baseplate, no dolly, no trailer, no hitch-weight math. The family car stays a normal car.
- **It fixes the car-seat problem.** The kids can ride in the RAV4 or Tucson in their normal, crash-tested car seats — the safest place for them on the road — instead of the legally-and-mechanically fraught living-area seating of the coach. That's a *major* safety win, and it interacts directly with the car-seat law laid out in the [family & kids document](05-family-and-kids.html).
- **It fixes bike-hauling flexibility.** Bikes can go on the family car's hitch rack or roof, off the back of the coach — see [carrying 6 bikes](08-carrying-bikes.html).
- **The catch is the driver.** Sara is likely **not comfortable driving a Class A** — which is *fine*, because in this arrangement she drives the **familiar family car**, and Doug drives the coach. But it means two drivers for every travel day, two vehicles' worth of fuel and tolls, and the coordination of a convoy with little kids split between vehicles. Whether that's a dealbreaker or a non-issue is exactly the kind of thing the [one-driver-family document](22-one-driver-family.html) works through, and something the [rent-before-you-buy plan](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) lets you test for real before committing a dime to towing hardware.

## Part 3 — Law vs. guidance: what you're actually required to do

This is where a lot of RV advice gets sloppy, blurring "the law says" with "experts suggest." We'll keep them strictly separate. New Jersey is your home state and where the coach will be registered, so we lead with NJ statute.

### What the LAW requires (New Jersey)

**Supplemental (auxiliary) braking on the towed vehicle.** New Jersey's braking statute, **[N.J.S.A. 39:3-67](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-67/)**, requires brakes on all wheels of a towed unit **except** *"any trailer or semitrailer of a gross weight not exceeding 3,000 pounds; provided, however, that the gross weight of any such trailer without brakes shall not exceed 40% of the gross weight of the towing vehicle."* That's a **compound test, verbatim in the statute itself**: to skip brakes, a towed unit must satisfy **both** the 3,000-lb ceiling **and** the 40%-of-tow-vehicle ceiling. Fail either prong and brakes are required ([N.J.S.A. 39:3-67, FindLaw](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-67/)). The statute separately requires that any towed unit that *does* need brakes have them **automatically applied on break-away** from the towing vehicle. Here's why this matters for *any* toad you'd realistically tow:

- Your RAV4 Limited AWD has a curb weight of roughly **3,570 lb** ([spec aggregators put the 2017 RAV4 AWD near 3,570 lb](https://www.edmunds.com/toyota/rav4/2017/features-specs/)); a gas AWD Tucson is roughly **3,460–3,620 lb**; a used Jeep Wrangler is **~4,000+ lb**. Every one of these already exceeds the statute's **3,000-lb ceiling on its own** — so the brakeless exemption is unavailable regardless of the 40% figure, and supplemental braking is required by the plain text of NJ law, not merely by industry convention.
- **Correcting an earlier draft's misreading of this document.** An earlier version of this section claimed the 3,000-lb figure was only an "RV-industry compilers' reading" layered on top of the statute, and that the 40% prong alone (which a ~3,500-lb car would pass, since 40% of a 16,000-lb-plus coach is ~6,400 lb) would let a light toad skip brakes. That was wrong: **"3,000 pounds" is verbatim statutory text**, joined to the 40% clause by "and" — both prongs must be satisfied, not either one. A car over 3,000 lb needs supplemental brakes in New Jersey on the statute's own terms. (The statute's phrasing of "trailer or semitrailer" is still being applied to a flat-towed car by analogy, as is standard RV-industry practice, since the text predates recreational dinghy-towing.)

**Breakaway system.** The same statute requires the towed unit's brakes to apply automatically if it separates from the coach — that's the breakaway cable/switch included in every proper flat-tow kit.

**Number of towed units (the "double-tow" rule).** New Jersey **[N.J.S.A. 39:4-54](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-4-54/)** states: *"No motor vehicle shall be used on the public highways while drawing more than 2 motor-drawn vehicles."* For you this is not a constraint — a coach towing one toad is a single drawn vehicle. But it does mean you **cannot** legally do the "toad *plus* a trailer of bikes behind the toad" trick in NJ if that would add up to more than the statute allows in a recreational combination; keep the setup to the coach + one towed thing.

**Combined length.** NJ **[N.J.S.A. 39:3-84](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-84/)** caps a recreation vehicle at **45 feet**, and a recreation-vehicle-plus-towed **combination at 65 feet**. A 35-ft coach + tow bar + a ~15-ft car is roughly **52–55 ft — comfortably under the 65-ft limit**. Length is *not* your binding constraint; braking and hitch rating are.

**Registration and plates — the part that trips people up.** *How you tow* changes *what you have to register:*

- **A flat-towed car keeps its own normal registration and plates.** You're towing a titled, registered vehicle — it stays exactly what it is. Nothing new to register beyond the equipment on it.
- **A tow dolly or a car trailer is itself a vehicle that must be separately registered in New Jersey**, with its own plate and its own proof of NJ insurance, before you can legally roll it down the road. Per the [NJ MVC trailer page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicletopics/trailer.htm), a trailer **under 2,500 lb** unladen must be **registered** (a plate is included in the fee) but is exempt from titling; a trailer **over 2,500 lb** unladen must be both **registered and titled**. NJ trailer registration renews **annually** and requires **proof of New Jersey insurance** at registration ([NJ MVC](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicletopics/trailer.htm)). A tow dolly specifically is handled as a **converter dolly** and may be registered on a gross-weight basis with its own plate under **[N.J.S.A. 39:4-54](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-39/section-39-4-54/)**.
- **Practical read for your family:** a dolly or trailer isn't just a purchase — it's a second annually-renewed registration, a second plate, and a line on your insurance. A flat-tow setup adds none of that (the toad is already your registered car). That paperwork asymmetry is a quiet point in favor of flat-tow-or-nothing over the dolly/trailer routes.

### What the LAW requires (the Northeast you'll actually drive)

You won't stay in New Jersey. The supplemental-braking threshold **changes at every state line**, and some Northeast neighbors are stricter than NJ. Because your toad will always be over ~1,000 lb, the practical read is: **install supplemental braking and you're legal everywhere.** Skipping it puts you offside in most of the region.

| State | Towed-vehicle supplemental brakes required above… |
|---|---|
| **New Jersey** | Over **3,000 lb** — verbatim in statute; brakeless exemption also requires ≤40% of tow vehicle, so *both* prongs must pass to skip brakes — [39:3-67](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-39/section-39-3-67/) |
| **New York** | **1,000 lb** ([Roadmaster map — industry summary](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Pennsylvania** | 3,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Connecticut** | 3,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Maryland** | 3,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Delaware** | 4,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Massachusetts** | 10,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |

*Note on sourcing: the individual state thresholds above come from an RV-industry compiled map (Roadmaster), not from each state's statute directly. It's the standard reference RVers use, but verify a specific state against its own code before relying on it for a close call. New York's 1,000-lb figure in particular means **any** toad triggers the brake requirement there.*

**Other legal must-haves everywhere:** safety chains/cables cross-connected between coach and toad; the toad's brake, tail, and turn signals must work (that's the diode wiring); and adequate mirrors so the driver can see past the towed load. These are standard equipment-law requirements bundled into every proper flat-tow kit.

> **What safety experts (and the physics) recommend — separate from the law.** Even where a statute would technically let a light toad ride brakeless, the supplemental-braking case is overwhelming: several tons of coach trying to stop a two-plus-ton toad with no brakes of its own lengthens stopping distance and can shove the whole rig in a panic stop. The braking-equipment makers and RV-safety writers uniformly recommend supplemental braking on **any** towed car regardless of the local threshold ([Roadmaster — industry guidance](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)). Our recommendation for your family: **install it on whatever you tow, and stop tracking the state-line differences.** It's a few hundred dollars of insurance against the one emergency stop that matters, and it keeps you legal everywhere at once.

### The hitch-rating reality — the number that decides everything for your cars

Here is the constraint that quietly kills the "just trailer the RAV4" plan behind a typical gas coach.

Most gas Class A motorhomes are built on the **Ford F-53 chassis**, and the **standard F-53 receiver hitch is rated at 5,000 lb of trailer weight** (with a 500-lb maximum tongue weight on the dead-weight setup) ([Ford / etrailer discussion of F-53 hitch ratings — community/vendor](https://www.etrailer.com/question-683869.html)). Ford does offer higher-GVWR F-53 configurations and a **7,000-lb** weight-distributing hitch option on some chassis, but the **base number you should assume until you verify a specific coach is 5,000 lb** ([2025 Ford RV & Trailer Towing Guide (PDF)](https://content.fordpro.com/content/dam/fordpro/us/en-us/pdf/towing-guides/2025FordRVandTrailerTowingGuide_fnl-Nov12.pdf)).

Now do the arithmetic for **your** cars, which can only go on a trailer:

| | Weight |
|---|---|
| Open car trailer (steel, empty) | ~2,000–3,200 lb ([Towing Service Hub — vendor/community](https://towingservicehub.com/blog/trailer-weight-guide)) |
| Open car trailer (aluminum, empty) | ~1,450 lb ([Trailer Outlet](https://www.traileroutlet.com/10032-2/)) |
| 2017 RAV4 Limited AWD (curb) | ~3,570 lb |
| **Steel trailer + RAV4** | **~5,570–6,770 lb** |
| **Aluminum trailer + RAV4** | **~5,020 lb** |

**Even with the lightest aluminum trailer, a RAV4 on a trailer is ~5,020 lb — right at or over the 5,000-lb standard hitch limit. On a normal steel trailer, you're 500–1,700 lb over.** The Tucson (AWD ~3,460–3,620 lb) lands in the same place. **You cannot legally or safely trailer either of your current cars behind a standard-hitch gas Class A.**

**What "exceeding the hitch rating" actually means** — this isn't a technicality, though the specific downstream consequences below are our synthesis of standard industry risk, not a claim backed by a single primary source, so treat them as reported-but-unverified and confirm with your own insurer/chassis warranty terms before relying on them:

- **Legally / safety:** you're operating outside the chassis manufacturer's rating. In a crash or a roadside inspection, an over-rating tow is plausibly a liability finding against you — verify before relying on this for a specific state or insurer.
- **Insurance:** if a claim investigation shows you were towing over the coach's rated capacity, insurers are commonly understood to be able to **deny the claim** on a modification/rating-violation basis — but we could not independently confirm a specific case or policy clause tying this to hitch-rating overage; verify against your own policy before relying on this.
- **Warranty / mechanical:** overloading the hitch and rear of the coach stresses the frame, brakes, and drivetrain the coach was never rated for, and this is reported to be able to void relevant warranty coverage — unverified against a specific manufacturer's warranty terms; confirm with the chassis/coach maker before relying on it.
- **Tongue weight:** the 500-lb tongue-weight cap matters too — a loaded car trailer's tongue weight can blow past 500 lb depending on how the car sits, which is a second way to exceed the rating even if total weight looked okay.

This is the finding that reframes the whole decision. **If you keep your current cars, your only compliant option behind a standard gas coach is a two-vehicle caravan (Path 4b) — or you spec a coach with a heavier-rated hitch.** If you want a *towed* toad, the practical answer is to **buy a light, flat-towable vehicle** (a used Jeep Wrangler is the canonical choice) whose flat-tow weight sits well under 5,000 lb and needs no trailer at all.

## Part 4 — Practicalities nobody warns you about

- **You cannot back up a flat-towed car. At all.** With a car flat-towed on a tow bar, reversing even a few feet can jackknife the toad and bend the tow bar or baseplate. In practice this means **you plan every stop as a pull-through** — gas stations, rest areas, campsites. It's a genuine skill-and-planning change, not a minor quirk, and it's a real argument for the two-vehicle caravan if you're not confident about always having an exit.
- **Connect/disconnect time.** A dialed-in flat-tow hookup is a few minutes each way. A tow dolly is longer (drive the car on, strap it, ratchet it down). A car trailer is longest (ramps, positioning, four tie-downs). Multiply that by "every travel day, with two little kids waiting."
- **Tire wear (flat-tow).** Flat-towing puts real highway miles on the toad's tires — those miles count against the toad's tire life even though its odometer usually isn't turning. Budget for it.
- **"Transmission lubrication" — myth vs. manual.** You'll read that you can flat-tow any automatic if you just add an aftermarket **transmission lube pump** or a driveshaft-disconnect. Sometimes that's mechanically true, but it is **not manufacturer-approved** for your RAV4 or Tucson, **can void the drivetrain warranty**, and is exactly the kind of "it worked for a guy on a forum" advice that turns into a $4,000 transmission. The manual's word is the one that governs your warranty and your insurance — and both your cars' manuals say **no wheels down**. Don't let a forum thread talk you past your own owner's manual.

## Bottom line for your family

1. **Your current cars (RAV4 AWD, Tucson) are trailer-only, and a loaded trailer likely exceeds a standard gas coach's 5,000-lb hitch.** So "tow the car we already own" is, realistically, off the table behind a typical gas Class A.
2. **That leaves three honest choices:** (a) **no toad + two-vehicle caravan** — Sara drives the familiar family car, kids ride safely in their real car seats, bikes go on the car (strong fit with your family and safety priorities); (b) **buy a light flat-tow toad** (used Jeep Wrangler is the default), ~$4,000–$5,000 in hardware on top of the car; or (c) **spec a coach with a heavier-rated hitch** if you're set on trailering your existing car.
3. **Whatever you tow, supplemental braking + breakaway is legally required** — you're over every relevant weight threshold in NJ and the Northeast, so build it in and stop worrying about the state-line differences.
4. **Test it before you buy it.** The [rent-before-you-buy plan](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) lets you try a travel day with and without a second vehicle — the cheapest possible way to find out whether a toad is worth thousands of dollars for *your* family, before spending them.

- [RV LIFE 2026 Dinghy Towing Guide](https://rvlife.com/2026-dinghy-towing-guide-an-rv-life-exclusive/) — current list of manufacturer-approved flat-towable vehicles; RAV4/Tucson absent; classic Jeep/Ford/Chevy choices.
- [Edmunds: What cars can be flat-towed behind an RV](https://www.edmunds.com/car-buying/what-cars-can-be-flat-towed-behind-an-rv.html) — explains the transmission-lubrication mechanism behind flat-tow restrictions.
- [Hyundai Tucson owner's manual — emergency towing (htmanual.net reproduction)](https://www.htmanual.net/emergency_towing-400.html) — AWD must be towed all-wheels-up; FWD emergency-only under 1 mile / 10 mph.
- [Auto Boss RV: Jeep Wrangler JL flat-tow package](https://autobossrv.com/jeep-wrangler-jl-flat-tow/) — current (2026) installed flat-tow kit pricing, $4,495–$4,997.
- [iRV2 forum: "cost to create a toad"](https://www.irv2.com/threads/cost-to-create-a-toad.2059878/) — community build-cost data points (~$5,000 all-in); community-experience source.
- [cartowdolly.com: how much a tow dolly costs](https://cartowdolly.com/how-much-does-a-car-tow-dolly-cost/) — dolly price range and FWD-only / no-AWD limitation; vendor/community source.
- [Trailer Outlet: 18-ft aluminum open car trailer spec](https://www.traileroutlet.com/10032-2/) — ~1,450-lb empty weight for a light aluminum car hauler; vendor spec.
- [Towing Service Hub: trailer weight guide](https://towingservicehub.com/blog/trailer-weight-guide) — empty-weight ranges for steel vs. aluminum car haulers; vendor/community source.
- [N.J.S.A. 39:3-67 — brake equipment required (FindLaw)](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-67/) — NJ statute (verified verbatim text): brakes required on all wheels except a trailer/semitrailer of gross weight not exceeding 3,000 lb, provided also that its gross weight without brakes doesn't exceed 40% of the towing vehicle's (both prongs required to skip brakes); any towed unit that needs brakes must have them automatically applied on break-away.
- [NJ MVC — Trailers, House Semi-trailers and Private Utilities](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicletopics/trailer.htm) — official NJ registration/titling rules: trailers under 2,500 lb register (plate included) but are title-exempt; over 2,500 lb must be registered and titled; annual renewal; proof of NJ insurance required.
- [N.J.S.A. 39:4-54 — trailers; number permitted; converter dolly (Justia)](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-39/section-39-4-54/) — a converter (tow) dolly may be registered on a gross-weight basis with its own plate.
- [N.J.S.A. 39:4-54 — trailers; number permitted (FindLaw)](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-4-54/) — "No motor vehicle...drawing more than 2 motor-drawn vehicles."
- [N.J.S.A. 39:3-84 — dimensional limitations (FindLaw)](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-84/) — recreation vehicle 45 ft max; RV-plus-towed combination 65 ft max.
- [Roadmaster braking-requirements map](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map) — RV-industry state-by-state supplemental-braking thresholds (NY 1,000 lb; PA/CT/MD 3,000 lb; DE 4,000 lb; MA 10,000 lb); industry summary, not primary statute.
- [etrailer: F-53 chassis maximum towing capacity Q&A](https://www.etrailer.com/question-683869.html) — standard F-53 receiver rated 5,000 lb trailer / 500 lb tongue; vendor/community source.
- [2025 Ford RV & Trailer Towing Guide (PDF)](https://content.fordpro.com/content/dam/fordpro/us/en-us/pdf/towing-guides/2025FordRVandTrailerTowingGuide_fnl-Nov12.pdf) — Ford chassis towing ratings, including higher-GVWR F-53 and weight-distributing hitch options.
- [Edmunds 2017 Toyota RAV4 specs](https://www.edmunds.com/toyota/rav4/2017/features-specs/) — RAV4 AWD curb weight reference (~3,570 lb) used in the hitch math.
