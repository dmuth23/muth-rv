# Towing a Car Behind the Motorhome (the "Toad")

A "toad" (RV-speak for the car you tow behind a motorhome — a pun on "towed") solves the single most annoying problem of motorhome life: once you've leveled the coach, hooked up power and water, and settled the kids, you do not want to break all of that down just to buy milk. A toad is your errand car, your date-night car, your "one kid spiked a fever at 9 p.m." car. Doug never considered one until now, so this document lays out **everything** — starting with the two cars already in your driveway, because the honest answer for *your* specific vehicles reshapes the whole decision.

**This document has been re-verdicted for the Class C pivot (July 2026).** The original version of this analysis was built around a gas Class A on the Ford F-53 chassis, whose standard receiver hitch is rated for only 5,000 lb (unverified — confirm before relying; this figure is well-corroborated as a general fact about the F-53's factory Class III hitch, but no single source cited in this document directly states the F-53 receiver rating on its own) — a number that killed trailering your current cars outright. The Class C shortlist runs on the **Ford E-450** chassis instead, and the E-450's factory-installed hitch on most RV builds is rated meaningfully higher. That single fact reopens the trailering question. Here is the punchline up front, and the rest of the document earns it:

> **Neither the 2017 Toyota RAV4 Limited nor the 2022 Hyundai Tucson can be flat-towed four-down, and neither AWD version can ride on a tow dolly either — that verdict is unchanged from the Class A analysis and doesn't depend on chassis.** What *has* changed: on a Class C built on the Ford E-450 chassis with a manufacturer-installed 7,500-lb hitch (common but **not universal** — verify per model), trailering your RAV4 or a Tucson-plus-trailer combo is genuinely within the hitch's rated capacity, where it was not on the F-53. The real constraints now are (a) confirming the *specific* coach you're looking at actually has the upgraded hitch and not the base 5,000-lb unit, (b) tongue weight, which is a tighter percentage limit than total weight, and (c) the fact that a full-size car trailer competing for space with a hitch-mounted bike rack is a real physical conflict on a shorter Class C. We'll show the math for both cars.

## Part 1 — Your actual cars, one at a time

Every discussion of toads online assumes you're starting from scratch and will buy whatever tows easily. You're not — you own two SUVs today. So the first question is simply: **can either of these be towed, and how?**

There are exactly three ways to tow any car behind a motorhome, and each car has to be checked against all three. **None of this part changes with the Class C pivot** — flat-tow and dolly eligibility is entirely about the *car's* drivetrain, not the coach's chassis.

1. **Flat-tow (four-down / "dinghy"):** all four wheels on the ground, car connected to the coach by a tow bar. Cheapest to hitch and unhitch, but only *specific* vehicles are engineered to survive it.
2. **Tow dolly:** the car's two *driven* wheels ride up on a small two-wheel trailer, the other two roll on the ground. Works only for front-wheel-drive cars (front wheels up, rear wheels rolling).
3. **Car trailer:** all four wheels up off the ground on a full trailer. Works for **anything** — but it's the heaviest and bulkiest option, and the one whose legality now hinges on the E-450's hitch rating.

### The 2017 Toyota RAV4 Limited (AWD, automatic)

> **Verdict box — 2017 RAV4 Limited AWD (unchanged)**
> - **Flat-tow four-down? NO.** Prohibited by the owner's manual.
> - **Tow dolly? NO.** AWD — no wheels may be on the ground while towed.
> - **Car trailer? YES** (all four wheels up) — subject to the weight math in Part 3, which is now *more favorable* on an E-450 Class C than it was on an F-53 Class A.

This isn't a judgment call — it's the manual. The RAV4 (XA40 generation, 2013–2018) owner's manual states plainly: *"Your vehicle is not designed to be dinghy towed (with 4 wheels on the ground) behind a motor home."* For **AWD** models specifically it goes further: *"never tow this vehicle with any of the wheels on the ground. This may cause serious damage to the transmission and dynamic torque control AWD system"* ([Toyota RAV4 XA40 owner's manual, dinghy-towing section (trav4.net reproduction)](https://www.trav4.net/dinghy_towing-82.html)).

That last sentence closes *both* the flat-tow and the tow-dolly doors: a dolly leaves two wheels on the ground, and the manual says **zero** wheels may touch. This tracks with the broader reality that modern automatic-transmission RAV4s are simply not on any manufacturer's flat-tow list — the 2026 dinghy-towing guides don't include the RAV4 at all ([RV LIFE 2026 Dinghy Towing Guide](https://rvlife.com/2026-dinghy-towing-guide-an-rv-life-exclusive/)). The mechanism is transmission lubrication: with the engine off and the wheels spinning the driveline, the transmission's internal oil pump isn't running, so the gears and the AWD coupling run dry and cook themselves ([Edmunds: What cars can be flat-towed](https://www.edmunds.com/car-buying/what-cars-can-be-flat-towed-behind-an-rv.html)).

So for the RAV4, a **car trailer is the only option** — and unlike the Class A finding, it's now a live option rather than a dead end. Hold that thought for Part 3.

### The 2022 Hyundai Tucson (gas AWD vs. gas FWD vs. hybrid)

The Tucson is more nuanced because it ships in three relevant flavors, and the drivetrain — **not** the model name — determines what you can do. Hyundai's guidance is framed as *emergency* towing (getting a broken car to a shop), not *recreational* flat-towing, and that distinction matters: Hyundai does **not** publish the Tucson as an approved recreational four-down dinghy vehicle, and it does not appear on the 2026 dinghy guides either ([RV LIFE 2026 Dinghy Towing Guide](https://rvlife.com/2026-dinghy-towing-guide-an-rv-life-exclusive/)). Treat any "you can flat-tow a Tucson" claim you find on a blog as **not manufacturer-approved**.

What Hyundai's own manual *does* say, in its emergency-towing instructions, tells you exactly how the driveline tolerates having wheels turning while towed ([Hyundai Tucson owner's manual, emergency towing (htmanual.net reproduction)](https://www.htmanual.net/emergency_towing-400.html)):

- **AWD (gas or hybrid):** *"must be towed with a wheel lift and dollies or flatbed equipment with all the wheels off the ground."* Zero wheels down — same as the RAV4. **No flat-tow, no dolly.**
- **FWD (gas):** may be towed short distances with the front wheels off the ground, but Hyundai caps even *emergency* front-wheels-down towing at **under 1 mile and 10 mph** to protect the automatic transaxle. That speed/distance limit makes it useless for real recreational flat-towing.

> **Verdict box — 2022 Hyundai Tucson (unchanged)**
> - **AWD (gas or hybrid): Flat-tow NO, dolly NO, trailer YES.** All four wheels must be up.
> - **FWD (gas): Flat-tow NO** (not manufacturer-approved; emergency-only under 1 mile / 10 mph). **Dolly — physically possible** (front wheels up, rear rolling — a dolly lifts the *driven* wheels of a FWD car), but Hyundai does not bless it for long-distance recreational use, so treat it as a gray area, not a green light. **Trailer YES** — and, per the pivot, more likely to actually fit under a Class C hitch's rating than it did under an F-53's.

**Which Tucson do you own?** Your household car is the practical question here. If it's the **AWD** Tucson, it behaves exactly like the RAV4: trailer-only. If it's a **FWD gas** Tucson, a tow dolly becomes *physically* an option — but without Hyundai's recreational approval you'd be relying on community practice, not the manual, and you'd own the risk to the transaxle and the warranty. Either way, **neither of your cars is a clean flat-tow candidate**, which is still the finding that drives the rest of this document.

*(One more note for completeness: the Tucson **Hybrid** and **plug-in hybrid** add an electric drive unit and high-voltage battery. Hybrids are almost universally *not* flat-towable — spinning the wheels can back-drive the electric motor — and Hyundai's all-wheels-up rule for the AWD hybrid confirms it. If your Tucson is a hybrid, it's trailer-only, full stop.)*

## Part 2 — The four paths, with real July-2026 costs

Now zoom out from your two cars to the whole menu. The Class C pivot doesn't change Paths 1, 2, or 4 — it changes Path 3's viability, which is the headline of this revision.

### Path 1 — Flat-tow a car that's actually built for it

This is the setup most flat-tow owners land on, because once it's installed, hitching up takes a couple of minutes and there's no trailer to store. The catch: you can only do it with a vehicle the manufacturer explicitly approves.

**The classic flat-towable choices** (from the current dinghy guides):

- **Jeep Wrangler (4WD)** — the perennial #1, because its transfer case has a true neutral. Also **Jeep Gladiator (4WD)** and **Jeep Grand Cherokee (4WD)**.
- **Ford Bronco, F-150 (4WD), Expedition (4WD)**; **Chevrolet Colorado (4WD), Silverado (4WD), Tahoe/Suburban (4WD)** ([RV LIFE 2026 Dinghy Towing Guide](https://rvlife.com/2026-dinghy-towing-guide-an-rv-life-exclusive/)).

Notice the pattern: they're mostly **body-on-frame 4WD trucks and Jeeps with a manual transfer case you can shift to neutral** — the opposite of your car-based, automatic-AWD crossovers. (A used Jeep Wrangler is the "just works" answer if you decide a flat-tow toad is worth buying — abundant on the used market, holds value, and every RV shop knows the install cold.)

**What it costs to set up (installed, as of July 2026):** a complete four-down kit is a **baseplate** (bolts to the toad's frame), a **tow bar** (connects toad to coach), **diode wiring with a charge line** (so the toad's brake/turn lights mirror the coach), **safety cables**, a **breakaway system**, and a **supplemental braking system** (more on why that's legally required in Part 3). Installed, budget roughly **$4,000–$5,000**; a current 2026 quote for a Jeep Wrangler JL package ran **$4,495–$4,997** including RV-side verification and training ([Auto Boss RV: Jeep Wrangler JL Flat Tow](https://autobossrv.com/jeep-wrangler-jl-flat-tow/)), and community build sheets cluster around **$5,000** all-in (e.g., one itemized job: ~$3,729 parts + $1,320 labor ≈ $5,088) ([iRV2 forum: cost to create a toad — community-experience thread](https://www.irv2.com/threads/cost-to-create-a-toad.2059878/)). That's *on top of* buying the toad itself.

A note specific to the Class C pivot: a flat-towed toad rides at the *back* of the coach on a tow bar, on the same drawbar/receiver a hitch-mounted bike rack would use. **Flat-tow and a rear hitch bike rack directly compete for the one hitch point on the coach** — see the hitch-vs-bikes conflict below.

### Path 2 — Tow dolly

A tow dolly is a small two-wheel trailer that carries the front (driven) wheels of a **front-wheel-drive** car while the rear wheels roll on the road. **It does not work for AWD** — an AWD car with any wheels turning risks differential and coupling damage ([Tow Smart Trailers / cartowdolly.com — community/vendor source](https://cartowdolly.com/how-much-does-a-car-tow-dolly-cost/)), which is exactly why your AWD RAV4 (and an AWD Tucson) are dolly-disqualified.

- **Cost:** a new dolly runs about **$2,200–$3,800** for a quality unit, with budget models near **$1,300** ([cartowdolly.com — vendor/community source](https://cartowdolly.com/how-much-does-a-car-tow-dolly-cost/)). Cheaper than a flat-tow install, and no baseplate to bolt to the car.
- **The storage problem nobody mentions until they're standing in a campsite:** once you've dollied the car *to* the campground and unloaded it, **where does the empty dolly go?** It's a 400–600-lb, 8-foot object you now have to park on your site (or wherever the campground allows), and you have to reload the car onto it — a jack-and-strap ritual — every time you break camp. For a family juggling two little kids at setup and teardown, that friction is real and recurring. On a shorter Class C with a tighter campsite footprint than a big Class A, this storage problem is *worse*, not better.

### Path 3 — Car trailer (re-verdicted: often viable on an E-450 Class C)

A full car trailer carries **all four wheels off the ground**, so it works for *anything*: your AWD RAV4, an AWD or hybrid Tucson, literally any car. It's the safest for the drivetrain (nothing is turning) and the most flexible. **This is the path whose verdict flips with the chassis pivot** — see Part 3 for the full math, but the short version: the Ford E-450's factory-installed hitch on many Class C floorplans is rated **7,500 lb (with a 750-lb tongue-weight allowance)** on some models, roughly 50% higher than the F-53's 5,000 lb / 500-lb tongue ([Winnebago Outlook specifications](https://www.winnebago.com/models/product/specifications/motorhomes/class-c/outlook) confirm 7,500 lb; the Jayco Redhawk **SE** trim, by contrast, is only 5,000 lb — see Part 3 for the trim mixup and correction). That's the number that reopens trailering.

- **Cost:** an open aluminum car hauler is light (~**1,450 lb** empty for an 18-ft aluminum deck) but pricier; a steel open hauler is cheaper but heavy (~**2,000–3,200 lb** empty) ([Trailer Outlet — vendor spec](https://www.traileroutlet.com/10032-2/); [Towing Service Hub trailer-weight guide — community/vendor source](https://towingservicehub.com/blog/trailer-weight-guide)). New open car haulers commonly run **$3,000–$6,000+**.
- **The downsides:** it's the **heaviest** toad option, competes directly with a bike rack for the coach's one rear hitch point (see below), the **bulkiest** to store at home and maneuver at a campground, and loading/unloading a car up ramps adds time at both ends. On a shorter 25–30-ft Class C — the size range the [size-tradeoffs research](16-open-questions.html) is actively weighing — towing a car trailer also adds meaningfully to total combination length, which interacts with park-length limits, the reason you're considering a Class C at all. Weigh the full rig length, not just the coach, against any park's stated max length before assuming a trailer fits.

### Path 4 — No toad at all

Don't skip this one. Plenty of families run a motorhome with no toad, and for your specific situation it may be the smartest starting point (especially paired with the rent-first plan). Two sub-flavors:

**4a — Truly no second vehicle.** You drive the coach everywhere. This means **breaking camp for groceries** — unhooking utilities and driving the coach to the store, then re-setting up on return. Manageable at a full-hookup site you're using as a base for a few days; genuinely painful if you're moving often or boondocking somewhere remote. Many people solve the "small errand" version of this with bicycles for close-by trips — which ties directly into your bike plans ([carrying 6 bikes](08-carrying-bikes.html)).

**4b — The two-vehicle caravan (Sara drives the family car).** Instead of *towing* a car, you simply **drive both**: Doug pilots the coach, Sara drives the RAV4 or Tucson, and you travel in a two-vehicle convoy. This is the option that quietly solves three of your hardest problems at once — and it remains fully valid even though trailering is now mathematically possible on a Class C, because it sidesteps hitch capacity, tongue weight, and the hitch-vs-bikes conflict entirely.

- **It sidesteps the whole towing problem.** No baseplate, no dolly, no trailer, no hitch-weight math. The family car stays a normal car.
- **It fixes the car-seat problem.** The kids can ride in the RAV4 or Tucson in their normal, crash-tested car seats — the safest place for them on the road — instead of the legally-and-mechanically fraught living-area seating of the coach. That's a *major* safety win, and it interacts directly with the car-seat law laid out in the [family & kids document](05-family-and-kids.html).
- **It fixes bike-hauling flexibility.** Bikes can go on the family car's hitch rack or roof, freeing up the coach's single rear hitch for nothing — or for a trailer, if you decide you want both. See [carrying 6 bikes](08-carrying-bikes.html).
- **The catch is the driver.** Sara is more likely to be comfortable driving a Class C than she would a Class A, given the C's smaller footprint and van-like cab — but "more comfortable" isn't the same as "fully comfortable," and this arrangement still means two drivers for every travel day, two vehicles' worth of fuel and tolls, and the coordination of a convoy with little kids split between vehicles. Whether that's a dealbreaker or a non-issue is exactly the kind of thing the [one-driver-family document](22-one-driver-family.html) works through, and something the [rent-before-you-buy plan](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) lets you test for real before committing a dime to towing hardware.

## Part 3 — Law vs. guidance: what you're actually required to do

This is where a lot of RV advice gets sloppy, blurring "the law says" with "experts suggest." We'll keep them strictly separate. New Jersey is your home state and where the coach will be registered, so we lead with NJ statute. **Nothing in this Part 3 legal section changes with the chassis pivot** — the braking, registration, and length statutes apply the same way to a coach-plus-trailer combination regardless of whether the coach is an E-450 Class C or an F-53 Class A. What *does* change is the hitch-rating subsection at the end, which is the crux of this revision.

### What the LAW requires (New Jersey)

**Supplemental (auxiliary) braking on the towed vehicle.** New Jersey's braking statute, **[N.J.S.A. 39:3-67](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-67/)**, requires brakes on all wheels of a towed unit **except** *"any trailer or semitrailer of a gross weight not exceeding 3,000 pounds; provided, however, that the gross weight of any such trailer without brakes shall not exceed 40% of the gross weight of the towing vehicle."* That's a **compound test, verbatim in the statute itself**: to skip brakes, a towed unit must satisfy **both** the 3,000-lb ceiling **and** the 40%-of-tow-vehicle ceiling. Fail either prong and brakes are required ([N.J.S.A. 39:3-67, FindLaw](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-67/)). The statute separately requires that any towed unit that *does* need brakes have them **automatically applied on break-away** from the towing vehicle. Here's why this matters for *any* toad you'd realistically tow:

- Your RAV4 Limited AWD has a curb weight of roughly **3,570 lb** ([spec aggregators put the 2017 RAV4 AWD near 3,570 lb](https://www.edmunds.com/toyota/rav4/2017/features-specs/)); a gas AWD Tucson is roughly **3,460–3,620 lb**; a used Jeep Wrangler is **~4,000+ lb**. A car-plus-trailer combination clears the statute's **3,000-lb ceiling** on the trailer's own weight alone in most configurations, let alone with the car loaded on it — so the brakeless exemption is almost never available for a real toad setup, and supplemental braking is required by the plain text of NJ law, not merely by industry convention.
- **A note on this section's own history.** An earlier draft of this document briefly misread the statute as offering a path around supplemental braking via the 40% prong alone; that reading was wrong. **"3,000 pounds" is verbatim statutory text**, joined to the 40% clause by "and" — both prongs must be satisfied, not either one. (The statute's phrasing of "trailer or semitrailer" is still being applied to a flat-towed car or a car trailer by analogy, as is standard RV-industry practice, since the text predates recreational dinghy-towing.)

**Breakaway system.** The same statute requires the towed unit's brakes to apply automatically if it separates from the coach — that's the breakaway cable/switch included in every proper flat-tow kit and every road-legal car trailer.

**Number of towed units (the "double-tow" rule).** New Jersey **[N.J.S.A. 39:4-54](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-4-54/)** states: *"No motor vehicle shall be used on the public highways while drawing more than 2 motor-drawn vehicles."* For you this is not a constraint — a coach towing one toad (whether flat-towed or on a single trailer) is a single drawn vehicle. But it does mean you **cannot** legally stack a toad *plus* a separate bike trailer behind it in NJ if that combination would exceed the statute; keep the setup to the coach + one towed thing.

**Combined length.** NJ **[N.J.S.A. 39:3-84](https://codes.findlaw.com/nj/title-39-motor-vehicles-and-traffic-regulation/nj-st-sect-39-3-84/)** caps a recreation vehicle at **45 feet**, and a recreation-vehicle-plus-towed **combination at 65 feet**. A 25–30-ft Class C + tow bar/trailer + a ~15–18-ft car-and-trailer combo comes in well under the 65-ft limit even with a full car trailer. Length is *not* your binding legal constraint at the state-statute level — but see the note in Part 2 about park-specific length limits, which is a *practical*, not statutory, constraint that matters more for a Class C buyer chasing shorter rigs than it did for the old Class A plan.

**Registration and plates — the part that trips people up.** *How you tow* changes *what you have to register:*

- **A flat-towed car keeps its own normal registration and plates.** You're towing a titled, registered vehicle — it stays exactly what it is. Nothing new to register beyond the equipment on it.
- **A tow dolly or a car trailer is itself a vehicle that must be separately registered in New Jersey**, with its own plate and its own proof of NJ insurance, before you can legally roll it down the road. Per the [NJ MVC trailer page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicletopics/trailer.htm), a trailer **under 2,500 lb** unladen must be **registered** (a plate is included in the fee) but is exempt from titling; a trailer **over 2,500 lb** unladen must be both **registered and titled**. A steel open car hauler at 2,000–3,200 lb empty may land on either side of that line depending on the specific trailer — check the spec sheet before assuming. NJ trailer registration renews **annually** and requires **proof of New Jersey insurance** at registration ([NJ MVC](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicletopics/trailer.htm)). A tow dolly specifically is handled as a **converter dolly** and may be registered on a gross-weight basis with its own plate under **[N.J.S.A. 39:4-54](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-39/section-39-4-54/)**.
- **Practical read for your family:** a dolly or trailer isn't just a purchase — it's a second annually-renewed registration, a second plate, and a line on your insurance. A flat-tow setup adds none of that (the toad is already your registered car). That paperwork asymmetry is a quiet point in favor of flat-tow-or-nothing over the dolly/trailer routes, even now that trailering is mathematically viable — worth weighing against the fact that trailering lets you keep the cars you already own.

### What the LAW requires (the Northeast you'll actually drive)

You won't stay in New Jersey. The supplemental-braking threshold **changes at every state line**, and some Northeast neighbors are stricter than NJ. Because your toad-plus-trailer combination will always be well over ~1,000 lb, the practical read is unchanged from before: **install supplemental braking and you're legal everywhere.** Skipping it puts you offside in most of the region.

| State | Towed-vehicle supplemental brakes required above… |
|---|---|
| **New Jersey** | Over **3,000 lb** — verbatim in statute; brakeless exemption also requires ≤40% of tow vehicle, so *both* prongs must pass to skip brakes — [39:3-67](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-39/section-39-3-67/) |
| **New York** | **1,000 lb** ([Roadmaster map — industry summary](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Pennsylvania** | 3,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Connecticut** | 3,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Maryland** | 3,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Delaware** | 4,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |
| **Massachusetts** | 10,000 lb ([Roadmaster](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)) |

*Note on sourcing: the individual state thresholds above come from an RV-industry compiled map (Roadmaster), not from each state's statute directly. It's the standard reference RVers use, but verify a specific state against its own code before relying on it for a close call. New York's 1,000-lb figure in particular means **any** toad triggers the brake requirement there.*

**Other legal must-haves everywhere:** safety chains/cables cross-connected between coach and toad; the toad's brake, tail, and turn signals must work (that's the diode wiring, or the trailer's own lighting harness); and adequate mirrors so the driver can see past the towed load. These are standard equipment-law requirements bundled into every proper flat-tow kit or road-legal trailer.

> **What safety experts (and the physics) recommend — separate from the law.** Even where a statute would technically let a light toad ride brakeless, the supplemental-braking case is overwhelming: a coach trying to stop a multi-thousand-pound toad with no brakes of its own lengthens stopping distance and can shove the whole rig in a panic stop. The braking-equipment makers and RV-safety writers uniformly recommend supplemental braking on **any** towed car regardless of the local threshold ([Roadmaster — industry guidance](https://www.roadmasterinc.com/media/braking/map)). Our recommendation for your family: **install it on whatever you tow, and stop tracking the state-line differences.** It's a few hundred dollars of insurance against the one emergency stop that matters, and it keeps you legal everywhere at once.

### The hitch-rating reality — re-verdicted for the Class C / E-450 pivot

This is the section that flips. Under the old Class A / F-53 analysis, the hitch math killed trailering outright. Under the Class C / E-450 analysis, it often doesn't — but "often" is doing real work in that sentence, and the details matter.

**Receiver hitch vs. frame-mounted hitch — get the terminology right.** Nearly every Class C's rear hitch is a **frame-mounted receiver hitch**: a steel drawbar bolted directly to the chassis frame rails at the back of the coach, terminating in a standard 2-inch receiver tube. This is different from a pickup truck's hitch mainly in how much reinforcement the frame gets from the factory or the RV manufacturer — it is *not* a bumper-mounted or lightweight aftermarket hitch. The rating stamped on that hitch (by the chassis maker or the RV manufacturer who installed it) is the hard ceiling, separate from — and often lower than — what the raw GVWR/GCWR math would suggest is available ([Lippert: dead weight vs. weight distribution hitch](https://www.lippert.com/blog/dead-weight-hitch)). **Do not back into a tow number from GCWR minus GVWR and assume the physical hitch supports it — always check the hitch's own stamped or spec-sheet rating.**

**What the Ford E-450 chassis itself provides.** The 2026 Ford E-450 Super Duty stripped chassis (the platform under most Class C motorhomes, including yours) carries a **14,500-lb GVWR** and a **22,000-lb GCWR** with the 7.3L V8 / 6-speed automatic ([2026 Ford E-Series Towing Guide](https://www.vdm.ford.com/content/dam/brand_ford/en_us/brand/towing/pdf/2026-Ford-E-Series-Towing-Guide.pdf)). Subtracting a fully-loaded coach weight from the 22,000-lb GCWR is where the "you can tow ~7,500–8,000 lb" figure that manufacturers advertise comes from — but that arithmetic describes the *chassis's* theoretical headroom, not the installed hitch's rating, and the two frequently diverge. Ford's own bare chassis ships with only a modest front receiver as standard equipment for utility use, not a heavy-duty rear towing hitch; the **RV manufacturer** installs the rear hitch that actually matters for your trailer, and that's the number to verify per coach.

**What real Class C floorplans on the E-450 actually ship with (verified per model, July 2026):**

| Model | Chassis | GVWR | GCWR | Hitch rating | Tongue weight |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Winnebago Outlook 26M / 31N | Ford E-450 | 14,500 lb | 22,000 lb | **7,500 lb drawbar** | **500 lb max** |
| Jayco Redhawk **SE** 31FF (E-450) | Ford E-450 | 14,500 lb | 22,000 lb | **5,000 lb** | not confirmed — verify per unit |
| Thor Four Winds (E-Series, 2026) | Ford E-Series | — | — | **8,000 lb** | not confirmed — verify per unit |

Sources: [Winnebago Outlook specifications](https://www.winnebago.com/models/product/specifications/motorhomes/class-c/outlook); [Jayco 2026 Redhawk SE 31FF specifications](https://www.jayco.com/rvs/class-c-motorhomes/2026-redhawk-se/31ff/) (SE trim — a **5,000-lb** hitch, not the 7,500-lb figure this document uses elsewhere for "standard" Redhawk; the non-SE 2026 Redhawk line ships the higher-rated hitch per [Jayco's standard Redhawk model page](https://www.jayco.com/rvs/class-c-motorhomes/2026-redhawk/) — confirm trim before relying on either number); Thor Four Winds 2026 spec summary via [RVUSA 2026 Four Winds 28G listing](https://www.rvusa.com/rv-guide/2026-thor-motor-coach-four-winds-28g-ford-class-c-specs-fp2664).

**The important caveat, stated plainly: this 7,500–8,000-lb hitch is common on E-450 Class Cs but is not automatic** — the Redhawk SE/standard mixup right above is itself a real-world example of how easily trims get confused. Several E-450-based coaches — including some older or lower-trim builds — still ship with the **same base 5,000-lb / 500-lb-tongue hitch** the F-53 uses, because the RV manufacturer chose not to install the heavier-duty rear frame reinforcement the bigger hitch requires. This is **unverified — confirm before relying**: an etrailer Q&A on a Ford E-450 RV hitch recommendation lists a replacement hitch at 500-lb tongue / 5,000-lb GTW, loosely consistent with some base factory hitches sitting at that class ([etrailer Q&A 259530 — vendor/community source](https://www.etrailer.com/question-259530.html)), but the companion iRV2 forum thread cited alongside it could not be independently re-verified (the page did not load on repeated attempts) and should not be treated as confirming evidence on its own ([iRV2 forum thread 1952086 — unverified, page did not load](https://www.irv2.com/threads/recommendations-class-c-w-7k-tow.1952086/)). **Treat 7,500 lb as a strong, verifiable-per-model possibility, not a guarantee — get the exact hitch rating in writing (window sticker or spec sheet) for any specific coach before assuming your trailer math works.**

**Now redo the arithmetic for your cars**, this time against both the base 5,000-lb and the upgraded 7,500-lb hitch scenarios:

| | Weight |
|---|---|
| Open car trailer (steel, empty) | ~2,000–3,200 lb ([Towing Service Hub — vendor/community](https://towingservicehub.com/blog/trailer-weight-guide)) |
| Open car trailer (aluminum, empty) | ~1,450 lb ([Trailer Outlet](https://www.traileroutlet.com/10032-2/)) |
| 2017 RAV4 Limited AWD (curb) | ~3,570 lb ([Edmunds](https://www.edmunds.com/toyota/rav4/2017/features-specs/)) |
| Gas AWD Tucson (curb) | ~3,460–3,620 lb |
| **Aluminum trailer + RAV4** | **~5,020 lb** |
| **Steel trailer + RAV4** | **~5,570–6,770 lb** |
| **Aluminum trailer + Tucson** | **~4,910–5,070 lb** |
| **Steel trailer + Tucson** | **~5,460–6,820 lb** |

**Against a base 5,000-lb hitch:** you're still right at or over the line, exactly as under the old F-53 analysis — an aluminum trailer plus either car lands right on 5,000 lb (essentially a wash, decided by exact trim weight and fluids), and a steel trailer pushes 500–1,800 lb over. **The chassis pivot alone does not save you if the specific coach you buy has the base hitch.**

**Against an upgraded 7,500-lb hitch (confirmed on the Winnebago Outlook; the standard, non-SE Jayco Redhawk E-450 is reported to match — verify per floorplan, see Part 3):** every combination in the table above clears the *total weight* limit with real margin — the tightest (steel trailer + RAV4 at the high end, ~6,770 lb) still leaves roughly 700 lb of headroom under 7,500 lb. **This is the genuine change: on a coach with the upgraded hitch, trailering either of your current cars is now within the manufacturer's rated capacity, where it categorically was not on an F-53.**

**But check tongue weight separately — it's the tighter constraint, not total weight.** A loaded car trailer's tongue weight typically runs **10–15% of the trailer-plus-cargo weight** for a well-balanced load ([Lippert: what is a weight distribution hitch](https://www.lippert.com/blog/what-is-a-weight-distribution-hitch)). For a RAV4-on-aluminum-trailer combo at ~5,020 lb total, 10–15% tongue weight is **~500–750 lb** — which sits right at the Winnebago Outlook's 500-lb cap (a poorly-loaded trailer could exceed it) but comfortably inside the Jayco Redhawk's 750-lb allowance. **Tongue weight, not gross trailer weight, is the number most likely to bite you** — it depends on exactly how the car sits on the trailer deck (how far forward/back), and it's worth having the loaded rig weighed at a CAT scale before your first real trip rather than trusting the trailer manufacturer's "typical" percentage.

**The hitch-vs-bikes conflict is real and specific to a Class C.** Both the trailer hitch and (per the [bike-carrying document](08-carrying-bikes.html)) a hitch-mounted bike rack for ~6 bikes want the *same* 2-inch receiver at the back of the coach. You cannot run a car trailer and a hitch rack simultaneously off one receiver. Realistic resolutions:

- **Bikes ride on the toad car itself** (roof or hitch rack on the RAV4/Tucson) if you're running the two-vehicle caravan (Path 4b) instead of trailering — this is the cleanest fix and the reason 4b stays attractive even now that trailering is mathematically viable.
- **A double-receiver hitch extension** exists as a product category, but stacking a loaded car trailer and a loaded 6-bike rack off one coach-frame hitch point is very likely to blow past both the weight rating and the tongue-weight rating simultaneously — treat this as impractical for your bike count, not a real option.
- **Bikes go inside the coach or on a roof rack**, freeing the hitch entirely for the trailer — worth pricing against the [bike document](08-carrying-bikes.html)'s own findings on what actually fits 6 bikes.

**What "exceeding the hitch rating" actually means** — this isn't a technicality, though the specific downstream consequences below are our synthesis of standard industry risk, not a claim backed by a single primary source, so treat them as reported-but-unverified and confirm with your own insurer/chassis warranty terms before relying on them:

- **Legally / safety:** you're operating outside the chassis or hitch manufacturer's rating. In a crash or a roadside inspection, an over-rating tow is plausibly a liability finding against you — verify before relying on this for a specific state or insurer.
- **Insurance:** if a claim investigation shows you were towing over the coach's rated capacity, insurers are commonly understood to be able to **deny the claim** on a modification/rating-violation basis — but we could not independently confirm a specific case or policy clause tying this to hitch-rating overage; verify against your own policy before relying on this. See the dedicated [RV insurance and toad coverage document](23-rv-insurance-and-toad-coverage.html) for more.
- **Warranty / mechanical:** overloading the hitch and rear of the coach stresses the frame, brakes, and drivetrain the coach was never rated for, and this is reported to be able to void relevant warranty coverage — unverified against a specific manufacturer's warranty terms; confirm with the chassis/coach maker before relying on it.

This is the finding that reframes the whole decision from where the Class A analysis left it. **If you keep your current cars, your options are no longer limited to "caravan or buy a new toad" — trailering is back on the table, but only on a coach you've personally confirmed has the upgraded 7,500-lb-class hitch, and only after you've resolved the hitch-vs-bikes conflict.** If the specific coach you're eyeing has the base 5,000-lb hitch, the old conclusion still holds: caravan (Path 4b), buy a light flat-towable vehicle, or spec a different coach.

## Part 4 — Practicalities nobody warns you about

- **You cannot back up a flat-towed car. At all.** With a car flat-towed on a tow bar, reversing even a few feet can jackknife the toad and bend the tow bar or baseplate. In practice this means **you plan every stop as a pull-through** — gas stations, rest areas, campsites. A loaded car trailer has the same problem in a milder form (backing a trailer is a learnable skill, but a car-on-a-trailer adds length and reduces visibility). It's a genuine skill-and-planning change either way, and it's a real argument for the two-vehicle caravan if you're not confident about always having a pull-through.
- **Connect/disconnect time.** A dialed-in flat-tow hookup is a few minutes each way. A tow dolly is longer (drive the car on, strap it, ratchet it down). A car trailer is longest (ramps, positioning, four tie-downs). Multiply that by "every travel day, with two little kids waiting."
- **Tire wear (flat-tow).** Flat-towing puts real highway miles on the toad's tires — those miles count against the toad's tire life even though its odometer usually isn't turning. Budget for it. (A trailered car's tires don't turn at all, so this doesn't apply to Path 3.)
- **"Transmission lubrication" — myth vs. manual.** You'll read that you can flat-tow any automatic if you just add an aftermarket **transmission lube pump** or a driveshaft-disconnect. Sometimes that's mechanically true, but it is **not manufacturer-approved** for your RAV4 or Tucson, **can void the drivetrain warranty**, and is exactly the kind of "it worked for a guy on a forum" advice that turns into a $4,000 transmission. The manual's word is the one that governs your warranty and your insurance — and both your cars' manuals say **no wheels down**. Don't let a forum thread talk you past your own owner's manual.
- **Rig length on a Class C.** Adding a car trailer to a 25–30-ft Class C narrows the size advantage that's the whole point of the Class C pivot. Before assuming a trailer is "free" length-wise, check the total combination against the specific park's stated length limit — see [size trade-offs](16-open-questions.html).

## Bottom line for your family

1. **Flat-tow and dolly eligibility for your current cars is unchanged from the Class A analysis: neither the RAV4 nor a Tucson can be flat-towed or dollied.** That part of the picture doesn't depend on chassis.
2. **What changed: trailering is genuinely back on the table on a Class C.** The Ford E-450 chassis under most Class C floorplans supports a manufacturer-installed hitch rated **7,500–8,000 lb** on some models (Winnebago Outlook confirmed; standard non-SE Jayco Redhawk E-450 reported, not independently confirmed — the Redhawk **SE** trim is only 5,000 lb, see Part 3) — well above the F-53's 5,000 lb that killed the idea under the old Class A plan. Against that upgraded hitch, both the RAV4-on-trailer and Tucson-on-trailer combinations fit with real margin.
3. **But it's not automatic — verify the specific coach's hitch rating before assuming this works.** Some E-450 Class Cs still ship with the base 5,000-lb hitch. Get the number in writing for any coach you're seriously considering, and separately verify tongue weight (10–15% of trailer weight), which is the tighter constraint.
4. **The hitch-vs-bikes conflict is real:** a car trailer and a 6-bike hitch rack both want the coach's one rear receiver. Resolve this before committing to trailering — likely by putting bikes on the toad car itself if you go the caravan route instead, or on a roof rack if you commit to the trailer.
5. **Whatever you tow, supplemental braking + breakaway is legally required** — you're over every relevant weight threshold in NJ and the Northeast, so build it in and stop worrying about the state-line differences.
6. **The two-vehicle caravan (Path 4b) remains a strong, simpler alternative** even now that trailering is mathematically viable — it sidesteps hitch math and the bike conflict entirely, and Sara is more likely to be comfortable driving a Class C than she would a Class A.
7. **Test it before you buy it.** The [rent-before-you-buy plan](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) lets you try a travel day with and without a second vehicle — the cheapest possible way to find out whether a toad is worth thousands of dollars for *your* family, before spending them.
