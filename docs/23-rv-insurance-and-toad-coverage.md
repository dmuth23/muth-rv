# RV Insurance and Toad Coverage: Policy Structure, Not Just Price

Two other docs already put dollar figures on this: [`10-ownership-costs.md`](10-ownership-costs.html) landed on **$600-$1,200/year** as the realistic recreational-use planning number, and [`20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.md`](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html) covered how storing the coach at the Brick, NJ home (versus a secure facility) moves the comprehensive portion of that premium. Neither of those docs — nor [`19-towing-a-car.md`](19-towing-a-car.html), which only touches insurance in the context of trailer/dolly NJ registration paperwork — explains how an RV policy is actually *structured*: what it's legally required to include, the single biggest coverage trap on a depreciating used coach, and — now that flat-towing a second vehicle is on the table (per the family's 2026-07-12 directive) — how that toad is covered while it's hitched up behind the motorhome. This doc fills that gap. It does not repeat the cost ranges above; it explains the policy mechanics that determine whether those dollars actually pay out when something goes wrong.

As with the rest of this docket: **what the law requires** and **what industry/insurers recommend** are kept strictly separate below. Insurance-company and vendor content is guidance and sales material, not statute — label it as such in your own head even where it sounds authoritative.

## 1. What the law requires (New Jersey)

A motorhome registers and insures in New Jersey as a **motor vehicle**, not as real estate — the same statutory insurance framework that applies to your RAV4 or Tucson applies to the coach.

**The law:** New Jersey's compulsory auto insurance statute, **[N.J.S.A. 39:6B-1](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-39/section-39-6b-1/)** (financial responsibility) together with **[N.J.S.A. 39:6A-3](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-39/section-39-6a-3/)** (compulsory liability limits), requires every registered motor vehicle in New Jersey to carry liability insurance before it can legally be operated or registered. As of **January 1, 2026**, the statutory minimum liability limits increased to:

- **$35,000 per person** for bodily injury
- **$70,000 per accident** for bodily injury
- **$25,000** for property damage

per the [New Jersey Department of Banking and Insurance bulletin on the increase](https://www.nj.gov/dobi/bulletins/blt25_06.pdf) and independent confirmation from [Sterling Risk's client alert](https://www.sterlingrisk.com/client-alert-new-jersey-auto-liability-minimums-increase-january-1-2026/) and [ILSA's compliance bulletin](https://www.ilsainc.com/bulletin/new-jersey-raises-minimum-auto-insurance-limits-effective-2026/), both summarizing the same DOBI action. This is a **statewide increase** phased in over two steps (the first phase took effect January 1, 2023); it is not RV-specific — it's the same minimum that applies to the family's RAV4 and Tucson today.

The [NJ MVC's motorhome page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/motorhomes.htm), already cited in `10-ownership-costs.md`, confirms proof of insurance is required at registration and the vehicle must pass inspection within 14 days of registering — line up the policy *before* the MVC visit, not after.

**What this means practically:** the $35k/$70k/$25k figures are a statutory *floor*, not a sensible number to actually carry on a $100-150k asset. Everything past that floor — how much liability coverage to buy, collision, comprehensive, and especially how the total loss payout works — is a **coverage-level choice**, not a legal requirement. That's guidance territory, covered next.

## 2. Agreed value vs. actual cash value — the core trap on a depreciating used coach

This is the single most consequential coverage decision on a used motorhome, and it's easy to not even know it's a decision.

**Actual cash value (ACV)** is what many carriers write by default. If the coach is totaled, the insurer pays what it's determined to be worth *at the time of the loss* — current fair market value, found by comparing similar RVs and applying depreciation for age, mileage, and condition — minus the deductible. Per insurer explainers on the mechanics: [Absolute Insurance Agency's agreed-value-vs-ACV explainer](https://www.insaia.com/agreed_value_vs_actual_cash_v.php) and [Affordable Insurance Group's replacement-value-vs-ACV piece](https://www.affordableinsgrp.com/blog/replacement-value-vs-actual-cash-value-for-rvs.aspx) — both vendor/agency guidance, not law — a new RV can depreciate roughly **18% in its first year alone**, and depreciation continues (more slowly) every year after. On a used coach that has *already* absorbed its steepest depreciation hit before this family even buys it, ACV coverage means: if it's totaled, the payout reflects whatever it's worth after continued depreciation from the purchase date — which can be meaningfully less than what's still owed on a loan, or less than the cost to replace it with a comparable used unit.

**Agreed value (AV)** — sometimes called stated value — fixes the payout amount at policy inception, agreed between the buyer and the insurer, and (with true agreed-value coverage) does not get further reduced by depreciation at claim time. Per the same vendor sources, "if your Agreed Value is $100,000 and there is a total loss, you will be paid $100,000 (less the deductible) although the current market value of the RV at the time of the loss may only be $60,000." Roamly's own guide states plainly, in vendor language, that on a used RV "agreed value is recommended as the superior option since it locks in a set payout amount rather than losing money to depreciation at claim time" ([Roamly — Personal RV Insurance Guide](https://www.roamly.com/learning-center/personal-rv-insurance)).

**Two wrinkles worth knowing before calling an agent (vendor guidance, verify per-carrier):**

- Not every insurer's "agreed value" is a permanently fixed number. Some carriers' agreed-value products still adjust for the vehicle's actual condition at the time of a loss rather than paying the flat stated number, may require a professional appraisal to set the starting value, and may stop offering agreed value past a certain coach age or policy term — this general pattern shows up repeatedly in owner-forum discussion of specific carriers' policy language, but no single insurer's own published page stating the exact mechanics and age cutoff was found in this research pass, so treat any specific age threshold as **unverified — get it in writing from the actual quote**. For an older used gas coach or diesel pusher, ask directly whether "agreed value" on that specific policy is truly fixed or condition-adjusted, and whether the coach's age disqualifies it from the product entirely.
- Some carriers instead offer a **"purchase price" or "total loss replacement" endorsement** (e.g., paying up to 120% of the original purchase price on newer units) rather than true agreed value — a related but distinct product; ask which one is actually being quoted.

**Bottom line for this family:** on a $100-150k financed used coach, ask every quote explicitly for **agreed value (or stated value) coverage**, not whatever the carrier defaults to, and get the fixed-vs-condition-adjusted question answered in writing before buying. This is the single highest-leverage insurance question in the whole purchase — worth more than shopping three more points off the premium.

## 3. Recreational vs. full-timer classification

Insurers sort RV owners into two broad usage buckets, and which one a policy is written under changes both the price and what's covered.

- **Recreational use** — the default class, covering weekend trips and occasional camping, with no special declaration required.
- **Full-timer** — triggered when the RV serves as the owner's **primary residence**, commonly defined by insurers as roughly **six months (about 150+ days) a year**, per [Roamly's full-timer guide](https://www.roamly.com/learning-center/full-time-rv-insurance) and its companion [Personal RV Insurance Guide](https://www.roamly.com/learning-center/personal-rv-insurance) (both vendor guidance; each insurer sets its own exact threshold — Roamly notes "definitions can vary" and that "the six-month rule isn't always black and white"). Full-timer policies add homeowners-style protections — personal liability while parked, higher personal-property limits, loss-assessment coverage, emergency-expense coverage if the coach becomes uninhabitable after a covered loss — and cost meaningfully more: roughly **$1,500-$4,000/year** versus the $600-$1,200/year recreational band already established in `10-ownership-costs.md`, per the same Roamly source.

**This family's profile — a few weeks a year of travel, the Brick, NJ home remaining the primary residence — is recreational use**, not full-timer. That's consistent with the pricing already used in `10-ownership-costs.md`.

**The edge case worth a sentence, per directive:** Doug plans to work aboard for extended stretches (dual monitors, no employer location rules). Extended time spent *working from* the coach on a trip is not the same thing as *declaring it your residence* — the full-timer trigger is about primary-residence status, not cumulative nights away from a fixed home. Nothing in the sources reviewed for this doc suggests a long WFH road trip alone reclassifies a policy, but this specific scenario (weeks-long remote-work stints without changing legal residence) isn't spelled out in any source found, so treat it as **unverified — ask the carrier directly** whenever a single trip runs long enough that it might start to look, on paper, like full-time occupancy. A five-minute phone call before an extended trip is cheap insurance against a denied claim on a technicality.

## 4. Coverage components — what's actually being bundled into "insurance"

An RV policy isn't one thing; it's several coverages stacked together, and it's worth knowing what each does before a quote call, per [Roamly's Personal RV Insurance Guide](https://www.roamly.com/learning-center/personal-rv-insurance) (vendor guidance, standard industry framing):

- **Liability** — bodily injury and property damage the coach causes to others. Legally required, as covered in Part 1.
- **Collision** — damage to the coach itself from an accident, regardless of fault.
- **Comprehensive** — theft, vandalism, weather, fire, animal strikes — the non-collision risks. Already covered in detail (including the driveway-storage angle) in `20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.md`.
- **Uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM)** — covers this family if hit by a driver with no or insufficient insurance; required by some states, optional add-on in others (per Roamly, "required in 22 states" — NJ specifics should be confirmed directly with a quote, as the source doesn't itemize which states).
- **Contents / personal-effects coverage** — this is the one easiest to assume is already handled and isn't. **Belongings inside the RV are generally NOT covered by a homeowners policy the way you'd assume** — Progressive's own consumer-facing answer page states that while a homeowners policy "may cover certain items stored in an RV, it could be limited," without spelling out exactly what's excluded ([Progressive — RV Personal Property Coverage](https://www.progressive.com/answers/rv-personal-property-coverage/)), and multiple insurer/agency sources describe the base RV policy itself as typically *not* including contents coverage by default — it's usually an add-on, often starting around $1,000-$10,000 with limits purchasable up to $100,000 ([weinsurefast.com — Does RV Insurance Cover Personal Belongings?](https://www.weinsurefast.com/blog/does-rv-insurance-cover-personal-belongings/), community/agency guidance). For a family bringing along WFH equipment (dual monitors, a work laptop) and kids' gear on every trip, this is worth a specific ask, not an assumption.
- **Full-timer / vacation-liability add-ons** — not needed for this family's recreational profile per Part 3; skip unless the classification conversation above changes.
- **Roadside assistance / towing** — worth calling out specifically because **standard auto roadside plans, including AAA, typically are not equipped to tow a 30-35 ft Class A.** RV-specific providers frame this directly: Good Sam's own comparison states "towing a Class A motorhome isn't the same as calling a tow truck for your sedan — you need professionals trained to handle large rigs" ([Good Sam vs. Coach-Net RV Roadside Assistance comparison](https://roadside.goodsam.com/resources/good-sam-vs-coach-net-rv-roadside-assistance), vendor content). Neither that page nor others reviewed give a specific dollar tow-cost delta between standard and RV-specific towing, so that figure is **unverified — get an actual quote** rather than repeating a number here. RV-specific roadside providers worth pricing: **Coach-Net**, whose Premier Coach plan (for motorized RVs) runs **$249/year** and advertises no mileage/dollar cap on towing a disabled RV to the nearest qualified repair facility ([Coach-Net Premier plans](https://coach-net.com/24-7-protect-roadside-assistance/), vendor pricing, verify current rate), and **Good Sam's** RV-specific roadside plans. Some RV insurance policies bundle a roadside endorsement rather than requiring a separate membership — ask the quoting agent which applies before buying a standalone plan on top.
- **Windshield/glass coverage** — the docket's chassis doc ([`03-chassis-weights-payload.md`](03-chassis-weights-payload.html)) does not document a specific F-53 glass-crack pattern, so no claim is made here about that being a known issue for this family's chassis; if a specific coach's inspection turns up chronic windshield chipping, ask about a glass-coverage rider at quote time.

## 5. The toad: how the second vehicle is actually covered while flat-towed

This is the section the docket has never answered — directive 6 puts flat-towing the RAV4 or Tucson (or buying a dedicated toad) on the table, and `19-towing-a-car.md` covers *whether* those cars can be towed and how a dolly/trailer gets registered, but not how insurance responds once something's hitched up and rolling.

**(a) The toad keeps its own auto liability policy — it does not become part of the RV policy just because it's hitched.** The RAV4 or Tucson (or a purpose-bought flat-tow toad) needs to stay insured under its own policy the entire time it's being towed. It does not get absorbed into, or automatically covered by, the motorhome's policy.

**(b) Liability while towed — commonly involves both policies, and this is a genuine gray area.** Community and forum discussion (not an insurer's own published policy language, so flagged per this docket's house style as **reported but unverified — confirm with your own carrier**) consistently describes the mechanism this way: if the toad, while hitched and being towed, causes damage to another vehicle or object, **the motorhome's liability policy is generally understood to be the one that responds**, because the motorhome is the vehicle actually in control ([iRV2 Forums — "Insurance Coverage for Toad" discussion](https://www.irv2.com/forums/f85/insurance-coverage-for-toad-402190.html); [RV Forum Community — "Who Insures The Toad While Towing?"](https://www.rvforum.net/threads/who-insures-the-toad-while-towing.835388/) — both owner/community discussion, not primary insurer documentation). No insurer's own published explainer stating this rule in black and white was found in this research pass; treat the mechanism as plausible industry practice, not a confirmed contractual guarantee, and have the quoting agent state it in writing for the specific policy being bought.

**(c) Physical damage to the toad itself while in tow — the murkier half.** The same community sources are consistent that **damage to the toad itself (a rock chip, being rear-ended while hitched, a bad hitch disconnect) is expected to fall to the toad's own collision/comprehensive coverage, not the motorhome's policy** — "if you were towing a toad without comprehensive or collision and it was being towed by the motorhome and there was an incident then there would be no coverage for the damage to the toad" (per the iRV2/RV Forum discussion cited above). Real anecdotal claim examples surfaced in vendor review pages reinforce this is a live risk, not a hypothetical: one policyholder reported a toad coming loose and striking the motorhome from behind, resulting in roughly $12,000 in RV repairs and a lengthy claims process before it was resolved ([Roamly — National General RV Insurance Review](https://www.roamly.com/insurance-reviews/national-general-rv-insurance), vendor review page citing a customer account — anecdotal). **Practical read: the toad needs its own comprehensive and collision coverage kept active for the whole trip, not just liability, or physical damage to it in tow may not be covered by anything.**

**(d) This interacts directly with the hitch-rating question already covered in `19-towing-a-car.md`.** That doc already flags — and this doc does not re-derive — that towing over the coach's rated hitch capacity is commonly understood to give an insurer grounds to deny a claim on a modification/rating-violation basis, though it likewise could not confirm a specific policy clause and marks that as reported-but-unverified (see [`19-towing-a-car.md`, "The hitch-rating reality" section](19-towing-a-car.html)). The two risks compound: an over-capacity tow setup that causes an accident is exactly the scenario where both "which policy pays for the toad" ambiguity and "was the tow setup even a covered configuration" ambiguity would collide in the worst possible claim.

**(e) The practical takeaway: confirm with the insurer BEFORE towing, not after.** Every source reviewed for this section — forum, vendor review, and vendor guide alike — converges on the same practical advice, restated by [RVLife's toad-vehicle overview](https://rvlife.com/toad-vehicles-rv/): "it's advisable to verify your specific coverage with your insurance agents, as every state is different, and every insurance company is different." Before the first trip towing anything — RAV4, Tucson, or a purpose-bought toad — get the RV insurer and the toad's own insurer to each confirm, in writing or in the policy declarations, exactly how a towing-related claim would be handled and by which policy. Don't rely on the general pattern described above as a guarantee for this family's specific carriers.

## 6. Shopping the policy

`10-ownership-costs.md` already lists the core carrier set worth quoting: **Progressive, GEICO, National General, and an RV specialist like Roamly or Foremost**, noting RV pricing varies more between carriers than auto insurance typically does. Two things to add specifically for this purchase:

- **Confirm the carrier actually writes a true RV-specific policy, not just a generalist auto policy with an RV rider bolted on.** Not every "auto insurance" company underwrites motorhomes the same way; some route RV business through a partner carrier entirely (for example, Good Sam's RV policies are run through National General; Roamly's are underwritten by a panel that includes Allstate, Safeco, Foremost, and National General, per [Roamly's Good Sam RV Insurance Review](https://www.roamly.com/insurance-reviews/good-sam-rv-insurance) and [National General RV Insurance Review](https://www.roamly.com/insurance-reviews/national-general-rv-insurance), vendor content). Ask directly who actually underwrites the policy being quoted.
- **On the same call, ask explicitly about (1) agreed-value terms** — fixed or condition-adjusted, and whether the specific coach's age still qualifies (Part 2) — **and (2) toad/towing endorsements** — whether a flat-tow or trailer setup needs a specific rider, and how a towing-related claim would actually be handled (Part 5). These two questions are easy to forget to ask and expensive to discover the answer to only after a claim.

## Bottom line for the family

The statutory floor (**$35k/$70k/$25k** liability as of 2026) is not the number to shop to — it's the minimum the MVC will accept, not a sensible protection level for a $100-150k asset and everyone riding in it. The two decisions worth spending real attention on: get **agreed value, not actual cash value**, in writing, confirmed as truly fixed rather than condition-adjusted; and if a toad enters the picture, get **both the RV insurer and the toad's own insurer** to confirm in writing how a towing-related claim splits between the two policies before the first tow, not after. Everything else — recreational-vs-full-timer classification, contents coverage, roadside towing — is a normal shopping-call checklist, not a trap, as long as it's asked about explicitly rather than assumed to be bundled in.
