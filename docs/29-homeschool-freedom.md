# Homeschooling as a Travel Superpower — and Its Honest Limits

Most families shopping for an RV are shopping against a calendar they don't control. School sets the summer, the winter break, the spring break, and everything else bends around it — including, as the archived version of this page once laid out in detail, exactly when their kids would be legally required to enroll ([archived: School Timing and Travel Cadence](archive/21-school-and-travel-cadence.html)). That page is retired now for a simple reason: **this family already decided to homeschool.** The calendar tether it analyzed so carefully doesn't apply to you. This page replaces it with something more useful — what having no school calendar actually buys you in practice, what it costs you socially if you're not deliberate about it, and the honest, unglamorous truth from families who've done it.

## The law, briefly and precisely

New Jersey's compulsory-education statute is short, and it's worth reading exactly as written rather than paraphrased, because the paraphrase is where people get it wrong.

**[N.J.S.A. 18A:38-25](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-18a/section-18a-38-25/)** reads: *"Every parent, guardian or other person having custody and control of a child between the ages of six and 16 years shall cause such child regularly to attend the public schools of the district or a day school in which there is given instruction equivalent to that provided in the public schools for children of similar grades and attainments or to receive equivalent instruction elsewhere than at school."*

Two things fall out of that sentence, and both matter to this family:

1. **"Or to receive equivalent instruction elsewhere than at school"** is the entire legal basis for homeschooling in New Jersey. There is no separate homeschool statute — this clause, inside the compulsory-attendance law, is it.
2. **The mandate only begins at age six.** Below six, New Jersey does not compel attendance at all. Your kids, born March 2022, don't turn six until March 2028 — so strictly speaking, nothing in this docket's 2026–2028 buying window even brushes up against a legal requirement. The homeschool decision is a values and practice choice for you right now, not a compliance deadline.

### What the state actually requires in practice — separated from what's merely common

New Jersey is one of the least-regulated homeschooling states in the country, and the state's own guidance is unusually direct about the limits of its authority. Per the **[NJ Department of Education's Homeschooling FAQ](https://www.nj.gov/education/safety/nontraditional/faq_homeschool.shtml)**:

| Question | LAW says | Common PRACTICE |
|---|---|---|
| Must you notify the district? | Not required, except narrow cases (denied enrollment appeal, or withdrawing an already-enrolled high schooler) | Many families notify anyway, for their own paper trail |
| Can the district approve your curriculum? | No — districts are "not required or authorized to review and approve the curriculum" | Families pick from established curricula (see below) largely for their own confidence, not compliance |
| Must homeschooled kids be tested? | No — districts are "not required or authorized to test a child educated elsewhere than at school" | Some families test informally to track progress |
| Must the parent be a certified teacher? | No — explicitly not required by law | N/A |
| What's the actual legal standard? | "Equivalent instruction" — per the 1967 case **State v. Massa**, this means *academic* equivalency only; social-development equivalency is not required ([NJ DOE FAQ](https://www.nj.gov/education/safety/nontraditional/faq_homeschool.shtml)) | Most families build in social contact anyway — see the counterweights section below |

The practical upshot: **New Jersey will not ask you to prove anything.** That's real freedom, but it also means the burden of making the education (and the socialization) actually work sits entirely with you — there's no state safety net checking in. Worth naming plainly: light regulation is a double-edged tool, not an unqualified convenience.

## What no-calendar-tether actually buys you

This is the part that's easy to state as a slogan — "we can travel anytime!" — and much more useful to state with numbers.

### Off-season and shoulder-season access, quantified

Camping demand is sharply seasonal, and the price/availability swing between peak summer and the shoulder months (roughly April–May and September–October) is not subtle:

- **Booking speed and price (as of July 2026):** for the most sought-after federal campgrounds, reserved summer sites are typically booked six months to a year in advance, and first-come-first-served sites can fill before sunrise — while, in THOR Industries' words, "during the shoulder season, same-day reservations at some of the best campgrounds are relatively easy to get, and these spots will cost you less than half of what they typically go for during the peak summer months" ([THOR Industries, Tips for RVing in National Parks During the Shoulder Season](https://www.thorindustries.com/stories/rv-national-parks-shoulder-season)).
- **The reservation window itself:** Recreation.gov opens most federal campground reservations on a rolling basis exactly six months ahead, at 10:00 a.m. Eastern daily — and the agency's own guidance warns that popular sites "can book up in minutes" once that window opens ([Recreation.gov — Tips for Making Campsite Reservations at Popular Locations](https://www.recreation.gov/articles/list/tips-for-making-campsite-reservations-at-popular-locations/281)). A family with zero calendar constraints can simply pick less-contested weeks and skip that six-month race entirely.
- **Crowding is a real, current problem in peak months** — 2026 news coverage describes marquee parks in July as feeling like "L.A. at rush hour" or "Disney World," a strain driven by concentrated summer visitation ([CNN, "American national parks feeling the strain this summer," July 2026](https://www.cnn.com/2026/07/10/travel/travel-national-parks-strain-summer-crowds)). Shoulder-season travel sidesteps this directly, not just as a matter of preference but of trip quality — shorter lines, calmer trails, more attentive rangers.

### Weekday access, quantified

Separate from season, weekday-vs-weekend matters on its own axis, and state park systems say so outright: midweek reservations are consistently easier to land than weekends, in part because many systems impose two-night (or three-night, on holiday weekends) minimum stays specifically on weekends that don't apply midweek — which structurally reduces weekday competition for the same sites ([Roadtrippers, The Guide to State Park Camping Reservations in All 50 States](https://roadtrippers.com/magazine/state-park-camping-reservations/); California State Parks camping guidance, [parks.ca.gov](https://www.parks.ca.gov/?page_id=23437)). A family that can roll in on a Tuesday is playing a fundamentally easier reservation game than one that only travels Friday–Sunday.

### Why this matters specifically for the cross-country, big-park mission

Your stated mission is national and state park reach — explicitly including harder-to-reach marquee parks like Sequoia and Canyonlands. Both are relevant to the size question this docket keeps circling back to, and both reward flexible timing:

- **Sequoia & Kings Canyon:** the Park Service is explicit that on the 16-mile Generals Highway stretch between Potwisha Campground and the Giant Forest Museum — built in the early 1920s — vehicles longer than 22 feet are not advised, and single vehicles longer than 40 feet are prohibited outright; longer rigs are steered to the alternate Big Stump/Highway 180 entrance instead ([NPS — Sequoia & Kings Canyon, Visiting With an Oversized Vehicle](https://www.nps.gov/seki/planyourvisit/oversized-vehicle.htm)). Mineral King's campgrounds bar RVs and trailers entirely ([same source](https://www.nps.gov/seki/planyourvisit/oversized-vehicle.htm)).
- **Canyonlands:** the Needles District's Squaw Flat Campground caps RVs at 28 feet; no hookups or dump stations are listed at the park's developed campgrounds — just vault toilets, fire rings, picnic tables, and seasonal potable water ([Utah.com, Canyonlands National Park camping guide](https://www.utah.com/destinations/national-parks/canyonlands-national-park/places-to-stay/inside-the-park/camping-rv/)).

Neither of those numbers dictates your final rig size on their own — that's a synthesis for a different doc — but they're direct evidence for why "we want to actually reach the marquee parks" and "bigger is safer" are in real tension, and why shoulder-season/weekday flexibility (smaller crowds, more first-come-first-served viability, less pressure to book the one 40-foot slot six months out) works *with* a smaller rig rather than against it.

## Roadschooling in practice — what real families report

*Everything in this section is community experience from RV-family blogs and outlets, not verified statistics or research. Read it as seasoned anecdote, weighted accordingly — not as a claim about outcomes for your specific kids.*

- **The rhythm that works is loose but consistent, not a copy of a classroom.** Long-running full-time families report the pattern that sticks is a few focused academic days each week, with travel and exploration filling the rest — not trying to replicate a 9-to-3 school day on the road ([The Wanderpreneurs, 5th Year of Roadschooling in an RV](https://www.thewanderpreneurs.com/blog/our-5th-year-of-homeschooling-in-an-rv-2022-2023); community experience).
- **Curriculum choices skew toward flexible, nature-and-place-based programs.** Community write-ups repeatedly point to curricula like The Good and the Beautiful (academics plus character and nature study) and Oak Meadow (Waldorf-inspired, hands-on) as fits for a moving classroom, precisely because they don't assume a fixed desk or daily bell schedule ([MiAcademy, Roadschooling: Homeschooling On the Go](https://miacademy.co/homeschool-curriculum/homeschooling-your-way/homeschool-styles/roadschooling/); community experience).
- **The road itself becomes curriculum.** Families describe building lessons directly around where they are — history at Gettysburg, geology and wildlife at Yellowstone — turning national and state parks (already your travel focus) into the classroom rather than a competing activity ([Cruise America, The Ultimate Guide to Roadschooling in an RV](https://www.cruiseamerica.com/trip-inspiration/guide-to-roadschooling); community experience).
- **You will not be alone.** Fulltime Families runs organized roadschooling community infrastructure — group meetups, shared curriculum recommendations, and campground-based social events aimed specifically at connecting traveling homeschool families with each other ([Fulltime Families, Roadschooling — How To Homeschool While RVing](https://www.fulltimefamilies.com/roadschooling-how-to-homeschool-while-rving/); community experience). Many full-timing families also invest in reciprocal museum/zoo memberships and an [America the Beautiful annual pass](https://www.nps.gov/planyourvisit/passes.htm) specifically because they get used constantly as part of the "classroom."
- **The pre-kindergarten-age years are the easiest window, by the community's own account.** Multiple sources note that traveling with kids who aren't yet tied to any enrolled program — your situation for the whole 2026–2028 window and beyond, since you've opted out of enrollment altogether — is the lowest-friction version of this lifestyle: no withdrawal conversations, no district notification triggers, maximum flexibility from day one ([Outdoorsy, The Complete Guide to Roadschooling](https://www.outdoorsy.com/blog/the-complete-guide-to-roadschooling); community experience).

## Honest counterweights — this is not a hype piece

No-calendar freedom is a genuine asset, but it is not free of trade-offs, and a docket that only listed the upside wouldn't be doing its job. Three things are worth naming plainly.

**Social rhythm doesn't happen automatically — you have to build it.** New Jersey's light-touch law means no one is checking whether your kids get peer contact; that responsibility sits entirely with you. The state has an active homeschool community — statewide directories list dozens of co-ops and enrichment groups, and there's at least one enrichment cooperative with casual, mixed-age social gatherings within reasonable range of Brick ([TheHomeSchoolMom, New Jersey Homeschool Co-ops & Academic Enrichment Classes](https://www.thehomeschoolmom.com/homeschooling-in-new-jersey/homeschool-co-ops-academic-enrichment-classes/); [TheHomeSchoolMom, New Jersey Homeschool Organizations & Support Groups](https://www.thehomeschoolmom.com/homeschooling-in-new-jersey/homeschool-organizations-support-groups/); community-reported CBEC group in Oceanport, NJ). Worth treating as a to-do, not a given: identify and join a local co-op *before* the travel-heavy season starts, so it's a standing anchor rather than an afterthought.

**Sports and activities require more deliberate planning than "sign up for the town league."** Traditional public-school sports are often unavailable or logistically awkward for homeschoolers depending on the specific league's rules; New Jersey does have dedicated homeschool sports and arts programming ([TheHomeSchoolMom, New Jersey Homeschool Sports & Arts Opportunities](https://www.thehomeschoolmom.com/homeschooling-in-new-jersey/homeschool-sports-arts-opportunities/)), but it's a narrower funnel than the default public-school pipeline, and it takes active research and enrollment rather than just showing up.

**Travel-heavy stretches and "home base" stretches need their own honest rhythm.** The families whose accounts read as sustainable (rather than as a burnout story) describe alternating: real travel seasons balanced against real stretches of being home in one place long enough for co-op, sports, and friendships to actually take root ([The Wanderpreneurs, 5th Year of Roadschooling](https://www.thewanderpreneurs.com/blog/our-5th-year-of-homeschooling-in-an-rv-2022-2023); community experience). For this family, that likely means treating Brick as a genuine home base with standing weekly commitments — not just a driveway the coach returns to — and building the travel calendar around protecting that rhythm, rather than the other way around.

**Abroad travel adds its own layer.** Your work ties may create travel opportunities in Europe, and homeschooling's flexibility is precisely what makes that survivable as a family — no withdrawal-and-re-enrollment fight, no missed-days conversation with a principal. But it also means the "build a local rhythm" work above has to happen in parallel with the possibility of being away entirely for a stretch. That's a real logistics question (which co-op activities can pause and resume; which can't) rather than a reason to avoid either kind of travel.

## The bottom line for Doug and Sara

- **The law is settled and simple:** [N.J.S.A. 18A:38-25](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-18a/section-18a-38-25/) only compels attendance from age six; the "equivalent instruction elsewhere than at school" clause is homeschooling's entire legal basis in NJ; the state does not review curriculum, does not test homeschooled kids, and requires no teacher certification ([NJ DOE FAQ](https://www.nj.gov/education/safety/nontraditional/faq_homeschool.shtml)).
- **No-calendar-tether is a quantifiable, not just vibes-based, advantage:** shoulder-season sites can run under half of peak summer price and be booked same-day where summer requires a six-month race ([THOR Industries](https://www.thorindustries.com/stories/rv-national-parks-shoulder-season); [Recreation.gov](https://www.recreation.gov/articles/list/tips-for-making-campsite-reservations-at-popular-locations/281)); weekday travel dodges weekend minimum-stay rules that concentrate demand ([Roadtrippers](https://roadtrippers.com/magazine/state-park-camping-reservations/)).
- **The marquee parks reward exactly this flexibility.** Sequoia and Canyonlands both impose real length limits and light infrastructure that favor a smaller, more schedule-flexible rig over a bigger one chasing a narrow summer booking window ([NPS Sequoia](https://www.nps.gov/seki/planyourvisit/oversized-vehicle.htm); [Utah.com Canyonlands](https://www.utah.com/destinations/national-parks/canyonlands-national-park/places-to-stay/inside-the-park/camping-rv/)).
- **The roadschooling community is large, organized, and full of practical playbooks** — this is a well-trodden path, not a fringe experiment ([Fulltime Families](https://www.fulltimefamilies.com/roadschooling-how-to-homeschool-while-rving/)).
- **Freedom isn't automatic community.** Co-op, sports, and a genuine Brick home-base rhythm need deliberate, upfront planning — the light regulation that makes travel easy also means no one else is building the social scaffolding for you.
