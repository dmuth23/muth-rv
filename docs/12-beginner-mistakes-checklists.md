# The Classic Mistakes Catalog and the Checklist Pack

Every RV forum has the same ten threads: "what I wish I knew before buying," "my worst dump station story," "how I found out my awning doesn't like wind." The mistakes are boring and repetitive precisely because they're avoidable — they're not bad luck, they're missing checklists. For a family buying a used 30-35' gas Class A to actually live and work out of on real trips (not a rig that sits in storage), the stakes are a little different from the average retiree-couple RV blog: you've got two 4-year-olds who need consistent nap-and-bedtime logistics, a work laptop and two monitors that need to survive the drive, six bikes competing for a fixed weight budget, and a plan to genuinely boondock, not just plug into 50-amp at a KOA. This document is the "don't learn this the hard way" reference — the mistakes that cost real money or real safety margin, followed by five checklists you can print and use on trip one.

## The Classic Mistakes

### 1. Buying the wrong floorplan for how you'll actually use it

The single most common regret in the RV forums isn't a mechanical failure — it's a layout that looked great on the lot and doesn't work in daily life. [Getaway Couple's rundown of the worst RV floor plans](https://www.getawaycouple.com/worst-rv-floor-plans/) and [Colton RV's list of first-time-buyer regrets](https://www.coltonrv.com/blog/common-issues-first-time-rv-buyers-regret-and-how-to-prevent-them/) both point to the same pattern: buyers get distracted by decor and finish quality on the walkthrough and don't test the layout against real use. For a family with two 4-year-olds, bunk-style layouts sound perfect until you notice the bathroom door swings directly into the bunk nook, meaning every 2 a.m. bathroom trip wakes both kids — and the space under those bunks is frequently occupied by the water heater and furnace, not available for bike or gear storage.

For Doug specifically, the floorplan mistake to watch is different: **no usable surface for a two-monitor remote-work setup.** A dinette that has to be broken down for every meal, or a slide that can't be extended while parked in a shaded, unlevel spot, is a "wrong floorplan for actual use" problem exactly like the bunk-bathroom issue — it's just invisible until you try to actually work a full day in it. Walk every floorplan candidate through: where do the monitors mount, is that surface stable while driving, and does opening it interfere with anything else (slide-outs, the kids' sleeping area, the aisle to the bathroom at night)?

One more layout note relevant to two 4-year-olds: not every Class A floorplan has enough factory belted, forward-facing seating positions to legally and safely install two car seats. Confirm actual belted positions (not just "seats") on any coach you're seriously considering — this is worth a walkthrough question to the dealer, not an assumption from the floorplan diagram.

### 2. Skipping the professional inspection

This is the mistake with the widest gap between "feels avoidable" and "actually gets skipped," especially by handy owners who assume they can eyeball everything themselves. [RV Help's inspection guide](https://rvhelp.com/blog/rv-inspection-checklist) and the [National RV Inspectors Association (NRVIA)](https://nrvia.org/2025/02/5-important-inspection-points-when-buying-a-used-rv/) are blunt about why: a certified inspector spends 6-9 hours on the coach and 3-6 more writing the report, finding things a driveway walkthrough cannot — subtle water intrusion, delaminated wall layers under a wallpapered surface, roof seams that look fine from the ground but are cracking under UV. A pre-purchase inspection is reported as running roughly $400-$1,500 broadly, with certified NRVIA motorhome inspections often specifically $700-$1,500+ ([RVshare on RV inspection cost](https://rvshare.com/blog/how-much-does-an-rv-inspection-cost/); [GoDownsize on RV inspection cost](https://www.godownsize.com/rv-inspection-cost/); [RV Help on inspection cost](https://rvhelp.com/blog/how-much-does-an-rv-inspection-cost/)); an $8,500-$15,000 average repair bill for problems *found* during an inspection is widely repeated in industry blogs but hasn't been traced to an authoritative primary source — treat it as an unverified rule of thumb rather than a confirmed statistic, and verify current pricing before budgeting against it. Handy is an advantage for doing your *own* upgrades later; it doesn't substitute for someone whose job is finding hidden water damage on a coach with unknown history. Full treatment, including chassis/drivetrain items, lives in `04-used-buying-inspection.md`; this entry is the "don't skip it" flag.

### 3. Overloading past cargo capacity — especially with six bikes aboard

Gasoline Class A motorhomes generally run tighter on payload than diesel pushers; reported cargo-capacity figures span a wide range, roughly 1,200-4,300 lb depending on age and trim, before a single bag is loaded ([iRV2's Cargo Carrying Capacity Guide](https://www.irv2.com/threads/cargo-carrying-capacity-guide.2060890/); [RVshare's RV weight-by-class guide](https://rvshare.com/blog/how-much-do-rvs-weigh/)) — verify current specs on the actual sticker rather than assuming a comfortable floor. Since 2008, motorhomes carry **OCCC (Occupant and Cargo Carrying Capacity)** on a yellow sticker inside an entry door — one number covering occupants, all fresh water, all propane, tools, gear, and cargo ([Lichtsinn RV's OCCC explainer](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/understanding-occupant-and-cargo-carrying-capacity-in-the-winnebago-vita/); [RV Forum Community thread on OCCC replacing CCC](https://www.rvforum.net/threads/new-rv-weight-labels-occc-replaces-ccc.804469/)). Critically, OCCC is *not* pre-calculated with the fresh tank already deducted — fresh water is treated as cargo that the owner must subtract from the OCCC figure themselves, alongside occupants, gear, and full propane ([NIRVC's RV numbers primer](https://www.nirvc.com/blog/rv-numbers-ratings-limits-and-capacity/); [Lichtsinn RV's OCCC explainer](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/understanding-occupant-and-cargo-carrying-capacity-in-the-winnebago-vita/)); genuine boondocking capability (a requirement here) means that water weight comes straight out of the same pool as bikes and gear, not a separately pre-accounted allowance.

Work the arithmetic from the actual sticker, not a brochure spec: OCCC 3,500 lb − 4 occupants (~600 lb) − 60 gal fresh water at 8.34 lb/gal (~500 lb) − a full propane fill (~35 lb) leaves roughly **2,365 lb** for every bag, tool, and bike. Six bikes plus a rack easily run 250-400+ lb, typically hung off the rear — the same leverage point that stresses a rear axle and hitch receiver together. Full worked math with real hitch ratings lives in `03-chassis-weights-payload.md` and `08-carrying-bikes.md`; the safety stakes are covered generally by [Wholesale Warranties on why RV weight matters](https://wholesalewarranties.com/blog/why-rv-weight-matters/) — overloading strains brakes and tires and can cause sway independent of driver skill.

### 4. Driving too many miles per day

New Class A drivers consistently over-plan mileage on early trips, then arrive exhausted, rushed, and more likely to make a docking or parking mistake at the destination. The two rules of thumb that recur across the RV community are the **2/2/2 rule** (no more than 200 miles per day, stop every 2 hours, arrive by 2 p.m.) and the looser **3/3/3 rule** (no more than 300 miles, arrive by 3 p.m., stay a minimum of 3 nights) — see [Cruise America's explainer](https://www.cruiseamerica.com/trip-inspiration/what-is-the-3-3-3-rv-travel-rule-why-it-s-importan) and the practical first-timer guidance at [RVShare](https://rvshare.com/blog/tips-for-driving-an-rv-first-time/). With two 4-year-olds aboard, the practical driver isn't the coach's fatigue limit, it's the kids' — build travel days around nap timing and frequent stops, not a mileage target. Treat 200-250 miles as a comfortable travel day, not a floor to hit.

### 5. Awning left out in wind

This is one of the fastest, most expensive "I turned my back for twenty minutes" failures. Manufacturer guidance and RV-service sources agree: most patio awnings should be retracted once sustained gusts pass **15-20 mph**, and gusts over **25 mph** can tear an awning off the coach entirely ([Morton's on the Move wind guide](https://www.mortonsonthemove.com/how-much-wind-can-an-rv-withstand/); [TJ's RV on awning wind limits](https://www.tjsrv.com/how-much-wind-can-an-rv-awning-withstand/)). Even awnings with automatic wind sensors can be damaged by a sudden gust before the sensor reacts. The practical rule from experienced RVers: retract the awning any time you leave the site, any time you're sleeping, and any time weather is even a maybe — it's always cheaper to re-extend an awning than to replace one.

### 6. Black tank valve left open at the campground

This is the mistake every new owner makes exactly once, usually a week or two into full-hookup camping, when leaving the black tank valve "open to drain continuously" seems like a reasonable way to avoid dealing with it. It isn't: with the valve open, liquid drains away immediately while solid waste stays behind and slowly builds into what RV techs call a "pyramid plug," directly under the toilet outlet ([Lasting Memories Mobile RV Services on why to never leave the black tank open](https://lastingmemoriesrv.com/maintenance-tip-monday-why-you-should-never-leave-your-rv-black-tank-valve-open/)). Left unaddressed, that solid mass can block the tank badly enough that waste backs up toward the toilet instead of out the drain — an expensive and unpleasant service call. The rule: black tank valve stays **closed** at all times except during an active dump, and you don't dump until the tank is roughly two-thirds full, because volume and pressure are what actually flush solids out when you do open it.

### 7. No departure checklist — antenna, steps, bay doors

Ask any long-time RVer for their worst "rookie moment" story and a large share involve driving away with something still deployed: the TV antenna still cranked up (hits the first low-clearance underpass or garage), the entry steps still extended, an exterior storage bay door unlatched, or the shore power cord and sewer hose not yet disconnected and stowed. [Morton's on the Move catalogs the 13 most commonly forgotten pre-departure steps](https://www.mortonsonthemove.com/commonly-forgotten-steps-on-rv-checklist/), and the fix every source agrees on is the same: use a written checklist every single time, not memory, and assign one person the job of doing a final walk-around before the wheels move. This is genuinely a case where a $0 printed checklist (see below) prevents a multi-thousand-dollar mistake.

### 8. Skipping the walkaround

Related to #7 but distinct enough to call out separately: a slow 360-degree walk around the entire coach immediately before driving — checking tire condition and pressure, looking under the coach for drips, confirming nothing is left on the ground (leveling blocks, wheel chocks, the water hose, a kid's bike helmet), and confirming no low-hanging cords or steps — catches problems a checklist item list alone won't, because it's a physical scan rather than a memory-jog. Do it even on travel day mornings when you're in a hurry to beat traffic; it takes ninety seconds and it's the single best "did we forget something" check available.

### 9. Winterizing mistakes in the Northeast

Central New Jersey gets real hard freezes, and RV plumbing does not forgive a skipped or half-done winterization. The two costliest errors, per RV technicians: (1) draining the water heater but forgetting to install the bypass kit first, which wastes antifreeze pumping it straight out the drain instead of protecting the lines, and (2) not draining the water heater tank at all, leaving 6-10 gallons to freeze, expand, and crack the tank — one of the most expensive winterizing mistakes possible ([RV Travel's 7 common winterization mistakes, per RV techs](https://www.rvtravel.com/7-common-rv-winterization-mistakes-rv-tech-double-check-2276/)). A third frequent miss is forgetting outdoor-kitchen/shower lines and the city-water inlet check valve, which can freeze and leak into the sidewall, causing slow hidden delamination rather than an obvious burst pipe ([RV Living's winterizing mistakes rundown](https://rvliving.com/rv-winterizing-mistakes/)). Only use **RV/marine-specific, non-toxic antifreeze** — automotive antifreeze is toxic and wrong for drinking-water plumbing. Frozen, cracked plumbing runs $2,000+ to repair, reason enough to do this correctly every fall, not just "most years."

### 10. The dealer-service-backlog trap

Less a moment-of-mistake, more a structural trap worth knowing before you need service: RV service departments nationwide are backed up, and warranty repairs take a reported average of about **50 days**, stretching to roughly **73 days** when a part is out of stock ([RV Miles' investigation into RV repair wait times](https://rvmiles.com/how-long-do-rv-repairs-take/)). Dealers commonly prioritize customers who bought their unit at that store, so a rig purchased at Camping World Lakewood will generally get faster service there than elsewhere — a real point in favor of buying where you intend to service. Takeaway: budget slack into any trip plan for "something breaks and the fix might not be fast," and don't expect the same-week turnaround a car dealer offers.

### 11. Overbuying gadgets before the first trip

The instinct to fully kit out the rig before ever camping in it — outdoor kitchen add-ons, a second generator, every gadget on the RV-accessories aisle — is understandable and almost universally regretted. [KOA's mistakes-to-avoid list](https://koa.com/blog/rv-mistakes-to-avoid/) and [Harvest Hosts' newbie mistakes rundown](https://www.harvesthosts.com/blog/5-rv-mistakes-newbies-make) both flag overpacking as a top-five beginner error: every item adds weight (see mistake #3 above — it all comes out of the same OCCC pool), most factory RVs already carry basic kitchen and utility equipment, and gear bought sight-unseen frequently turns out to be redundant or the wrong size once you've actually lived in the coach for a weekend. The better sequence: take the first two or three trips with minimal gear, keep a running list of what you actually reached for and what you didn't, then buy the second round of equipment based on real experience rather than a showroom impulse. This also naturally protects the cargo-capacity budget six bikes are already competing for.

## Checklist Pack

### Pre-Purchase Walkthrough Checklist

Use this on the lot before you're emotionally committed — pair it with a paid professional inspection before final purchase, not instead of one.

- [ ] Confirm the OCCC and GVWR on the actual yellow certification sticker (inside an entry door) — not the brochure
- [ ] Ask for the coach's actual empty (as-equipped) weight if available, ideally from a CAT scale receipt
- [ ] Test every slide-out in and out at least twice
- [ ] Run the generator under load (turn on the A/C and microwave) for at least 10-15 minutes
- [ ] Check roof for soft spots, resealed seams, and recent caulk (sign of a past leak) — walk it if safely accessible
- [ ] Press on wall panels near windows, the shower surround, and the entry step area for soft spots (delamination/water intrusion)
- [ ] Run every appliance: fridge (both AC and propane/DC modes), water heater, furnace, stovetop, oven, microwave
- [ ] Fill and drain the fresh tank; run water at every faucet and check for leaks under sinks
- [ ] Flush the toilet and confirm the black tank valve operates smoothly
- [ ] Check tire age via DOT date code (not just tread) — RV tires age out well before they wear out
- [ ] Test all house batteries under a real load, and check age/type (lead-acid vs. lithium)
- [ ] Confirm there's a stable, usable work surface for a two-monitor setup, and where it would mount
- [ ] Count actual forward-facing, belted seating positions for two car seats
- [ ] Check awning fabric and arms for tears, sag, or prior wind damage
- [ ] Verify hitch/rear bumper rating in writing if you plan to mount a bike rack there
- [ ] Get full maintenance/service records if this is a private-party sale
- [ ] Schedule (or budget roughly $400-1,500, often $700-1,500+ for certified NRVIA motorhome inspections — verify current pricing) a certified NRVIA inspection before final offer

### Departure Checklist (every time you leave a campsite)

- [ ] Antenna/satellite dish lowered
- [ ] TV/cable and any external Wi-Fi booster disconnected and stowed
- [ ] Awning fully retracted and locked
- [ ] Slide-outs retracted
- [ ] Entry steps retracted
- [ ] All exterior bay/storage doors closed and latched
- [ ] Shore power cord disconnected, coiled, and stowed
- [ ] Sewer hose disconnected, rinsed, and stowed (see dump procedure below if tanks need dumping first)
- [ ] Fresh water hose disconnected and drained
- [ ] Leveling jacks/blocks retracted and stowed — nothing left under the tires
- [ ] Interior: cabinets and fridge latched, loose items secured (bikes' worth of gear especially)
- [ ] 360-degree walkaround: check tires, check under the coach for drips, confirm nothing left on the ground
- [ ] One person does final visual confirmation before the driver starts the engine

### Arrival/Setup Checklist

- [ ] Level the coach (side-to-side first, then front-to-back) before extending anything
- [ ] Chock wheels if on any grade
- [ ] Extend slide-outs only after leveling is confirmed
- [ ] Connect shore power; confirm correct amperage (30A vs 50A) before energizing
- [ ] Connect fresh water through a pressure regulator
- [ ] Connect sewer hose with a proper downhill slope to the campground connection; leave black/grey valves **closed**
- [ ] Extend awning only if wind and weather look stable, and only when someone will be present to retract it if conditions change
- [ ] Extend entry steps
- [ ] Set up antenna/satellite if using
- [ ] Do a kid-safety scan of the site: road proximity, fire ring, water hazards, other campers' pets

### Dump Station Procedure

Correct order matters — dumping grey first wastes its rinsing power, and leaving the black valve open "to drain" is mistake #6 above.

1. Put on disposable gloves before touching any hose or valve
2. Pull the coach close enough that the sewer hose sits without excess tension
3. Connect the sewer hose to the RV outlet first, lock the fitting, then connect the other end to the dump inlet
4. Open the **black tank** valve fully; let it drain completely until flow reduces to drips
5. If your rig has a black tank flush/rinser, run it now with the black valve still open
6. Close the black tank valve fully — confirm it before moving to grey
7. Open the **grey tank** valve; the grey water rinses residue out of the sewer hose
8. Close the grey tank valve
9. If the station has a hose-rinse spigot, rinse the sewer hose thoroughly before disconnecting
10. Disconnect the hose from both ends, rinse again if needed, and stow it in its own dedicated compartment (never store it with clean gear)
11. Remove gloves, dispose of them, and wash/sanitize hands
12. Do not dump until the black tank is at least about two-thirds full — a fuller tank flushes solids more effectively than a barely-used one

Source basis: [Happy Campers' dump and flush guide](https://www.happycampers.store/blog/how-to-properly-dump-and-flush-your-rv-black-tank-complete-guide/) and [Unique Camping + Marine's black/grey dump walkthrough](https://uniquecampingmarine.com/blogs/dumping-rv-holding-tanks-series/how-to-dump-your-black-and-gray-water-tanks).

### Pre-Storage / Winterize Checklist (New Jersey climate)

Do this before the first hard freeze, typically mid-to-late fall in central NJ — don't wait for a cold-snap warning to start.

- [ ] Drain the fresh water tank completely
- [ ] Drain the grey and black tanks and give them a final clean flush
- [ ] Drain the water heater tank fully (turn off/let cool first if propane or electric element was recently on)
- [ ] Install the water heater **bypass kit** before adding antifreeze — this is the #1 skipped step and wastes antifreeze while leaving the heater unprotected
- [ ] Use compressed air (40 PSI or less) to blow remaining water out of all lines if you have the equipment, or proceed straight to antifreeze
- [ ] Pump **RV/marine-specific non-toxic antifreeze** (never automotive antifreeze) through every faucet, hot and cold, until pink fluid runs consistently
- [ ] Pour antifreeze down every drain (sink, shower, outdoor shower/kitchen if equipped) to protect the P-traps
- [ ] Flush the toilet with antifreeze until it runs pink at the outlet
- [ ] Check and protect the city water inlet and its check valve — a commonly forgotten freeze point
- [ ] Remove, clean, and store the fresh water hose and filter indoors
- [ ] Disconnect and store house batteries (or maintain with a smart trickle charger) somewhere temperature-stable
- [ ] Fill the fuel tank and add fuel stabilizer if the coach will sit more than a couple months
- [ ] Set tire pressures to spec and consider tire covers if stored outside — sun/ozone damage continues even in cold months
- [ ] Seal roof vents, check caulking/sealant one more time before winter moisture exposure
- [ ] Set mouse/pest deterrents — a genuine and common off-season problem, not just folklore

Source basis: [RV Travel's 7 common winterization mistakes](https://www.rvtravel.com/7-common-rv-winterization-mistakes-rv-tech-double-check-2276/) and [RV Living's winterizing mistakes guide](https://rvliving.com/rv-winterizing-mistakes/).

## Bottom line for this family

The mistakes that matter most for this specific purchase cluster around one theme: **a fixed weight budget (OCCC) that six bikes, full boondocking water, four people, and a work setup are all competing for at once.** Get the real sticker number before falling in love with a floorplan, and don't let "it's a big coach, it can hold anything" be an assumption — gas Class A payload is tighter than it looks, especially loaded for genuine primitive camping. Second, pay for the professional inspection even though Doug is handy — the value isn't fixing things, it's finding hidden water/delamination damage that a driveway walkthrough can't catch on a coach with unknown history. Third, the checklists above cost nothing and prevent almost all of the "how did I let that happen" stories in the forums; print them, laminate them, and use them from trip one — muscle memory replaces the checklist after a season, but until then the checklist is what keeps a good weekend from turning into an expensive one. Finally, budget mentally (not just financially) for the dealer-service-backlog reality — a used gas Class A will need service eventually, and "eventually" in this industry currently means weeks, not days.
