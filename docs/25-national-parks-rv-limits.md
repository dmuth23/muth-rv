# Where It Can Actually Go: National Park RV Length Limits

*As of July 2026.*

## The premise correction, up front

When we talk about "will the rig fit," it's tempting to frame it as a class question — "can a Class C get into Yosemite?" That's the wrong question, and it's worth saying plainly: **national parks essentially never ban a *class* of RV.** We found exactly one genuine class-based rule in the 14 parks below (Yellowstone's Fishing Bridge RV Park requires hard-sided units, for grizzly safety — see the table). Everywhere else, the rule NPS actually publishes is a **site-by-site length limit**, sometimes paired with a **road-access limit** (a tunnel, a bridge, a mountain road) that's a completely separate constraint from anything at the campground.

That's good news and a caveat in the same breath. Good news: a Class C in the roughly 25-32 ft range under discussion (see [The Class C Landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html) — size is still the open question here, and this doc is meant to inform that call, not assume an answer) fits the *large majority* of what's below — it's Class A-scale 35-40 ft rigs that get boxed out repeatedly. Caveat: "the campground" isn't one number. Length caps vary **by loop and even by individual site** within the same campground, and several official NPS pages don't publish a single figure at all — they push you to check the individual site listing on Recreation.gov. We've flagged every row where that's the case rather than guessing.

One more distinction that matters for trip planning: a **campsite length limit** (can you park there) and a **road-access limit** (can you drive there at all) are different gates, and a park can have either, both, or neither. Glacier and Sequoia are the two clearest examples of a road gate that's stricter than any campsite in the park — see the callout below.

### Parks with no drive-in RV camping (exclude from itinerary)

- **Congaree National Park (SC)** — both Longleaf and Bluff campgrounds are **tents/hammocks only**; RVs are prohibited outright, park-wide. Not a length or class rule — a total vehicle-camping ban. [nps.gov/cong](https://www.nps.gov/cong/planyourvisit/camping.htm)
- **Voyageurs National Park (MN)** — no drive-in RV campgrounds exist; all sites are boat-access only. NPS itself directs RV visitors to options outside the park boundary. [nps.gov/voya](https://www.nps.gov/voya/planyourvisit/camping.htm)
- **Cape Cod National Seashore (MA)** — NPS "does not operate any camping facilities." The only overnight vehicle option is a niche self-contained-vehicle ORV beach permit, not a conventional RV site; nearest conventional option is the state-run Nickerson State Park. [nps.gov/caco](https://www.nps.gov/caco/planyourvisit/self-contained-vehicle-scv-camping.htm)
- **New River Gorge — Stone Cliff Beach Campground (WV)** — walk-in tent only, no vehicle camping. [nps.gov/neri](https://www.nps.gov/neri/planyourvisit/camping.htm)

## Our two targets, up close

> **Sequoia & Kings Canyon — the Generals Highway reality**
>
> This is the park where road access, not campsite size, decides everything. The southern approach (Ash Mountain/Hwy 198, through Potwisha and up Generals Highway to Giant Forest) carries hard advisories: over 22 ft "not advised" between Potwisha and the Giant Forest Museum, and single vehicles over 40 ft are **prohibited outright**. Potwisha and Buckeye Flat campgrounds sit right on this restricted stretch.
>
> **The workaround that matters for us:** enter instead via **Highway 180 from Fresno/Grant Grove** and drive south into Giant Forest. That route entirely avoids the Potwisha-to-Giant-Forest chokepoint. **Lodgepole, Dorst Creek, Azalea, and Sunset** campgrounds are all reached this way and are not gated by the 22 ft advisory — Lodgepole and Dorst Creek don't even publish a separate campground-level length cap. Treat any "120 ft max site" figure you see on aggregator sites as noise, not an official number.
>
> Mineral King Road is an absolute no: RVs and trailers are **not permitted** in its campgrounds at all, regardless of length. **Bottom line for a rig in the 30-32 ft range:** come in via Hwy 180/Grant Grove, camp at Lodgepole/Dorst Creek/Azalea/Sunset, and skip the Ash Mountain/Hwy 198 southern entrance entirely. [Source: nps.gov/seki](https://www.nps.gov/seki/planyourvisit/oversized-vehicle.htm)

> **Canyonlands — Squaw Flat and Willow Flat**
>
> Needles District (**Squaw Flat**) caps most sites at **28 ft combined** (vehicle + trailer), reached via the paved UT-211 — a normal approach, not a 4WD concern. No hookups, no water, no dump station. At 28 ft, this is one of the tightest campgrounds in the whole survey — see the fit analysis below, where Squaw Flat and Willow Flat are the first two campgrounds to drop off the list as length grows past 27 ft.
>
> Island in the Sky (**Willow Flat**) is commonly cited at 28 ft too, but that figure comes from third-party trip planners, not an NPS or Recreation.gov page we could confirm directly — **treat it as unverified**. The bigger planning fact here isn't the length: Willow Flat's 12 sites are **first-come-first-served only**, not bookable on Recreation.gov at all, and it fills daily in peak shoulder seasons (late Mar–Jun, early Sep–mid Oct). For a family trip, that means arriving early with no reservation fallback — call the park at 435-719-2313 to confirm both the site fit and realistic arrival time. [Source: nps.gov/cany](https://www.nps.gov/cany/planyourvisit/camp-isky.htm)

## Road-access gotchas that aren't about campsites at all

> **Zion — the Mt. Carmel Tunnel, post-2026 rule change**
>
> As of **June 7, 2026**, Zion eliminated the ranger-escort/tunnel-permit system entirely. There's no more paying for an escort through an oversized load — the cutoffs are now hard and absolute: single vehicles over **35 ft 9 in long, 11 ft 4 in tall, or 7 ft 10 in wide**, or combined (tow + trailer) over **50 ft**, simply cannot use the Zion-Mt Carmel Highway/tunnel at all. A rig in the 30-32 ft range should clear the length threshold, but the practical implication is a routing one: plan to enter/exit via the **South (Springdale) entrance**, not the east-side Hwy 9 tunnel route. [Source: nps.gov/zion](https://www.nps.gov/zion/planyourvisit/large-vehicles.htm)

> **Glacier — Going-to-the-Sun Road**
>
> GTSR prohibits any vehicle or combination over **21 ft long or 8 ft wide** (including mirrors) on the segment between Avalanche Creek and Rising Sun, and vehicles over roughly 10 ft tall risk rock overhangs west of Logan Pass. A family rig in the 25-32 ft range under discussion **cannot drive the scenic road itself** — full stop. The good news: the west-side campgrounds (Apgar, Fish Creek) and east-side campground (St. Mary) are all reachable via perimeter highways (US-2, US-89, Camas Rd) without ever touching the restricted segment. Plan to see Going-to-the-Sun Road via the free park shuttle or a smaller day vehicle, not the RV. [Source: nps.gov/glac](https://www.nps.gov/glac/planyourvisit/gtsrinfo.htm)
>
> *For reference, Grand Canyon's Hermit Road runs a related pattern — shuttle-only Mar–Nov, with private vehicles under 22 ft permitted only Dec–Feb.* [Source: nps.gov/grca](https://www.nps.gov/grca/planyourvisit/rv.htm)

## Fit analysis: how much of this actually fits a 24 / 27 / 30 / 32 ft rig

**Methodology, stated plainly so the numbers are checkable:** we counted a campground as "fits" a target length if it has *at least one documented site* at or above that length, using the **largest length figure documented for that campground** — an official NPS/Recreation.gov figure where one exists, or the third-party estimate flagged "unverified" in the tables above where no official figure exists (those are still counted, per the caveat below, but marked). Campgrounds with **no numeric figure at all** (official or third-party) — Watchman, North Campground (Bryce), Big Meadows, Cades Cove, Elkmont, Smokemont, Army Camp, Dunewood, and Sage Creek — are **excluded from the denominator entirely**, not silently assumed to fit or not fit. Road-access-only entries and "no RV camping" parks are also excluded.

**Campgrounds with a usable length figure: 42**

| Target length | Campgrounds that fit | Share |
|---|---|---|
| 24 ft | 42 of 42 | 100% |
| 27 ft | 42 of 42 | 100% |
| 30 ft | 40 of 42 | 95% |
| 32 ft | 36 of 42 | 86% |

What actually happens as the rig gets longer, campground by campground:

- **Up to 27 ft, every campground in this set has a site that fits** — length simply isn't the constraint yet. This matches the family's own field experience that a 25-footer felt too small in person: length headroom isn't the scarce resource at that end of the range.
- **Crossing 30 ft costs two campgrounds**: Needles/Squaw Flat and Willow Flat (both Canyonlands) top out at 28 ft combined and drop off the list.
- **Crossing 32 ft costs four more**: Mather (Grand Canyon), Sunset (Bryce Canyon), Aspenglen, and Timber Creek (Rocky Mountain) all cap at 30 ft.
- So the honest read of the data: **a rig up to ~27 ft clears essentially everything in this set; the losses are real but incremental through 30 ft; past 32 ft you start trading away a bigger slice of options**, including two of our own named targets' immediate neighbors (Canyonlands drops out first). This is evidence for keeping length modest, not a verdict on an exact number — see [The Class C Landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html) for how that trades off against interior livability.
- **"Fits" means at least one site, not every site.** Many rows (Cedar Pass, Elk Mountain, Devils Garden, Big Meadows, Fruita) are explicitly loop- or site-dependent — a family should always confirm the *specific* site, not just the campground name, before booking.
- This is a sample of 14 marquee parks plus several eastern units, not all 63 national parks or the broader NPS system. Treat the percentages as directionally useful for this shortlist, not a statistically rigorous national figure.

## How to verify any specific site before you book

The numbers above are a planning tool, not a substitute for checking the actual site you're about to reserve. Every time:

1. **Use Recreation.gov's site-level filters.** On any campground's Recreation.gov page, the site list view lets you filter by "RV/Trailer Max Length" and see each individual site's stated maximum — this is the authoritative number, not the campground-level summary (which, as shown above, is often a range or simply absent).
2. **Check for loop-specific rules**, especially generator hours and hookup type, which frequently differ by loop within the same campground (Acadia, Great Smoky Mountains, and Shenandoah all do this).
3. **Call the ranger station or campground office directly** when the online listing is ambiguous, JS-rendered, or simply missing a figure (we hit this at Mammoth Cave, Everglades, and Island in the Sky). A five-minute call beats arriving to find your rig doesn't fit the pad.
4. **Separately verify the access road**, not just the campsite — Sequoia's Generals Highway, Glacier's Going-to-the-Sun Road, and Zion's Mt. Carmel Tunnel are three examples in this docket where the campsite itself would fit the rig but the road to reach it (or an alternate scenic road inside the park) would not.
