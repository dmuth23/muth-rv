# Fire, CO, and Propane Safety — and the Roadside Assistance Gap

None of the other documents in this docket cover what happens when something goes wrong onboard, or what happens when the rig breaks down on I-95 with two four-year-olds in car seats. That's a real gap, and it's the one place in this whole research project where being wrong costs more than money. This document closes it: detector placement and testing, fire extinguishers, a coach-specific evacuation plan sized to preschoolers, and a straight comparison of roadside plans so you actually have working coverage on day one — not a vague reference to "discussed in your other docs" that never gets resolved.

The short version: a 35' gas Class A with propane appliances and one entry door is not a house, and it is not a car. It needs its own safety plan, and that plan needs to exist before your first night with the kids aboard, not after a scare.

## Why this coach is not your house

Three things make an RV materially different from your home in Lakewood, and all three matter more with toddlers aboard:

**It runs on propane, indoors, all the time.** Your furnace, water heater, refrigerator, and stove very likely all run on LP gas, and unlike a house, the tank and the appliances travel with you over bumps, through temperature swings, and get serviced (or not) by whoever owned the rig before you. Propane is heavier than air and pools low — near the floor, in the basement bay, under the mattress — which is exactly where your kids sleep and play.

**Fire spreads faster, and there's less of it to escape.** [RV Travel's analysis of a real RV fire rescue](https://www.rvtravel.com/rv-fire-rescue-reveals-fast-seconds-matter/) notes RV fires leave occupants only moments to react — smoke often becomes dangerous before flames even block the exit, because a coach is small and densely packed with synthetic material (foam cushions, carpet, laminate) that burns hot and smokes heavy. A house gives you three to five minutes to flashover in a single room; in an RV, "the whole box" is a lot closer to "the room."

**Most floorplans give you one real exit plus one emergency window.** [NFPA 1192](https://www.nfpa.org/product/nfpa-1192-standard/p1192code), the RV industry's safety standard (adopted by RVIA-certified builders including Winnebago), requires every RV to have a minimum of two exits remote from each other, and every sleeping area to have two unobstructed paths out — usually the main door plus a designated emergency egress window near the back. That's the legal minimum, not generous headroom. On a 35-footer, if the bedroom is in the back and the main door is up front, that back window is your kids' only fire exit if the hallway is blocked by smoke or flame — practice opening and exiting through it before you need to.

## Detectors: what you need, where they go, and how often to test them

A used Class A should already have propane/CO and smoke detectors installed as OEM equipment (required by RVIA/NFPA 1192 for certified builders like Winnebago). Your job on a used rig is to verify they're present, correctly placed, not expired, and that you test and maintain them going forward.

**LP (propane) gas detector.** Propane is heavier than air and sinks, so these mount low — most manufacturers specify within 12–18 inches of the floor, often near the entry step or the stove/furnace area. [Keystone RV's owner guidance](https://www.keystonerv.com/owners/rv-safety-devices-how-to-maintain) confirms low mounting is standard for LP sensors; don't let anyone relocate it higher "to keep it clean" — that defeats the sensor.

**CO (carbon monoxide) detector.** CO is close to the same density as air, so placement guidance varies by manufacturer — some units go about 5 feet up a wall, others go on the ceiling near sleeping areas. Follow the installed unit's manual, and make sure there's coverage near or in the bedroom, since CO poisoning kills people in their sleep. This is not a hypothetical: [CDC data cited in recent reporting](https://www.forensicsdetectors.com/blogs/articles/rv-camper-co-detector) attributes roughly 70–85 U.S. deaths a year to portable-generator CO poisoning, and RVs/campers are a disproportionate share — including [two men who died at Talladega Superspeedway](https://blog.goodsam.com/nascar-rv-camper-killed-by-carbon-monoxide/) from a *neighboring* generator's exhaust drifting into their camper. On a gas Class A, your own generator's exhaust, a neighbor's generator, or a tow vehicle idling near an open window are all realistic campground CO sources — materially different from a house, where you don't park next to a running engine.

**Smoke detector.** Ceiling-mounted, battery-backed (so it still works if you lose shore power/coach battery mid-fire), placed centrally per NFPA 1192. Verify it's not the type that's wired only to 12V coach power with no battery backup — some older/cheaper installs get this wrong.

**Testing and replacement schedule — put this on a calendar, not "I'll remember":**
- Test smoke and CO alarms monthly (press the test button).
- Test the LP detector roughly twice a year using an unlit butane lighter — open the valve near the sensor port without igniting it, per guidance from [The RV Doctor](http://www.rvdoctor.com/2001/01/recreation-vehicle-safety-devices.html) and [RV Enthusiast Magazine](https://rventhusiast.com/rvexpert/staying-safe-testing-and-replacing-lp-gas-carbon-monoxide-and-smoke-detectors/).
- **Check the manufacture date on every detector at purchase.** Combination CO/LP units have a finite sensor life — typically 5–7 years — after which they can fail silently (still "look" powered, but the sensor no longer reliably detects gas). Since you're buying used, this is a same-week task: find the date stamp on the back/side of each unit and replace anything expired or undated before the first overnight trip. Replacement combo units run $30–$80 and are a straightforward DIY swap for a handy owner.
- Smoke detectors: replace every 10 years regardless of test results (standard household guidance, same logic applies in a coach).

## Fire extinguishers: type, count, and service

The RV-specific baseline, per [NFPA 1192](https://www.nfpa.org/product/nfpa-1192-standard/p1192code) as summarized by [industry fire-safety references](https://usmadesupply.com/resources/building-codes-standards/fire-extinguisher-standards/nfpa-10), is a **10 lb B:C-rated extinguisher in every motorized RV**, mounted within 24 inches of the primary exit. That's the legal minimum and it's aimed at fuel/electrical fires (B = flammable liquids/gas, C = electrical) — it is explicitly **not** required to carry an A rating, which is what you need for ordinary combustibles: the wood cabinetry, foam cushions, carpet, and clothing that make up most of an RV's interior.

For a family rig, don't stop at the legal minimum:

- **Keep the OEM B:C extinguisher** near the entry door as required, but add a second **multi-purpose ABC extinguisher** (2.5–5 lb, dry chemical) somewhere central — a common placement is near the galley/kitchen, since cooking fires are one of the more likely ignition sources with a family using the rig regularly.
- Consider a small extinguisher near the bedroom too, given the "one exit path" geometry discussed above — you want a shot at knocking down a small fire before deciding to evacuate instead of fight it.
- **Monthly**: visual check — gauge in the green, pin/seal intact, no visible corrosion or damage, still mounted securely (a used rig's mount hardware is worth checking on day one).
- **Annually**: professional inspection/recharge is the NFPA 10 standard for commercial settings; for a personal rig, at minimum weigh/inspect yearly and replace disposable units per the manufacturer's stamped service life (dry-chemical extinguishers are typically rated 6–12 years before recharge or replacement is required).
- Dry-chemical residue is corrosive and messy — if you ever actually discharge one near electronics (including your WFH monitor setup), plan on a full cleaning, not a wipe-down.

## An evacuation plan sized to two four-year-olds

A generic "get out fast" plan doesn't work for preschoolers, who wake up disoriented, may hide from smoke/noise instead of moving toward it, and can't operate an emergency window latch under stress. Build the plan around their limitations, not yours:

1. **Know both exits from every bed in the coach**, and know it means nothing until you've physically practiced it. Walk each kid through opening the main door and, separately, through opening and climbing out the designated emergency egress window (do this once with the rig stationary and safe, ideally at home before your first trip). [Terry Town RV's guide to emergency windows](https://www.terrytownrv.com/blog/safety-first-operating-your-rvs-emergency-windows/) is a good walkthrough of how the release mechanism actually works — these are not the same as a house window, and the first time you touch the latch should not be during a fire.
2. **Assign a "grab and go" role, not a "grab stuff" role.** With two adults and two four-year-olds, the plan should be one adult takes one kid, other adult takes other kid, full stop — nobody is tasked with grabbing bags, phones, or pets on the way out. Pre-stage anything irreplaceable (medication, documents) somewhere with quick single-motion access, but the rule under real smoke is bodies first.
3. **Set a family staging point outside** — a fixed spot a safe distance from the coach (e.g., "by the picnic table" or "at the site marker," something concrete a four-year-old can be told and asked to repeat back) — so a headcount happens immediately and nobody goes back in.
4. **Practice the door-side exit path being blocked.** Preschoolers default to the door they know. Do at least one drill where you tell them "pretend the front door is blocked" and have them go out the back window path with you, so it's not the first time they've considered the alternative.
5. **CO is a no-warning emergency — the plan is different.** Unlike fire, you may get no sensory cue at all. If the CO alarm sounds, the response is: everyone outside immediately (don't investigate, don't open windows first), then call for help from outside. Brief your wife on this distinction explicitly, since the instinct to "check what's wrong" first is the dangerous one with CO.
6. **Re-drill every season**, not just once. Kids forget, and floorplans/gear placement may shift as you add the bike rack, monitor mount, and other customizations discussed elsewhere in this docket — a drill from month one may not reflect month twelve's layout.

## The roadside assistance gap — closed

Doc 09 (upgrades and must-haves) tells you to "lean on roadside assistance rather than a heavy parts tub" and references "Coach-Net or FMCA's plan, discussed in your other docs" — but no other doc actually compares them or tells you what to buy. Here's that comparison, plus the AAA reality check, because getting this wrong means finding out you're not covered at the exact moment you need to be.

### Why your existing AAA membership probably isn't enough

Standard AAA membership does not cover RVs or vehicles over roughly 10,000 lbs — and a 35' gas Class A on an F-53 chassis will be well north of that (loaded weight in the 18,000–24,000 lb range is typical for this class, well into "12-ton" territory). [AAA does offer an RV-specific add-on tier (AAA Plus RV / RV coverage)](https://rvbuyersusa.com/aaa-rv-roadside-assistance-the-complete-2026-guide-for-motorhome-owners/), but it typically caps towing distance around 100 miles and requires you to proactively provide your rig's dimensions/weight so dispatch can send an appropriately rated heavy-duty wrecker — regular AAA tow trucks are not built to lift a Class A. Bottom line: **check what tier you actually have before you need it.** If your membership is the standard household tier, it will not tow this coach at all; you need the specific upgraded RV tier, and even then it's capped and general-purpose, not RV-specialist.

**What this means for you:** Coach-Net is repeatedly cited across [iRV2](https://www.irv2.com/) and owner forums as the most consistently recommended option specifically for larger rigs, because their dispatchers understand RV dimensions and send the right equipment on the first call — a generic tow company sending a flatbed that can't handle a 35-foot, 20,000+ lb coach is a real and reported failure mode, not a hypothetical. Good Sam is meaningfully cheaper and has a broader benefits stack (Platinum Complete adds mobile mechanic dispatch, which can fix you on-site without a tow at all), but is a generalized roadside product, not an RV specialist. FMCA sits in between on price with the weakest trip-interruption and winch-out caps of the three. **For a family that boondocks genuinely (not just campground hookups) and will be far from services with two small kids in the vehicle, the RV-specialist dispatch quality of Coach-Net is worth the premium over Good Sam's lower price** — the failure mode you're insuring against isn't "no tow shows up," it's "the wrong tow truck shows up and now you wait hours for the right one." If budget is tight, Good Sam Platinum Complete is a legitimate second choice, especially since its mobile-mechanic benefit may resolve issues without ever needing a tow.

### What actually happens after a blowout or breakdown, with kids in the coach

Be concrete about this because it changes how you plan the trip, not just which card is in your wallet:

- **Tire blowout on the F-53 (very likely on a used gas Class A given tire age):** F-53 owners on iRV2 consistently point to blowout-induced loss of steering control as the scenario that actually kills people, not the blowout itself — see forum discussion around the [Safe-T-Plus steering stabilizer](https://www.irv2.com/threads/f53-chassis.2046504/), which doc 09 already recommends installing early ("Stage 1 handling package," $1,200–1,800 DIY). That mod is a genuine safety item, not just ride-quality, precisely because of this failure mode. After a blowout you're on the shoulder with a coach that can't be safely driven further — this is a tow call, not "limp to the next exit" (no full-size spare is standard on most gas Class As; you're waiting for mobile tire service or a tow either way).
- **Where you get towed:** to the "nearest qualified service center" under all three plans above — meaning a shop capable of servicing a Class A chassis, which is not every garage. In a rural stretch this can genuinely mean 50–100+ miles, and the RV-specialist dispatchers (Coach-Net, FMCA) are specifically better at knowing which shops within range actually qualify, versus a generic AAA dispatcher who may send you somewhere that turns you away on arrival.
- **How long you wait:** highly variable — anywhere from under an hour (suburban NJ/NY corridor, good coverage) to several hours in rural stretches, especially if a heavy-duty wrecker has to be sourced regionally rather than being on-call locally. This is the practical argument for the RV-specialist plans: fewer wrong trucks dispatched means fewer restart-the-clock delays.
- **Does the family stay in the coach?** If you're off the roadway on a stable shoulder/pull-off and it's not a fire/CO event, staying inside the coach (not the traffic-side, ideally exit away from traffic) is generally safer and more comfortable for two four-year-olds than standing roadside for an unknown wait — you have AC/heat, bathroom, snacks, and screens. If you're still partially in a travel lane or the coach is unstable (blown steer tire, listing), get everyone out and well clear of traffic first, then wait. This is a judgment call in the moment, but decide the default now rather than improvising it with adrenaline running.

## Bottom line for this family

Buy and confirm working smoke, CO, and LP detectors on day one of ownership — check every unit's manufacture date, since a used rig's detectors may already be past their 5–7 year sensor life and look fine while being useless. Add a second ABC extinguisher to whatever OEM B:C unit is already mounted, and put "monthly detector test + extinguisher glance" on a recurring calendar reminder, not a mental note. Before your first overnight trip with the kids, physically walk both of them through the main door exit and the emergency window exit, and agree on an outside staging point — this is a 20-minute investment that matters more than almost anything else in this docket. For roadside coverage, don't assume your household AAA covers this coach — verify the tier, and unless budget is the binding constraint, get Coach-Net's Premier Coach plan (~$249/yr) specifically because its RV-specialist dispatch reduces the "wrong truck shows up" delay that matters most when you're stranded with two four-year-olds; Good Sam Platinum Complete (~$120/yr) is the reasonable budget alternative, especially for its on-site mobile-mechanic option. Either way, buy the coverage before the first trip — this closes the gap doc 09 opened and never resolved.
