# Fire, CO, and Propane Safety — and the Roadside Assistance Gap

None of the other documents in this docket cover what happens when something goes wrong onboard, or what happens when the rig breaks down on I-95 with two young kids in car seats. That's a real gap, and it's the one place in this whole research project where being wrong costs more than money. This document closes it: detector placement and testing, fire extinguishers, a coach-specific evacuation plan sized to preschoolers, and a straight comparison of roadside plans so you actually have working coverage on day one — not a vague reference to "discussed in your other docs" that never gets resolved.

The short version: a 28-31' motorhome with propane appliances and one entry door is not a house, and it is not a car. This applies to the [gas Class C](18-class-c-landscape.html) this docket now targets just as much as it applied to the larger Class A and diesel coaches an earlier phase of this search considered (see the archived [diesel primer](archive/17-diesel-primer.html) for that history) — the fire, CO, and propane hazards below are effectively identical across coach types and sizes, since they all carry LP appliances, synthetic interiors, and a single main door. It needs its own safety plan, and that plan needs to exist before your first night with the kids aboard, not after a scare.

## Why this coach is not your house

Three things make an RV materially different from your home in Brick, and all three matter more with kids aboard:

**It runs on propane, indoors, all the time.** Your furnace, water heater, refrigerator, and stove very likely all run on LP gas, and unlike a house, the tank and the appliances travel with you over bumps, through temperature swings, and get serviced (or not) by whoever owned the rig before you. Propane is heavier than air and pools low — near the floor, in the basement bay, under the mattress — which is exactly where your kids sleep and play.

**Fire spreads faster, and there's less of it to escape.** [RV Travel's analysis of a real RV fire rescue](https://www.rvtravel.com/rv-fire-rescue-reveals-fast-seconds-matter/) notes RV fires leave occupants only moments to react — smoke often becomes dangerous before flames even block the exit, because a coach is small and densely packed with synthetic material (foam cushions, carpet, laminate) that burns hot and smokes heavy. A house gives you three to five minutes to flashover in a single room; in an RV, "the whole box" is a lot closer to "the room."

**Most floorplans give you one real exit plus one emergency window.** [NFPA 1192](https://www.nfpa.org/product/nfpa-1192-standard/p1192code) — the RV industry's *voluntary* safety standard that RVIA-certified builders (including Winnebago) build to, not a statute you can be cited under — requires every RV to have exits remote from each other and an emergency egress window, so sleeping areas have two unobstructed paths out: usually the main door plus a designated egress window near the back. That's the standard's minimum, not generous headroom. On a 35-footer, if the bedroom is in the back and the main door is up front, that back window is your kids' only fire exit if the hallway is blocked by smoke or flame — practice opening and exiting through it before you need to.

## Detectors: what you need, where they go, and how often to test them

A used motorhome should already have propane/CO and smoke detectors installed as OEM equipment (required by [NFPA 1192](https://www.nfpa.org/product/nfpa-1192-standard/p1192code) for RVIA-certified builders like Winnebago). *Law vs. guidance:* this is the industry's voluntary construction standard the coach was built to, not a statute you can be cited under for a missing or dead detector — which is exactly why verifying and maintaining them yourself matters. Your job on a used rig is to verify they're present, correctly placed, not expired, and that you test and maintain them going forward.

**LP (propane) gas detector.** Propane is heavier than air and sinks, so these mount low — most manufacturers specify within 12–18 inches of the floor, often near the entry step or the stove/furnace area. [Keystone RV's owner guidance](https://www.keystonerv.com/owners/rv-safety-devices-how-to-maintain) confirms low mounting is standard for LP sensors; don't let anyone relocate it higher "to keep it clean" — that defeats the sensor.

**CO (carbon monoxide) detector.** CO is close to the same density as air, so placement guidance varies by manufacturer — some units go about 5 feet up a wall, others go on the ceiling near sleeping areas. Follow the installed unit's manual, and make sure there's coverage near or in the bedroom, since CO poisoning kills people in their sleep. (What safety experts recommend, separate from any detector rule: the [CPSC advises running a portable generator outdoors and at least 20 feet from the coach with exhaust pointed away from doors and windows](https://www.cpsc.gov/Newsroom/News-Releases/2022/CPSC-Releases-New-Report-on-Carbon-Monoxide-CO-Fatalities-Urges-Generator-Safety-in-New-PSA) — a detector is a last line of defense, not a substitute for distance.) This is not a hypothetical: the [U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC)](https://www.cpsc.gov/Newsroom/News-Releases/2022/CPSC-Releases-New-Report-on-Carbon-Monoxide-CO-Fatalities-Urges-Generator-Safety-in-New-PSA) estimates an average of about **85–100 people a year** die from CO poisoning caused by portable generators — the 2022 report put the figure near 85, and [CPSC's more recent reporting has the average closer to 100 as the trend has risen](https://www.cpsc.gov/Newsroom/News-Releases/2023/New-CPSC-Report-Shows-Upward-Trend-in-Carbon-Monoxide-CO-Fatalities). Most of those deaths (about 81%) happen at home rather than in campers, so this is a general generator hazard, not one that RVs suffer disproportionately — but the *exposure pattern* aboard a coach is genuinely different: your own generator's exhaust, a neighbor's generator parked a few feet away, or a tow vehicle idling near an open window are all realistic campground CO sources, unlike a house where you don't park next to running engines. The failure mode is real — see [two men who died at Talladega Superspeedway](https://blog.goodsam.com/nascar-rv-camper-killed-by-carbon-monoxide/) from a *neighboring* generator's exhaust drifting into their camper (community reporting).

**Smoke detector.** Ceiling-mounted, battery-backed (so it still works if you lose shore power/coach battery mid-fire), placed centrally per NFPA 1192. Verify it's not the type that's wired only to 12V coach power with no battery backup — some older/cheaper installs get this wrong.

**Testing and replacement schedule — put this on a calendar, not "I'll remember":**
- Test smoke and CO alarms monthly (press the test button).
- Test the LP detector roughly twice a year using an unlit butane lighter — open the valve near the sensor port without igniting it, per guidance from [The RV Doctor](http://www.rvdoctor.com/2001/01/recreation-vehicle-safety-devices.html) and [RV Enthusiast Magazine](https://rventhusiast.com/rvexpert/staying-safe-testing-and-replacing-lp-gas-carbon-monoxide-and-smoke-detectors/).
- **Check the manufacture date on every detector at purchase.** Combination CO/LP units have a finite sensor life — typically 5–7 years — after which they can fail silently (still "look" powered, but the sensor no longer reliably detects gas). Since you're buying used, this is a same-week task: find the date stamp on the back/side of each unit and replace anything expired or undated before the first overnight trip. Replacement combo units run $30–$80 and are a straightforward DIY swap for a handy owner.
- Smoke detectors: replace every 10 years regardless of test results (standard household guidance, same logic applies in a coach).

## Fire extinguishers: type, count, and service

**What the standard requires (industry standard, not a statute).** The RV-specific baseline is set by [NFPA 1192](https://www.nfpa.org/product/nfpa-1192-standard/p1192code), the RV industry's voluntary safety standard that RVIA-certified builders build to — it is *not* a federal or NJ law you can be cited under, but it is the spec your certified used coach was built to. As summarized by [industry fire-safety references](https://firesystems.net/2021/05/03/are-fire-extinguishers-required-on-recreational-vehicles/), the standard requires at least one portable extinguisher **mounted within 24 inches of the primary exit or means of escape**, installed per [NFPA 10](https://usmadesupply.com/resources/building-codes-standards/fire-extinguisher-standards/nfpa-10). Older editions specified a **10-B:C** unit for motorhomes (B = flammable liquids/gas, C = electrical); [current industry summaries report the spec has been updated to a **1-A:10-B:C** unit](https://usmadesupply.com/resources/building-codes-standards/fire-extinguisher-standards/nfpa-10) — meaning a modern-spec RV extinguisher now *does* carry a small A rating for ordinary combustibles (wood cabinetry, foam cushions, carpet, clothing) in addition to the fuel/electrical rating. On a used rig you may find either generation of extinguisher mounted, so check the label.

**What safety practice recommends (guidance, beyond the minimum).** For a family rig, don't stop at whatever single unit the standard specifies:

- **Keep the OEM B:C extinguisher** near the entry door as required, but add a second **multi-purpose ABC extinguisher** (2.5–5 lb, dry chemical) somewhere central — a common placement is near the galley/kitchen, since cooking fires are one of the more likely ignition sources with a family using the rig regularly.
- Consider a small extinguisher near the bedroom too, given the "one exit path" geometry discussed above — you want a shot at knocking down a small fire before deciding to evacuate instead of fight it.
- **Monthly**: visual check — gauge in the green, pin/seal intact, no visible corrosion or damage, still mounted securely (a used rig's mount hardware is worth checking on day one).
- **Annually**: professional inspection/recharge is the NFPA 10 standard for commercial settings; for a personal rig, at minimum weigh/inspect yearly and replace disposable units per the manufacturer's stamped service life (dry-chemical extinguishers are typically rated 6–12 years before recharge or replacement is required).
- Dry-chemical residue is corrosive and messy — if you ever actually discharge one near electronics (including your WFH monitor setup), plan on a full cleaning, not a wipe-down.

## An evacuation plan sized to two young kids

A generic "get out fast" plan doesn't work for preschool- and kindergarten-age kids (yours are 4 now, 5 from March 2027, 6 from March 2028 — plan for the whole 4-to-6 range across your shopping-and-owning window), who wake up disoriented, may hide from smoke/noise instead of moving toward it, and can't reliably operate an emergency window latch under stress. Build the plan around their limitations, not yours:

1. **Know both exits from every bed in the coach**, and know it means nothing until you've physically practiced it. Walk each kid through opening the main door and, separately, through opening and climbing out the designated emergency egress window (do this once with the rig stationary and safe, ideally at home before your first trip). [Terry Town RV's guide to emergency windows](https://www.terrytownrv.com/blog/safety-first-operating-your-rvs-emergency-windows/) is a good walkthrough of how the release mechanism actually works — these are not the same as a house window, and the first time you touch the latch should not be during a fire.
2. **Assign a "grab and go" role, not a "grab stuff" role.** With two adults and two young kids, the plan should be one adult takes one kid, other adult takes other kid, full stop — nobody is tasked with grabbing bags, phones, or pets on the way out. Pre-stage anything irreplaceable (medication, documents) somewhere with quick single-motion access, but the rule under real smoke is bodies first.
3. **Set a family staging point outside** — a fixed spot a safe distance from the coach (e.g., "by the picnic table" or "at the site marker," something concrete a 4-to-6-year-old can be told and asked to repeat back) — so a headcount happens immediately and nobody goes back in.
4. **Practice the door-side exit path being blocked.** Preschoolers default to the door they know. Do at least one drill where you tell them "pretend the front door is blocked" and have them go out the back window path with you, so it's not the first time they've considered the alternative.
5. **CO is a no-warning emergency — the plan is different.** Unlike fire, you may get no sensory cue at all. If the CO alarm sounds, the response is: everyone outside immediately (don't investigate, don't open windows first), then call for help from outside. Brief Sara on this distinction explicitly, since the instinct to "check what's wrong" first is the dangerous one with CO.
6. **Re-drill every season**, not just once. Kids forget, and floorplans/gear placement may shift as you add the bike rack, monitor mount, and other customizations discussed elsewhere in this docket — a drill from month one may not reflect month twelve's layout.

## The roadside assistance gap — closed

Doc 09 (upgrades and must-haves) tells you to "lean on roadside assistance rather than a heavy parts tub" and references "Coach-Net or FMCA's plan, discussed in your other docs" — but no other doc actually compares them or tells you what to buy. Here's that comparison, plus the AAA reality check, because getting this wrong means finding out you're not covered at the exact moment you need to be.

### Why your existing AAA membership probably isn't enough

Standard AAA membership does not cover RVs or vehicles over roughly 10,000 lbs — and a 28-31' gas Class C on a Ford E-450 chassis will be well north of that once loaded: the E-450 cutaway chassis has a **GVWR of 14,500 lbs**, and a loaded 28-31' coach commonly sits in the 12,000-14,500 lb range, comfortably over the standard AAA cutoff even though it's meaningfully lighter than a 35' Class A's typical 18,000-24,000 lb loaded weight ([Ford 2025 E-Series Towing Guide](https://www.vdm.ford.com/content/dam/brand_ford/en_us/brand/towing/pdf/2025-Ford-E-Series-Towing-Guide.pdf); see also the chassis-weight breakdown in [the Class C landscape doc](18-class-c-landscape.html)). A shorter unit on the lighter E-350 chassis (GVWR as low as 10,050 lbs SRW) could in principle land closer to the line — but "possibly qualifies for standard coverage" is not a plan; verify the actual loaded weight of your specific unit rather than assuming either way. [AAA does offer an RV-specific add-on tier (AAA Plus RV / RV coverage)](https://rvbuyersusa.com/aaa-rv-roadside-assistance-the-complete-2026-guide-for-motorhome-owners/), but it typically caps towing distance around 100 miles — and, importantly, [AAA Plus RV also caps the *dollar* value of towing/extrication/tire service at roughly $500 per service call and $1,000 per membership year](https://rvbuyersusa.com/aaa-rv-roadside-assistance-the-complete-2026-guide-for-motorhome-owners/), which a single heavy-duty tow can still blow through even on a lighter Class C. It also requires you to proactively provide your rig's dimensions/weight so dispatch can send an appropriately rated wrecker — a regular AAA tow truck may not be rated to lift even a mid-size motorhome. Bottom line: **check what tier you actually have before you need it.** If your membership is the standard household tier, don't assume it covers this coach; you need the specific upgraded RV tier, and even then it's capped and general-purpose, not RV-specialist.

**What this means for you:** Coach-Net is repeatedly cited across [iRV2](https://www.irv2.com/) and owner forums as the most consistently recommended option specifically for larger rigs, because their dispatchers understand RV dimensions and send the right equipment on the first call — a generic tow company sending a flatbed that can't handle a 35-foot, 20,000+ lb coach is a real and reported failure mode, not a hypothetical. Good Sam Platinum Complete has a broader benefits stack (it adds mobile-mechanic dispatch, which can fix you on-site without a tow at all) but is a generalized roadside product, not an RV specialist. Watch the pricing, though: Good Sam's headline "cheap" number is a **first-year intro rate (~$120) for new members only** — at renewal its RV Platinum Complete plan is [~$239.95/yr regular](https://roadside.goodsam.com/plan-benefits/rv/platinum-complete), essentially level with [Coach-Net's Premier Coach ~$249](https://coach-net.com/24-7-protect-roadside-assistance/) (as of July 2026). So the *durable* price gap between them is roughly $10/yr, not the ~$130 the first-year rates suggest. FMCA sits a bit lower on price with the weakest trip-interruption and winch-out caps of the three. **For a family that boondocks genuinely (not just campground hookups) and will be far from services with young kids in the vehicle, Coach-Net's RV-specialist dispatch is the safest default** — the failure mode you're insuring against isn't "no tow shows up," it's "the wrong tow truck shows up and now you wait hours for the right one," and since Coach-Net and renewed Good Sam cost about the same, the specialist network is close to a free upgrade at the recurring price. Good Sam Platinum Complete is a legitimate alternative — take it if you value the on-site mobile-mechanic benefit or want the first-year intro discount — but don't choose it *because* it looks much cheaper long-term, because it isn't.

### What actually happens after a blowout or breakdown, with kids in the coach

Be concrete about this because it changes how you plan the trip, not just which card is in your wallet:

- **Tire blowout on the E-450/E-350 cutaway chassis (a real risk on a used coach given tire age — see the age-not-tread guidance in [the checklist doc](12-beginner-mistakes-checklists.html)):** the same steering-control failure mode that concerns Class A owners applies to any cutaway-chassis coach — a blown steer tire on a top-heavy vehicle is a loss-of-control risk, not just an inconvenience, regardless of size class. After a blowout you're on the shoulder with a coach that can't be safely driven further — this is a tow call, not "limp to the next exit" (a full-size spare is not standard on most gas Class Cs; you're waiting for mobile tire service or a tow either way).
- **Where you get towed:** to the "nearest qualified service center" under all three plans above — meaning a shop capable of servicing a cutaway-van-chassis motorhome, which is not every garage (Ford dealers that service E-Series vans/cutaways are the most reliable bet, but not every rural Ford dealer stocks RV-specific parts). In a rural stretch this can genuinely mean 50–100+ miles, and the RV-specialist dispatchers (Coach-Net, FMCA) are specifically better at knowing which shops within range actually qualify, versus a generic AAA dispatcher who may send you somewhere that turns you away on arrival.
- **How long you wait:** highly variable — anywhere from under an hour (suburban NJ/NY corridor, good coverage) to several hours in rural stretches, especially if a heavy-duty wrecker has to be sourced regionally rather than being on-call locally. This is the practical argument for the RV-specialist plans: fewer wrong trucks dispatched means fewer restart-the-clock delays.
- **Does the family stay in the coach?** If you're off the roadway on a stable shoulder/pull-off and it's not a fire/CO event, staying inside the coach (not the traffic-side, ideally exit away from traffic) is generally safer and more comfortable for two young kids than standing roadside for an unknown wait — you have AC/heat, bathroom, snacks, and screens. If you're still partially in a travel lane or the coach is unstable (blown steer tire, listing), get everyone out and well clear of traffic first, then wait. This is a judgment call in the moment, but decide the default now rather than improvising it with adrenaline running.

## Bottom line for this family

Buy and confirm working smoke, CO, and LP detectors on day one of ownership — check every unit's manufacture date, since a used rig's detectors may already be past their 5–7 year sensor life and look fine while being useless. Add a second ABC extinguisher to whatever OEM B:C unit is already mounted, and put "monthly detector test + extinguisher glance" on a recurring calendar reminder, not a mental note. Before your first overnight trip with the kids, physically walk both of them through the main door exit and the emergency window exit, and agree on an outside staging point — this is a 20-minute investment that matters more than almost anything else in this docket. For roadside coverage, don't assume your household AAA covers this coach — [standard AAA doesn't cover RVs/vehicles over roughly 10,000 lbs, which a loaded 28-31' Class C on an E-450 chassis (GVWR 14,500 lbs) will exceed, and even the AAA Plus RV tier caps towing near 100 miles](https://rvbuyersusa.com/aaa-rv-roadside-assistance-the-complete-2026-guide-for-motorhome-owners/) — so verify the tier and your specific unit's loaded weight, then get Coach-Net's Premier Coach plan (~$249/yr as of July 2026) specifically because its RV-specialist dispatch reduces the "wrong truck shows up" delay that matters most when you're stranded with young kids. Good Sam Platinum Complete is a fair alternative, but note its ~$120 price is a first-year-only intro rate that renews near ~$240 — about level with Coach-Net — so pick it for its on-site mobile-mechanic benefit, not because it's durably cheaper. Either way, buy the coverage before the first trip — this closes the gap doc 09 opened and never resolved.
