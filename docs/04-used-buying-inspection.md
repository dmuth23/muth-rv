# How to Actually Buy a Used Class C: Channels, Inspection, and Negotiation

Your primary target is a used, gasoline **Class C** in the size range the [Class C landscape doc](18-class-c-landscape.html) narrows down — most commonly built on the Ford **E-450** (or lighter E-350) cutaway chassis — in a **$60,000-$90,000 financed** budget. (Earlier phases of this search looked at Class A and diesel-pusher rigs; that material is archived at [archive/02-model-shortlist.html](archive/02-model-shortlist.html) and [archive/17-diesel-primer.html](archive/17-diesel-primer.html) for reference, but Class C is now the target.) The mechanics of buying used — where to shop, how to inspect, how to check title — carry over directly from that earlier research. The hard part, and the part that actually protects your money, is *how* you buy it. A 10-20 year old motorhome is a house, an engine, and a small marina's worth of plumbing all riding on one chassis, and the used market has real landmines: water damage hidden behind wallpaper (Class Cs have their own specific version of this — the cab-over, below), sellers who don't own clear title, and dealers who will happily sell you a rig with a leaking roof if you don't know to check. This document is the process — where to shop, who to buy from, what a professional inspection actually buys you, and the DIY checks you should do yourself before you ever write a check. You have two young, homeschooled kids and a work-from-the-rig job riding on this decision going right the first time.

## Where to shop: the four channels

**[RV Trader](https://www.rvtrader.com/) and [RVUSA](https://www.rvusa.com/)** are two of the biggest used-RV listing aggregators — think Autotrader/Cars.com for RVs. Both pull inventory from dealers nationwide and let you filter by class, length, fuel type, and price, which is the fastest way to see what a used gas Class C actually costs right now in your region and budget band. Expect mostly dealer listings with some private-party mixed in.

**Facebook Marketplace** has become one of the most active private-party RV markets — often the best prices because there's no dealer margin, but you're doing all your own vetting: no dealer safety check, no standard financing path, and scam risk. Wire-transfer-before-you've-seen-it schemes are common enough that the FTC specifically warns about phony online vehicle listings: if a seller wants payment by wire transfer or gift card, it's a scam, and you should never wire money to anyone you haven't met in person ([FTC Consumer Advice: Put the brakes on phony online car sales](https://consumer.ftc.gov/consumer-alerts/2019/06/put-brakes-phony-online-car-sales)). Never send money before an in-person viewing and a professional inspection.

**PPL Motor Homes** (Houston, with a few satellite lots) is the largest RV consignment operation in the country — [PPL Motor Homes](https://www.pplmotorhomes.com/). It's important to understand PPL is *not a dealer*: you are buying a private owner's RV "as-is," with PPL acting as a paid go-between. Per iRV2 forum members, PPL can accept offers down to 90% of asking price on the seller's behalf without checking back, and will forward lower offers to the seller — so there's real negotiating room, but no dealer warranty or reconditioning safety net. It's worth searching PPL's inventory even if you don't buy from them, because it's a large, fairly transparent pool of consignment pricing.

**RV auctions** (dealer trade-in liquidation, repo auctions) are a poor fit here — no pre-purchase inspection window, "as-is/where-is," and buyers report frequent undisclosed mechanical and water damage. Skip these for a family rig you're going to live and work in.

**Dealer purchase** (Camping World, Lazydays, General RV, independent regional dealers) costs more — forum and consignment-industry discussion describes a dealer premium over comparable private-party deals that scales with the rig's price, and appears smaller in dollar terms at a $60-90k Class C price point than the five-figure gaps reported for larger Class A deals — verify current pricing/specs before relying on any specific number for your class and price point. In exchange for the premium you get financing under one roof, trade-in capability, in-house service, and (usually, though not always) a pre-delivery inspection and a "we-owe" punch-list to fix defects before or shortly after delivery.

## Camping World specifically: what buyers actually report

You toured rigs at Camping World Lakewood, so it's worth being candid about the brand's reputation since you'll likely cross-shop their used inventory. Camping World is the largest RV retailer in the country, and its review profile is mixed — [Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/www.campingworld.com) shows a pattern well below 3/5 stars, with recurring complaints: rushed or skipped pre-delivery inspection, missing accessories at delivery (cords, hoses, tank not filled), slow service departments, and, less commonly but seriously, units delivered with undisclosed prior damage suggesting a rushed post-accident reassembly (see [My Grand RV forum](https://www.mygrandrv.com/threads/pros-and-cons-of-buying-an-rv-from-camping-world.2184370/)). This doesn't mean don't shop there — their scale means real inventory and financing convenience, and individual store quality varies a lot (ask other RVers about the Lakewood location specifically, not just the brand). It does mean: **treat a Camping World "we inspected it" claim as a starting point, not a substitute for your own inspector**, and read the "we-owe" punch list line by line before signing, since getting post-sale warranty work actually completed is the single most common complaint nationally.

## The independent inspection: non-negotiable

This is the highest-leverage $500-1,500 you will spend in the entire purchase process, and it is not optional for a rig this size and age.

**Why a dealer or seller's word isn't enough:** roof delamination, wet subfloor, and chassis frame issues are frequently invisible to an untrained eye and can cost $10,000-$30,000+ to fully remediate — money that dwarfs the inspection fee many times over.

**NRVIA certification** (National RV Inspectors Association) is the standard credential to look for — [nrvia.org](https://nrvia.org/). Their inspector directory has moved to [RV Help's search tool](https://rvhelp.com/blog/how-much-does-an-rv-inspection-cost), so search there for a certified inspector near wherever the rig you're considering is located (you're paying for their travel too, so a rig 200 miles away costs more to inspect than one at Camping World Lakewood).

**Level 1 vs. Level 2:** a Level 1 inspection is a general visual and functional walkthrough. A **Level 2** inspection is the one you want for a purchase this size — it's a deeper, hands-on inspection where NRVIA Level 2 inspectors have completed a 5-day hands-on course and exam, and per multiple sources they typically spend 6-9 hours on-site examining the unit plus another 3-6 hours writing a detailed report with photos, covering roof, walls, chassis, all appliances, electrical, plumbing, slide mechanisms, and a road test evaluation. That report becomes your negotiating document and your written record of the rig's condition on the day you bought it.

**Cost:** figure **$400-$1,200** for most Class C motorhomes in this size range, per [RV Help](https://rvhelp.com/blog/how-much-does-an-rv-inspection-cost) and [RVBlogger](https://rvblogger.com/blog/rv-inspection-cost/) — a Class C's smaller footprint generally prices at or below the low-to-mid end of the wider Class A range those sources also quote. Some inspectors quote up to $1,500+ depending on region and travel. Against your **$60,000-$90,000** financing target (as of July 2026), that's roughly 0.5-2% of the purchase price — cheap insurance against a five-figure surprise.

**Timing matters:** schedule the inspection *before* you're financially committed, ideally with a purchase agreement contingent on a passing inspection (standard practice for private-party deals; ask a dealer to build the same contingency into a sales contract). Be present for at least part of it — a good inspector will walk you through what they're finding in real time, which is worth more than the PDF report alone.

## Your DIY pre-offer checklist (do this before you even call an inspector)

Do this yourself on any rig before spending money on a formal inspection — it filters out the obvious no's quickly.

**Cab-over delamination — THE #1 killer on a Class C, check this before anything else:** the cab-over sleeping berth that hangs over the truck cab is the single most weather-exposed, leak-prone structure on this vehicle class, and its front cap is widely flagged as the notorious spot where water gets into a Class C in the first place ([CanaDream, Used RV Inspection Checklist](https://www.canadream.com/blog/may/used-rv-inspection-a-practical-buyer-s-checklist-tips/)). It sits directly over the cab, takes wind and rain head-on at highway speed, and its seams (where the cab-over shell meets the main body) are a common failure point that a general "check the roof" pass can miss if you don't specifically climb up and press on it. Once water gets deep into the cab-over structure, repairs get expensive fast — hidden wood rot, ruined insulation, and delamination of the luan panel and fiberglass skin, per owner repair accounts ([JustAnswer, Repairing rotted luan/fiberglass delamination on a Class C](https://www.justanswer.com/rv-motorhome/86usm-repairing-rotted-luan-fiberglass-delamination-class.html); [iRV2, Class C with delamination problem](https://www.irv2.com/forums/f87/class-c-with-delamination-problem-554633.html)). **Do this specifically, every time:**
- Press firmly on the cab-over ceiling and side walls from inside, and run bare hands over the cab-over's exterior front cap and side seams; soft spots, bubbling, or flex under your palm mean moisture has already gotten between the outer skin and the structural substrate.
- Sight down the cab-over's exterior panels in raking light (early morning/late day) for the slightest bubble or depression — visible waviness in a wall panel is a tell, not a cosmetic quirk, and a bubbled panel is a walk-away, not a negotiating point ([RecNation, RV Inspection Checklist](https://www.recnationstorage.com/blog/rv-inspection-checklist/)).
- Check inside the cab-over bunk itself for staining, a musty smell, or soft mattress-deck flex — this is where a slow leak shows up first, since it's the highest point on the coach and gravity does the rest.

**Water intrusion and delamination elsewhere on the coach:**
- Run bare hands over the rest of the exterior sidewalls and the main roof cap edges, both inside and out, the same way as the cab-over check above ([RVHelp](https://rvhelp.com/blog/rv-inspection-checklist), [RecNation](https://www.recnationstorage.com/blog/rv-inspection-checklist/)).
- Check ceilings and walls around every window, roof vent, and A/C unit for staining, warping, or a musty smell.
- Pull back rugs/carpet edges near slide-out seams and exterior doors and check the subfloor for soft spots — press firmly with your foot, walking the whole floor.
- If the seller has one, ask for a moisture-meter reading history or let your NRVIA inspector do one — professional inspectors carry moisture meters and will probe walls and ceiling (cab-over included) at multiple points as standard practice.

**Roof and seals:**
- Get up on the roof (safely, or have the inspector do it) and check all caulking/Dicor sealant around vents, antennas, A/C units, and seams for cracking, gaps, or lifted edges — these are cheap to reseal preventively but expensive once they've let water in for a season.
- Ask directly: when was the roof last resealed, and is there documentation?

**Tires — check DOT date codes, not just tread:**
- Every tire has a DOT code on the sidewall; the last 4 digits are week + year of manufacture (e.g., "2319" = week 23 of 2019). This format is set by federal regulation — [49 CFR 574.5](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-B/chapter-V/part-574/section-574.5) requires the last two symbols of the date code to identify the last two digits of the year and the two before them the week.
- *What the law requires:* there is **no federal statute mandating tire replacement at a fixed age** — tire age limits are manufacturer guidance and safety recommendations, not law, for a privately-owned RV. *What safety experts recommend:* Michelin's RV/motorhome technical bulletin recommends **annual specialist inspection starting at 5 years of age, and replacement of any tire 10 years or more from date of manufacture (including the spare) "as a simple precaution even if such tires appear serviceable,"** because it's impossible to predict replacement need from tread alone — rubber degrades chemically with age ([Michelin RV/Motorhome Tire Service Life Guide (PDF)](https://www.michelinb2b.com/wps/b2bcontent/PDF/3_TB_Service_Life_RV_Motorhome.pdf)). Michelin's bulletin doesn't state a fixed 7-year cutoff — it just notes most tires will need replacement before reaching 10 years based on inspection findings; a "7 years" figure circulates as a conservative rule of thumb in RV/tire forums but isn't Michelin's documented position (community rule of thumb, not manufacturer policy). A 30-35' Class C on a dual-rear-wheel E-450 commonly runs 6 tires; a shorter E-350 single-rear-wheel unit may run 4. Budget roughly $1,500-3,000+ to replace a full set if they're past that window, and use it as a negotiating line item.

**Generator hours and chassis systems:**
- Ask for the generator hour-meter reading — most onboard gas generators (Onan/Cummins) are rated for a service interval around 100-200 hours between major service; high hours (500+) with no service records is a flag.
- Start the generator cold and let it run under an actual load (turn on the A/C) — listen for surging, check exhaust for excess smoke.
- Check chassis and engine bay for fluid leaks (engine oil, transmission, coolant, power steering) and rust/corrosion on the frame rails, especially in a rig that spent time in road-salt states — relevant for anything that's wintered in the Northeast.

**Slide-outs and appliances:**
- Cycle every slide-out in and out fully at least twice; listen for grinding, watch for uneven travel, and check the slide seals for tearing or gaps once extended.
- Run every appliance: fridge on both AC and propane (if applicable), water heater, furnace, all A/C units, stovetop/oven, microwave — a "everything works" claim from a listing means nothing until you've personally run each item.
- Test all slide and leveling jack function with the engine off, on battery power alone, to confirm house batteries hold real capacity.

**Engine/chassis and driveline checks specific to E-450-based gas Class Cs:** most 30-35' gas Class Cs ride on the Ford E-450 (or lighter E-350) cutaway chassis — see the [Class C landscape doc](18-class-c-landscape.html#e-350-vs-e-450-two-different-weight-budget-stories) for the full E-350/E-450 GVWR and tow-rating comparison. Beyond the general fluid-leak and frame-rust checks below, the E-450's cutaway chassis has driveline quirks worth checking specifically:
- **Driveline vibration at speed** is a commonly reported E-450 issue — u-joint wear, a carrier bearing needing to be shimmed tighter, and worn wheel bearings are the usual suspects; drive it at 40+ mph and note any vibration that changes when you let off the gas, which points at the driveline rather than tire balance ([Ford Truck Enthusiasts Forums, E450 driveline vibration](https://www.ford-trucks.com/forums/1727833-e450-class-c-motorhome-driveline-vibration-advise.html); [SuperSteer, Why your E450 RV doesn't drive right](https://supersteerparts.com/why-your-ford-e450-rv-doesn-t-drive-right-and-what-to-check-first)).
- **Driveline alignment on stretched frames.** Builders extend the E-450 frame well behind the rear axle to reach 30+ ft, which changes driveline geometry and makes proper alignment more critical than on a stock cutaway — ask your inspector to specifically check driveshaft angle and pinion-bearing play at the rear differential, not just u-joints ([Ford Truck Enthusiasts Forums](https://www.ford-trucks.com/forums/1727833-e450-class-c-motorhome-driveline-vibration-advise.html)).
- **Confirm chassis and GVWR trim on the specific unit** — builders mix E-350 and E-450 chassis even within the same nameplate, and the difference materially affects tow capacity and cargo-carrying capacity; don't assume from length or model name alone.

## Title and lien checks — do this before you fall in love with a rig

- **Confirm the seller's name matches the title exactly**, and if there's a lien recorded, get written payoff confirmation and a lien release plan *before* money changes hands — a private-party motorhome with an outstanding loan is a common source of deals falling apart or, worse, buyers getting stuck.
- **Run a VIN history check.** Standard Carfax-style vehicle history reports exist for *motorized* RVs (they're built on a truck/commercial chassis with a normal VIN) — services like [VINData](https://www.vindata.com/rvs), [FaxVIN](https://www.faxvin.com/vin-decoder/rv-motorhome) (single reports typically run under $10, with multi-report packages cheaper per report — check the current price on-site as of July 2026), and [RVchex](https://www.rvchex.com/) can surface salvage titles, flood/water-damage branding, prior accidents, odometer discrepancies, and theft records; FaxVIN draws on NMVTIS and state registries. This does **not** replace a physical inspection — history reports only show what's been *reported*, and plenty of water damage never generates an insurance claim — but a salvage or flood brand on the title is an instant walk-away for a family living rig.
- *What the law requires:* New Jersey requires you to transfer the title through the [NJ MVC](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/transowner.htm) — a motor home carries the same obligations as any other motor vehicle. The transfer must be done in person at an MVC agency (not by mail), and a NJ or dealer-reassigned title must be transferred within 10 working days of the sale to avoid a $25 penalty; the title fee is $60, or $85 if there's a lienholder (as of July 2026, per NJ MVC). Confirm the seller isn't in a state with different lien-release paperwork requirements if you're buying out of state (common, since RV Trader/PPL listings are nationwide). *What's just good practice:* everything above about matching names and getting a written lien payoff — prudent, not statutory, but it's what keeps a private deal from unraveling.

## The test drive

Don't skip this even though it's "just a motorhome" — a 30-35' Class C on an E-450 cutaway chassis drives nothing like a car, and you specifically need to feel this before committing:
- Drive on a highway on-ramp and at highway speed; E-450 cutaway chassis are known on RV forums for body roll, wander, and a harsh ride compared to a car — some of this is normal for the platform, but excessive wander can also mean worn steering/suspension components needing repair, and pair this with the driveline-vibration check above.
- Brake firmly (safely, empty road) and feel for pulling, grabbing, or a spongy pedal — big concern given the weight involved.
- Test at low speed in a parking lot for tight turning and backing visibility, especially relevant since you'll be maneuvering this into campground sites with kids around.
- If possible, drive it loaded close to how you'd actually travel — or at minimum, ask about GVWR/CCC (cargo carrying capacity) numbers, since ~6 conventional bikes plus a family of four's gear adds up fast and is a real number to check against the specific unit's capacity, not just the floorplan's advertised specs. (See the [Class C landscape doc's CCC section](18-class-c-landscape.html) for how to read those numbers on an E-350/E-450 chassis, and [carrying the bikes](08-carrying-bikes.html) — note you're weighing conventional bikes only, no e-bikes.)

## Price guides and negotiation

**J.D. Power RV Values** (the tool formerly branded NADA RV Guide — [jdpower.com/rvs](https://www.jdpower.com/rvs)) is the standard baseline: enter year/make/model/options and it returns a suggested list price plus low-retail and average-retail values. Use it as your floor reference, then cross-check actual asking prices on RV Trader, RVUSA, and PPL for the same model/year to see where the *real* market is trading — guide values can lag actual market moves in either direction.

Practical negotiation approach for this family's situation:
1. Pull the J.D. Power value range and 3-5 comparable current listings before making an offer.
2. Get the NRVIA Level 2 report **first**, then negotiate off documented defects, not vague "it's old" arguments — an itemized $3,000 in needed roof/seal work and a tire set past Michelin's 10-year replacement threshold is a concrete number a seller or dealer can't easily wave away.
3. On a dealer deal, negotiate the "we-owe" list (what they'll fix before/after delivery) as hard as the price — an item left off the we-owe list often never gets fixed once you've paid.
4. Since you're not in a rush (your window is late 2026 through 2028), use that patience as leverage — sellers and dealers both discount harder for a buyer who can walk away and come back next month.

## Market timing 2026-2028

Multiple industry sources agree on the seasonal pattern: **winter (January-February)** is consistently cited as the best window for used RV deals — foot traffic drops, dealers are motivated to clear inventory ahead of spring, and RV shows in that window (Florida RV SuperShow, etc.) concentrate deals. **Fall (October-November)** is the secondary sweet spot — dealers clearing inventory before winter storage season, per [Great American RV](https://greatamericanrv.com/blog/best-time-to-buy-an-rv-seasonal-pricing-guide/) and [RecNation](https://www.recnationstorage.com/blog/what-is-the-best-time-of-year-to-buy-an-rv/). Broader market context: industry reporting cited by RV retailers describes 2026 as a stabilizing buyer's market after the post-COVID pricing surge, with used values continuing a gradual softening trend. Given your no-rush late-2026-through-2028 window, the practical takeaway is to actively shop the January/February and October/November windows in whichever year you pull the trigger, rather than buying on impulse mid-summer when dealer lots are busiest and least motivated to deal — though treat specific "X% price drop" figures from retailer blogs as directional marketing claims, not verified statistics.

## Extended warranties: the honest verdict

Skip the marketing pitch and look at what actually happens: an extended service contract (often mis-called a "warranty," which technically only the manufacturer can offer) is a bet that something big breaks before the coverage runs out, sold by a company that prices it to win that bet more often than you do. That said, it's not irrational for a family with one household budget and no slush fund for a surprise $1,700 refrigerator, $900 A/C unit, or $1,200 water heater failure — the honest case for buying one is budget-smoothing and peace of mind, not expected-value math.

The real risk isn't the concept, it's the fine print and the claims process: forum and BBB complaint patterns consistently show denials hinging on technicalities (a "covered" leak at a pipe but not at a joint, say), and slow/bureaucratic pre-authorization processes that leave people without a working rig for weeks — [BBB complaints for one major provider](https://www.bbb.org/us/ga/kennesaw/profile/extended-warranty-contract-service-companies/americas-rv-warranty-0443-28109151/complaints) illustrate the pattern. **If you buy one:** read the exclusions list line by line before signing (not the brochure — the actual contract), buy from a provider with a track record of honoring claims on forums like iRV2/Forest River Forums (search the specific provider name before buying), and understand that "pre-authorized repair at an authorized facility" language can strand you far from home. Given that you're handy and plan to do your own upgrades and maintenance anyway, you may get more value from self-insuring (setting aside the warranty premium as a maintenance fund you control) than from a third-party contract — but if the peace of mind matters to your wife and the family budget, it's a defensible purchase as long as you've read the actual contract.

## Bottom line for this family

1. **Shop RV Trader, RVUSA, and PPL Motor Homes in parallel**, and don't rule out Camping World's used inventory (including the Lakewood store you already toured) — just don't take their inspection claims at face value; verify independently regardless of who's selling.
2. **Budget $400-1,200 for an NRVIA Level 2 inspection on every serious candidate**, non-negotiable for a used investment you're going to work and live in with young kids. Build it as a contingency into any offer.
3. **Do the DIY walkthrough yourself first, cab-over delamination check first of all** (then tire DOT codes, driveline vibration test, slide cycling, appliance test) to filter candidates before spending inspection money on obvious no's.
4. **Run a VIN history check** ($9-25) and confirm clean title/lien payoff before any money changes hands — cheap insurance against a much bigger problem.
5. **Test-drive loaded or ask hard questions about GVWR/CCC** given ~6 conventional bikes plus family gear — this is a real capacity question, not a formality. The [toad](19-towing-a-car.html) adds its own weight and towing-capacity math on top of the coach's own numbers.
6. **Time your final purchase to January-February or October-November** within your 2026-2028 window for maximum leverage; you have the patience advantage, use it.
7. **Extended warranty is a personal-comfort call, not a math-driven one** — read the actual contract, check the specific provider's claims-honoring reputation, and consider self-insuring instead given your DIY skills.
