# Total Cost of Ownership: What $100-150k Financed Really Buys, and Costs, Every Year

The sticker price is the least important number in this purchase. Whatever you buy — a newer used gas Class A, an older diesel pusher, or a newer large Class C — will also cost real, recurring money every year: financing, insurance, storage, fuel, maintenance, campground nights, none of which shows on the windshield price tag. For a family road-tripping for years with kids aging from 4 to 6 across the shopping window, and with Doug working full-time from inside it, the annual "keep the lights on" number matters as much as the purchase price. This document anchors everything to the family's actual budget — **a $100,000-$150,000 financing target, flexible for a good deal (as of July 2026)** — covers what that band buys in each of the three coach types on the table, walks through realistic loan terms and monthly payments, and gives straight New Jersey specifics: sales tax, registration, insurance, storage. It cross-links the [diesel primer](17-diesel-primer.html), the [Class C landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html), and the [at-home storage in Brick, NJ](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html) docs where those go deeper.

**A note on what "$100-150k financed" means here.** Throughout this document the band is read as the **purchase price** of the coach (the amount a loan would be written against, before a down payment reduces what's actually borrowed). That's the number NJ sales tax and the monthly-payment math both key off, so every figure below stays consistent with that reading. If a great deal comes in under six figures — which happens often in the used gas and older-diesel worlds — everything here scales down with it.

## What the $100-150k band actually buys, July 2026

The same budget buys very different rigs depending on which coach type you point it at. This is the single most useful frame for the whole search: the money is fixed, the trade-offs are not.

### Newer used gas Class A (2-5 years old)
This is where $100-150k stretches furthest and lands on the *newest* rig. Used gas Class A listings on [RV Trader](https://www.rvtrader.com/Winnebago-Class-A/rvs-for-sale?type=Class+A%7C198067&make=Winnebago%7C2307464) put late-model 34-36' units squarely in the band — a 2022 Winnebago Adventurer 35F has listed around **$144,000**, and low-mileage 2020-2023 Sunstar/Vista/Adventurer coaches routinely fall between roughly **$90,000 and $150,000**. New versions of these same coaches sticker far higher — the new [Winnebago Sunstar starts at $208,885](https://www.winnebago.com/models/product/motorhomes/class-a/sunstar) and the [new Adventurer at **$252,851**](https://www.winnebago.com/models/product/motorhomes/class-a/adventurer) — so buying used in-band means letting the first owner absorb the brutal early depreciation while you still get a nearly-current coach. This is the "most rig, newest, on the Ford F-53 gas chassis Doug can wrench on himself" option.

### Older diesel pusher (roughly 2012-2016)
Diesel pushers are no longer excluded from this search (see the [diesel primer](17-diesel-primer.html) for the full pros/cons). The catch is the entry point: on [RV Trader](https://www.rvtrader.com/Used-Diesel-Pusher-Class-A/rvs-for-sale?type=Class+A%7C198067&model=diesel+pusher&condition=U), used diesel pushers average around **$215,000** and the listing range runs from roughly **$49,000 to $780,000+**. To stay in the $100-150k band you're buying an *older* diesel — think 2012-2016 coaches with some miles: a **2014 Newmar Bay Star**, a **2016 Thor Palazzo 33.3** bunkhouse (300hp Cummins, Allison transmission), or a **2014 Newmar Ventana LE** at the top of the band. So the same money that buys a nearly-new gas coach buys a 10-14-year-old diesel. That age gap matters twice over — it drives up maintenance risk *and* it can bump the lender age cutoffs covered below.

### Newer large Class C / Super C (30-35'+)
A large Class C is the third path, and it splits in two. **Conventional** large Class C coaches (Ford/Chevy chassis, gas or small diesel) in the 30-35' range land comfortably in-band newer — [Camping World's used inventory](https://rv.campingworld.com/rvclass/used-class-c-rvs) shows a **2022 Nexus Rebel 35R (34'11")** around **$140,000** and newer 28-30' Thor Chateau units near **$98,000-$103,000**. A **Super C** (heavy Freightliner/Ford F-550/600 chassis, big Cummins diesel — see the [Class C landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html)) is mostly *above* the band new — [Super C pricing](https://www.rvuniverse.com/listings/for-sale/class-super-c-motorhomes/150021) generally starts around **$150,000** and averages $250,000-$300,000 — but a lightly-used one slips in: a **2021 Thor Magnitude SV34 (35'6")** has listed around **$135,000**. So $100-150k buys either a newer conventional large C with room to spare, or a used Super C at the top of the band. A Class C paired with a towed car ("toad") is the setup that lets Sara drive a smaller front cab — see the [toad options](19-towing-a-car.html) doc.

**The one-line takeaway:** the same $100-150k buys the *newest* rig as a gas Class A, a *mid-age* rig as a Super C, and an *older* rig as a diesel pusher. Newer means less near-term maintenance risk; older diesel means a tougher drivetrain but 10+ years of accumulated wear to inspect for and a possible financing-age problem.

## Financing in 2026: terms, rates, down payment, and the age cutoff

RV loans are not car loans. Lenders stretch them far longer, gate them on the *age* of the rig, and that length is where families get hurt.

**Rates (as of July 2026):** RV loan APRs vary widely by lender and credit tier. Per [Bankrate's July 2026 RV loan page](https://www.bankrate.com/loans/personal-loans/rv-loans/), the overall spread across featured lenders runs roughly **6.49% for excellent credit up to 35.99%** for subprime borrowers. Strong-credit borrowers can still see rates near 6.5%; [LendingTree's 2026 RV-loan guide](https://www.lendingtree.com/auto/rv/) cites a comparable **5.99%-19.95%** working range depending on amount financed, RV type and age, location, and credit. For a $100-150k rig with good credit, **plan on roughly 7-9% APR** from an RV-specialty lender or credit union — and shop credit unions and RV lenders (Good Sam Finance, My Financing USA, Trident Funding, Southeast Financial, Navy Federal) against your own bank, because rates vary meaningfully by lender type.

**Down payment:** most RV lenders require **10%-20% down**, per [LendingTree](https://www.lendingtree.com/auto/rv/). On a $120,000 purchase that's **$12,000-$24,000** in cash at closing. Zero-down loans exist but are risky — the less you put down, the deeper and longer you sit underwater (below). A healthy 15-20% down is the single best defense against negative equity.

**Terms:** secured RV loans commonly run **10-15 years (120-180 months)**, and some lenders stretch to **20 years (240 months)** on larger balances, per [Bankrate](https://www.bankrate.com/loans/personal-loans/rv-loans/) and [LendingTree](https://www.lendingtree.com/auto/rv/). The 20-year term exists purely to shrink the monthly payment — it does not reflect how long the RV lasts as collateral, and it produces far more total interest and a far longer underwater period. **Cap yourself at 12-15 years even if a longer term is offered.**

**The age cutoff — this one interacts directly with the diesel option.** Most traditional RV lenders **won't finance a motorhome older than about 10 years**, and many draw the line at 10-15, per [Let's RV](https://letsrv.com/rv-age-financing-limits/) and [RV Loan Wizard's 2026 guide](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/how-to-finance-rv-over-10-years-old). Specialty lenders reach a bit further — Southeast Financial, for instance, finances units up to about **12 model years** old. Older rigs also draw **higher rates, shorter terms, larger required down payments, and a mandatory professional inspection**. Mileage matters too: some lenders cap gas motorhomes at roughly **60,000 miles** and diesels at **100,000 miles** for financing, per [industry lender summaries](https://letsrv.com/rv-age-financing-limits/). **Why this bites the diesel plan:** the in-band diesel pushers above are 2012-2016 coaches — as the shopping window runs into 2027-2028, a 2013 diesel is 14-15 model years old and may fall outside a mainstream lender's window entirely, forcing a specialty lender, a bigger cash down payment, or a higher rate. A newer gas Class A or newer large Class C sidesteps this cleanly.

**Monthly payment illustrations** (principal + interest only; insurance/tax not included), at 8% APR:

| Amount financed | 12-year term | 15-year term |
|---|---|---|
| $100,000 | ~$1,082/mo (~$13,000/yr) | ~$956/mo (~$11,500/yr) |
| $125,000 | ~$1,353/mo (~$16,200/yr) | ~$1,195/mo (~$14,300/yr) |
| $150,000 | ~$1,624/mo (~$19,500/yr) | ~$1,433/mo (~$17,200/yr) |

A 15-year term shaves the monthly payment but adds years of interest and underwater risk; a 12-year term costs more per month but builds equity faster. A meaningful down payment reduces every number in this table — financing $110,000 instead of $135,000 (a ~18% down payment on a $135k rig) is the difference between a comfortable and a tight monthly line.

**Why long loans go underwater:** a motorhome depreciates fast early (roughly 18-30% in year one for gas Class A, less steep for diesel, per [RV depreciation analysis](https://rvcostcalculator.com/rv-depreciation-calculator)), and a 15-20-year loan amortizes far more slowly than that, especially with little down. The gap between what you owe and what the rig is worth is negative equity — owing $40,000+ on a rig a dealer appraises at $32,000 is a routine story, per [Good Sam community accounts](https://community.goodsam.com/discussions/rvtipstricks/underwater-loan/703824). Your plan already avoids the worst of it (used, buy-to-keep, not upgrade-every-few-years); reinforce it with **15-20% down, a 12-15 year term, and no plan to trade in soon**.

**The second-home mortgage interest deduction:** because a Class A, large Class C, or diesel pusher has sleeping, cooking, and bathroom facilities, the IRS treats it as a qualifying "home" — if the loan is secured by the RV itself, the interest may be deductible on Schedule A as second-home mortgage interest, per [TurboTax's guidance](https://ttlc.intuit.com/turbotax-support/en-us/help-article/tax-credits-deductions/mortgage-interest-rv-mobile-home-tax-deductible/L3FfU5z1h_US_en_US) and a [2026 tax-guide summary](https://247wallst.com/personal-finance/2026/07/11/your-rv-qualifies-for-the-same-mortgage-interest-deduction-as-a-house-but-most-owners-never-claim-it/). Two catches: **you must itemize**, and **you get only one "second home" slot per year**. Treat this as a possible bonus your accountant confirms, not a given in your affordability math.

## Cost deltas by coach type: gas Class A vs. diesel pusher vs. Class C

The purchase price is only the opening move — the three coach types diverge on the *recurring* costs too. Here is the honest side-by-side. See the [diesel primer](17-diesel-primer.html) and [Class C landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html) for the full mechanical picture; this is the money view.

**Fuel.** Gas Class A on the Ford F-53 chassis returns **roughly 6-9 mpg**, per [Fuelly's aggregated owner data](https://www.fuelly.com/truck/ford/f53). Diesel pushers run **7-10 mpg** on pricier diesel fuel — [Lichtsinn RV's comparison](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/diesel-pusher-versus-gas-motorhome/) notes the real-world spread is "an average of 7-10 with either gas or diesel," so the mpg edge is smaller than folklore suggests and is partly offset by diesel's higher price per gallon. A large Class C typically gets **8-12 mpg** (lighter than a Class A), per [industry cost guides](https://www.rvezy.com/blog/rv-insurance-costs) and Class C fuel-economy data. For 4,000-6,000 recreational miles/year the fuel-cost differences among the three are real but modest — a few hundred dollars a year, not thousands.

**Maintenance.** This is the biggest delta. A gas Class A runs **roughly $2,000/year** in basic annual service (less if Doug DIYs), because the Ford chassis is simple and Ford service is everywhere, per [Lichtsinn](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/diesel-pusher-versus-gas-motorhome/). A **diesel pusher costs meaningfully more to service and Doug would shop it out, not DIY** — owners on [The RV Forum](https://www.rvforum.net/threads/cost-to-maintain-class-a-diesel-pusher-yearly.828944/) report averaging around **$2,000-$3,000/year** once tires and batteries are amortized in *(community anecdote — owner forum reports, not a manufacturer figure)*. Diesel oil changes run **$300-$400** (large oil capacity plus fuel and coolant filters) vs. ~$150 for a gas chassis, and diesels add DEF (diesel exhaust fluid — 1-3 refills a year) and periodic fuel-system and turbo attention. A conventional Class C sits **close to the gas Class A** on maintenance (same family of gas truck chassis); a **Super C** trends toward diesel-pusher costs because it carries a commercial diesel drivetrain.

**Tires.** Gas Class A and conventional Class C typically run **19.5" tires**, budget **$1,500-$2,500 for a set of six** ([tire cost guide](https://tooltroopers.com/blogs/tooltroopers/how-much-do-rv-tires-cost-by-class)). Diesel pushers and Super C coaches run larger **22.5" commercial truck tires at roughly $300-$700+ each**, so a full set of six runs **$1,800 to $4,000+**, per the same guide. On any coach type, tires get replaced on a **5-7 year age schedule regardless of tread** — rubber degrades chemically and a highway blowout on a 15,000+ lb rig is a safety event. Check the DOT date code on any used rig ([`04-used-buying-inspection.html`](04-used-buying-inspection.html)); on an older in-band diesel, near-term tire replacement is close to a certainty.

**Insurance.** Class A (gas or diesel) recreational-use premiums for this family's profile land around **$600-$1,200/year** (details below). Class C tends to run **similar or slightly less** — $1,000-$2,500/year is the broad Class C range per [RVezy's 2026 guide](https://www.rvezy.com/blog/rv-insurance-costs), with recreational, few-weeks-a-year use landing at the low end. A higher-value diesel pusher or Super C insures for more than a modest gas coach simply because the insured value is higher.

**Depreciation.** Gas Class A depreciates fastest — good for a used buyer, worse if you sell soon. Diesel pushers **hold value better** ([Lichtsinn](https://www.lichtsinn.com/blog/diesel-pusher-versus-gas-motorhome/)), so an older in-band diesel may lose less going forward, partly offsetting its higher service cost. This is a genuine trade-off, not a verdict — the diesel is a defensible choice for a buy-and-hold family that wants a tougher drivetrain and doesn't mind shop-only service.

## Insurance: recreational family use, not full-time

Because this is a family recreational rig — not a full-time residence — insurance costs land on the lower end of each class's range.

Class A motorhomes are the most expensive RV class to insure generally, running **$1,150-$2,500+/year** for typical use, per [Casey Insurance](https://caseyinsurancecompanies.com/blog/average-class-a-motorhome-insurance-costs-guide/) and [Roamly](https://www.roamly.com/learning-center/how-much-does-class-a-rv-insurance-cost). But **recreational users who camp only a few weeks a year — exactly this family's profile — often pay closer to $300-$800/year**, per [RVezy's 2026 cost guide](https://www.rvezy.com/blog/rv-insurance-costs). Realistic planning number: **$600-$1,200/year**, scaling with the rig's value (a $140k diesel pusher insures for more than a $95k gas coach), your driving record, and coverage level.

Two things reliably lower the premium: **storing the RV in a locked, covered facility for 4+ months a year can earn a 25-53% storage discount**, and bundling with an existing auto/home carrier, a safe-driving course, or "storage suspension" coverage (comprehensive-only while it sits idle over winter) can knock another 10-35% off, per the same sources. Get quotes from at least 3 carriers (Progressive, GEICO, National General, and an RV specialist like Roamly or Foremost) — RV pricing varies more between carriers than auto insurance does.

**NJ-specific note:** New Jersey requires proof of insurance at registration and the vehicle must pass inspection within 14 days of registering, per the [NJ MVC's motorhome page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/motorhomes.htm) — line up insurance before you go to the MVC, not after.

## New Jersey registration, title, and sales tax

New Jersey's process is straightforward, but the sales tax bite is real and due at registration, not at the point of sale.

**Sales tax (what the law requires):** New Jersey charges **6.625% sales/use tax on the purchase price** of a new or used vehicle, including motorhomes, per the [NJ Division of Taxation](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/moveqa.shtml). Critically, **the buyer pays this directly to the MVC at registration** when buying private-party, not to the seller — so budget the full 6.625% as cash due at the counter. On the $100-150k band that's a substantial number:

| Purchase price | 6.625% NJ sales tax |
|---|---|
| $100,000 | $6,625 |
| $125,000 | $8,281 |
| $150,000 | $9,938 |

Dealers typically fold this into the deal paperwork and financing, but the rate is identical either way.

**Registration fee (verified July 2026):** despite the coach's size, NJ registration is cheap — motorhomes register at the highest **passenger** weight class, and per the [NJ MVC registration and title fee schedule](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/regfees.htm), a heavy vehicle over 3,500 lbs (Weight Class 8) costs **$84.00/year if within 2 model years, $71.50/year if older**. That's a pleasant surprise — a 15,000+ lb motorhome registers for roughly the price of a mid-size car, not a commercial truck. Confirm your exact figure with the [MVC's fee calculator](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/regfee/index.html).

**Title and lien fees:** title transfer is **$60**, or **$85 with a lien** (i.e., financing it), per the [NJ MVC](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/regfees.htm). Registration transfer is $4.50.

**Required documents:** title or Manufacturer's Certificate of Origin, completed application, valid driver's license, proof of insurance, odometer reading. If buying private-party, get a proper bill of sale and confirm clean title before paying — see [`04-used-buying-inspection.html`](04-used-buying-inspection.html).

**Inspection:** New Jersey requires the vehicle be inspected within **14 days of registration**, per the [NJ MVC motorhome page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/motorhomes.htm). Budget a same-week trip to an inspection station into the purchase timeline.

## Storage: driveway in Brick first, paid storage as the fallback

The family's plan is to keep the rig **at home — likely the driveway, outside, in Brick, NJ**. Whether that's actually allowed is a local-ordinance question, and the answer drives this whole cost line, so it gets its own document: **[`20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html`](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html)** covers Brick Township's specific rules on RV parking (size limits, setbacks, screening, and whether a 30-35' Class A is permitted on a residential driveway at all). Read that before assuming free home storage — many NJ towns restrict or prohibit long-term RV parking on residential property, and Brick's code may too.

**If home storage isn't permitted (or the rig doesn't fit the lot),** paid storage becomes a real recurring cost. New Jersey storage rates by type, per [storage-cost surveys](https://homeguide.com/costs/rv-storage-cost) and NJ listings:

- **Outdoor, uncovered:** $30-$50/month — cheapest, full weather exposure; fine with a good cover and diligent roof/seal upkeep.
- **Outdoor, covered (carport-style):** $30-$100/month — meaningfully extends roof-seal and UV life for a modest premium.
- **Indoor:** $50-$125+/month — best protection, real premium, and often a waitlist near the Shore/Central Jersey corridor in summer.

**Planning number if you end up paying:** **$50-$100/month ($600-$1,200/year)** for a solid covered-outdoor or basic indoor spot near Brick. If the driveway works, this line drops to near zero — which is exactly why doc 20 matters.

## Fuel math: what the miles actually cost

At a round **$3.50/gallon** (adjust for real prices when you buy — this is illustrative), across the mpg ranges of the three coach types:

| Annual mileage | Gas A @ 6 mpg | Mid @ 7.5 mpg | Class C @ 10 mpg |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3,000 mi/yr | $1,750 | $1,400 | $1,050 |
| 6,000 mi/yr | $3,500 | $2,800 | $2,100 |
| 10,000 mi/yr | $5,833 | $4,667 | $3,500 |

For a family doing a handful of road trips plus some longer summer travel, **3,000-6,000 miles/year and a $1,400-$3,500 annual fuel budget** is realistic. Diesel's per-mile fuel cost lands between the gas-Class-A and Class-C columns once you account for diesel fuel typically costing more per gallon than regular gas — the mpg edge and the fuel-price penalty roughly wash for recreational mileage.

## Annual maintenance: the reality, not the brochure

This is the category new RV owners most reliably underbudget, because an RV is simultaneously a vehicle, a house, and a boat (plumbing, roof membrane, propane systems) — all three age and need service independently, on *any* coach type.

**Overall annual budget:** plan **$3,000-$7,000/year** for a Class A, per [RV Life Guide](https://rv-life-guide.com/rv-maintenance-costs/) — this *includes* amortized big-ticket items like tires. A widely-echoed discipline worth adopting: **take your estimated annual maintenance cost, add a 20% buffer, and divide by 12** to fund a monthly reserve rather than treating repairs as surprises, per [OGRVS](https://ogrvs.com/rv-maintenance-costs-what-youll-really-spend/).

**Specific line items (gas Class A / conventional Class C):**
- **Chassis service (oil, lube, brakes):** $150-$300 per visit, roughly annually or every 5,000-7,500 miles — work Doug can DIY on a gas chassis.
- **Roof reseal and inspection:** DIY (Dicor lap sealant) **$50-$200 in materials** twice a year; professional annual inspection/reseal **$250-$600 at a dealer, $150-$400 mobile**, per [RV Life Guide](https://rv-life-guide.com/rv-maintenance-costs/). Highest-leverage preventive item on the whole list — a neglected roof seal is the #1 cause of the water-intrusion damage covered in [`04-used-buying-inspection.html`](04-used-buying-inspection.html).
- **Tires:** $1,500-$2,500 for a set of six (19.5") — replaced on a 5-7 year age schedule regardless of tread.
- **Generator service:** $150-$500+/year (oil, filters, plugs on a gas Onan-type unit) — non-negotiable given genuine boondocking plans.
- **Winterizing (NJ-specific):** Central NJ gets hard freezes, so winterizing before the rig sits idle is mandatory. Professional winterizing runs **$85-$150** (a NJ shop quoted $125 plus antifreeze, per [Get RV Winterizing](https://getrvwinterizing.com/s/nj/rv-and-motorhome-winterizing)), dewinterizing $75-$200 professionally or $80-$100 DIY. Doug can likely do both himself for under $100/year.

**Diesel-specific additions** *(would be shop-serviced — Doug would not DIY a diesel)*: **oil changes $300-$400** (large capacity, extra filters), **22.5" tires $1,800-$4,000/set**, **DEF** (1-3 refills/year), plus periodic fuel-system, turbo, and air-brake attention. Owners on [The RV Forum](https://www.rvforum.net/threads/cost-to-maintain-class-a-diesel-pusher-yearly.828944/) report all-in annuals of roughly **$2,000-$3,000 once tires/batteries are amortized** *(community anecdote)*.

**Realistic all-in annual maintenance for this family:**
- **Gas Class A / conventional Class C:** **$2,500-$4,500/year** (lower than the general $3,000-7,000 range because Doug DIYs winterizing and basic service), higher in a tire-replacement or major-appliance year.
- **Diesel pusher / Super C:** **$3,000-$5,000+/year** — shop labor rates, pricier oil/tires, and, on an older in-band diesel, a higher probability of a big-ticket item (injectors, turbo, air-brake work) coming due.

## Campground and membership costs for a hookups-mostly family

Given the plan — mostly campgrounds with electric/water hookups, with genuine boondocking capability as backup — here's what nights cost and which memberships pay for themselves.

**Nightly rates:** private campgrounds (KOA and similar) average **$40-$80/night** nationally, with **KOA Holiday** (the family-friendly tier) typically **$55-$75/night for a full-hookup site**, per [RVmapper's KOA guide](https://www.rvmapper.com/blog/koa-campgrounds-koa-rewards-guide) and [Camper Report](https://camperreport.com/average-koa-campground-costs-a-guide-with-30-example-prices/). State and county parks run cheaper — **$25-$45/night** — but with fewer amenities and stiffer summer-weekend booking competition, which matters on a school-calendar schedule.

**Membership fit for this family** (weekend and week-long trips, hookups-mostly, occasional real boondocking), per [RVmapper's 2026 comparison](https://www.rvmapper.com/blog/rv-campground-membership-comparison) and [Escapees](https://www.escapees.com/blog/the-best-campground-discount-programs-and-camping-memberships-for-rvers-in-2025):

- **Good Sam ($30/year) — worth it.** 10% off at a large network plus fuel discounts at Pilot/Flying J. Pays for itself in one or two stays.
- **Passport America (~$44/year) — worth it for this pattern.** 50% off at participating campgrounds, though often weekday/2-night-max restricted — which suits a family traveling midweek around a work-from-the-rig schedule.
- **Harvest Hosts + Boondockers Welcome (~$180/year combined) — a genuine fit** given the boondocking requirement. Farms, wineries, museums, and private driveways for free or low-cost overnights — builds real boondocking experience in low-stakes settings. See [`07-power-boondocking.html`](07-power-boondocking.html) for the systems that make dry camping comfortable with kids aboard.
- **Thousand Trails ($400-$800+ entry) — likely NOT worth it initially.** Pays off at 30-60+ nights/year at their parks. Skip until a season or two of real travel shows you're consistently near them.
- **KOA Rewards (free)** — no downside; a minor bonus.

**Start with Good Sam + Passport America (~$75/year)** in year one, add Harvest Hosts/Boondockers Welcome once you're exercising the boondocking capability, skip Thousand Trails until your routes justify it.

## Bottom line: a realistic year of ownership

Add it up for a realistic year on a **$120,000 rig, financed with ~15% down (~$102,000 financed) at 8% over 15 years**, doing 4,000-6,000 recreational miles. The loan payment now dominates — at this budget it is by far the biggest line, which is exactly why the down payment and term choices above matter so much.

| Category | Annual estimate |
|---|---|
| Loan payment ($102k financed @ 8%, 15 yr) | ~$11,700 |
| Insurance | $600-$1,200 |
| Storage (driveway in Brick ≈ $0; paid fallback) | $0-$1,200 |
| Fuel (5,000 mi @ 7.5 mpg, $3.50/gal) | ~$2,333 |
| Maintenance (gas A; diesel/Super C runs higher) | $2,500-$4,500 |
| Memberships (Good Sam + Passport America) | ~$75 |
| Campground nights (20 nights/yr @ $60, less discounts) | ~$1,000-$1,100 |
| **Total recurring annual cost** | **~$18,200-$22,100** |

If home storage in Brick works out, the low end drops toward **~$18,200**; a higher balance, a diesel's service premium, or paid storage push toward the high end and beyond. A shorter 12-year term raises the loan line to ~$13,000/year but builds equity faster.

**One-time, purchase-adjacent costs** on top of that: NJ sales tax (6.625% — **$6,625-$9,938** on the $100-150k band), title/lien fee (**$60-$85**), first-year registration (**~$72-$84**), the down payment (**$12,000-$24,000** cash), and a pre-purchase inspection (**$400-$1,500**, covered in [`04-used-buying-inspection.html`](04-used-buying-inspection.html)).

None of this is a reason to reconsider — a used motorhome in this band is a genuinely sound choice for the family's plan. But going in with the honest **~$18,000-$22,000/year** "keep it running and stored" number, *on top of* the loan payment and the cash needed at closing, means budgeting for the RV like the second-vehicle-plus-part-time-house it actually is — and choosing the coach type (newer gas, older diesel, newer large C) with those recurring deltas in full view. The [rent-before-you-buy](13-rent-before-you-buy.html) plan is the cheapest way to pressure-test all of it before the money is committed.
