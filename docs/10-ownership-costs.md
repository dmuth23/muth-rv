# Total Cost of Ownership: The Real Numbers Behind a Used Gas Class A

The sticker price is the least important number in this purchase. A used 30-35' gas Class A in the realistic $55,000-$110,000 band will also cost real, recurring money every year — financing, insurance, storage, fuel, maintenance, campground nights — none of which shows on the windshield price tag at Camping World. For a family road-tripping for years with two young kids, and with Doug working full-time from inside it, the annual "keep the lights on" number matters as much as the purchase price. This document covers every recurring cost band with real figures, flags the two ways families most often get hurt financially (loans that go underwater, and treating maintenance as a surprise instead of a budget line), and gives straight New Jersey specifics: sales tax, registration, storage, insurance. A used gas Class A is a genuinely sound choice for this family's plan — but going in with real numbers beats going in optimistic.

## Purchase price bands and the depreciation curve

Gas Class A motorhomes depreciate faster than almost any other RV category, and that's actually good news for a used buyer: someone else already ate the worst of the loss.

New gas Class A units in the Sunstar/Vista/Adventurer size and class range from roughly $150,000-$250,000+ — the new [Winnebago Sunstar starts at $208,885](https://www.winnebago.com/models/product/motorhomes/class-a/sunstar) and the new Adventurer at $252,851. Nobody in this family's position should be paying that; it's included only to show how steep the fall is from there.

**The depreciation curve, per multiple RV valuation sources:**
- **Year 1:** 18-30% loss — Class A gas units depreciate the fastest of any major RV segment in year one, per [RV cost calculator analysis](https://rvcostcalculator.com/rv-depreciation-calculator) and [dealer wholesale data](https://dealerbackstock.com/how-rvs-actually-depreciate-by-category-the-data-every-dealer-should-know-before-buying-wholesale/).
- **Years 2-5:** roughly 6-8% per year on the remaining value. An $85,000 new unit is worth about $68,000 after year one and roughly $45,000 by year five, per the same sources.
- **After year 5:** depreciation slows to roughly 5-7% annually and flattens further from there.

This is why the strategic move — echoed across dealer-side and consumer-side sources alike — is to buy a **rig that's already 5-10+ years old**, letting the first owner absorb the brutal early drop. Used listings on [RV Trader](https://www.rvtrader.com/Winnebago-Class-A/rvs-for-sale?type=Class+A%7C198067&make=Winnebago%7C2307464) put used Sunstars in roughly the **$47,000-$150,000 range** depending on year/mileage/condition, and used Adventurers/Vistas show similarly wide bands — a 2019 Adventurer 35F listed at $79,000 versus a 2022 example at $144,000. For a 30-35' gas Class A in the 8-15 year age range with reasonable mileage, **plan on a realistic purchase-price band of $55,000-$110,000**, low end reflecting older/higher-mileage/cosmetically rough units, high end reflecting late-model, low-mileage, well-kept rigs. See [`04-used-buying-inspection.md`](04-used-buying-inspection.md) for avoiding overpaying for hidden damage inside that band.

## Financing in 2026: terms, rates, and the underwater trap

RV loans are not car loans. Lenders stretch them far longer, and that length is where families get hurt.

**Rates:** as of mid-2026, RV loan APRs vary widely by lender and credit tier. Per [Bankrate's own July 2026 RV loan page](https://www.bankrate.com/loans/personal-loans/rv-loans/), the overall spread across its featured lenders runs roughly **6.49% up to 35.99%** for subprime borrowers — meaningfully wider than some secondary summaries suggest — with per-lender ranges like LightStream (6.49%-13.89%) and Upstart (6.20%-35.99%). Bankrate states approval generally requires a credit score of **670 or higher**, not a hard 680 cutoff. Strong-credit borrowers can still see rates near 6%, but treat any single "top-tier vs. subprime" number as approximate and verify current pricing directly on Bankrate before shopping. For a used rig in the $55,000-$110,000 band with good credit, expect something in the **7-10% APR** range from a typical RV-specialty lender or credit union in 2026 — shop credit unions and RV-specific lenders (Good Sam Finance, My Financing USA, Trident Funding, Southeast Financial) against your bank, since rates vary meaningfully by lender type.

**Terms:** RV loan terms commonly run **10-15 years (120-180 months)**, and some lenders go as long as **20 years (240 months)** for larger loan amounts. The 20-year term exists purely to shrink the monthly payment — it does not reflect how long the RV will realistically last as collateral, and per [multiple financing sources](https://rvbrands.org/current-rv-loan-interest-rates-and-terms/) it results in far more total interest paid and a much longer period of being underwater. **Cap yourself at 10-12 years even if a longer term is offered** — this is the single highest-leverage financing decision you'll make.

**Why long loans go underwater:** a motorhome depreciates 18-30% in year one and 6-8%/year after that; a 15-20 year loan amortizes much more slowly than that early on, especially with a small down payment. The gap between what you owe and what the rig is worth is negative equity, and per [Good Sam's community forums](https://community.goodsam.com/discussions/rvtipstricks/underwater-loan/703824) and [industry negative-equity guides](https://www.blackseries.net/blog/negative-equity-rv-trade-in-your-options-blackseries.html), it's common on long-term, low-down-payment RV loans — owing $40,000+ on a rig a dealer only appraises at $32,000 is a routine story. Your plan already avoids the trap (used, not new; buy-to-keep, not upgrade-every-few-years) — reinforce it with **15-20% down, a 10-12 year term, and no plan to trade in soon**.

**The second-home mortgage interest deduction:** because a Class A motorhome has sleeping, cooking, and bathroom facilities, the IRS treats it as a qualifying "home" — if the loan is secured by the RV itself, the interest may be deductible on Schedule A as second-home mortgage interest, per [TurboTax's guidance](https://ttlc.intuit.com/turbotax-support/en-us/help-article/tax-credits-deductions/mortgage-interest-rv-mobile-home-tax-deductible/L3FfU5z1h_US_en_US) and a [2026 tax-guide summary](https://247wallst.com/personal-finance/2026/07/11/your-rv-qualifies-for-the-same-mortgage-interest-deduction-as-a-house-but-most-owners-never-claim-it/). Two catches: **you must itemize** (after 2017's tax law changes, most middle-income households no longer benefit from itemizing), and **you get only one "second home" slot per year** — if you already deduct interest on another vacation property, you have to choose. Treat this as a possible bonus your accountant confirms, not a given in your affordability math.

## Insurance: recreational family use, not full-time

Because this is a family recreational rig — not a full-time residence — insurance costs land on the lower end of the Class A range.

Class A motorhomes are the most expensive RV class to insure generally, running **$1,150-$2,500+/year** for typical use, per [Casey Insurance](https://caseyinsurancecompanies.com/blog/average-class-a-motorhome-insurance-costs-guide/) and [Roamly](https://www.roamly.com/learning-center/how-much-does-class-a-rv-insurance-cost). But **recreational users who camp only a few weeks a year — which is exactly this family's profile — often pay closer to $300-$800/year**, per [RVezy's 2026 cost guide](https://www.rvezy.com/blog/rv-insurance-costs) and corroborating sources. Realistic planning number for this family: **$600-$1,200/year**, depending on the rig's value, your driving record, and coverage level (full-timers pay more because insurers treat the RV like a primary dwelling with liability exposure closer to homeowner's coverage).

Two things reliably lower the premium further: **storing the RV in a locked, covered facility for 4+ months a year can earn a 25-53% storage discount**, and bundling with an existing auto/home carrier, a safe-driving course, or "storage suspension" coverage (comprehensive-only while it sits idle over winter) can knock another 10-35% off, per the same sources. Get quotes from at least 3 carriers (Progressive, GEICO, National General, and an RV specialist like Roamly or Foremost) — RV insurance pricing varies more between carriers than auto insurance does.

**NJ-specific note:** New Jersey requires proof of insurance at registration and the vehicle must pass inspection within 14 days of registering, per the [NJ MVC's motorhome page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/motorhomes.htm) — line up insurance before you go to the MVC, not after.

## New Jersey registration, title, and sales tax

New Jersey's process is straightforward but the sales tax bite is real and due at registration, not at the point of sale.

**Sales tax:** New Jersey charges **6.625% sales/use tax on the purchase price** of a new or used vehicle, including motorhomes, per the [NJ Division of Taxation](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/moveqa.shtml). Critically, **the buyer pays this directly to the MVC at registration**, not to a private-party seller — budget the full 6.625% as cash due at the counter if buying from an individual. On a $75,000 used Class A, that's **$4,969**. Dealers typically fold this into deal paperwork and financing, but the rate is the same either way.

**Title and registration fees:** the title transfer fee is **$60 (or $85 with a lien** — i.e., financing it), per the [NJ MVC](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/motorhomes.htm). Registration fees are weight-based (heavier rigs cost more annually) — confirm the exact figure for your rig's GVWR at the MVC.

**Required documents:** title or Manufacturer's Certificate of Origin, completed Application form BA-49, valid driver's license, proof of insurance, odometer reading. If buying private-party, get a proper bill of sale and confirm clean title before paying — see [`04-used-buying-inspection.md`](04-used-buying-inspection.md) for the full checklist.

**Inspection:** New Jersey requires the vehicle be inspected within **14 days of registration**. Budget a same-week trip to an inspection station as part of the purchase timeline.

## Storage: the recurring cost most first-time buyers underbudget

A 30-35' Class A does not fit in a typical suburban Lakewood-area driveway (many NJ towns also have ordinances against RV parking on residential streets/driveways for extended periods — check your town's code before assuming). That means real, recurring storage cost, every month, year-round.

New Jersey storage rates by type, per [storage-cost surveys](https://homeguide.com/costs/rv-storage-cost) and NJ listings on [RVShare](https://rvshare.com/rv-storage/new-jersey/north-jersey) and [Extra Space](https://www.extraspace.com/storage/facilities/us/new_jersey/jersey_city/id/rv-boat-storage/):

- **Outdoor, uncovered:** $30-$50/month — cheapest option, full sun/weather exposure, fine if you keep up with roof/seal maintenance and a good RV cover.
- **Outdoor, covered (carport-style):** $30-$100/month — meaningfully extends roof-seal and UV-related exterior life for a modest premium.
- **Indoor storage:** $50-$125+/month — best protection (no UV, no precipitation, no freeze-thaw cycling on the exterior), but real premium and often has a waitlist near the Shore/Central Jersey corridor in summer since demand is high.

**Realistic planning number for this family: $50-$100/month ($600-$1,200/year)** for a solid outdoor-covered or basic indoor spot within a reasonable drive of Lakewood.

## Fuel math: what 6-9 mpg actually costs

This is where "gas vs. diesel" gets real in dollars, and it's worth doing the actual arithmetic rather than trusting a gut feel.

Gas Class A motorhomes on the Ford F-53 chassis — what all three models you liked (Sunstar, Adventurer, Vista) are built on — get **roughly 6-9 mpg** in real-world driving, per [Fuelly's aggregated owner data](https://www.fuelly.com/truck/ford/f53) and owner threads on [iRV2](https://www.irv2.com/forums/f23/ford-f53-fuel-mileage-increases-617389-2.html) and [Good Sam Community](https://community.goodsam.com/t5/motorhome-group/the-end-all-thread-on-class-a-gas-mpg-with-v10/td-p/1657721). **V10-engine rigs (the most common gas engine in this size class for years) tend to run 6-7 mpg**, while the newer **7.3L "Godzilla" V8** (Ford's V10 replacement, standard in newer F-53 chassis) does slightly better — one owner logged **7.54 mpg average** over nearly 2,800 miles, ranging 6.75-7.97 by terrain, per [Gone with the Wynns](https://www.gonewiththewynns.com/bounder-gas-rv-fuel-economy-mpg/). A rig with the 7.3L V8 (roughly 2020+ model years) will save real money over 10,000+ miles/year versus an older V10 rig — worth asking engine year on any used listing.

**The actual dollar math**, at a round $3.50/gallon (adjust for whatever gas is doing when you're actually buying — this is illustrative):

| Annual mileage | @ 6 mpg | @ 7.5 mpg (mid) | @ 9 mpg |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3,000 mi/yr (occasional trips) | $1,750 | $1,400 | $1,167 |
| 6,000 mi/yr (regular road-tripper) | $3,500 | $2,800 | $2,333 |
| 10,000 mi/yr (heavy summer use) | $5,833 | $4,667 | $3,889 |

For a family doing a handful of road trips plus some longer summer travel, **3,000-6,000 miles/year and a $1,750-$3,500 annual fuel budget** is realistic. This is the honest trade-off against a diesel pusher (typically 8-10 mpg, on pricier fuel) — but a diesel rig costs $20,000-$60,000+ more used and adds its own costs (DEF, pricier oil changes, turbo/injector work), so the fuel-economy gap doesn't come close to closing that purchase-price gap for recreational, not full-time, mileage.

## Annual maintenance: the reality, not the brochure

This is the category new RV owners most reliably underbudget, because unlike a car, an RV is simultaneously a vehicle, a house, and a boat (plumbing, roof membrane, propane systems) — and all three systems age and need service independently.

**Overall annual budget:** plan **$3,000-$7,000/year** for a gas Class A, per [RV Life Guide](https://rv-life-guide.com/rv-maintenance-costs/) — this *includes* amortized big-ticket items like tires, not just routine service, which is why it's meaningfully higher than a car budget. Widely echoed advice worth taking seriously: **take your estimated annual maintenance cost, add a 20% buffer, and divide by 12** to fund a monthly reserve rather than treating repairs as surprises, per [OGRVS](https://ogrvs.com/rv-maintenance-costs-what-youll-really-spend/).

**Specific line items:**
- **Chassis service (oil changes, chassis lube, brakes):** similar to a heavy truck — figure $150-$300 per service visit, roughly annually or every 5,000-7,500 miles.
- **Roof reseal and inspection:** DIY (inspecting/touching up Dicor lap sealant) runs **$50-$200 in materials** twice a year; a professional annual inspection/reseal runs **$250-$600 at a dealer or $150-$400 mobile**, per [RV Life Guide](https://rv-life-guide.com/rv-maintenance-costs/). This is the highest-leverage preventive item here — a neglected roof seal is the #1 cause of the water-intrusion damage covered in [`04-used-buying-inspection.md`](04-used-buying-inspection.md), and catching it early beats the delamination repair that follows from ignoring it.
- **Tires:** budget **$1,500-$2,500 for a full set** (a 35' Class A commonly runs 6 tires, dual rear axle). Critical fact: **tires get replaced on a 5-7 year age-based schedule regardless of remaining tread**, since rubber degrades chemically with age and a sidewall blowout at highway speed on a 15,000+ lb vehicle is a safety event, not an inconvenience. Check the DOT date code on any used rig — see [`04-used-buying-inspection.md`](04-used-buying-inspection.md) — and if tires are already 5+ years old, treat a near-term $1,500-2,500 replacement as a certainty.
- **Generator service:** $150-$500+/year for routine service (oil, filters, plugs on a gas Onan-type unit) — non-negotiable given genuine boondocking plans, where the generator is primary power, not backup.
- **Winterizing (NJ-specific):** central New Jersey gets hard freezes, so winterizing before the RV sits idle is mandatory — skipping it risks cracked pipes and a burst water heater. Professional winterizing runs **$85-$150** (a NJ shop quoted $125 plus antifreeze, per [Get RV Winterizing](https://getrvwinterizing.com/s/nj/rv-and-motorhome-winterizing)), dewinterizing **$75-$200** professionally or **$80-$100 in materials DIY**. Doug can likely do both himself for well under $100/year once he's done it with a checklist.

**Realistic all-in annual maintenance number for this family: $2,500-$4,500/year** in a typical year (lower than the $3,000-7,000 general Class A range because Doug will DIY winterizing/dewinterizing and basic checks), with the understanding that the years a tire set or a major appliance repair comes due will run higher.

## Campground and membership costs for a hookups-mostly family

Given the plan — mostly campgrounds with electric/water hookups, with genuine boondocking capability as backup rather than primary mode — here's what nights actually cost and which memberships pay for themselves.

**Nightly campground rates:** private campgrounds (KOA and similar) average **$40-$80/night** nationally, with **KOA Holiday** (the family-friendly tier, most relevant here) typically running **$55-$75/night for a full-hookup site**, sometimes $65-$75+ in peak summer, per [RVmapper's KOA guide](https://www.rvmapper.com/blog/koa-campgrounds-koa-rewards-guide) and [Camper Report](https://camperreport.com/average-koa-campground-costs-a-guide-with-30-example-prices/). State and county parks run cheaper — often $25-$45/night — but with fewer amenities and more booking competition for summer weekends, which matters on a school-calendar-driven trip schedule.

**Membership comparison for this family's specific profile** (weekend and week-long trips, hookups-mostly, genuine but occasional boondocking), per [RVmapper's 2026 comparison](https://www.rvmapper.com/blog/rv-campground-membership-comparison) and [Escapees](https://www.escapees.com/blog/the-best-campground-discount-programs-and-camping-memberships-for-rvers-in-2025):

- **Good Sam ($30/year) — worth it, low-risk.** 10% off nightly rates at a large network plus fuel discounts at Pilot/Flying J. Pays for itself in one or two stays.
- **Passport America (~$44/year) — worth it for this family's pattern.** 50% off at participating campgrounds, though restricted to weekdays/2-night max at many locations — fits a family often traveling midweek around a work-from-the-rig schedule better than weekend-only travelers.
- **Harvest Hosts + Boondockers Welcome (~$180/year combined) — a genuine fit given the boondocking requirement.** Access to farms, wineries, museums, and private driveways for free or low-cost overnight stays (mostly dry, some partial hookups) — builds real boondocking experience in low-stakes settings before going further off-grid. See [`07-power-boondocking.md`](07-power-boondocking.md) for the battery/solar/generator systems that make this comfortable with two young kids aboard.
- **Thousand Trails ($400-$800+ entry) — likely NOT worth it, at least initially.** Pays off for people camping 30-60+ nights/year at their specific network parks. A family doing periodic road trips is unlikely to recoup entry cost — skip until a season or two of real travel shows you're consistently near their parks.
- **KOA Rewards (free)** — no downside; a minor bonus, not a primary strategy.

**Bottom recommendation on memberships:** start with **Good Sam + Passport America (~$75/year combined)** in year one — cheap, flexible, no real downside — and add **Harvest Hosts/Boondockers Welcome** once you're ready to actually exercise the boondocking capability you're building into the rig. Skip Thousand Trails unless a season of real trips shows your routes consistently hit their parks.

## Bottom line for this family

Add it up for a realistic year of ownership on a $70,000-$90,000 used gas Class A, financed with a healthy down payment over 10-12 years, doing 4,000-6,000 recreational miles a year:

| Category | Annual estimate |
|---|---|
| Loan payment (e.g., $65k financed @ 8%, 12 yr) | ~$8,400 |
| Insurance | $600-$1,200 |
| Storage | $600-$1,200 |
| Fuel (5,000 mi @ 7.5 mpg, $3.50/gal) | ~$2,333 |
| Maintenance (incl. amortized tires/roof) | $2,500-$4,500 |
| Memberships (Good Sam + Passport America) | ~$75 |
| Campground nights (say 20 nights/yr @ $60, minus discounts) | ~$1,000-$1,100 |
| **Total recurring annual cost** | **~$15,500-$18,800** |

That's before the one-time purchase-adjacent costs: NJ sales tax (6.625% — **$4,600-$6,000** on a $70-90k purchase), title/registration (~$60-85 plus weight-based annual fee), and a pre-purchase inspection ($400-$1,500, covered in [`04-used-buying-inspection.md`](04-used-buying-inspection.md)).

None of this should be a surprise or a reason to reconsider gas-and-used — it's a genuinely sound choice for this family's plan, and every number above is *cheaper* than the diesel-and-new alternative you've correctly ruled out. But going into the purchase with the honest ~$16,000-19,000/year "keep it running and stored" number, on top of the loan payment, means budgeting for the RV like the second vehicle-plus-part-time-house it actually is — not like a one-time purchase that's done once you drive it home from Lakewood.
