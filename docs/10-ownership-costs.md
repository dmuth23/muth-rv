# The Full Class C Cost of Ownership: What $60-90k Financed Really Costs, Every Year

The sticker price is the least important number in this purchase. Whatever gas Class C ends up in the driveway, it will also cost real, recurring money every year — financing, insurance, storage, fuel, tires, maintenance, campground nights — none of which shows up on the windshield price tag. For a family road-tripping with kids aging from 4 to 6 (now 5 and 6) across the shopping window, working full-time from inside the coach, this document anchors every dollar figure to the family's **actual, current budget: $60,000-$90,000 financed, used, gas Class C (as of July 2026)** — down from an earlier $100,000-150,000 band that included Class A and diesel. If you're holding an older printout of this docket with six-figure numbers on it, discard it; this page supersedes it. It cross-links the [Class C landscape](18-class-c-landscape.html), [carrying six bikes](08-carrying-bikes.html), and [the rig at home in Brick, NJ](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html) docs where the underlying mechanics go deeper, and it feeds directly into the usage-cadence math in the next document.

**A note on what "$60-90k financed" means here.** The band is read as the coach's **purchase price** — the amount a loan is written against, before a down payment reduces what's actually borrowed. That's the number NJ sales tax and the monthly-payment math both key off. If a great deal comes in under $60k — which happens in the used Class C world, especially on older or higher-mileage units — everything below scales down with it.

## What $60-90k buys in a used gas Class C, July 2026

This band does **not** buy the newest trim of anything. As the [Class C landscape doc](18-class-c-landscape.html) lays out in full, $60-90k mostly lands on **older model years (roughly 2016-2021), higher mileage, or value-tier floorplans** rather than late-model, low-mile units. Two chassis dominate this size and price range:

- **Ford E-450** (the heavier, more common chassis for 28-35' units, ~14,500-16,500 lb GVWR depending on trim) — the workhorse of this segment for decades.
- **Ford E-350** (lighter, shows up on shorter 24-27' units) — a genuine option if the [park-length math](18-class-c-landscape.html) pushes the family toward a shorter coach.

Both are gas-only in this family's plan (diesel Super C pricing starts well above this band and is [archived](archive/17-diesel-primer.html) as no longer in scope). Two engine generations show up used: the older **Triton V10 (6.8L)**, standard on E-450 chassis through roughly 2019, and the newer **7.3L "Godzilla" V8**, which Ford introduced in Super Duty/E-Series cutaways starting around model year 2020. Which one is under the hood matters for the fuel math below — check the build sheet or VIN decode on any specific unit before assuming.

**Reported market snapshots (unverified against a live listing at time of writing — reconfirm before budgeting):** 28-30' Thor Chateau and similar value-tier Class C units in the $60-90k band tend to be 2018-2021 model years with 20,000-50,000 miles on the odometer; sub-27' units on the lighter E-350 chassis (e.g., a Coachmen Leprechaun 240FS or similar) can land toward the lower half of the band even a few years newer. See the [Class C landscape doc](18-class-c-landscape.html) for the full per-model breakdown — this document only handles the money.

## Financing in 2026: terms, rates, down payment, and the age cutoff

RV loans are not car loans. Lenders stretch them long and gate them on the *age* of the rig — both matter more at this lower price band than they did in the old six-figure framing, because a $60-90k used Class C is often *older* in absolute model-year terms than a $100-150k+ Class A.

**Rates (as of July 2026).** RV loan APRs vary widely by lender, credit tier, and whether the loan is new-vs-used paper. Per [Bankrate's July 2026 RV loan page](https://www.bankrate.com/loans/personal-loans/rv-loans/), the overall spread across featured lenders runs roughly **6.49% for excellent credit up to 35.99%** for subprime borrowers. Used-RV-specific paper tends to run a bit higher than the headline new-RV rate — [RV Loan Wizard's 2026 rate guide](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/rv-loan-interest-rates) puts typical used-RV APRs around **5.5%-13.0%**, with marketplace averages closer to **8-9%** in early 2026, and flags that lenders commonly add **0.5-2 percentage points** for a used, older rig versus a new one. [LendingTree's 2026 RV-loan guide](https://www.lendingtree.com/auto/rv/) cites a comparable **5.99%-19.95%** working range depending on amount financed, RV type/age, and credit. **Plan on roughly 7.5-10% APR** for a $60-90k used gas Class C with good credit from an RV-specialty lender or credit union — a full point or two above what the same buyer might see quoted on a brand-new coach — and shop credit unions and RV lenders (Good Sam Finance, Trident Funding, Southeast Financial, Navy Federal, Solarity, Mountain America) against your own bank.

**Down payment.** Most RV lenders require **10-20% down**, per [LendingTree](https://www.lendingtree.com/auto/rv/), and financing more than 80-90% of an *older* used rig's value is exactly the loan-to-value profile lenders price up. On a $75,000 purchase (mid-band), that's **$7,500-$15,000** in cash at closing. A healthy 15-20% down is the best defense against negative equity on a coach that's already a few years into its depreciation curve.

**Terms.** Secured RV loans commonly run **10-15 years (120-180 months)**, and some lenders stretch to **20 years** on larger balances, per [Bankrate](https://www.bankrate.com/loans/personal-loans/rv-loans/) and [LendingTree](https://www.lendingtree.com/auto/rv/). A longer term shrinks the monthly payment but does not reflect how long an already-6-10-year-old coach will realistically serve as loan collateral — **cap yourself at 10-12 years** on a used purchase in this band, shorter than the 12-15-year cap that made sense for a newer, pricier rig.

**The age cutoff.** Most traditional RV lenders **won't finance a motorhome older than about 10 years**, and many draw the line at 10-15, per [Let's RV](https://letsrv.com/rv-age-financing-limits/) and [RV Loan Wizard's 2026 guide](https://rvloanwizard.com/guides/how-to-finance-rv-over-10-years-old). Specialty lenders reach further — Southeast Financial finances units up to about **12 model years** old. Older rigs also draw **higher rates, shorter terms, larger required down payments, and a mandatory professional inspection**. Mileage caps apply too: some lenders cap gas motorhomes at roughly **60,000-75,000 miles** for standard financing terms, per [industry lender summaries](https://letsrv.com/rv-age-financing-limits/). **This bites directly in this band:** a well-priced 2017-2018 Class C with 40,000+ miles, purchased in 2027-2028, could be pushing 10-11 model years old by the time financing closes — worth checking a specific unit's age and mileage against a lender's cutoff *before* falling in love with the listing.

**Monthly payment illustrations** (principal + interest only; insurance/tax not included), at **8.5% APR** (the realistic midpoint for this band and rig age):

| Amount financed | 10-year term | 12-year term |
|---|---|---|
| $60,000 | ~$744/mo (~$8,930/yr) | ~$672/mo (~$8,060/yr) |
| $75,000 | ~$930/mo (~$11,160/yr) | ~$840/mo (~$10,080/yr) |
| $90,000 | ~$1,116/mo (~$13,390/yr) | ~$1,007/mo (~$12,080/yr) |

A meaningful down payment reduces every number here — financing $65,000 instead of $85,000 (roughly 20% down on an $85k rig) is the difference between a comfortable and a tight monthly line.

**Why long loans go underwater — and why it bites harder on an older used rig.** A motorhome depreciates fastest in its early years, and a used Class C in this band is already several years past that steepest drop — but it keeps depreciating, and a 12-15+ year loan can easily out-amortize the coach's remaining useful financed life, especially with little down. Owing more than a rig appraises for is a routine story on long, low-down RV loans, per [Good Sam community accounts](https://community.goodsam.com/discussions/rvtipstricks/underwater-loan/703824). Reinforce against it with **15-20% down, a 10-12 year term, and no plan to trade in soon**.

**The second-home mortgage interest deduction still applies.** Because a Class C with sleeping, cooking, and bathroom facilities qualifies as a "home" under IRS rules, loan interest may be deductible on Schedule A as second-home mortgage interest if the loan is secured by the RV itself, per [TurboTax's guidance](https://ttlc.intuit.com/turbotax-support/en-us/help-article/tax-credits-deductions/mortgage-interest-rv-mobile-home-tax-deductible/L3FfU5z1h_US_en_US) and a [2026 tax-guide summary](https://247wallst.com/personal-finance/2026/07/11/your-rv-qualifies-for-the-same-mortgage-interest-deduction-as-a-house-but-most-owners-never-claim-it/). Two catches: **you must itemize**, and **you get only one "second home" slot per year**. Treat this as a possible bonus your accountant confirms, not a given in the affordability math.

## Insurance: a Class C in NJ, recreational use

Because this is a family recreational rig — not a full-time residence — insurance lands on the lower end of the Class C range, and generally **below** what the same family would have paid to insure a Class A.

Class C motorhomes broadly run **$1,000-$2,500/year** for recreational use, per [RVezy's 2026 cost guide](https://www.rvezy.com/blog/rv-insurance-costs), with some sources putting the average closer to **$600-$1,000/year** for a well-underwritten policy as of April 2026, per [Dragon's 2026 motorhome insurance guide](https://mydragon.us/blog/how-much-is-motorhome-insurance). **New Jersey specifically** runs **$500-$1,500/year for part-time recreational use** and **$1,500-$3,500/year for full-timers**, per [Roamly's New Jersey RV insurance page](https://www.roamly.com/states/rv-insurance-new-jersey) — this family's profile (a few weeks a year of travel, Brick, NJ remaining the primary residence) is squarely recreational, not full-timer. **Realistic planning number for this family: $700-$1,400/year**, scaling with the specific coach's insured value, driving record, and coverage level — this is meaningfully cheaper than the old Class A framing's $1,150-$2,500+/year range, because a Class C on a lighter chassis with a lower insured value is a cheaper risk to underwrite across the board.

Two things reliably lower the premium further: **storing the RV in a locked, covered facility for 4+ months a year can earn a 25-53% storage discount**, and bundling with an existing auto/home carrier, taking a safe-driving course, or adding "storage suspension" coverage (comprehensive-only while it sits idle over winter) can knock another 10-35% off, per [RVezy](https://www.rvezy.com/blog/rv-insurance-costs). Get quotes from at least three carriers (Progressive, GEICO, National General, and an RV specialist like Roamly or Foremost) — RV pricing varies more between carriers than standard auto insurance does.

**NJ-specific note:** New Jersey requires proof of insurance at registration, and the vehicle must pass inspection within **14 days** of registering, per the [NJ MVC's motorhome page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/motorhomes.htm) — line up the policy before the MVC visit, not after.

## New Jersey registration, title, and sales tax

This layer doesn't change with the Class C pivot — the state doesn't care what class of motorhome it is, only its value and weight class.

**Sales tax (what the law requires).** New Jersey charges **6.625% sales/use tax on the purchase price** of a new or used vehicle, including motorhomes, per the [NJ Division of Taxation](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/moveqa.shtml). The buyer pays this directly to the MVC at registration when buying private-party — budget the full 6.625% as cash due at the counter:

| Purchase price | 6.625% NJ sales tax |
|---|---|
| $60,000 | $3,975 |
| $75,000 | $4,969 |
| $90,000 | $5,963 |

Dealers typically fold this into deal paperwork and financing, but the rate is identical either way.

**Registration fee (verified July 2026).** Despite the coach's size, NJ registration is cheap — motorhomes register at the highest **passenger** weight class, and per the [NJ MVC registration and title fee schedule](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/regfees.htm), a heavy vehicle over 3,500 lbs (Weight Class 8) costs **$84.00/year if within 2 model years, $71.50/year if older**. A used Class C in this band will virtually always fall in the older, cheaper tier. Confirm the exact figure with the [MVC's fee calculator](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/regfee/index.html).

**Title and lien fees.** Title transfer is **$60**, or **$85 with a lien** (i.e., financing it), per the [NJ MVC](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/regfees.htm). Registration transfer is $4.50.

**Required documents.** Title or Manufacturer's Certificate of Origin, completed application, valid driver's license, proof of insurance, odometer reading. If buying private-party, get a proper bill of sale and confirm clean title before paying — see [used-buying and inspection](04-used-buying-inspection.html).

**Inspection.** New Jersey requires the vehicle be inspected within **14 days of registration**, per the [NJ MVC motorhome page](https://www.nj.gov/mvc/vehicles/motorhomes.htm). Budget a same-week trip to an inspection station into the purchase timeline.

## Fuel: the E-450/E-350 V10 vs. 7.3L reality, from owner data

Fuel economy is the most-mythologized number in this whole search, so this section leans on aggregated owner logs rather than brochure claims.

**Triton V10 (6.8L), the older and more common engine in this price band.** Fuelly's aggregated owner data across hundreds of Ford E-450 Super Duty logs (spanning years and drivetrain configurations, mostly V10) centers real-world fuel economy in roughly the **7-9 mpg range**, with individual fill-ups on 2018-era V10 Class C motorhomes reported between **7.4 and 9.0 mpg** ([Fuelly: Ford E-450 Super Duty owner data](https://www.fuelly.com/car/ford/e-450_super_duty)). This tracks with the general RV-industry consensus that a gas cutaway chassis under a Class C body returns high-single-digit mpg unloaded, dropping further with a loaded coach, a bike rack, and any grade.

**7.3L "Godzilla" V8, the newer engine (roughly MY2020+).** Owner reports on RV-specific forums are more favorable than the older V10, though still highly conditions-dependent: one 2021 Axis 24.1 owner (a shorter, lighter Class C) reports **11-12+ mpg at 65 mph on flat interstate**, dropping to about **11 mpg on secondary roads**; an Odyssey 24B owner averaged **8.6 mpg** initially, improving to **9.5-9.8 mpg** after break-in; a 2021 Coach House 271XLFR owner reported **12.78 mpg** on a mostly-flat interstate run ([community owner reports aggregated via iRV2, Thor Forums, and Good Sam Community forums](https://www.irv2.com/threads/ford-7-3-v-8-ford-gas-engine.2047053/) — **community anecdote, not manufacturer data**). The honest range across both engines and real driving conditions: **7-12 mpg**, with **8-10 mpg** the realistic planning band for a loaded 28-33' Class C on mixed highway driving, and the lower end likely if towing a bike rack's worth of drag or driving mountain grades.

**What this means in dollars.** At a round **$3.50/gallon** (adjust for real prices when you buy — this is illustrative):

| Annual mileage | @ 7 mpg (V10, loaded/hilly) | @ 9 mpg (realistic planning mpg) | @ 11 mpg (7.3L, favorable conditions) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3,000 mi/yr | $1,500 | $1,167 | $955 |
| 6,000 mi/yr | $3,000 | $2,333 | $1,909 |
| 10,000 mi/yr | $5,000 | $3,889 | $3,182 |

For a family doing a handful of weekend trips plus one or two longer cross-country reaches (Sequoia, Canyonlands) a year, **4,000-7,000 miles/year and a $1,500-$2,700 annual fuel budget** is a realistic planning range — meaningfully cheaper than the old Class A framing simply because the Class C is a lighter, more efficient rig, and cheaper still if the used coach turns out to carry the newer 7.3L engine.

## Tires and maintenance: E-450/E-350-specific numbers

A Class C on a Ford cutaway chassis is mechanically simpler and cheaper to keep than a Class A or diesel pusher — this is one of the real financial upsides of the pivot, not just a driving-comfort one.

**Tires.** Both the E-450 and E-350 run **16"-19.5" tires** depending on trim and GVWR rating — most 28'+ E-450 Class C units run **19.5" commercial-rated tires**, budget **$1,500-$2,500 for a full set of six** ([tire cost guide by RV class](https://tooltroopers.com/blogs/tooltroopers/how-much-do-rv-tires-cost-by-class)), the same figure as a gas Class A since it's largely the same tire size and load range. Lighter E-350-based units may run smaller, cheaper 16" light-truck tires. **Replace on a 5-7 year age schedule regardless of tread** — rubber degrades chemically, and a blowout on a loaded coach is a safety event, not just an inconvenience. Check the DOT date code on any used unit ([used-buying and inspection](04-used-buying-inspection.html)) — on a used coach already 6-9 years old at purchase, near-term tire replacement should be assumed, not hoped against.

**Chassis maintenance.** A Ford E-Series/Super Duty gas cutaway is one of the most widely serviced chassis in the country — any Ford dealer or independent shop can turn a wrench on it, and it's squarely within Doug's own DIY comfort range (oil, filters, brakes). Budget **$150-$300 per visit**, roughly annually or every 5,000-7,500 miles.

**Roof, seals, and the systems layer.** Independent of chassis, the coach body ages like a house — roof membrane, plumbing, propane. DIY roof resealant (Dicor lap sealant) runs **$50-$200 in materials** twice a year; a professional inspection/reseal runs **$250-$600 at a dealer, $150-$400 mobile**, per [RV Life Guide](https://rv-life-guide.com/rv-maintenance-costs/). This is the highest-leverage preventive line item on the whole list — a neglected roof seal is the #1 cause of the water-intrusion damage covered in [used-buying and inspection](04-used-buying-inspection.html), and it applies identically regardless of coach class.

**Generator.** Budget **$150-$500+/year** (oil, filters, plugs on a gas Onan-type unit) — non-negotiable given the genuine boondocking plans in [power & boondocking](07-power-boondocking.html).

**Winterizing (NJ-specific).** Central NJ gets hard freezes, so winterizing before the rig sits idle is mandatory. Professional winterizing runs **$85-$150** (a NJ shop quoted $125 plus antifreeze, per [Get RV Winterizing](https://getrvwinterizing.com/s/nj/rv-and-motorhome-winterizing)), dewinterizing **$75-$200** professionally or **$80-$100 DIY**. Doug can likely do both himself for under $100/year.

**Realistic all-in annual maintenance for a used Class C in this band: $2,200-$4,000/year**, at the lower end of the general $3,000-7,000/year RV maintenance range cited by [RV Life Guide](https://rv-life-guide.com/rv-maintenance-costs/) because a gas cutaway chassis is simple, parts and labor are cheap and everywhere, and Doug DIYs winterizing and basic service. A widely-echoed discipline worth adopting regardless of the exact number: **take your estimated annual maintenance cost, add a 20% buffer, and divide by 12** to fund a monthly reserve rather than treating repairs as surprises, per [OGRVS](https://ogrvs.com/rv-maintenance-costs-what-youll-really-spend/). A used coach at 6-9+ years old at purchase should run toward the higher end of this range in any year a major system (roof, awning, appliance, or the tire set above) comes due.

## Storage: driveway in Brick first, paid storage as the fallback

The plan is the same regardless of coach class: keep the rig **at home — likely the driveway, outside, in Brick, NJ.** Whether that's actually allowed is a local-ordinance question with its own full treatment: **[the rig at home in Brick, NJ](20-rv-at-home-brick-nj.html)** covers Brick Township's specific rules (§ 288-8 of Chapter 288) on driveway RV storage — size, sight-triangle setbacks, registration status, and the specific conditions that make the plan "likely fine, subject to confirmation." A **Class C is, if anything, an easier case** for that ordinance than a 35-foot Class A was under the old framing, since most Class C candidates in this band run 27-33 feet.

**If home storage isn't permitted (or a specific coach doesn't fit the lot),** paid storage becomes a real recurring cost. New Jersey storage rates by type, per [storage-cost surveys](https://homeguide.com/costs/rv-storage-cost) and NJ listings:

- **Outdoor, uncovered:** $30-$50/month — cheapest, full weather exposure; fine with a good cover and diligent roof/seal upkeep.
- **Outdoor, covered (carport-style):** $30-$100/month — meaningfully extends roof-seal and UV life for a modest premium.
- **Indoor:** $50-$125+/month — best protection, real premium, and often a waitlist near the Shore/Central Jersey corridor in summer.

**Planning number if you end up paying:** **$50-$100/month ($600-$1,200/year)** for a solid covered-outdoor or basic indoor spot near Brick — unchanged from the old framing, since storage rates are driven by facility type and local market, not coach class. If the driveway works, this line drops to near zero.

## Campground costs: NPS vs. state parks vs. private — the numbers that drive the usage table below

This is the section that didn't exist in the old TCO framing, because the mission itself has changed: the primary use case is now **national and state park camping**, including long cross-country reaches (Sequoia, Canyonlands), not just a mostly-hookups pattern with occasional boondocking. Three tiers, three very different price points:

**National Park Service (NPS) campgrounds.** Fees vary enormously by park and popularity, but a consistent pattern emerges from primary sources: **non-electric standard sites commonly run $25-$36/night**, and **electric-hookup sites (where offered — many NPS campgrounds have none) commonly run $40-$55/night.** Rocky Mountain National Park, for example, charges **$35/night for a standard non-electric site in summer and $55/night for one of its limited electric sites** ([NPS: Rocky Mountain National Park camping](https://www.nps.gov/romo/planyourvisit/camping.htm)). Sequoia & Kings Canyon — one of the family's named target destinations — charges a flat **$32/night** at most of its developed campgrounds (Potwisha, Azalea, Crystal Springs, Sunset, Lodgepole), none of which offer hookups ([NPS: Sequoia & Kings Canyon camping](https://www.nps.gov/seki/planyourvisit/campgrounds.htm)). **Planning number: $30-$40/night at most NPS campgrounds, understanding many of those nights will be dry (no hookups)** — which is exactly why the boondocking capability from [power & boondocking](07-power-boondocking.html) matters as much for the national-park mission as it does for cost.

**State parks (New Jersey and beyond).** New Jersey's own state park system charges **$20/night for standard sites (NJ residents) and $25/night (non-residents)**, with wilderness sites cheaper still (**$17 resident / $22 non-resident**) and primitive sites priced per-person (**$3-$5**), per the [NJ DEP's official fee schedule](https://dep.nj.gov/parksandforests/related-fees/) — though this page's fee table does not break out electric-hookup pricing specifically; check [ReserveAmerica's NJ listings](https://www.reserveamerica.com/explore/search-results?searchTerm=New%2BJersey&type=state&stateCode=NJ) for the per-site hookup surcharge on a specific park before budgeting. Nationally, state and county parks broadly run **$25-$45/night**, cheaper than NPS electric sites and meaningfully cheaper than private campgrounds, but with fewer amenities and stiffer summer-weekend booking competition — which matters on a family's schedule even without a school calendar forcing peak-season travel.

**Private campgrounds (KOA and similar).** The private tier is the most expensive and the most amenity-rich. **KOA Holiday** properties (the family-friendly, full-hookup tier) run **$55-$75/night in peak season**, dropping to roughly **$50-$68/night effective** after the 10% KOA Rewards discount; **KOA Resort** properties in high-demand corridors run **$85-$130+/night**, per [RV Troop's 2026 KOA cost survey](https://rvtroop.com/koa-campground-cost) and [Camper Report's KOA pricing guide](https://camperreport.com/average-koa-campground-costs-a-guide-with-30-example-prices/). Private campgrounds are the reliable fallback for full hookups, pools, and playgrounds the kids will genuinely enjoy — but at 2-3x the NPS/state rate.

**The blended planning number.** For a family mixing all three tiers — national/state parks for the marquee destinations, private campgrounds for convenience stretches and shoulder-season certainty — a reasonable blended average across a real season of travel is **$40-$55/night**, weighted toward the cheaper public-land tiers given the mission is explicitly parks-first. Membership discounts help at the margins:

- **Good Sam ($30/year) — worth it.** 10% off at a large private network plus fuel discounts at Pilot/Flying J.
- **Passport America (~$44/year) — worth it.** 50% off at participating campgrounds, often weekday/2-night-max restricted — a good fit for a homeschooled family that can travel midweek.
- **America the Beautiful Pass ($80/year) — worth it given the NPS-heavy mission.** Covers entrance fees at NPS sites and provides a **50% campsite discount** for Senior/Access pass holders; the standard annual pass covers entrance but check current-year rules on the campsite discount specifically, since it's traditionally tied to the Senior/Access tiers rather than the standard annual pass ([NPS fee guidance](https://www.nps.gov/romo/planyourvisit/camping.htm) references a 50% discount for Senior/Access Pass holders specifically — verify against the standard pass before counting on it).
- **Harvest Hosts + Boondockers Welcome (~$180/year combined) — a genuine fit** given the real boondocking requirement between park stays; free or low-cost overnights at farms, wineries, and private driveways.
- **Thousand Trails ($400-$800+ entry) — likely NOT a fit for this mission.** It pays off through *volume at Thousand Trails' own private-park network*, which pulls the opposite direction from a parks-first, national/state-park travel plan. Skip it.

Add it up for a realistic year on a **$75,000 rig (mid-band), financed with ~18% down (~$62,000 financed) at 8.5% over 12 years**, doing 5,000-6,000 miles a year of mixed park-and-highway driving. Unlike the old six-figure Class A framing, the loan payment is smaller in absolute terms here, which is exactly the point of the pivot.

|---|---|
| Loan payment ($62k financed @ 8.5%, 12 yr) | ~$8,330 |
| Insurance | $700-$1,400 |
| Storage (driveway in Brick ≈ $0; paid fallback) | $0-$1,200 |

None of this is a reason to reconsider — a used Class C in this band is a genuinely sound match for a national/state-park-first mission with a lighter, more maneuverable coach and a real drop in financed risk versus the old six-figure Class A framing. But the honest **~$13,500-$17,200/year "keep it running and stored" number is before a single campground night is booked** — and given the mission is explicitly built around real park travel, nights-per-year is not a footnote here, it's the other half of the budget. ## Per-night cost at different usage levels

This is the number the next document — usage cadence and how many nights a year this family actually travels — builds directly on. Using the **subtotal before campground nights (~$15,350/year, the midpoint of the range above)** and the **blended campground rate of $40-55/night**, here is what a year of Class C ownership costs at four different usage levels, expressed as an effective **cost per night actually used**:

| Nights/year | Fixed costs (loan+ins+storage+fuel¹+maint+memberships) | Campground fees @ $40-55/night | Total annual cost | Effective cost per night |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 20 nights/yr | ~$15,350 | $800-$1,100 | ~$16,150-$16,450 | **~$808-$823/night** |
| 40 nights/yr | ~$15,350 | $1,600-$2,200 | ~$16,950-$17,550 | **~$424-$439/night** |

¹ Fuel scales modestly with nights used too (more nights generally means more driving), but the fixed-cost subtotal above already assumes a representative 5,500 mi/year across a real travel pattern; treat the per-night figures as directionally right rather than exact at the low and high ends of the range.

**The one-line takeaway:** the fixed costs (loan, insurance, storage, base maintenance) don't move much with usage — they're paid whether the coach goes out 20 nights or 80 — so **the single biggest lever this family has over the effective cost of ownership is simply using the rig more.** A coach that's driveway-decoration for 20 nights a year costs roughly **3.5x more per night** than the identical coach put to work for 80 nights. This is the frame the next document — [travel cadence and how many nights a year this family will realistically hit](27-honest-math.html) — picks up directly: the financial case for this purchase gets dramatically stronger the more it actually gets used, which makes the cadence question a money question, not just a lifestyle one.
