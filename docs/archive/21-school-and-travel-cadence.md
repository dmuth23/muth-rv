# School Timing and Travel Cadence: How Often, and When, This Family Actually Travels

Almost every other document in this docket is about *what to buy*. This one is about *how you'll use it* — the rhythm of your travel year — and it matters just as much, because cadence quietly drives the money. A coach that sleeps in a Brick driveway 50 weekends a year and a coach that rolls for four months straight are the same purchase making completely different financial sense. And for this family the cadence question has a hard deadline built in: the kids, both born in March 2022, are heading for kindergarten in the middle of your shopping window. Once school starts, the calendar stops being fully yours.

This is a decision Doug and Sara are actively working through, so this page isn't here to pick an answer. It's here to lay out the timeline honestly — including where the *law* draws lines versus where you simply have *choices* — sketch the realistic cadence archetypes with their genuine trade-offs, report what veteran RV families with school-age kids actually say about each, and then close the loop back to the purchase math.

## The timeline: when do the kids actually have to be in school?

Two separate rules govern this, and they are easy to blur. One sets **when a child *may* start kindergarten** (a local school-district age cutoff). The other sets **when a child is *legally required* to be in school at all** (a state compulsory-attendance statute). They are not the same age, and the gap between them is exactly the window this family can exploit.

### What the law requires — the district's kindergarten age cutoff

In New Jersey, individual districts set their own kindergarten entry-age cutoff, and **Brick Township Public Schools requires that a child be five years old on or before October 1** of the school year in which they enroll ([Brick Township Public Schools, Registration](https://www.brickschools.org/our-district/registration-2/), reported via [Brick, NJ Patch — Kindergarten Registration Underway](https://patch.com/new-jersey/brick/kindergarten-registration-underway-brick-schools)). The district notes there are no exceptions to this age requirement, and proof of age is an original birth certificate.

Run your kids' March-2022 birthdays against that cutoff:

- **September 2026** — the kids are 4, turning 5 the following March. They are **too young** for kindergarten this year; they miss the October 1 cutoff by months.
- **September 2027** — both kids turned 5 in March 2027, so both are **comfortably over five by October 1, 2027**. This is their **on-time kindergarten year**.

So the most likely reality — for both children, regardless of the size difference between them — is **kindergarten beginning September 2027.** (The bigger-vs-smaller-for-age gap between your two kids affects clothing, car-seat thresholds, and playground dynamics, not this date: the cutoff keys off the birth certificate, not the growth chart, and both kids share a birth month.)

### What the law requires — New Jersey's compulsory-attendance age

Here is the part that gives you a year of freedom. New Jersey's compulsory-attendance statute, [N.J.S.A. 18A:38-25](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-18a/section-18a-38-25/), requires attendance only of **children "between six and 16 years"** — its title is literally "Attendance required of children between six and 16." The operative text: *"Every parent, guardian or other person having custody and control of a child between the ages of six and 16 years shall cause such child regularly to attend the public schools of the district or a day school in which there is given instruction equivalent to that provided in the public schools ... or to receive equivalent instruction elsewhere than at school."* Below age six, attendance is **not compelled by law**.

Your kids turn six in **March 2028.** So the legal picture across the window looks like this:

| School year | Kids' age | Kindergarten status | Legally *compelled* to attend? |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2026–27 | 4 → 5 | Too young to enroll | **No** — under 6 |
| 2027–28 | 5 → 6 (in Mar 2028) | On-time K year | **Not until they turn 6 in March 2028** |
| 2028–29 | 6 → 7 | 1st grade | **Yes** |

Read that middle row carefully, because it's the crux. In **2027–28**, kindergarten is available and is the normal, expected path — but for most of that year the kids are still five, and **NJ law does not compel their attendance until their sixth birthday in March 2028.** That means the 2026–27 year is entirely free of school constraints, and even the 2027–28 kindergarten year has legal give in it if you ever wanted a long mid-year trip. Kindergarten in New Jersey is, in the strict legal sense, **optional** — the mandate begins at six.

> **A necessary caution — law is not the same as advice.** That the law permits something is not a recommendation to do it. Pulling a child out of an enrolled kindergarten mid-year, or delaying entry, is a real decision with academic and social weight, and it's Doug and Sara's to make — likely in conversation with the school. This section only establishes *what the statute allows*, so the family can decide from facts instead of assumptions. The strong practical read: **2026–27 is genuinely, legally free travel time; from September 2027 onward, school is the default and any deviation is a deliberate family choice, not a formality.**

### The one clean implication

The single most actionable fact on this page: **the 2026–27 travel year is a gift.** The kids are too young for kindergarten and under the compulsory-attendance age, so a coach bought in late 2026 buys a full year of go-anywhere-anytime family travel with zero school calendar — before the gate comes down in September 2027. That's a concrete argument for buying earlier rather than later, developed at the end of this page.

## The cadence archetypes

Once school is in the picture (September 2027 on), families settle into recognizable rhythms. None is "correct" — each trades money, mileage, and family time differently. Here are the four that fit your situation, honestly rendered.

### 1. The weekender + school-break family

**The rhythm:** Fridays-to-Sundays in the shoulder seasons, plus the bigger blocks the school calendar hands you — a week at Thanksgiving, the winter break, spring break, and the long runway of summer. The coach lives in the Brick driveway between trips and never travels far on a school night.

**Pros:** Fully compatible with conventional school enrollment — no roadschooling, no district conversations, no academic risk. Trips stay close to home (the Northeast and mid-Atlantic are full of good weekend-range destinations), so drive fatigue in a 35-foot coach stays low. Easiest cadence for Sara if she's not driving the coach, since legs are short.

**Cons:** This is the **lowest-utilization** pattern, and utilization is what justifies a six-figure purchase. If the coach only rolls 20–30 nights a year, your cost-per-night is brutal (see the money section below). Weekend-only use also means you're constantly doing setup and teardown — 30–60 minutes each way — for a payoff of a single night or two.

### 2. The summer-touring family

**The rhythm:** The coach mostly sleeps from September to June, then does one or two long, ambitious trips across the summer — think two-to-six weeks touring national parks, or a slow northern New England / Maritimes loop, or a westward push.

**Pros:** Concentrates use into the window when the kids are out of school and the weather cooperates, so it sidesteps the school calendar entirely. A single big summer trip can rack up more nights than a whole year of weekending, improving cost-per-night. It's the classic "family memories" cadence.

**Cons:** Summer is peak everything — peak crowds, peak prices, and peak reservation competition (see below; popular Northeast and national-park sites can vanish minutes after the booking window opens). It also front-loads all your RV wear and stress into a few intense weeks, and leaves the asset idle — but still costing you in payments, insurance, and storage — for nine months.

### 3. The shoulder-season opportunist — your genuine superpower

**The rhythm:** Travel in the "off-peak" edges — May, June, September, October — when Doug works remotely from the coach and the family simply relocates for a week or two at a time to less-crowded, cheaper campgrounds. Fewer summer-peak trips, more spring and fall.

**Pros:** This is the cadence your specific circumstances unlock better than most families'. **Doug works from home with no employer location rules** ([see WFH & Connectivity](../06-wfh-connectivity.html)), which means the family isn't chained to school-holiday travel dates the way a two-office-job family is. Shoulder-season sites are cheaper, emptier, and dramatically easier to reserve, and shoulder weather in much of the country is better for RVing than blazing July. **The catch — and it's the whole catch — is that this superpower expires when the kids start kindergarten in September 2027.** During 2026–27 it's unrestricted. After that, shoulder-season weekday travel means either aligning with school breaks or making a roadschooling choice. So the shoulder-season pattern is *most* powerful in exactly the free year you already have.

**Cons:** After September 2027 it collides with the school calendar unless you homeschool. Spring and fall in the Northeast can also be weather-variable (cold snaps, mud season). And it still leans on Doug being able to deliver full workdays from the coach — a thing the [rent-before-you-buy trial](../13-rent-before-you-buy.html) is specifically designed to test.

### 4. The full-time / roadschool path (context, not a recommendation)

**The rhythm:** The coach *is* the home, or close to it, and school happens on the road ("roadschooling"). Families do this for a season or for years.

**The legal footing in NJ is unusually light.** New Jersey is one of the least-regulated homeschooling states in the country. Under the same statute above ([N.J.S.A. 18A:38-25](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-18a/section-18a-38-25/)), a child may "receive equivalent instruction elsewhere than at school," and the New Jersey Department of Education's own guidance confirms the practical reality: **the district is not authorized to review or approve your curriculum, is not authorized to test a homeschooled child, and the parent is not required to be a certified teacher** ([NJ DOE, Homeschooling FAQ](https://www.nj.gov/education/safety/nontraditional/faq_homeschool.shtml)). Notification to the district is required only in narrow situations (e.g., withdrawing an already-enrolled high-school student, or if a district wrongly refuses enrollment) — there is no routine registration, testing, or reporting burden for a family teaching young children at home. The only legal standard is **academic equivalency**, and enforcement is minimal.

**Why it's here as context only:** the light legal footing means the *option* exists cleanly in NJ if the family ever wants a long trip during the school year — you would not be fighting the state to do it. But full-timing with two young kids is a genuine lifestyle change, not a scheduling tweak, and nothing in this docket recommends it. It's included so the family knows the door is legally open, not because you should walk through it.

## What veteran RV families actually report

*The following are community experiences from RV-family blogs and outlets, not verified statistics — read them as seasoned anecdote, weighted accordingly.*

- **On the part-time / summer-and-breaks rhythm:** community write-ups consistently frame renting-and-touring over summer break as the low-commitment on-ramp, and describe families running a traditional curriculum three or four days a week with the rest reserved for field trips when they do travel longer — a middle path between full-time and staying home ([Cheapism, Homeschooling in an RV](https://www.cheapism.com/homeschooling-in-an-rv/); community experience).
- **On roadschooling working as a rhythm, not just a curriculum:** long-running family blogs like The Wanderpreneurs (five-plus years full-timing) report that the successful pattern is a *loose, consistent* schedule — a few focused academic days, travel and exploration on the others — rather than trying to replicate a 9-to-3 classroom on the road ([The Wanderpreneurs, 5th Year of Roadschooling](https://www.thewanderpreneurs.com/blog/our-5th-year-of-homeschooling-in-an-rv-2022-2023); community experience).
- **On not being alone:** the roadschooling community is large and organized — Fulltime Families runs a roadschooling Facebook group with tens of thousands of members trading itineraries, curriculum, and campground tips ([Outdoorsy, Complete Guide to Roadschooling](https://www.outdoorsy.com/blog/the-complete-guide-to-roadschooling); community experience). For a family that WFHs and might dabble in shoulder-season travel, this is a real support network, not a fringe.
- **On the value of the pre-school years:** across these accounts, the recurring note from families is that the *pre-kindergarten* window is the easiest time to travel — no curriculum obligations, maximum flexibility — which lines up exactly with the free 2026–27 year the law hands this family ([Outdoorsy, Complete Guide to Roadschooling](https://www.outdoorsy.com/blog/the-complete-guide-to-roadschooling); community experience).

The honest through-line: families who travel *more* nights per year are happier with the purchase economics, and the ones who default to weekend-only use most often regret how little the expensive coach actually gets used.

## How cadence feeds back into the purchase

Cadence isn't a lifestyle footnote — it's a direct input to whether this purchase makes financial sense, and even to *when* you should buy.

### Usage days versus cost-per-night

The math is unforgiving and simple: the annual cost of owning the coach (financing, insurance, storage, and depreciation — detailed in [Total Cost of Ownership](../10-ownership-costs.html)) is largely **fixed whether you travel 20 nights or 120.** Divide that fixed annual number by nights used and you get your true cost-per-night — and it swings enormously with cadence.

A weekender family logging ~25 nights a year is paying several times more per night than a summer-touring or shoulder-season family logging 80–100 nights against the same fixed costs. This is the strongest financial argument *against* buying if the honest expectation is weekend-only use — and the strongest argument *for* the higher-cadence patterns if the family genuinely wants to travel. **Before committing six figures, put a realistic nights-per-year number on paper and run it through the [ownership-costs](../10-ownership-costs.html) math.** The [rent-before-you-buy trial](../13-rent-before-you-buy.html) is also the cleanest way to discover your family's *real* appetite for nights-on-the-road before the purchase locks it in.

### Reservation lead times shape the summer patterns

If your cadence leans on summer, understand the booking reality up front. Most Recreation.gov federal campgrounds release sites on a **rolling six-month advance window** — reservation dates become available daily at **10:00 a.m. Eastern, six months ahead**, so a July 15 arrival becomes bookable around January 15 — and Recreation.gov itself warns that the most popular sites "can book up in minutes" ([Recreation.gov — Tips for Making Campsite Reservations at Popular Locations](https://www.recreation.gov/articles/list/tips-for-making-campsite-reservations-at-popular-locations/281); [Recreation.gov booking guide, Campnab](https://campnab.com/blog/your-ultimate-guide-to-booking-a-campsite-on-recreation-gov)). This is a strong point *for* the shoulder-season pattern: October and May sites are far easier to reserve than peak July, with better odds and lower prices. See [Trip Planning & Driving](../11-trip-planning-driving.html) for the full booking-lead-time and routing detail; the takeaway for cadence is that a summer-only family must plan bookings roughly half a year out, while a shoulder-season family enjoys far more spontaneity.

### The argument for buying earlier — the free 2026–27 year

Pull the timeline and the money together and a genuinely useful conclusion falls out. **Buying in late 2026 unlocks the one school-free travel year this family will have for the next decade.** In 2026–27 the kids are under the compulsory-attendance age, too young for kindergarten, and Doug's location-free WFH means the family can travel weekdays, shoulder seasons, anytime — the shoulder-season superpower at full strength, with no calendar fighting back. That's a concrete, time-limited reason not to let the purchase drift to 2028.

The counter-argument is real too: the purchase window runs through 2028 precisely so the family isn't forced to rush into the wrong coach, and a better deal or a better-inspected unit is worth waiting for (see [Used Buying & Inspection](../04-used-buying-inspection.html)). **The resolution isn't "buy now no matter what" — it's "know what waiting costs."** Waiting past mid-2027 doesn't just risk a worse deal; it spends the single free travel year the calendar will ever hand you at these ages. That's a cost worth naming out loud in the buy-timing conversation, even if the family ultimately decides a great 2028 coach is worth missing it.

## The bottom line for Doug and Sara

- **The dates are settled by fact, not preference:** on-time kindergarten is **September 2027** (Brick's five-by-October-1 cutoff), and **compulsory attendance doesn't legally begin until the kids turn six in March 2028** (N.J.S.A. 18A:38-25).
- **2026–27 is a legally free, school-unconstrained travel year** — the easiest travel the family will have for years, and a real argument for buying earlier.
- **The shoulder-season pattern is your standout superpower** thanks to location-free WFH — but it's at full strength only until kindergarten starts.
- **Cadence drives the money.** Higher nights-per-year is what makes a six-figure coach pencil out; weekend-only use is the pattern most families regret. Decide your honest nights-per-year *before* you buy, using the [rent trial](../13-rent-before-you-buy.html) to find the real number.
- **Roadschooling is legally easy in NJ** if you ever want it — context, not a recommendation.
